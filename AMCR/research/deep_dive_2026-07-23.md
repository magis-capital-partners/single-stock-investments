## What this business is
Amcor plc (NYSE: **AMCR**) is one of the world's largest packaging manufacturers. **[Fact]** It develops and produces flexible packaging (films, pouches, lidding), rigid packaging (bottles, containers, closures), and specialty cartons for food, beverage, healthcare, home care, and personal-care customers. **[Fact]** The company completed an all-stock merger with **Berry Global Group** on **April 30, 2025**, exchanging **7.25** Amcor ordinary shares for each Berry share and expanding the listed equity base to about **462 million** shares outstanding at March 31, 2026. **[Fact]** FY2025 net sales were **$15.0 billion** (up **10%** versus the prior year on a reported basis) and pro forma combined revenue would have been about **$23.2 billion** per the acquisition footnote in the FY2025 annual report. **[Fact]** The business reports two operating segments: **Flexibles** and **Rigid Packaging**, with nine-month FY2026 segment net sales of **$8.0 billion** and **$2.4 billion**, respectively. **[Inference]** Owner economics are real but noisy in the integration year: FY2025 operating cash flow was **$1.39 billion** while capital spending was **$0.58 billion**, implying about **$1.75 per share** of owner cash on the current share count, before merger-related restructuring and elevated integration capex in recent quarters.

## Why the market might be wrong
There is **no classic dated mispricing signal** (HK predictive attribute: **none**). **[Inference]** Generalists may still price Amcor as a low-growth commodity packager with merger indigestion, underweighting the step-change in scale, customer diversification, and synergy potential from Berry. **[Fact]** Nine-month FY2026 net sales rose **72%** to **$17.1 billion** and operating income rose **36%** to **$1.25 billion** versus the prior-year period, showing the combined revenue base is already flowing through the income statement. **[Inference]** Bears may overweight near-term earnings dilution (FY2025 diluted continuing EPS **$0.32** versus **$0.51** prior year) and **$13.8 billion** of long-term debt without giving credit to resin pass-through mechanics and multi-year cost synergy programs typical in packaging roll-ups. Return here is **normalized owner cash at price through integration**, not a sum-of-the-parts on goodwill.

## Risks & inversion
**Primary risk:** **Berry integration and synergy shortfall** leave elevated leverage (**$13.8 billion** long-term debt) without proportional owner-cash growth.

- **Resin/raw-material spread compression** if pass-through lags spot prices.
- **Goodwill and intangible impairment** if rigid packaging growth disappoints ($10.4 billion goodwill from Berry).
- **Equity dilution** from post-merger offerings (424B5 filings) if cash flow trails debt service.

**Inversion:** If we are wrong, Amcor is a must-own packaging oligopoly trading like a bond-proxy: you collect a mid-single-digit return while synergies quietly compound and the market eventually re-rates scale.

**Lens failure mode:** Operating lens fails if leverage forces a distressed restructuring (levered lens) or if synergy value is better expressed as a breakup SOTP (event lens). Filings do not support switching primary lens today.
