## What this business is

Amcor plc (NYSE: **AMCR**) is one of the world's largest packaging manufacturers. **[Fact]** It develops and produces flexible packaging (films, pouches, lidding), rigid packaging (bottles, containers, closures), and specialty cartons for food, beverage, healthcare, home care, and personal-care customers. **[Fact]** The company completed an all-stock merger with **Berry Global Group** on **April 30, 2025**, exchanging **7.25** Amcor ordinary shares for each Berry share and roughly doubling the equity base to about **2.3 billion** shares outstanding. **[Fact]** FY2025 net sales were **$15.0 billion** (up **10%** versus the prior year on a reported basis) and pro forma combined revenue would have been about **$23.2 billion** per the acquisition footnote in the FY2025 annual report. **[Fact]** The business reports two operating segments: **Flexibles** and **Rigid Packaging**, with nine-month FY2026 segment net sales of **$8.0 billion** and **$2.4 billion**, respectively. **[Inference]** Owner economics are real but noisy in the integration year: FY2025 operating cash flow was **$1.39 billion** while capital spending was **$0.58 billion**, before merger-related restructuring and elevated integration capex in recent quarters.

## Executive summary

Amcor is a global packaging compounder that just absorbed Berry Global, creating a **~$23 billion** revenue platform across flexibles and rigids. FY2025 operating cash flow was **$1.39 billion**; after **$0.58 billion** of capital spending, owner cash is about **$0.48 per share** on a normalized post-merger share count of **2.3 billion** (between filed FY2025 cash flow and pro forma scale). Nine-month FY2026 results show revenue and operating income growing on the combined base, but leverage and integration costs keep near-term free cash conversion uneven.

At today's price, the Lawrence base case implies about **8% per year** on normalized owner cash, below our **15%** accumulate hurdle. The business carries a **stable** durable advantage (Munger moat) from scale, qualification barriers, and customer intimacy, with **partial** asymmetric payoff (Pabrai dhando): downside is bounded by essential packaging demand, but upside requires synergy delivery and deleveraging. Marvin proposes **watch** until integration proof points stack up or the market offers a better entry. No approved external sources are in the base case.

## Risks & inversion

**Primary risk:** **Berry integration and synergy shortfall** leave elevated leverage (**$13.8 billion** long-term debt) without proportional owner-cash growth.

- **Resin/raw-material spread compression** if pass-through lags spot prices.
- **Goodwill and intangible impairment** if rigid packaging growth disappoints ($10.4 billion goodwill from Berry).
- **Equity dilution** from post-merger offerings (424B5 filings) if cash flow trails debt service.

**Inversion:** If we are wrong, Amcor is a must-own packaging oligopoly trading like a bond-proxy: you collect a mid-single-digit return while synergies quietly compound and the market eventually re-rates scale.

**Lens failure mode:** Operating lens fails if leverage forces a distressed restructuring (levered lens) or if synergy value is better expressed as a breakup SOTP (event lens). Filings do not support switching primary lens today.
