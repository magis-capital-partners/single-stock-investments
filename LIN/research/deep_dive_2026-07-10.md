## What this business is
Linde plc (LIN) is the world's largest industrial gases company, formed from the 2018 merger of Praxair and Linde AG. **[Fact]** It sells atmospheric and specialty gases through on-site plants (long take-or-pay contracts), merchant bulk delivery, and packaged cylinders to customers in manufacturing, healthcare, electronics, chemicals, and energy. **[Fact]** FY2025 sales were **$34.0 billion** (+3% versus FY2024), with four reportable segments: Americas, APAC, EMEA, and Linde Engineering. **[Fact]** CEO Sanjiv Lamba leads an Irish-domiciled company listed on Nasdaq; headquarters are in the United Kingdom and United States.

---

## Why the market might be wrong
The market may treat Linde as a bond-proxy quality compounder and pay a premium multiple for oligopoly stability, underweighting that **$4.6 billion** of FY2025 share repurchases and rising long-term debt (**$20.7 billion** versus **$15.3 billion** prior year) compress future per-share growth if industrial volumes soften. **[Inference]** Generalists may anchor on adjusted earnings growth (+7% FY2025 diluted earnings per share) while the stock trades near **49 times** GAAP free cash flow per share at **$531.30**, leaving little margin of safety. **[Fact]** Q1 2026 sales rose **8%** and diluted earnings per share **13%**, so near-term fundamentals are strong; there is no dated predictive attribute, only a **descriptive** premium-multiple setup.

---

## Executive summary
Linde is the global leader in industrial gases with durable on-site contracts, pricing power, and consistent capital return through dividends and large buybacks. At **$531.30** per share (2026-07-10), base expected annual return is **-2.11%** per year on FY2025 free cash flow of **$10.78** per share, far below our **15%** hurdle. Durable competitive advantage (Munger moat) is **stable**: oligopoly structure and high switching costs on installed plants. Asymmetric payoff (Pabrai dhando) is **partial**: strong cash generation and dividend limit distress, but leverage-funded buybacks at a full price cap upside. We propose **watch** until either price dislocates or owner cash per share compounds faster than the current multiple implies.

### Mental models in plain English

**Oligopoly supply (Munger):** Three global players dominate merchant and on-site gases. **[Fact]** Switching an on-site hydrogen, nitrogen, or oxygen plant is costly and slow, supporting multi-decade contracts (customer supply agreements up to 30 years per 10-K disclosures).

**Density and scale (Stahl):** Post-merger route density lowers delivery cost per unit in merchant bulk and cylinders. **[Inference]** Scale supports margin leadership versus regional independents, though not immunity from industrial recessions.

**Capital return discipline (Pabrai/MOI):** Management returned **$4.6 billion** via repurchases and **$2.8 billion** in dividends in FY2025 while investing **$5.3 billion** in capital spending. **[Fact]** Long-term debt rose **$5.3 billion** net, funding buybacks as well as growth capex. **[Inference]** Incentive alignment is generally positive on return on capital, but leverage adds a bear-case tail **[HUMAN REVIEW]** if buybacks continue at peak prices.

## Risks & inversion
**Primary risk:** Paying a premium multiple for a quality oligopoly while debt-funded buybacks raise leverage into an industrial downturn, leaving equity with low returns despite stable operations.

- **Leverage:** Long-term debt rose **35%** in FY2025 to **$20.7 billion** while repurchasing **$4.6 billion** of stock (10-K FY2025).
- **Cyclical volumes:** Merchant and packaged gas volumes track industrial production; recession would slow growth assumptions.
- **Capital intensity:** Capital spending **$5.3 billion** must stay productive to sustain **$10+** free cash flow per share.
- **Inversion (Munger):** What kills the thesis? Two years of flat owner cash per share while buybacks continue above **$4 billion** and the stock stays above **35 times** free cash flow would force bear-case growth and erode dhando to **none**.

**Lens failure mode (operating):** If normalized owner cash stalls near **$11** per share while the stock stays above **$450**, the market is paying infrastructure scarcity prices without enough per-share cash growth to clear a **15%** return hurdle.
