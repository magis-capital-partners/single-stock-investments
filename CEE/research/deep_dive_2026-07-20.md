## What this business is

The Central and Eastern Europe Fund is a New York–listed closed-end fund run by DWS. It owns a portfolio of mostly Polish and Hungarian equities (banks, energy, insurers, retailers) plus a frozen Russian equity sleeve. Since 14 March 2022 the fund has carried those Russian holdings at zero under sanctions and capital controls, even though the share counts are still disclosed. The publicly reported net asset value therefore describes only the liquid book. The share price usually tracks that reported figure closely.

## Why the market might be wrong

The interesting mispricing is not the thin discount to reported net asset value. It is that reported net asset value itself may understate economic ownership by writing the entire Russian book to zero. The predictive attribute is a Level-3 / sanctions zero mark: local Moscow prices still exist for many names, but a United States fund cannot freely realize them. If any path to recovery, private sale, or eventual unblocking opens, that sleeve is a free (or cheap) option on top of a solvent Central European equity portfolio. Buybacks when the fund trades below reported net asset value are a secondary catalyst, not the main edge.

## Executive summary

CEE is a shadow-net-asset-value idea disguised as a near-par closed-end fund. At about $20.48 versus reported net asset value of about $21.32, the stock looks only a few percent cheap. That comparison ignores Russia. Using the last detailed schedule (July 2025) and current Moscow exchange prints, a mark-to-market illustration adds roughly $5 to $9 per share of Russian equity value before any friction. That implies an economic net asset value near the mid-$20s to about $30, or a mid-20s to low-30s percent discount to price. The base case in this report still values Russia at zero until a human sets a recovery probability, so the published base return is only about **4% per year** from liquid-book growth with a sticky reported discount. The asymmetric payoff (Pabrai dhando) is the Russia option: downside is mostly the liquid Central European book and fees; upside is any non-zero recovery. Stance stays **watch** until recovery odds and share-count freshness are reviewed.

## Business & moat

This is a permanent-capital equity wrapper, not an operating company. DWS selects Central and Eastern European stocks; shareholders own a slice of that portfolio minus fees (net expense ratio about 1.26%). The liquid engine today is concentrated in Poland and Hungary (financials, energy, industrials). That book is Level 1 and is what reported net asset value reflects.

The Russia sleeve is the structural oddity. The July 2025 schedule still lists Sberbank, Gazprom, Lukoil, Magnit, Alrosa, and several depositary-receipt lines, all at zero, with footnote that valuation uses significant unobservable inputs. Cost basis on that sleeve was about $30.7 million when fund net assets were about $100 million. Some non-sanctioned depositary receipts were sold privately in 2024; remaining local shares and sanctioned receipts stay stuck. That is a classic option-scan hit: the accounting mark is not the economic claim.

There is no product moat at the fund level. Durability lives in the underlying issuers and in closed-end structure (no daily redemptions). Alignment tools include open-market repurchases when shares trade below reported net asset value. Fees and any future tender/liquidation friction are the structural drags.

## Risks & inversion

**Primary risk:** Russia never becomes realizable (sanctions permanent, expropriation, or forced local sales at a pittance). Then the shadow net asset value was a mirage and you only earned liquid-book returns minus fees.

**Upside / downside from price:** Downside is mostly the liquid Central European book near reported net asset value (~$21) minus fees and spreads. Upside is any non-zero Russia recovery on top of that floor (illustration ~$5 to $9 per share before friction).

- Share counts in the July 2025 schedule may be stale; depositary-receipt lines may not map one-for-one to local Moscow shares.
- Moscow prints are not dollars in a New York account.
- Expense ratio and wide spreads can erase a thin reported discount.
- Country beta: Poland/Hungary drawdowns hit the liquid floor.

**Lens failure mode:** Treating CEE as a plain closed-end discount to reported net asset value, or treating Moscow mark-to-market as cash you can spend tomorrow.

#### IRR arithmetic (show your work)

1. Price today = $20.48.
2. Reported net asset value = $21.32 → discount −3.9%. This is **not** the edge.
3. Base complete net asset value keeps Russia at $0, so floor ≈ $21.32.
4. Base five-year payoff ≈ price × (1.04)^5 ≈ $24.9 (liquid growth, sticky discount).
5. Annualized: ($24.9 / $20.48)^(1/5) − 1 ≈ **4% per year**.
6. Sensitivity (not base): if even half of the local-only Russia MTM (~$2.64/sh) were credited into a five-year payoff on top of the liquid path, economic upside rises sharply; probability and timing are **[HUMAN REVIEW]**.
7. Full local-only MTM NAV ~$26.60 would be about a 23% discount at today’s price; that gap is the shadow edge size, not a promised return.

**Returns statement:** About **4% per year** in the base case that keeps Russia at zero. The investment case is the Russia option on top of that floor, not the 4% itself.
