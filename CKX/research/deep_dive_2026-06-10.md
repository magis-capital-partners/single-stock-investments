## What this business is
CKX Lands, Inc. is a Louisiana land and mineral royalty company listed on NYSE American. **[Fact]** The company owns and manages approximately **7,023 net acres** across Louisiana parishes, earning passive income from oil and gas royalties, timber sales, surface easements, and occasional land sales. **[Fact]** CKX does not explore for or operate oil and gas wells; third-party operators produce on leased acreage while CKX collects royalties ranging from **0.0045%** to **7.62%** across **20** producing fields. **[Fact]** The company has **two** part-time employees, **no** debt, and **no** dividend program. **[Fact]** Following a **November 18, 2025** sale of **6,548** wholly-owned acres for **$8,618,022** cash, roughly **90%** of remaining net acres are co-owned undivided interests requiring unanimous co-owner consent for major decisions. **[Fact]** The Board has evaluated strategic alternatives since **August 2023**, including potential sale of the company or additional asset dispositions.

## Why the market might be wrong
The market may treat CKX as a stagnant micro-cap land stub whose earnings spike was a one-time land sale, missing the ongoing strategic-alternatives process and the cash-rich balance sheet relative to run-rate royalties. **[Fact]** FY2025 net income was **$3.0 million** (**$1.47** per share) but included a **$3,557,868** land-sale gain; operating profit excluding that gain was roughly **$83,000**. **[Fact]** Cash and equivalents totaled **$18.0 million** at **December 31, 2025** (**~$8.78** per share) against stockholders equity of **~$10.47** per share at **March 31, 2026**. **[Inference]** At **~$10.59** (June 10, 2026), the stock trades near GAAP book while cash alone covers a large fraction of the quote, with co-owned acreage and royalty streams as a free option. **[Inference]** Predictive attribute: **dormant asset** plus **market structure discount** (thin float, no analyst coverage, no dividend screen inclusion).

---

## Executive summary
CKX is a debt-free Louisiana land and royalty vehicle: **7,023** net acres, **$18 million** cash, and three operating segments (oil and gas royalties, timber, surface rents). The November 2025 sale of **6,548** acres at roughly **$1,316** per acre demonstrated management's willingness to monetize wholly-owned land inside an active strategic-review process. Normalized owner cash is roughly **$0.22** per share from FY2025 operating cash flow, far below headline earnings inflated by the land gain. Our base case annual return is **3.7%** per year on a dated payoff path to **~$15.50** per share over seven years, anchored on cash floor plus partial acreage monetization. Cash per share provides a strong partial dhando floor, but co-owner governance friction, depleting royalties, and process uncertainty keep moat unproven. We propose **watch** pending a definitive strategic outcome or repeatable per-acre monetization above run-rate cash yield.

## Risks & inversion
**Primary risk:** The strategic-alternatives process fails to produce a value-maximizing transaction, leaving shareholders with a depleting royalty stream, co-owner gridlock on the **5,533** net co-owned acres, and elevated G&A relative to **~$84K** normalized operating profit.

- **Inversion:** If management continues **~$1,316/acre** land sales on additional wholly-owned or partitionable tracts and redeploys cash via accretive repurchases, per-share value could compound even without a full company sale.
- Co-owner unanimity requirement may block timber harvests, leases, or bulk sales that would otherwise unlock value.
- Hurricane damage (e.g., **Hurricane Laura 2020** damaged **~33%** of standing timber) can interrupt royalties and timber income.
- Oil and gas price declines reduce royalty checks without CKX operating leverage to cut costs.
- Micro-cap illiquidity can widen bid-ask spreads and trap patient capital without a catalyst.

**Lens failure mode (asset payoff):** If November 2025 acreage was uniquely valuable timberland not representative of co-owned marsh or undivided interests, the apparent acreage option is smaller than the sale comp implies and the asset lens collapses to a low-yield cash bond.
