## What this business is

American Water Works is the largest investor-owned water and wastewater utility in the United States. **[Fact]** The company serves about **3.6 million** active customers across **14 states** through regulated local monopoly franchises (`investor-documents/sec-edgar/10-K_20260218_rpt20251231_acc0001410636_26_000034.htm`). **[Fact]** FY2025 consolidated operating revenue was **$5.14 billion** (up **9.7%** versus **$4.68 billion** in FY2024). **[Fact]** The **Regulated Businesses** segment contributed **$4.72 billion** (**92%** of revenue); **Market-Based Businesses** contributed **$417 million** (**8%**). **[Fact]** FY2025 diluted earnings per share were **$5.69** on net income of **$1.11 billion**; operating cash flow was **$2.06 billion** while capital spending was **$3.13 billion**. **[Inference]** Owner economics are set by state public utility commissions through allowed returns on rate-base investment, not open-market water pricing.

## Why the market might be wrong

There is **no classic dated mispricing signal** (HK predictive attribute: **none**). **[Inference]** Generalists may treat AWK as a bond-proxy utility and anchor on dividend yield (~2% at ~$140) while underweighting rate-base growth funded by heavy capital spending that eventually flows into earnings. **[Fact]** In October 2025 the company announced a **stock-for-stock merger with Essential Utilities**; shareholders approved the deal on **February 10, 2026**, with expected close by **Q1 2027** pending regulatory approvals (`DEF 14A_20260324...htm`). **[Inference]** The market may be pricing merger completion risk and integration uncertainty rather than standalone regulated earnings power. **[Inference]** Bears may focus on negative GAAP free cash flow (capex exceeds operating cash flow) without crediting regulatory recovery of growth investment through infrastructure surcharges and general rate cases.

## Executive summary

American Water operates regulated water and wastewater networks for **3.6 million** customers in **14 states**, with **92%** of revenue from state-regulated tariffs. FY2025 operating cash flow was **$2.06 billion**; after maintenance capital (approximated by **$894 million** depreciation), normalized owner cash is about **$5.97 per share**. The company invests heavily (**$3.13 billion** capex in FY2025) to replace aging pipe and meet environmental rules, recovering costs through rate cases.

At about **$140 per share** (placeholder; mechanical refresh updates spot price), the Lawrence base case implies about **6.8% per year** on maintenance-adjusted owner cash, below our **15%** accumulate hurdle. The regulated local monopoly supports a **narrow** moat and **partial** asymmetric payoff (Pabrai dhando): downside is bounded by allowed returns, but equity is sensitive to regulatory lag, interest rates on **$12.8 billion** long-term debt, and the pending **Essential** merger. Marvin proposes **watch** on standalone economics; merger pro forma is **[HUMAN REVIEW]**. No approved third-party sources are in the base case.

## Risks & inversion

**Primary risk:** Regulatory denial or delay of cost recovery (including Essential merger approvals), compressing allowed returns while **$12.8 billion** of debt magnifies interest expense.

- **Regulatory lag:** Capex spends years before full revenue recovery in tariffs
- **Merger execution:** Integration, cultural fit, and break-fee / deal-failure scenarios
- **Affordability politics:** State commissions may limit rate increases despite infrastructure needs
- **California subsidiary:** Alternative water supply risk for Carmel River diversions (10-K risk factors)

**Lens failure mode (operating):** If we modeled GAAP free cash flow (negative) instead of maintenance-adjusted owner cash, the stock would look uninvestable; the regulated utility lens requires separating recoverable growth capex from owner distributable cash.

**Inversion:** What would make this fail? Sustained denial of rate increases, Essential merger blocked with stranded costs, or credit downgrade forcing dividend cut.
