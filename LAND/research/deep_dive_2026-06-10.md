## What this business is

Gladstone Land Corporation is an externally managed real estate investment trust that owns and leases farmland to independent farmers across the United States. **[Fact]** At December 31, 2025 the portfolio comprised **144 farms** totaling approximately **98,688** gross acres in **14 states**, plus roughly **55,532** acre-feet of California water assets ( **55,649** acre-feet at March 31, 2026). **[Fact]** The company earns revenue primarily from triple-net leases (tenants pay property taxes, insurance, and maintenance), participation rents tied to crop prices and yields, and occasional direct-operated crop sales on a small subset of farms. **[Fact]** FY2025 total operating revenue was **$88.3 million**; adjusted funds from operations were **$0.393** per diluted share; the company paid a monthly dividend of **$0.0467** per share (**$0.5604** annualized). **[Fact]** Gladstone Management Corporation, controlled by David Gladstone, serves as external adviser under a contract charging **0.60%** per year of gross tangible real estate plus incentive fees above a **7.0%** annualized hurdle on adjusted common equity.

---

## Why the market might be wrong

The market may treat LAND as a passive high-yield REIT whose dividend is fully covered by recurring rent, missing the portfolio rotation and lease-repositioning story underway in 2025–2026. **[Fact]** Management sold **13 farms** (**12,502** acres) for **~$95.4 million** in FY2025, recognizing **~$21.3 million** of net gains, and modified leases on "Repositioned Farms" to trade lower fixed base rent for higher participation on permanent crops (including a **~$4.9 million** pistachio bonus payment in Q1 2026). **[Inference]** At **~$9.23** (June 10, 2026), the stock trades at roughly **44%** below GAAP book equity of **~$16.50** per share, a discount consistent with small-cap REIT yield-screen exclusion and concern that the monthly dividend exceeds normalized cash earnings. **[Inference]** Predictive attribute: **farmland lease REIT** plus **market structure discount** (externally managed, preferred equity layer, monthly payout cadence).

## Executive summary

Gladstone Land is a diversified U.S. farmland landlord: triple-net leases on row and permanent crops, California water rights, and an active sell-and-reinvest rotation program. FY2025 AFFO of **$0.393** per share covered only **~70%** of the **$0.56** annual dividend; Q1 2026 coverage fell to **~54%** as fixed base rent declined after dispositions while participation rent remained lumpy. Leverage is manageable (**~$486 million** total indebtedness, mostly fixed-rate) but Q1 ATM issuance added **3.7 million** shares. At **~$9.23**, our base case annual return is **2.5%** per year on normalized owner cash of **$0.38** per share (ten-year model, **14×** exit multiple). Book equity provides a partial floor, but external adviser fees and dividend coverage risk prevent a full dhando classification. We propose **watch** pending proof that participation rents and reinvested sale proceeds restore AFFO above the dividend.

## Risks & inversion

**Primary risk:** The monthly dividend (**$0.5604** per year) remains uncovered by normalized AFFO, forcing dilutive equity issuance or asset sales that shrink the acreage base without restoring per-share cash flow.

- **Inversion:** If Repositioned Farms participation rent stabilizes above **$20 million** per year and reinvested disposition proceeds earn accretive yields, AFFO could exceed the dividend without a payout cut, compressing the book discount.
- Rising interest rates on new Farm Credit lines (post-Q1 **$37 million** revolver) could pressure acquisition spreads.
- Tenant default on row-crop leases in drought-prone states would impair base rent.
- External adviser incentive fees trigger above **7%** annualized return on adjusted equity, aligning partly with shareholders but adding cost versus self-managed peers.

**Lens failure mode (asset payoff):** If book equity reflects stale cost basis on farms that would sell below carrying value (unlike FY2025 gains), the apparent **44%** discount to book is illusory and the asset lens collapses to a low-return yield trap.
