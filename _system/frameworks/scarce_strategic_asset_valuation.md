# Scarce strategic asset valuation power zone

## Purpose

Use this method when a security owns durable rights to scarce land, water, minerals, infrastructure access, or another location-specific resource that can be monetized repeatedly. The method separates current cash engines, reinvestment opportunities, dormant inventory, and uncontracted projects so a compelling narrative cannot silently enter the base case.

TPL is the reference implementation. The framework is reusable, but the assumptions are not.

## Admission test

A company belongs in this power zone only when all four core conditions pass:

1. **Scarcity:** the asset or right cannot be readily recreated at comparable cost and location.
2. **Durability:** ownership or contractual control survives long enough for multiple monetization cycles.
3. **Economic claim:** public shareholders have a clearly reconciled claim on the relevant cash flow and assets.
4. **Observable monetization:** at least one current cash engine demonstrates that the asset is commercially useful.

At least two of these optional characteristics should also be present:

- multiple uses of the same asset without consuming it;
- capital-free royalties, tolls, or leases;
- network effects from contiguous acreage or infrastructure density;
- high-return incremental investment with measurable cohorts;
- credible projects that can graduate from option to contract.

Do not force the framework onto an ordinary commodity producer, leveraged infrastructure owner, real-estate developer, or speculative land bank merely because it owns acres or pipes.

## Four valuation engines

### 1. Capital-free producing rights

Examples: mineral royalties, produced-water royalties, easement royalties, and recurring land-use tolls.

Value from starting revenue, after-tax owner-cash conversion, explicit growth, duration, terminal owner cash, and discount rate. Do not charge the owner for capital supplied by the operator. Do not add undeveloped units already contributing to the producing run-rate.

### 2. Reinvestment-driven infrastructure

Examples: operated water systems, disposal networks, rail infrastructure, and owned processing capacity.

Growth is not free. For each forecast period:

`growth investment = incremental steady owner cash / incremental after-tax ROIC`

Owner cash equals cash after maintenance capital less that growth investment. This makes growth valuable only when incremental returns exceed the opportunity cost of capital.

### 3. Unit inventory NAV

Examples: visible drilling inventory, dormant mineral rights, residual surface acreage, water rights, and rail corridors.

`value = units × value per unit × realization probability × economic claim × (1 − friction)`

Inventory states must be mutually exclusive. A producing unit cannot simultaneously be visible undeveloped inventory, and a contracted site cannot remain in residual land.

### 4. Milestone project options

Examples: data centers, power projects, desalination, carbon storage, or a new rail connection.

`option value = success-state PV × milestone probability × ownership − remaining committed cost`

Required milestones depend on the project but normally include customer, site, power or feedstock, permits, capital structure, unit economics, and commissioning. A project graduates to contracted value when those economics become enforceable.

## Return-on-capital scoreboard

Report four measures separately:

- capital-free owner-cash growth;
- three-year rolling incremental after-tax ROIC by investment cohort;
- maintenance capital as a percentage of revenue;
- per-share compounding after debt, dilution, distributions, acquisitions, and repurchases.

Accounting ROIC may be misleading when land or rights have a near-zero book basis. Never use that distortion as a substitute for incremental investment economics.

## Hard-to-vary assumption rule

Every decision-critical input must have:

- a formula;
- a primary evidence path;
- a base-rate or historical cross-check;
- a counter-explanation;
- a falsifier that changes the modeled value.

Company commentary can establish mechanisms and milestones. It cannot by itself establish market share, project probability, terminal duration, or successful economics.

## Extension plan

### Phase A — classify before valuing

Create a one-page asset-state map and ownership reconciliation for each candidate. Reject or downgrade the power-zone fit before building a model if scarcity, duration, economic claim, or current monetization fails.

### Phase B — normalize the common ledger

Use the same component states across candidates:

1. producing capital-free cash engines;
2. operated/reinvestment businesses;
3. contracted assets;
4. visible inventory;
5. dormant inventory;
6. milestone project options;
7. cash, debt, minority claims, taxes, and reserves.

The ledger is common; the driver data and discounting remain security-specific.

### Phase C — pilot the closest names

| Security | Expected fit | Primary engine | Required adjustment before use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LB — LandBridge** | Strong but not identical | Surface royalties, easements, resource sales, and residual acreage | Reconcile the Up-C/public economic claim, sponsor units, debt, acquisitions, and related-party WaterBridge revenue before valuing acreage. |
| **WBI — WaterBridge Infrastructure** | Partial | Reinvestment-driven produced-water network | Treat as leveraged merchant infrastructure, not a capital-free landowner. Model maintenance capital, growth cohorts, debt, customer concentration, sponsor control, and related-party access. |
| **AZLCZ — Aztec Land & Cattle** | Strong asset fit, weak liquidity | Lease cash flow, land/mineral unit NAV, railway look-through | Reconcile stapled interests, Apache Railway ownership, tax-basis reporting, mineral/surface overlap, minority claims, and OTC liquidity discount. |

### Phase D — compare without homogenizing

Publish a power-zone comparison using stable fields:

- percentage of base value from producing cash;
- percentage from reinvestment-driven growth;
- percentage from dormant inventory and projects;
- net debt and fixed claims;
- incremental ROIC;
- public-shareholder economic claim;
- evidence readiness;
- expected return at the current price.

Do not rank companies by gross acreage, revenue growth, or total component value alone.

### Phase E — Investment Committee routing

- **Horizon Kinetics / Stahl:** scarcity, duration, multiple monetization, and anti-fragile optionality.
- **Marathon / Capital Returns:** reinvestment runway, capacity response, capital intensity, and incremental ROIC.
- **Howard Marks / Oaktree:** debt, refinancing, customer concentration, cycle stress, and liquidity.
- **Klarman / Baupost:** unit NAV, realization friction, shareholder claim, and catalysts.
- **Hohn:** quality and durability of the current operating engines.

The committee must vote separately on current operating value, inventory value, and project options. The aggregator may not average a disputed option probability into an apparently precise consensus value.

## TPL refresh rules

Update the model when any of these changes materially:

- royalty production, realized price, or drilling cadence;
- produced-water volume, water-oil ratio, AMI coverage, or revenue per barrel;
- water-sales volume, pricing, gross margin, maintenance capital, or growth-capital cohorts;
- visible versus dormant NRA mapping;
- acreage-use tiers and named infrastructure projects;
- data-center customer, power, interconnection, capital, or commissioning milestones;
- Phase 2 desalination unit economics or Phase 3 FID;
- capital allocation, debt, dilution, or repurchases.
