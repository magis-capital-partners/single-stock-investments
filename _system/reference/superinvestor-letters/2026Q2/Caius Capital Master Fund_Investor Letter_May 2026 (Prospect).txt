Market Update
May was the mirror image of April. After two months of escalation and many false dawns, the U.S. – Iran conflict
tilted again to de-escalation. Although the Strait remained closed in practice, the direction of travel meant that Brent
ended May at $92.56, down approximately 19% on the month, marking its worst monthly performance since the
Covid-19 pandemic. In our mind, whilst markets never priced a significant disruption, by the end of May a full and
prompt resolution of the conflict was consensus.
As we write this in June, the MOU has been signed by both sides. For all practical purposes, oil-flows through the
Strait have increased by ~1.5mbpd over the last month due to successful US military pressure, but remain far below
pre-war levels. Exxon, Vitol and Trafigura all warned in recent weeks that global inventory levels are reaching critical
levels and that we would see far higher prices without a rapid reopening of the Strait. The clear favourability of the
final MOU towards Iran may reflect the increasing pressure to avoid this scenario. Against the market pricing a full
and prompt reopening of the Strait, we remain cautious and think convexity in the market is to the downside.
Our View
Whether the war really does end tomorrow or in a month or in six months time, we believe that sufficient damage
has been done to materially affect fundamentals in Europe for an extended period. Most obviously, in response to
the oil shock the ECB has already hiked rates by 25bps and guided that further hikes are likely. This is against a
backdrop of already poor fundamentals: Europe is experiencing its fourth consecutive year of weak growth,
refinancing costs remain materially higher than when much of today's debt was originally issued, and corporate
balance sheets have been absorbing pressure for several years.
One of the most striking disconnects that we see today is the difference between economic reality and consensus
earnings expectations in Europe. Total STOXX 600 earnings were effectively flat between 2022 and 2025 (growing
just 1% over that period), yet consensus is currently forecasting approximately 14% earnings growth in 2026 alone.
What makes this particularly unusual is that those forecasts imply earnings growth accelerating significantly through
the remainder of the year following modest Q1 growth of approximately 3% headline (and only 1.5% excluding
energy): forecasts imply 13% in Q2, 19% in Q3, and 18% in Q4 (see bar chart overleaf). This is despite the fact that
consensus Eurozone GDP growth is moving toward 0.6% for 2026, down from the expectations of 1.2% in January,
and well below the 1.4% of 2025. Many of these forecasts were built on the assumption that the Strait of Hormuz
would reopen by the end of May (as noted above, it remains closed). We are not suggesting earnings must collapse,
rather, current earnings expectations are very high and are most likely not going to be met for the remainder of the
year – as indeed they were not in Q1. Markets appear priced for an earnings acceleration despite a growth and rates
backdrop that continues to weaken. When expectations are high, even modest disappointments can create
significant pricing dislocations across both equity and credit markets. The asymmetry is increasingly stark; the
upside from stronger-than-expected growth appears limited whilst the downside from earnings disappointments
appears increasingly underappreciated.

So, what could happen if European earnings miss in 2026? The table above not our forecast, it is rather a scenario
analysis of what could happen. The STOXX 600 currently trades on approximately 15x forward earnings, placing it
around the 70th percentile of historical valuations over the last 25 years. This is not cheap, but neither is it uniquely
expensive. However, those valuations are dependent on consensus expectations of approximately 14% earnings
growth and if earnings simply remain flat, as they were 2022-2025 (even in nominal terms), the implied multiple rises
to approximately 17x, closer to the 90th percentile of historical valuation ranges - and this temporal range includes
large earnings recovery stories post-GFC and post-Covid when multiples were justifiably elevated. Assuming flat
earnings, we show the market impact of the multiple adjusting to 14x (which is its long-term average) and we also
show the impact of some earnings decline if there was a brief period of negative growth in Europe. We don’t need to
put probabilities on these; we think they are all credible scenarios that an investor should take into account when
investing in the current markets.
Given these risks, we remain very well hedged with our MTM and Gap betas at 0.09 and 0.22 respectively. While we
are increasingly identifying attractive opportunities across the market, we remain highly selective and patient with
respect to entry levels. We have reduced our LMV to 108% from 122% earlier in the year through some successful
exits to allow us to act decisively should valuations reach levels we consider compelling.

Portfolio Review
During the month we initiated a new long equity position in an aerospace/defense company. This group is family
controlled and has experienced enormous growth since the Ukraine war, to a point where it IPO’d early this year,
with the shares rallying from €24 to €33. It was recently attacked by a short seller who highlighted (what we think
were) already well disclosed governance weaknesses. Having been a large creditor during 2024, we know the
company well and took the opportunity to build a position in the equity at ~35% discount to the IPO price and at a
discount to the wider market multiple. We also took the opportunity to increase our short credit position (via CDS) in
a multinational chemicals - specialty company which – despite reporting weak Q1 results and lowering Q2 guidance
– still trades in line with its pre-war levels. Elsewhere, we exited our equity position in a finance – other services
company, redeploying the capital into the credit which has benefited from the recent improvement in the balance
sheet, and closed our credit positions in three energy investments: an oil & gas drilling company, an oil company -
exploration & production and an oil – field services company, each of which reached our price targets.
The largest contributor to performance during the month was our credit position in a finance – other services
company, adding +81bps gross (+63bps net). The SEK 7.5bn equity raise announced alongside the Q1 results drove a
material deleveraging of the balance sheet, meaningfully reducing refinancing risk across the 2027 and 2028
maturities, and paving the way for a likely ratings upgrade. Our second-largest contributor was our position in the
equity and credit of an oil – field services company at +40bps gross (+31bps net), where performance was driven
principally by positive earnings together with the conversion of a letter of intent into a contract for one of their
vessels, and the arrival of a new investor. Our equity position in a commercial bank –non US was the third largest
contributor at +22bps gross (+17bps net), following the bank's solid Q1 2026 results and the payment of its final
dividend for FY2025. Our equity position in an oil & gas drilling company, was the fourth largest contributor at
+22bps gross (+17bps net), supported by strong Q1 results and favourable macroeconomic tailwinds. Rounding out
the top five contributors was our credit position in a medical labs & testing services company, which added +19bps
gross (+15bps net) following a solid set of Q1 results, with the business supported by elevated phenol prices.
Moving on to detractors, our credit position in an electric-distribution company was the largest detractor this month
at -60bps gross (-49bps net), following weaker-than-expected Q1 results as lower Turkish power prices - driven by
record rainfall which bolstered hydro generation from the country's dams - weighed on performance. Our portfolio
hedge was the second-largest detractor at -37bps gross (-30bps net) in what was a strong month for European
equities. Our credit position in water company was the third largest detractor at -27bps gross (-22bps net) as the
requisite government approval process remains on hold. An oil company – exploration & production,  was the
fourth largest detractor at -14bps gross (-12bps net) as our long equity position underperformed its position-level
hedges during May despite posting solid Q1 results and successfully raising $400m to refinance an existing
obligation (the removal of which potentially enables the company to increase its dividend payout ratio going
forward). Finally, our short credit position (via CDS) in a multinational chemicals - specialty company detracted -
8bps gross (-7bps net). During the month, the CDS and equity notably diverged and we took the opportunity to
increase our short exposure. Absent any change in underlying fundamentals, we expect pricing to normalise.

13. C ORRELATION  A statistical term which defines the

coefficient that is –1 means that the manager is
perfectly negatively correlated with the index or
manager against which it is regressed (move same
amount in opposite directions); a coefficient of 0

of +1 means perfect positive correlation (move the same
amount in the same direction). (Source: eVestment).

15. S HARP E  RATIO  This statistic is computed by
subtracting the return of the risk-free index (typically 91-
day T-bill or other cash benchmark) from the return
of the manager to determine the risk-adjusted excess
return. This excess return is then divided by the standard
deviation of the manager. A manager taking on risk,
instead of investing in cash, is expected to generate
higher returns and Sharpe measures how well the
manager generated returns with that risk. It is a
measurement of efficiency utilizing the relationship
between annualized risk-free return and annualized
standard deviation. The higher the Sharpe Ratio, the
greater efficiency produced by the manager. For
example, a Sharpe Ratio of 1 is better than a ratio of 0.5.
(Source: eVestment)
Rf = Risk-free rate

of the market as a whole and the volatility of the
manager. A positive alpha indicates that a manager has
produced returns above the expected level at that risk
level, and vice versa for a negative alpha. Alpha is the Y
intercept of the regression line. (Source: eVestment)

17 . BETA This is a measure of a portfolio’s volatility.
Statistically, beta is the covariance of the portfolio in
relation to the market. A beta of 1.00 implies perfect
historical correlation of movement with the market. A
higher beta manager will be more volatile than the
market, whereas a lower beta manager will be less
volatile. For example, a 1.10 beta portfolio has
historically been 10% more volatile than the market.

( ‘D MC ’) A measure of the manager’s performance in

it means that during that specific time period, the
