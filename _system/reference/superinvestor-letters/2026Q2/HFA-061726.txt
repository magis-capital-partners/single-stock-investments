investment functions rose 9.75%, increasing by 1,185 employees to 17,012.

Small Firm’s AUM Rises Exponentially
A small Princeton, N.J., global-macro shop has nailed
hundreds of millions of dollars in commitments from
two investors.
Birkoa Capital , which launched its flagship Birkoa
Max Global Macro Fund four years ago, has followed up
the new commitments with blockbuster performance
in April and May. Birkoa, which manages $14.3 million
across two funds, has paused marketing efforts while it
works to onboard the allocations, each of which is nine
figures. The identities of the investors couldn’t be deter -
mined.
The second quarter brought a strong recovery for the
offerings, with a 32% jolt for the flagship in April marking
its first positive month since October. That carried into
May, with Birkoa Max gaining 25% for the month and
41% year-to-date through May. A two-year-old delevered
version of the strategy, Birkoa Pro Global Macro Fund,
rose 15% in April and 18% last month.
In a May 3 investor letter, founder and chief invest-
ment officer Pranjit Kalita wrote that he orchestrated
the second-quarter turnaround with a sharp shift ear -
lier in the year away from the funds’ “software-heavy AI
exposure” and by exiting oil positions near the top and
deploying the proceeds into additional semiconductor
and chip companies trading at depressed levels, such as
Arm Holdings and Intel.
“We exited approximately 80% of those [software]
positions, including Atlassian, Salesforce, C3.ai, and
GitLab, while retaining only smaller exposures to Snow -
flake and MongoDB, ” Kalita wrote. “Over the past sev-
eral months, this decision has been validated. The names
we exited have continued to trend lower.
“Even the positions we retained have declined further,
but given our significantly reduced exposure, the impact
on the portfolio has been minimal. This reset freed up
capital for higher-conviction opportunities. ”
Since that update, those remaining software positions
have recovered from their January lows or are steadily
climbing back up.
Recent outperformance, however, has been driven by
its exposure to memory-chip makers, including Boise,
Idaho-headquartered Micron Technology. A position
spanning three years in Japan-based SoftBank also has
been a contributor to positive performance.
“Micron is up more than 50% in the past month alone
– an aggressive re-rating from a cyclical memory name
toward a core AI-infrastructure supplier – with its entire
2026 high-bandwidth memory supply already sold out
under fixed-price, long-term contracts, ” Birkoa wrote in
a second note to investors on May 31. “This is precisely
the memory-demand thesis driving our semiconductor
book. ”
The firm invests globally with an emphasis on stocks,
bonds, exchange-traded funds and commodities. Equi-
ties might include crypto-adjacent names – such as
exchanges, miners and financial-technology platforms –
but not digital assets themselves.
Birkoa Max, which targets a 20% annualized return,
generated 22.1% annualized from inception in May 2022
through April 30. Over the same period, the S&P 500
Index returned 14.8% on an annualized basis, while the
MSCI World Index rose 13.7%.
The fund’s delevered counterpart, Birkoa Pro, also has
exceeded its own targeted annualized return of 15%. That
offering has gained 20.8% on an annualized basis from
May 2024 through April 30 – on par with the 19.9%
return for the S&P Index and 19.1% for the MSCI.
The flagship returned 40% in 2025, while Birkoa Pro
ended the year up 23%. 

Asia-Focused Indus Nears Return to Apex
It’s taken more than a decade, but Asia equities man-
ager Indus Capital is within arm’s length of its peak
assets under management of $6.8 billion.
The New Y ork firm, which manages $6.3 billion, is
enjoying one of the strongest years in its 25-year his-
tory with four investment products generating double-
digit returns. Indeed, performance and steady inflows
in recent years have propelled assets under management
to near Indus’ July 2015 peak after they dropped to their
lowest level since then at $3.2 billion in 2022.
Redemptions were prompted by several years of
change that included co-founder retirements, a restruc-
turing of the firm and a broader shift in investor prefer -
ence toward private equity strategies.
But things are now looking up for Indus as its hedge
fund roster – two pan-Asia funds and three Japan-
focused vehicles – has reversed course, attracting a
steady flow of assets on the back of strong yearly returns.
The bulk of the firm’s assets, which include alloca-
tions that Indus attracted in early June, are in Indus
Select, a concentrated long-only vehicle whose invest-
ments span Asia. That fund, with $4.3 billion under
management, gained 32.5% year-to-date through May,
outclassing the MSCI Asia Pacific Index’s 22.9% return.
In 2025, the offering climbed 28.3%, just edging the
benchmark’s 28% gain.
Overall, Japan has been the largest contributor to
performance this year for Indus. After years of disap-
pointing investors, Japanese equities are benefiting
from a catch-up trade rewarding an increasing number
of companies that have embraced shareholder-friendly
reforms.
Elsewhere in Asia, Indus has focused attention on
technology and semiconductor-laden Taiwan and South
Korea, while avoiding complex markets such as India.
The firm has been underweight China, but that might
change as the firm evaluates opportunities there.
Indus’ other regional product, Indus Pacific Oppor -
tunities Fund, which had $624 million under manage-
ment at the end of May, looks to identify companies
that fit within well-specified long and short investment
patterns. The fund, which climbed 16.7% in the Janu-
ary-to-May period, typically runs with 50% to 60% net
exposure to the MSCI Asia Pacific Index.
The firm also runs three long-only Japan-focused
vehicles.
Indus Japan Long-Only Fund, which had $657 mil-
lion under management last month, comes in two
flavors, hedged and unhedged. The hedged version
generated a 21.5% return in the first five months of this
year compared to the MSCI Japan U.S. Dollar Hedged
Index’s 19.7% gain in the same period. The unhedged
companion to Indus’ Japan fund climbed 18% in the
same time frame, beating the MSCI Japan U.S. Dollar
Index’s 16.1% increase.
Another offering, Indus Japan Fund, which had $127
million under management on May 31, is a long/short
vehicle that has climbed 19.8% this year. That’s essen-
tially on par with MSCI’s Japan U.S. Dollar Index, and it
outpaced the hedged version of that benchmark.
Returns for Indus Japan Active Extension Strategy,
which had $432 million under management last month,
couldn’t be learned. The strategy is managed for a single
investor.
Indus Capital was founded in 2000 by six former
partners at Soros Fund Management: David Kowitz,
Sheldon Kasowitz, Byron Gill, James Shannon, Ben
De Somma and Nowell Chernick. Kowitz and Kasowitz
were co-managing partners until 2017, when they par -
tially relinquished their fund-manager responsibilities
and started a partnership succession that concluded in
2019 with their retirements.
The firm restructured in 2019, streamlining head-
count and products – including shutting down products
exposed to China and emerging mar -

Mudrick Augments Co-Investment Menu
Brand-name distressed-debt shop Mudrick Capital
is expanding its co-investment program to include three
high-conviction opportunities.
“These are situations where Mudrick has already com-
pleted extensive diligence and is acting as the lead or co-
lead investor, providing investors the opportunity to invest
alongside our existing positions on attractive terms, ” the
$2.7 billion New Y ork firm, led by Jason Mudrick, told
prospective investors in an update last week.
What the firm is referring to as “Opportunity A ” is a
first-lien term loan that arose out of a financial restruc-
turing, known as a liability management exercise, or
LME, involving a large-cap company. Mudrick is target-
ing a gross internal rate of return of 15% to 20% for the
one-year loan.
Opportunity B is a distressed term loan in which
Mudrick is a major shareholder and occupies a seat on
the board of directors. The loan, which has a payment
period of one to two years, is aiming for a 3x gross mul-
tiple on invested capital.
Opportunity C is a term loan following a Mudrick-
led LME. Further restructuring is expected, followed by
a merger or sale of the company. The loan has a three-
to-five-year payment period and is targeting a 2.25x
gross multiple on invested capital.
The firm wrote that many of its best investments have
been co-investments and that it has seen a rising num-
ber of inquiries from investors about them.
Separately, Mudrick provided an update of its
Mudrick Liability Management Solutions Fund, a
shorter-duration drawdown vehicle designed to capital-
ize on LMEs. Mudrick Capital is targeting $500 million
for the fund, which is planning a first equity close in the
third quarter.
Mudrick is offering prospective investors free co-
investment rights in exchange for a $50 million commit-
ment in the fund, while investments of a lesser amount
result in no management fees and a 10% performance
fee over an 8% hurdle.
“We believe this is a point-in-time opportunity set
catalyzed by an extended period of zero rates and loose
covenants, ” Mudrick Capital wrote. “We believe the LME
opportunity set is still in its nascent stages and will be a
ripe hunting ground for best-in-class distressed special-
ists for the next 3-5 years.
“ Amidst continued volatility in the private debt mar -
kets, and with many software maturities within leveraged
loans set to expire in 2028, we believe these market devel-
opments only further catalyze the LME opportunity set. ”
The opportunities arise as Mudrick’s main fund con-
tinues to struggle.
After nine straight winning years, including a half-
dozen annual gains in the double digits, Mudrick Dis-
tressed Opportunity Fund lost 24.4% in 2025 and was
down 22.2% year-to-date through last month, despite a
3.9% rise in May, its best monthly gain in 11 months.
The fund’s annualized gain from inception in July 2009
through last month slipped to 6.9%, compared with an
annualized return of 8.15% for the With Intelligence
North America Distressed Debt Hedge Fund Index.
The losses have largely been attributed to the Sep-
tember bankruptcy filing of auto parts company First
Brands Group  and Mudrick’s high-profile bet on flying
taxi company Vertical Aerospace.  While analysts were
bullish on Vertical Aerospace at the start of 2025, the
stock price has since dropped from $11.41 per share in
January of last year to $2.25 at the start of this week.
The firm’s other 17-year-old vehicle, the slightly
smaller Mudrick Stressed Credit Master Fund, was up
7% through May, topping the 2.3% year-to-date rise
of the With Intelligence Fixed Income/Credit Asset
Weighted Index. That brought the fund’s annualized
gain since inception in March 2009 to 8.9%, versus a
6.7% increase for With Intelligence’s fixed income/
credit index. 

ica, followed by Oracle and ON Semiconductor. The
biggest month-over-month movers were firms that
experienced a more than 10% increase in the number
of funds shorting their names. Those companies were
ON Semiconductor and Omnicon in the large-cap cat-
egory, Hyatt Hotels from the mid-cap group, and Flow -
ers Foods and CBIZ among small caps.
The Hazeltree Crowding Report tracks long and
short positions among 600 or so hedge funds around
the globe. The report is based on anonymized data from
1,600 securities on the firm’s securities-finance plat-
form.

to the SEC only the number of staffers employed by U.S.-based affiliates. Others don’t report employment figures at all. To
make the ranking as comprehensive as possible, Hedge Fund Alert reached out to non-U.S. firms known to have sizable
staffs. If those firms declined to provide the size of their total staffs and investment staffs, HFA relied on the SEC filings.

Strategies, in 2009 after leaving AIG Investments, a highly

measure.

framework used for any other asset class, DARC makes

liquidity in private markets possible, ” stated the paper.

maturity calculations to private equity returns.
