Startup’s Short Targets Legacy Energy Co.
Expect several more activist campaigns this year from
Sunlight Partners, the startup green-energy fund man-
ager that last week made its first short bet seeking to expose
environmental fraud, misrepresentation or negligence.
Depending on market conditions and opportunity, the
firm is targeting six to eight campaigns per year.
The first big short from the Australia-domiciled Sunlight
Partners Pty Ltd vehicle came last week against 159-year-
old boiler maker Babcock & Wilcox, which Sunlight con-
tends is looking to refit inefficient old power plants to run
artificial-intelligence data centers. Sunlight noted that the
hybrid gas-turbine-and-steam-turbine-boiler plants that
Babcock & Wilcox is proposing to build to operate the
data centers typically burn more gas and emit substantially
more carbon dioxide than modern gas-turbine plants.
“The problem is that B&W’s boilers are an entirely
obsolete technology and its speed-to-power argument
does not survive scrutiny, ” Sunlight wrote in a research
report released last week as part of the shorting campaign.
“ A gas steam plant is a 30-to-40-year, front-of-meter util-
ity asset that would generate electricity at 35-42% thermal
efficiency as opposed to 58-60% efficiency for modern
combined-cycle gas turbine plants, requires more than 24
hours to cold-start, and will be grid-connected in one of
the most oversupplied power markets in the United States. ”
Sunlight Partners told investors in an update this week
that Babcock & Wilcox’s shares fell 5% in the first hour
after Sunlight released its report on May 26, before rallying
and then ending the first week down 6% from the start of
the campaign.
“Short interest jumped 60% the day after publication, from
9.6m to 15.6m shares short, suggesting significant market
buy-in for our thesis, ” the firm wrote in this week’s update.
Babcock & Wilcox did not publicly address the short
campaign. In a November announcement about the con-
version of its plants, B&W said its solutions for the data
centers “are proven technologies that utilize natural gas
efficiently” and represent “an ideal solution to power data
centers. ”
Sunlight, which launched its fund at the beginning of
May, is finalizing a Cayman Islands master fund structure.
The firm, which has locations in Australia and Boston, has
also registered as an Exempt Reporting Adviser with the
SEC and is on pace to soon open to investors outside of
Australia.
Sunlight’s strategy is quantitatively driven and devel-
oped through rigorous back-testing.
The firm’s leadership team includes chief investment
officer Will Funnell, chief executive Daniel Stacey and
partner and portfolio manager Nathaniel Victor.
The firm blends Funnell’s derivatives expertise at Cita-
del Securities, Stacey’s research background at The Wall
Street Journal and Victor’s equities experience at Inve -
nomic Capital. 
Big April Has Consistent Riser Near $4B
A quietly steady firm that has had no down years since
its formation in 2020 is approaching $4 billion of assets
after a surge in allocations.
Debt-fund manager Carronade Capital gained 8.9%
through the first four months of 2026, including a 4.7%
jump in April – the second-best month in the Darien,
Conn., firm’s history and the 12th straight month of posi-
tive gains for the Carronade Capital Partners fund.
By comparison, the With Intelligence Fixed Income/
Credit Hedge Fund Index gained 1.4.% in April, bringing
its year-to-date total rise to 1.5%.
Carronade has $3.75 billion under management, up
from $2.8 billion in January. The 33%-plus asset boost
shows that in addition to its market-beating performance
this year, the fund attracted additional capital from inves-
tors.
Carronade’s annualized gain since inception in July
2020 is 11% through April, topping the 6.2% rise of the
With Intelligence Fixed Income/Credit Hedge Fund Index.
The firm’s chief investment officer, Dan Gropper, was
a managing director at both Fortress Investment Group
and Aurelius Capital and was a portfolio manager and
analyst at Paul Singer’s Elliott Management.
Carronade’s strength is in its metronome-like consis-
tency.
The distressed-investing specialist has never gained
more than 12.5% or less than 3.6% for any year, while
maintaining a very good Sharpe ratio of 2.0.
It has never risen more than 5.5% in any month, but it
has only 15 negative months across six years and has just
three down months over the past three years.
Carronade’s top holdings by far at the end of the first
quarter were put options on the State Street SPDR S&P 500
ETF Trust.
Even after the humbling experience of firing three
investment professionals in early 2024 over what Gropper
termed “unprofessional behavior” stemming from viola-
tions of Carronade’s inclusivity standards, the firm gained
praise from an allocator over its candor and quick action
and followed up with its two best full years of gains: 12.3%
in 2024 and 12.5% last year. 
Typhon Maps First Structured Products
Typhon Capital continues to expand beyond its roots
as a commodities-focused multi-manager platform, roll-
ing out a structured-products business that packages
niche alternative-investment strategies into exchange-
listed notes, including offerings with principal protection.
The Miami manager has raised $10 million for its first
U.S.-domiciled issuance, a note tied to arbitration claims.
Typhon issued that note in October through its Argos

A Cayman Islands version, which raised $40 million, is
set to launch imminently. Unlike the U.S. product, the off-
shore security’s lone client did not opt for principal pro-
tection. The Argos platform in the Cayman Islands was
formed in 2016 to feed client capital into the firm’s liquid
hedge fund strategies, but Typhon recently expanded the
Cayman entity’s mandate to support credit offerings like
this one.
The note offerings are tied to settlements of legal dis-
putes sourced by Typhon sister company JurisTrade, an
electronic marketplace that connects investors with law
firms and owners of legal claims. Proceeds of the notes
are lent to an arbitration-claims manager to finance that
firm’s marketing and claims-processing operations. Note-
holders earn returns from the claims manager’s repay-
ment of the debt facility.
The offering represents the early stages of a broader
securitization effort. Typhon is evaluating seven addi-
tional strategies tied to note issuance and is separately
developing a principal-protected product linked to a
diversified vehicle that would allocate across the firm’s
lineup of futures, global-macro and quantitative-equity
strategies.
The return of higher interest rates has breathed new
life into a structure that was dormant during the zero-
rate era. “Now that you have yield, it opens opportuni-
ties for some compelling structures in credit markets, ”
Typhon founder James Koutoulas said.
Typhon, which runs 15 strategies on its platform, over-
sees less than $200 million today, down from a peak of
roughly $400 million in May 2022. Like many smaller
managers, the firm has struggled to compete for insti-
tutional allocations against the industry’s largest multi-
strategy firms. Koutoulas is betting that structured
products with downside protection will add an allure to
his firm’s strategies.
The U.S.-domiciled notes have terms ranging from 18
to 27 months and provide principal protection along with
an 8% annual coupon. Investors also participate in gains
generated by the underlying arbitration-claims portfolio,
with returns capped at an annualized rate of as much as
18%. The principal guarantee is provided by an undis-
closed AA-rated insurance company in exchange for pre-
miums paid by the note’s proceeds.
The investor in the Cayman Islands version is forgoing
the insurance protection, which allows for returns that
could reach 40%, according to Koutoulas. The Cayman
Islands version of the note is structured as an exchange-
listed private-credit note also backed by portfolios of
claims sourced through JurisTrade.
Typhon is working to list the offshore note on the
Vienna MTF , a multilateral trading facility operated by
the Vienna Stock Exchange, so investors can trade them
prior to maturity.
As for the planned diversified hedge fund-linked issu-
ance, that would employ a more-traditional principal-
protection structure. Roughly 75% of note proceeds
would be invested in a zero-coupon bond designed to
return investors’ principal at maturity, while the remain-
ing 25% would be deployed on margin across Typhon’s
underlying trading strategies.
Principal-protected notes have periodically surfaced
in the hedge fund industry for more than two decades.
Large banks aggressively marketed the structures dur -
ing the early 2000s, combining zero-coupon bonds with
options tied to hedge fund indexes or funds of funds. The
products gained popularity among investors, particularly
in Europe and Canada, seeking equity-like returns with
reduced downside risk.
Demand faded after the 2008 financial crisis exposed
that the protection was only as strong as the credit of the
issuing institution.
Koutoulas, a securities lawyer, founded Typhon in
2008. 

fund’s portfolio – and conviction paid off this year, how-
ever, as the coin shot up 81.5% last month and 183.2%
year-to-date through May 31.
Hyperliquid, the ninth-largest cryptocurrency by
market capitalization, is increasingly catching the atten-
tion of larger and more institutional investors. The
firm’s confidence in the blockchain is based on, among
other things, its token structure, composability, user
experience and user growth. Unlike with many tokens,
the Hyperliquid protocol uses nearly all of its revenues
to buy back and burn Hype tokens.
Motus continues to see catalysts for Hyperliquid,
including a May 14 arrangement the exchange entered
into with Coinbase Global and Circle Internet Group,
a separate push into prediction markets and a promising
outlook for U.S. regulation.
Motus also gained in the first five months on a few
smaller, more-thematic bets, such as the Venice AI
token and staking protocol Kinetic.
Co-founders Alex Kriete, Frank Cavallo and Greg

Conference-Business Staffers Leave Apex
Apex Group’s capital-introduction conference busi-
ness, Apex Invest, has lost key employees whose departure
has halved the unit’s staff.
For reasons that are unknown, four employees departed
in the last couple of weeks, which leaves the conference
business with four staffers, down from eight employees in
May, and down more from last year.
The senior staffers who just departed are Apex Invest
events leader Colette Leong-Son, marketing head Nita-
sha Khosla and operations and partnerships head Lauren
Whiteside. Another operations employee, Jazeel Hashim,
also left.
That follows the fourth-quarter departures of Philipp
Levy and Anthony Lombardi. Levy was the chief of Apex
Invest’s overall business, which also includes a broker-
dealer sales operation and an information-sharing plat-
form, while Lombardi, who worked with the conference
business, was managing director for investor relations.
Remaining at the events operation are Lana Callahan,
Patrick Clark, Hannah Holliday and Isabelle Carruthers.
Apex Invest typically hosts about four to five confer -
ences a year. This year, the firm held an event in Nashville
in April and is planning to host another in Zurich in Octo-
ber and one in Abu Dhabi in November. A fourth event
that had been slated for Singapore last month didn’t hap-
pen. It was postponed to an as-yet-unannounced date.
For the Nashville gathering, in addition to the fee Apex
charged managers to attend, the firm rolled out an extra,
optional, placement-agent service to attending managers
in which they would be charged a fee based on investor
commitments they raised at the event. Such a service with
the related success fee is a rarity for a capital-introduction
conference scene.
Apex Group, which is majority owned by private equity
firm Genstar Capital, is a Bermuda-headquartered finan-
cial-services business that operates a well-known fund-
administration service. It launched Apex Invest following
its 2022 purchase of Context365, which included the Con-
text Summits business that had previously hosted a large
annual cap-intro gathering each winter at the Fontaineb-
leau Miami Beach hotel. 

$7.4 billion to $10.4 billion, and the average for the 10 larg-

Law firm Seward & Kissel, which released its fifth SMA
Snapshot Report in the fall, has found that average separate-
account allocations were ticking up for firms across their life
cycles, with more tenured firms seeing the average check size
increase from $75 million in 2024 to $95 million in 2025.
The average separate-account investment with emerging
managers jumped from $10 million to $28 million, which
Seward & Kissel said, “ … might point to an increase in high
pedigree launches that garnered SMA investor interest. ”
Its analysis also indicated that equity, credit and macro
strategies each made up about a third of separate accounts,
with equity-focused strategies losing significant ground to
credit and other approaches in 2025.
“There was little distinction in the data for mature and
newer managers. We believe that the renewed interest in
non-equity strategies may be due to investor concerns of
an overheated equities market coupled with the oppor -
tunities presented in the overall global macro climate, ”
Seward’s report noted. “ Approximately 1/3 of the strategies
implemented deviated from the manager’s flagship liquid
investment strategy due to mandates primarily related to:
long-only exposure; higher position concentrations; greater
leverage; bespoke market opportunities; or tax efficiency. ”
While separate accounts are praised for their improved
transparency, customization and risk controls, they can also
burden smaller managers, in particular, with operational
complexities and might make it more challenging to raise
capital for commingled funds, said respondents to HFA ’s
annual outlook survey. The rising popularity also means
more assets are concentrating around larger, more estab-
lished firms, some noted.
About three-quarters of respondents who answered a
question on the impact of separate accounts said that ulti-
mately they’re changing the hedge fund landscape.
“This shift reflects a long-term transformation in the allo-
cation and management of institutional capital rather than a
temporary trend, ” one respondent said. “Managers who do
not provide SMA solutions may face considerable challenges
when seeking institutional capital, as the market increas-
ingly prioritizes these capabilities. … Large funds, having
pioneered SMA adoption, continue to play a dominant
role due to their capacity to manage the complexity inher-
ent in these structures. Those managers unable or unwilling
to adapt may struggle to meet the elevated expectations of
institutional investors. ” 
