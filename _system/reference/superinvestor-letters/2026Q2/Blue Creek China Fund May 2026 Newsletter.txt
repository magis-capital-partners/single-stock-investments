China's Economic Imbalance Is Near a Turning Point
Over the past five years, China's economy has been defined by strong exports
and weak domestic demand. The latest data suggest this imbalance has reached an
unhealthy level and may be approaching a turning point.
In May, China's exports surged 19% year-over-year. Growth was broad-based.
High-tech sectors such as semiconductors, new energy and EVs remained strong,
while traditional manufacturing exports also accelerated. Part of the reason is that
energy constraints in Southeast Asia have pushed some production capacity back to
China. Domestic demand remains weak. Retail sales fell 0.6% year-over-year in May,
while fixed-asset investment declined 10%. Excluding the pandemic period, this is
the first time in the past 40 years that consumption and investment have both
contracted during a normal economic cycle. Domestic demand has weakened to an
extreme level.
There are, however, some early signs of improvement. Existing home prices in
tier-one cities have risen for three consecutive months. Housing listings continue to
fall, while transaction volumes in 2026 are running well above last year. Average
rental yields across China's top 50 cities have reached 2.3%, above bank deposit rates.
Further easing could have a meaningful impact. Relaxing home purchase restrictions
and introducing mortgage subsidies would support housing demand. Given the
importance of real estate to household wealth, a housing recovery would likely
translate into stronger consumption. The government also launched an urban renewal

program in May. It plans to invest RMB 15-20 trillion over the next five years in old
residential communities, urban village redevelopment and underground infrastructure.
More importantly, local governments will be allowed to use bond issuance as equity
capital and raise additional bank financing, significantly easing leverage constraints.
These developments suggest domestic demand may be nearing an inflection point.
At the same time, global AI demand remains in the early stages of a powerful
expansion. Token usage continues to grow exponentially. Large U.S. platform
companies are investing not only all of their operating cash flow, but also additional
debt and equity capital into AI infrastructure. The industry still has substantial
financing capacity. China has also announced plans to invest RMB 2 trillion in
computing infrastructure. This cycle looks very different from the internet bubble in
2000. AI demand is real and growing rapidly across content generation, coding and
agents. On the supply side, the semiconductor industry is highly concentrated,
barriers to entry are high, and management teams remain disciplined on capacity
expansion. For now, there is little sign that supply will outpace demand.
Over the past several months, we have increased our exposure to AI-related
opportunities while maintaining positions in companies tied to China's domestic
demand recovery. These businesses combine strong competitive positions, robust
cash generation, attractive shareholder returns and compelling valuations. As a result,
our portfolio is more balanced today than it was six months ago.

Fund Strategy
 Investment philosophy: research-driven fundamental-oriented value investing to pursue long-term
above-average returns.
 Investment criteria: the fund invests in companies with 1) a strong business model, 2) a strong
management team, and 3) strong corporate governance.
 Due diligence system: the fund adopts a “360 degree” due diligence approach, which includes interviewing
management, customers, supply chain, competitors, regulators, and industry experts.
