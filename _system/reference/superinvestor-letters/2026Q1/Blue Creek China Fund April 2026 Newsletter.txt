Five Layers Of AI 
Jensen Huang, CEO of NVIDIA, describes AI as consisting of five layers: 
energy, chips, infrastructure, models, and applications. The evolution of model 
capabilities is the core force driving demand across every layer of the AI stack. 
Represented by Anthropic, the industry’s primary direction of progress over the 
past year has been improving coding capabilities. Coding has gradually replaced 
traditional chatbots as the main engine of AI iteration. The market has come to realize 
that enterprises are far more willing to pay for AI when it delivers meaningful 
productivity gains, while consumer willingness to pay for large models has not 
materially improved, and consumer monetization models are evolving much more 
slowly. As coding capabilities approach a very high level, models will not only be 
able to understand and assign instructions, but also execute them precisely through 
machine language. At that stage, machines effectively become autonomous Agents 
capable of carrying out complex human tasks, creating the next major wave of AI 
adoption. Agent-based systems drive exponential growth in token consumption. 
However, on the supply side, whether in hardware capacity or power infrastructure, 
expansion remains largely linear. This is creating a significant supply-demand 
imbalance across the entire AI ecosystem. 
The bottleneck in compute is no longer limited to GPUs. It has expanded into 
memory, because Agents must understand context and store reasoning outputs in 
KV-cache; otherwise, repeated computation would lead to massive inefficiencies. The

bottleneck has also spread to optical communication, as data transfer between GPUs 
and across GPU racks increasingly constrains computing speed. Demand pressure is 
now extending to CPUs as well, since Agents must decompose objectives into 
multi-step plans, invoke tools, execute code, and verify outputs — all of which 
require deep CPU involvement. As investors increasingly recognize the coming 
explosion of Agent applications, AI hardware supply-chain stocks have rallied 
sharply across the board. The SOX surged 70% in just 40 trading days. The last 
comparable move occurred in early 2000, when the index rose 79% over the same 
period. Severe supply-demand imbalance, investor FOMO, and large-scale short 
covering collectively fueled this rally. 
Over the long run, however, the ultimate winners are still likely to emerge at the 
application layer. AI has already begun to significantly disrupt software businesses 
whose revenues are primarily enterprise-driven, while the impact on 
consumer-oriented companies remains relatively limited. In software, for example, 
the previous transition from on-premise software to cloud-based subscription services 
mainly delivered cost efficiencies. In the future, however, advances in coding 
capabilities are likely to dramatically reduce software development costs, leading to 
an explosion in personalized software creation. U.S. enterprises, in particular, may 
increasingly rely on customized internal software rather than standardized SaaS 
products. China is structurally different. Because software engineering labor costs are 
relatively low, Chinese enterprises have historically relied more on customized 
software solutions. As a result, the market for generalized SaaS products in China

remains comparatively small. For Anthropic, this represents a major opportunity. The 
company is moving beyond the model layer and pushing into the application layer, 
using flexible AI-native software tools to compete directly with traditional enterprise 
software incumbents. Given its enterprise-focused revenue model, we remain highly 
constructive on its long-term prospects. Among consumer-facing companies, we are 
particularly optimistic about Google and Tencent. Google, aside from the energy 
layer, has deep strategic positioning across the other four layers of the AI stack. More 
importantly, AI improvements are enhancing search quality, which in turn strengthens 
monetization through advertising. The value of Google’s search entry point has not 
been weakened by AI — if anything, it has become more important. Tencent, 
meanwhile, maintains strong competitive moats and user stickiness across gaming, 
short-video platforms, financial payments, and mini-program ecosystems. As 
Agent-oriented model capabilities continue to improve, and domestic AI chips scale 
rapidly in shipment volume, Agent applications built around mini-program 
ecosystems could become one of the most important future opportunities. Direct task 
completion through natural-language instructions may ultimately drive substantial 
incremental growth for Tencent’s cloud and advertising businesses. 
In the short term, markets are likely to continue chasing companies benefiting 
most directly from supply-demand imbalances and explosive earnings growth. Our 
focus, however, remains on businesses with stronger long-term certainty and more 
durable competitive advantages.

Fund Strategy 
 Investment philosophy: research-driven fundamental-oriented value investing to pursue long-term 
above-average returns. 
 Investment criteria: the fund invests in companies with 1) a strong business model, 2) a strong 
management team, and 3) strong corporate governance. 
 Due diligence system: the fund adopts a “360 degree” due diligence approach, which includes interviewing 
management, customers, supply chain, competitors, regulators, and industry experts.
