Dear Partner, 
Plustick Partners (QP), LP (“Plustick” the “Partnership” or the “Fund”) returned +3.3% net in the March 
quarter. While our January performance was strong, some of that reversed when the market weakened after the 
U.S. and Israel attacked Iran. Despite that, the Fund was up for the quarter while the S&P sank 4.6%.1 
Spectrum investments have been a driver of much of our relative outperformance this past year. On May 14th 
we will hold our annual meeting in New York, and we hope many of you will attend. At the annual meeting, we 
will do a deep dive into the reasons our spectrum-related securities have rallied.2 
Below is a brief history of our Dish Network/EchoStar spectrum investment we initiated in June of 2025, which 
highlights our financial and behavioral stakeholder research coming together for the Fund’s benefit.  
Late last spring the Chairman of the FCC threatened to take back several very valuable spectrum licenses that 
EchoStar (“SATS”) had previously acquired. (Dish Network took the name EchoStar when the companies 
merged in 2023). The threat was based on the FCC’s belief that EchoStar was not actively using the spectrum as 
mandated by the FCC, alleging EchoStar was hoarding spectrum. In response, the Chair of EchoStar, Charlie 
Ergen, threatened to place the company in bankruptcy, potentially tying up the spectrum in court for years. 
EchoStar had around $30 billion in debt on its balance sheet, and investors naturally panicked. The stock tanked 
on the bankruptcy threat, trading down to $18 and we purchased it. Normally that is a very bad investment 
strategy. However, our analysis of the behaviors and the capital structure suggested a different story. Charlie 
Ergen controls the stock with a majority stake, which represents most of his net worth, and the company had an 
incredibly complex capital structure. In that complex web, Ergen had engineered it so that the stock was 
SENIOR to most of the debt on the operating business. We believed we were aligned properly and of all things 
had the first claim on the company’s valuable spectrum assets. Our ability to value spectrum was an advantage 
here as well. In our experience stocks rarely reflect the market value of spectrum that is not being used. In this 
case the stock reflected almost no value for the spectrum, which had been purchased for tens of billions of 
dollars about a decade ago. (Another interesting factor was that the Dish/EchoStar stock at $18 was essentially 
the SAME PRICE as it was in 1999.) 
On the behavioral side, Ergen is getting older and logically had some incentive to make a move. The FCC 
Chairman wanted the spectrum to in the hands of a company or companies that could deploy it. Ergen met with 
the President and the FCC Chair in June 2025 and at that point agreed to sell some of EchoStar’s spectrum to 
cure the FCC complaint. SATS market cap was about $7 billion. The first sale was to AT&T for $23 billion and 
the stock ran to the 60’s. Then Ergen announced that he was selling additional spectrum to SpaceX for a 
combination of cash and SpaceX stock representing $19.6 billion in value. The stock portion was based on a 
SpaceX valuation of $400 billion. At that time, according to public reports, SpaceX had recently raised money 
at an $800 billion valuation. We thought SpaceX was worth more and held on as our EchoStar stock moved into 
the 80’s. This winter SpaceX began interviewing investment bankers for a potential public offering. Rumor on

Overview of Long Positions 
We added a position in Venture Global (“VG”). VG is one of the largest exporters of liquified natural gas 
(LNG) in the U.S. The company went public in 2025, and the stock has been cut in half primarily due to 
concerns about lawsuits from certain competitors who are also customers. Most of those suits have been 
resolved, and we think the remaining claim is not substantive. VG has a proprietary process for liquifying gas 
that is both significantly cheaper and faster to construct. Consequently, its projects produce cash much more 
quickly and it can sell gas profitably at a lower price than its competitors. About 30% of its 2026 capacity was 
available to be sold at spot prices in March, which should boost cashflow given the closed Strait of Hormuz. 
VG is growing like a software business at a rate unheard of for a heavy industry company. By 2027, based on 
projections, VG’s export capacity could double and match that of Qatar (currently the largest exporter in the 
world). We believe that the company could generate $100 billion in free cash flow between now and 2030. It 
has a market cap of about $30 billion currently.  
We added to our position in Sable Offshore (“SOC”) after meeting with management in March. Sable produces 
oil off the coast of Santa Barbara, but production was shut due to political opposition in California. The stock 
moved up when the Defense Production Act (DPA) was invoked on March 13, 2026, to enable production to 
address energy supply risks. California imports 75% of its oil, and the war could disrupt some of those imports. 
California has roughly 40 military installations which are at risk of losing their supply of refined products. State 
politicians have objected publicly to Sable producing, and California courts have sought to stop the production.  
We also added positions in SM Energy, Apache, and Occidental partially as hedges against the impact of the 
war on the U.S. economy. We like these names for fundamental reasons as well. These positions helped our 
performance in the quarter. 
We added to our holdings of Moderna (“MRNA”) stock as our conviction in the promise of their cancer 
therapies increased. Moderna is developing personalized mRNA cancer vaccines in collaboration with Merck. 
Early indications are that the drug increases the effectiveness of Keytruda, Merck’s blockbuster cancer therapy. 
In recently presented clinical data in a study of locally advanced or metastatic melanoma, the combined 
treatment showed a response rate of over 80%. Two patients in the study had a complete response, which means 
the total absence of any signs of remaining cancer. Results of a separate five-year trial show the risk of 
recurrence or death in melanoma patients was cut by about half when combined with Merck’s Keytruda. Based 
on these results, Moderna was granted the FDA’s fast track designation. Keytruda accounted for almost half of 
Merck’s sales last year - $29.5 billion. However, due to a variety of factors, analysts project it will do just $7 
billion by 2032. In our view this MRNA therapy could meaningfully increase sales of Merck’s biggest drug. We 
believe Merck has every incentive to help usher this through the FCC process. 
Amazon announced that it had reached an agreement to acquire Globalstar for roughly $11.6 billion. We had 
speculated that Amazon could be an acquirer, albeit about eight years early. It was nice to see the value of the 
company’s satellite and spectrum assets finally realized. This investment has been a winner, although it took 
longer than we had expected. (To put it mildly).

We added a new position in Anterix during the quarter when the FCC announced that it would vote to increase 
the bandwidth of Anterix’s spectrum by 60%. The stock did not, in our view, reflect the value of the company’s 
spectrum at the narrower levels and we thought it was kind of crazy we had an opportunity to buy the stock 
where we did. Anterix management announced it had retained Morgan Stanley due to in-bound acquisition 
interest. The stock has moved up about 50% since we purchased it. The company currently has an enterprise 
value of about $826 million and trades at about 10 times trailing earnings. We believe the company could be 
worth closer to $2 billion in a reasonable upside case. Anterix’s spectrum is licensed for critical infrastructure 
use, typically utilities. We think an enterprising management team could argue that AI is critical infrastructure, 
which could make Anterix worth a lot more than $2 billion.  
Our AI infrastructure names, Applied Digital, Nebius, and Innodata, were basically flat during the quarter. 
Applied Digital announced a new hyperscaler customer just after the quarter ended and the stock moved up. We 
exited our Supermicro stock, prior to allegations that with a company official had diverted $2.5 billion in 
Nvidia AI chips to China.   
We trimmed our solar companies, including Sunrun, First Solar and T1 Energy. While power demand is 
growing, these stocks don’t appear to be benefitting from the demand. Orsted had a nice move when a federal 
court removed the injunction and allowed work to continue on the large Revolution Wind project off the coast 
of Rhode Island.   
Overview of Short Positions 
We don’t have a ton to update on the short front. Our short exposure remains relatively modest at about 10%. 
This is a function of the opportunity set in the market that we are seeing. Because credit conditions are 
relatively loose, and because of the advent of so-called liability management exercises (or “LMEs”), 
bankruptcies have been relatively rare.  Consequently, we have had to be nimble on the shorts, adding and 
covering when we think it is appropriate, as opposed to shorting things to bankruptcy. We added shorts in Ineos 
bonds and McAfee bonds. Ineos is a European chemicals company with a lot of leverage and dealing with 
rising input costs due to the Iran situation. McAfee is an anti-virus software company that we think could be 
disrupted by advances in AI and competition from larger and better-capitalized rivals.
