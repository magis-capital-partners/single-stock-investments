Quarterly Commentary 
 
We have addressed the recent poor performance of the fund in some detail in the last two quarterly letters, 
and discussed it in meetings and calls with many of our investors during this period. It is disappointing to be 
reporting on a further drawdown in this letter, as the negative trends we have previously elaborated have not 
yet abated. We summarise these below, but we welcome any investor who would like a further in -person 
update to contact us directly to schedule time with our team. 
 
The main explanatory factor for the fund’s continued underperformance has been the ongoing weakness in 
Quality stocks. There is no definitive way of calibrating this, but UBS’ HOLT data suggest that this is now – by 
quite some margin - the biggest market relative drawdown for European quality stocks in any 18 month period 
since the start of their dataset in 1990. 1 Typically, quality stocks would outperform during periods of 
geopolitical disruption – a function of the defensiveness conferred by strong balance sheets and high profit 
margins. The current conflict in the Middle East has not, as yet, meaningfully affected the overall pattern.  
 
One comfort we draw from this disappointing period is that the long portfolio remains on track to deliver good 
earnings growth at a low -teens % rate. We further believe that this growth, while not immune to geopolitical 
or macro pressures , will prove very much more resilient than the earnings growth from the broader market . 
Meanwhile, the significant derating of our portfolio over the past eighteen months - 16% absolute and 18% 
market relative - has left it valued at a level of market-relative attractiveness that we have not seen for nearly 
a decade. Whilst we can never  discount the risk of an even  further derating , and acknowledging that it is  
impossible to call when market trends may reverse, we are genuinely excited by this combination of resilience 
and growth with the valuation risk skewed to the upside . We are confident that this will compound into 
attractive returns over the medium term. 
 
Alcon 
 
Alcon, the global leader in eye care, has developed a broad portfolio of products serving billions of people 
worldwide affected by visual impairments, including cataracts, refractive errors, presbyopia, diabetic 
retinopathy, glaucoma, and dry eye disease. As we will discuss, this is an attractive industry addressing a 
fundamental human need, underpinned by long -term growth drivers, strong defensive characteristics, and a 
highly sticky customer base.  
 
Alcon started as a small pharmacy in Fort Worth, Texas in 1945. It was acquired by Nestlé in 1977, then 
subsequently sold to pharma -giant Novartis in 2010. Alcon struggled under its parent company, whose 
differing R&D priorities led to significantly lower investment in this division, ultimately hindering its ability to 
innovate. Unsurprisingly, this resulted in underwhelming growth, prompting Alcon to spin out of Novartis in 
2019 to reinvigorate innovation and accelerate both product development and sales growth.  Thus far, it has 
certainly achieved this goal; a raft of successful product launches has enabled Alcon to meet (on a constant 
currency basis) each of the 2025 financial targets that were set at its 2021 Capital Markets Day. Indeed, 
Alcon’s renewed focus on innovation led to more than ten new product releases in the past year alone. W e 
expect this trend to continue, particularly given the structural increase in Alcon’s R&D investment to 8–10% of 
sales going forward , compared with a 7 –8% range in the years leading up to the 2019 IPO, on a significantly 
smaller revenue base.  
 
Alcon is widely regarded as having the broadest product offering among its peer group, and addresses a 
myriad of eye conditions. While we cannot cover every disease area in this newsletter, we focus below on 
cataracts and contact lenses , the key areas within its Surgical and Vision Care divisions , respectively. Alcon

Cataracts 
Most people will develop cataracts at some point in later life, with an estimated 70-90% of the 80+ age cohort 
affected by them. Proteins in the eye’s lens degrade over time, resulting in a cloudy lens that gradually blocks 
more and more light from passing through. Eventually, if left untreated, the lens becomes completely opaque 
and leads to functional blindness. As such, we do not view cataract surgery as truly elective and cataract 
removal is the most frequently performed surgical procedure in the US. The procedure itself – which removes 
the cloudy natural lens and replaces it with an artificial Intraocular Lens (IOL) –takes only around ten minutes 
when carried out by the most efficient surgeons.  Alcon sells the equipment used by surgeons; the 
consumables used during the procedure; and the IOL itself. 2 The most recent data suggests there are over 5 
million cataract surgeries in the US each year and approximately 32 million globally. Procedural volumes have 
grown at 4-5% per year over the last decade, steadily above the ongoing global population growth of the 65+ 
age cohort.  
 
Besides the structural tailwind from an ag eing population, we are also seeing an increase in uptake of 
Advanced Technology Intraocular Lenses (AT -IOL) when having cataract surgery. AT -IOLs are premium lenses 
that offer superior functionality, 3 and in the US are paid for out of pocket by patients. They have various 
benefits; for example, they obviate the need for reading glasses, which are often necessary when patients 
choose a fully reimbursed monofocal lens. Global AT -IOL penetration has typically been increasing by 50 -
100bps per annum, and in Q4 2025 reached an all -time high of 16.6%. Our market research surveys and 
discussions within the industry suggest a ceiling of around 40%, meaning that there is still plenty of scope for 
further market growth. This is very much to Alcon’s benefit as AT -IOLs are one of the highest-margin products 
within the portfolio, selling for roughly 10 times the price of a monofocal lens in the US. Given that Alcon 
already holds high market share within AT-IOLs, a single percentage point increase in AT-IOL penetration has a 
roughly equivalent benefit to three points of AT-IOL market share gain.  
 
We feel confident that AT-IOLs will continue to gain market share as surgical training in this category of lenses 
continues to expand, and word of mouth about the benefits spread s to patients’ families and friends. Recent 
reimbursement pressure on traditional monofocal procedures is likely to further incentivise surgeons to 
promote AT-IOLs to patients, as premium lens surgeries can generate approximately $2K of additional income 
per eye. Anecdotal evidence suggests this trend is only just beginning.  
 
Contact lenses 
Myopia, or shortsightedness, is a common condition that is estimated to impact 35% of the global population. 
The prevalence of myopia has significantly increased from 22% in 2000 and this trend is expected to continue; 
it is likely that by 2050 over half of the world’s population will be shortsighted. This is due to several factors, 
including, unsurprisingly, increased screen time , but also decreased natural sunlight exposure .4 Nonetheless, 
only a small fraction of those affected wear contact lenses; an estimated 140 million people worldwide, 45 
million of whom are in the US. Based on the total myopic population, these figures imply penetration rates of 
ca.5% globally and c a.34% in the United States, with other mature markets such as Japan approaching 30%.  
Over the last decade, as penetration has gradually increased, contact lenses have shown consistent mid -
single-digit % growth in value.

Besides the structural tailwind from increasing prevalence of myopia, we are also seeing a steady shift to 
premium lenses. Daily lenses are much preferred by optometrists and patients – if they can afford the price 
premium – as they are more comfortable and hygienic than reusables, most of which are replaced monthly. In 
the last five years alone, daily lenses have gained about five percentage points in unit 5 and revenue share of 
the contact lens market, and they now represent ca.40% of units and ca.60% of revenue respectively. Alcon 
has a strong portfolio in both categories but has focused more of its innovation efforts on premium lenses, 
leading to a 29% global market share of dailies compared to a 22% share of reusables as of FY 2024. Our 
market research and discussions with optometrists support the belief that customers exhibit strong brand 
loyalty and are generally reluctant to switch products.  Despite this, Alcon has gained over 200 bps of global 
market share in the last four or five years, as it has significantly enhanced its product offering. 
 
The growth opportunity 
As discussed above, Alcon operates in attractive growth markets driven by ageing demographics, as well as 
the increasing prevalence and treatment of certain diseases.  According to the sales figures for FY 2025, Alcon 
has the biggest global market share both in the ophthalmic surgical market and in the vision care market. Its 
leading positions are protected by high barriers to entry; exacting regulatory requirements ; the strong need 
for innovation; knowledgeable sales representatives; and customer service. Its $37bn TAM is expected to 
grow in the mid -single-digits every year for the foreseeable future. Furthermore, the Consumables, Contact 
Lenses, and Ocular Health segments exhibit recurring-revenue-like characteristics.  
 
At its Capital Markets Day last year, Alcon set out its aim to achieve 6 -8% organic revenue growth  over the 
long-term, well ahead of the market growth rate. We believe success will depend on Alcon’s ability to execute 
new product launches effectively. Accordingly, we leverage our market research to track new product 
releases. Our surveys and calls with surgeons suggest they are gaining solid traction in the market thus far; key 
launches like the Unity surgical platform and the PanOptix Pro trifocal IOL drove accelerating sales growth for 
the Surgical division in H2 2025.  
 
Above-market organic growth, alongside margin expansion driven by manufacturing efficiencies and well -
established operating leverage on SG&A, underpins our forecast of low -teens EPS growth for Alcon, 
accompanied by improving returns on capital.  
 
Attractive valuation 
Alcon currently trades on a 19x P/E, approaching three standard deviations below its historic market -relative 
average since it was first listed. We believe this is mostly driven by a de -rating of the entire MedTech sector, 
which has faced continued pressure from rotation into other sectors, as well as above average headline noise 
around regulatory headwinds and reimbursement changes over the last couple of years. More specifically to 
Alcon, last year saw a slowdown in global cataract procedures, with approximately 3% growth compared to a 
long-term historical average of 4 –5%. While growth naturally fluctuates from year to year, 3% is at the lower 
end of the normal range. Despite slower procedural growth we take comfort from two things: first, there is no 
slowdown in the number of cataracts requiring treatment; and second, we continue to see a steady increase 
in AT-IOL penetration.  
 
The market has also been concerned about several new competitor launches, specifically in AT -IOLs over the 
last couple of years. We believe Alcon’s new products such as  PanOptix Pro and soon-to-come Vivity Pro will 
reinforce its #1 position ; o ur market research and expert calls support this view . PanOptix Pro is now 
launching in Japan and Australia and is set to launch in Europe soon; the rollout has been slightly delayed due 
to the need to meet higher-than-anticipated demand in the US. We estimate that Alcon still holds at least 55% 
market share in AT-IOLs.

Amadeus 
 
We have written about Amadeus several times over recent years, most comprehensively in June 2022 and 
with subsequent updates in our year -end letters. As such, this is not intended to be a comprehensive 
articulation of the investment thesis, but rather a discussion of how we think the business is likely to be 
affected by the emergence of AI, and why we believe that the recent share price weakness has created an 
attractive opportunity to add to this position. 
 
Over the past year, but most notably in the first quarter of 2026, the market grouped Amadeus into a broad 
bucket of putative “AI losers”, alongside many other businesses perceived to be vulnerable to disruption. Our 
view is that this reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of the company’s role in the travel ecosystem, the 
value it provides to its various counterparties, and how difficult this functionality is to replicate. Indeed, rather 
than being disrupted, we believe it is more likely that Amadeus will prove to be a net beneficiary of the advent 
of AI. 
 
Our approach to this opportunity is also informed by how we have positioned the portfolio more broadly. 
Over the last two years, we significantly reduced our overall exposure to this portion of the market, reflecting 
the heightened uncertainty around the potential impact of AI on a range of software and marketplace 
businesses. However, as the technology has evolved and its capabilities and limitations become clearer, we 
have started to form more grounded views on the potential outcomes for individual companies, allowing us to 
selectively add back to positions where we see mispricing. 
 
Air Distribution 
Amadeus’s Air Distribution business sits at the centre of the airline retailing ecosystem, acting as the global 
marketplace and technology infrastructure that connects airlines with travel sellers such as travel agencies, 
online travel agencies (OTAs) and corporate travel agents (TMCs). Through its Global Distribution System 
(GDS), Amadeus aggregates flight schedules, fares, availability, and ancillary services from hundreds of airlines 
and distributes them to thousands of travel sellers worldwide, enabling them to search, book, and service 
flights in real time. Through a single standardised platform, airlines can access a vast network of demand and 
travel sellers can access content from almost all commercial airlines. The system is extremely complex 
because it must reconcile many different airline reservation systems, fare rules, inventory controls, and

The alternative to a GDS would be myriad separate technical integrations between airlines and travel sellers, 
each with different data formats, booking logic, servicing rules and commercial agreements. Such a system

into airline reservation systems; and cost -effectively handling high volumes of mission -critical transactions. 
The GDS also embeds the commercial, contractual and settlement infrastructure of the ecosystem, which 
would be equally difficult to replicate. As such, our view is that AI does not change this dynamic and that there 
is little, if any, incentive for a new entrant to replicate GDS rails. Furthermore, by utilising the GDS channel, a

directly with an airline.

Our belief is that agentic workflows will likely be channelled via existing intermediaries, such as OTAs, rather

waste time and money attempting to (re-) solve the plumbing of the travel industry when a highly reliable and 
cost-effective solution is available. The second reason is economic; there is not a straightforward mechanism 
today for an agent to be paid to make a booking directly with an airline, whereas there is when the booking is 
channelled via intermediaries. This will potentially allow the agent to monetise its position at the top of the

We also believe the actions and statements of companies such as Google and OpenAI support our argument. 
Starting with Google, which clearly stated that it has no intention of becoming a merchant of record, 
suggesting that it intends to utilise intermediaries for the fulfilment of an agentic booking. One should 
consider that its starting position is a highly lucrative advertising -driven business that extracts a significant 
share of industry economics without involvement in the complex downstream activities  which offer a smaller 
available profitable pool. Furthermore, Google is arguably a more relevant indicator of how this ecosystem is 
likely to evolve, given its deep domain experience in travel and its long -standing efforts to aggregate and 
monetise travel demand. It has learnt first -hand the challenges of aggregating airline content through its 
decade-long and ultimately unsuccessful attempt to build a complete metasearch platform; and remember 
that metasearch is a much simpler platform to build because it isn’t transactional. It is perhaps less clear how 
OpenAI ultimately intends to construct its agentic offering, but recent moves suggest that it is stepping away 
from direct involvement in transactional commerce and is instead focused on enabling third party 
applications. 
 
It seems likely that an agentic booking process will become a feature of the travel industry, but we see this as 
an opportunity rather than a threat to Amadeus. A significant portion of airline bookings, particularly for 
leisure travel, are made directly with the airline and therefore bypass the Amadeus rails. These bookings are 
arguably most susceptible to disruption by an agentic workflow, which as we’ve explained above, are likely to 
be intermediated by Amadeus’ customers.  
 
Air IT 
Amadeus’ Air IT business provides the core technology systems that airlines use to run their commercial 
operations, most notably the Passenger Service System (PSS), which manages reservations, inventory and 
departure control. 
 
The PSS is a highly complex, deterministic system that is deeply embedded within airlines and tightly 
interconnected with the wider travel ecosystem, which includes other airlines, airports and border control 
systems. It processes millions of transactions each year in real time and is completely mission critical; if it fails, 
an airline cannot sell tickets or operate flights. Our market research and discussions with airlines support our 
view that this business is not only unthreatened by the emergence of AI but is more likely to be a beneficiary 
as AI strengthens the case for airlines to migrate to a modern Offer -Order architecture, which enables 
dynamic pricing, richer product bundling, improved ancillary monetisation and more personalised customer 
offers, while simplifying servicing through a single unified order.  
 
The migration to an Offer –Order architecture is strategically important for Amadeus both economically and 
competitively. By moving airlines away from legacy systems and towards a more flexible retailing model, 
Amadeus expands the functional scope of the PSS and increases the value it captures per passenger. It is also 
likely to improve Amadeus’ already strong competitive positioning. Sabre has struggled for years with a highly 
leveraged balance sheet and has fallen further behind Amadeus as it has been unable to fund the investments 
to develop a new suite of products. Airlines themselves are unlikely to have the capacity and capability to 
develop modern systems, which we believe increases Amadeus’ chances of winning over some of the handful 
of large airlines that currently use in-house PSSs.

Conclusion 
We believe the market continues to misunderstand the fundamentals of Amadeus’ business, 
underappreciating both the value its distribution platform provides to ecosystem participants and the deep 
embeddedness of its Air IT systems. Furthermore, AI potentially enhances both divisions by re -intermediating 
bookings, accelerating airline technology modernisation, strengthening Amadeus’ competitive position, and 
creating cost efficiencies in software engineering. The stock currently trades on ca.12x 2027 earnings, while its 
robust balance sheet (0.9x net debt/EBITDA) and strong cash generation (7.5% FCF yield)  support continued 
capital returns through dividends and share buybacks. As such, we have taken advantage of this disconnect to 
add to our position.
