Market Commentary 
Asian markets were broadly mixed in the first quarter. The flat headline regional return masked sharp 
dispersion across countries, as strong gains in January and February were erased in March, following the 
escalation of the Iran conflict and the associated surge in global energy prices. 
Korea (+20%) and Taiwan (+10%) were the best-performing markets, supported by an increasingly clear 
acceleration in the memory cycle and continued strength in semiconductor capital equipment, advanced 
packaging, and AI server orders (Figure 1).

Iran Conflict and Energy Markets 
The dominant macro event of the quarter was the escalation of the Iran conflict in late February and the 
subsequent closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which drove Brent crude prices materially higher and 
disrupted global LNG and refined product flows. Asian equity markets were among the most affected 
globally given the region's dependence on Middle Eastern energy imports. Energy price sensitivity is 
particularly acute for Japan, South Korea, Thailand, India, the Philippines, and Vietnam, all of which rely 
heavily on imported oil and gas to power their industrial bases (Figure 2).

The currency reaction reinforced the equity market response. The US dollar strengthened materially on 
safe-haven flows, while most Asian currencies weakened. The largest declines were in the Korean won  
(-6.0%), Thai baht (-4.3%), Indian rupee (-4.0%), and Philippine peso (-2.8%). Only the Australian dollar 
(+3.1%) and the Chinese renminbi (+1.3%) appreciated against the US dollar during the quarter. The 
combination of weaker currencies and higher imported energy costs raised inflation expectations across 
the region and pressured central banks to defer easing cycles, particularly in countries where headline 
CPI is sensitive to fuel and food prices. 
A conditional ceasefire took effect on April 8 and has since been extended. While a durable resolution 
has not yet been reached, the direction of travel appears encouraging, and oil prices have retraced a 
portion of the spike. 
FTSE Russell Emerging Market Upgrade Confirmed 
The quarter ended with a landmark confirmation for Vietnam’s capital markets. On April 7, FTSE Russell 
confirmed that Vietnam will be upgraded to Secondary Emerging Market status in September 2026, 
following the government’s successful implementation of the Global Broker model, the final structural 
requirement for index eligibility.  
Vietnam will be included in four FTSE indices, with the upgrade implemented in four tranches over a 
twelve-month period beginning September 2026 (Figure 3).

Vitzrocell (Korea) 
Vitzrocell was our largest contributor in the first quarter, with the stock appreciating approximately 
100%. A 30% rally into mid-March was driven by improving earnings expectations, with the balance of 
gains coming after the escalation of the Iran conflict. 
Vitzrocell is a leading manufacturer of primary lithium batteries with strong market share across smart 
grid, oil and gas, and defense end markets. At entry, we viewed the stock as materially mispriced at a 
single-digit P/E given its defensible positioning, long-duration customer relationships, and exposure to 
mission-critical applications. The thesis was anchored by a stable core business with underappreciated 
upside: the smart grid segment, approximately 50% of revenue, provides predictable earnings, while oil 
and gas and defense, each approximately 20%, offered re-rating potential. We expected defense 
demand to accelerate amid rising geopolitical tensions, with supply constrained by limited capacity, 
localization requirements, and the exclusion of Chinese suppliers from key markets. 
This dynamic played out faster than we anticipated. As the Iran conflict escalated, investor focus shifted 
toward underappreciated Korean defense exposure. In March, Korean media reported that the US 
Department of Defense had met with leading domestic primary battery manufacturers regarding supply

Samsung Electronics Preferred Stock (Korea) 
Samsung Electronics Preferred Stock was our second-largest contributor during the quarter. The stock 
rose 28%, driven by strong Q4 2025 earnings reported in late January and growing investor recognition 
that the memory upcycle is both stronger and more durable than the market had assumed. In February, 
Samsung commenced mass production of HBM4, the first in the industry to do so, ahead of SK Hynix and 
Micron, and signed an MOU with AMD in mid-March, validating the company's effort to close the high-
bandwidth memory gap. By quarter-end, TrendForce was confirming contract DRAM and NAND prices 
running up 60% or more quarter-on-quarter for the upcoming Q2, well ahead of where consensus had 
been positioned at the start of the year. 
We continue to find the preferred shares the more attractive instrument within the Samsung capital 
structure. The preferred-to-common discount widened to 32% by quarter-end, about double the 5- and 
10-year average of 15%. We expect this discount to narrow over time, supported by Korea's Value-up 
agenda, recent Special Taxation Act amendments reducing dividend tax rates on qualifying high-payout 
companies, and Samsung's evolving capital return policy. The preferred stock trades at 3.7x our FY2026e 
earnings estimate, undemanding for a business whose operating profit we expect to grow more than 
six-fold this year off a depressed 2025 base. 
LIG Defense & Aerospace (Korea) 
Another top contributor was LIG Defense & Aerospace, formerly known as LIG Nex1. The company is a 
top three Korean defense firm, and the stock has seen a powerful re-rating since the start of the Iran 
conflict. We sold our position during the quarter as the stock approached our target price and we 
became concerned that Q4 earnings could miss expectations, which they did. Although the stock has 
continued to re-rate on market expectations for additional orders from the Middle East, it now trades at 
55x 2026e P/E, a valuation that provides little margin of safety if the Iran conflict de-escalates or 
quarterly earnings volatility disappoints investors. We exited with a +173% IRR over 19 months. 
FnGuide (Korea) 
FnGuide was another top contributor, rising 118% during the quarter. FnGuide is a Korean financial 
services company that operates a dominant domestic subscription financial market data business, 
effectively the Bloomberg of Korea, as well as a rapidly growing custom equity index business that 
licenses underlying indices to Korean ETFs.

The company reported Q4 2025 results in March that confirmed the index-segment margin inflection we 
had been anticipating. Revenue grew 20% y/y and operating profit grew 57% y/y, both ahead of our 
expectations. Index revenue grew 39% y/y as a wave of KRX-approved thematic ETF launches drove 
indexed AUM from approximately $10 billion at the start of 2025 to more than $34 billion by February 
2026. With approximately 80% of the cost base fixed, operating margin expanded by 5.9 percentage 
points to 30.1%, with the fourth quarter running at 32 to 33%. The thematic ETF mix, including semi/AI, 
defense, shipbuilding, and dividend products, charges 5x the fees of plain-beta products, which has so 
far insulated FnGuide from the broad-market ETF fee war that began in Korea in February 2025. The 
stock trades at 16x 2026e P/E against profit growth of approximately 75% y/y next year. 
BuySell Technologies (Japan) 
BuySell Technologies was a notable contributor, re-rating 25% on strong FY2025 results and FY2026 
guidance that was ahead of expectations: 29% revenue growth, 38% operating profit growth, and 
approximately 12% above consensus. Management also raised its FY2027 operating profit target by 
54%, implying approximately 88% organic growth over 2025 to 2027. Underlying operating metrics 
continue to improve, including record customer revisit rates, 70% growth in store procurement value, 
and an expanded footprint of 490 locations. The recent acquisition of DelightZ extends BuySell's 
geographic reach with expected near-term accretion. We continue to view BuySell as a compelling 
compounder with a long runway in Japan's underpenetrated on-site purchasing market.

The sell-off reflects a broad de-rating of global IT Services companies driven by the prevailing narrative 
that generative AI will structurally displace demand for human labor in technology outsourcing. We 
expect the industry's growth model to evolve as AI adoption matures: revenue growth will become less 
headcount-driven, and pricing for commoditized work may face near-term pressure. However, we 
believe the current de-rating applies this concern too broadly and too soon across the sector. 
The evidence from peer results through Q1 2026 supports this view. At scale, AI is proving net positive 
for IT Services demand: the largest vendors are booking record total contract values driven by multi-year 
AI transformation programs that more than offset weakness in discretionary spending. The companies 
facing genuine pressure are smaller generalist vendors without strong vertical specialization or AI 
platform differentiation. FPT sits firmly in the former category. 
Although FPT is a mid-sized competitor in a sector dominated by large Indian and Western vendors, it 
has a strong track record of consistent market share gains. Its concentration in Japan, where it grew 20% 
y/y in constant currency in Q1, provides differentiated geographic exposure that few non-Japanese 
vendors can replicate. Equally important is FPT's deepening focus on industry vertical specialization and 
AI-integrated delivery, evidenced by 70+ proprietary LLMs and a growing pipeline of large-scale AI 
transformation contracts. This combination of domain expertise and AI capability positions FPT to 
continue taking share from less specialized competitors. 
FPT's order intake acceleration is consistent with this view, and we expect revenue growth to re-
accelerate as record backlog converts to revenue. The stock currently trades at approximately 12x 2026e 
earnings with approximately 15% earnings growth. We retain our position with high conviction. 
Hon Hai (Taiwan) 
Hon Hai, also known as Foxconn, is the largest ODM assembler of servers and consumer electronics in 
the world. We purchased the stock in late 2025 with a thesis that sales of AI server racks and iPhones 
would exceed expectations. Sales did beat expectations, consistent with our thesis, but the stock 
declined on an earnings miss related to margin pressure and a higher tax rate driven by changes in 
international tax policy, as well as investor rotation into AI names with greater exposure to ongoing 
supply constraints. In early April, following a bounce in the stock, we exited the position to redeploy 
capital into more compelling opportunities. We exited with a 5% loss over 5 months. 
Techcom Securities (Vietnam) 
Techcom Securities is a new position we initiated in late February. The company is the securities 
subsidiary of Techcombank, one of Vietnam's largest private-sector banks, and holds approximately 60% 
share of Vietnam's corporate bond underwriting market. The company IPOed on the Ho Chi Minh Stock 
Exchange in October 2025.

New Investment: Tsugami (Japan) 
In March, we initiated a new investment in Tsugami, a niche-dominant Japanese machine tool company 
and the global leader in Swiss-type CNC automatic lathes, with an estimated 50 to 60% global market 
share in a highly specialized industry with only a small number of scaled competitors. The company's 
machines are used to manufacture high-precision components for end markets including automotive, 
electronics, medical devices, industrial applications, and increasingly AI-related infrastructure. 
The key component of our thesis is that Tsugami is entering a structural demand inflection driven by AI 
liquid cooling, which has grown from a negligible share of orders one year ago to approximately 23% of 
the order book, overtaking automotive as the company's largest end market. This mix shift is important 
because it reduces Tsugami's historical reliance on the automotive capex cycle while introducing a new, 
faster-growing source of demand. Order intake in the first two months of 2026 was running more than 
40% y/y, despite weakness in automotive orders. 
Despite this improving growth profile, Tsugami continues to trade at an attractive valuation. At our 
entry price, the stock was valued at approximately 8.5x FY3/27e P/E and 4.8x EV/EBIT on our estimates, 
with ROE above 20%. We believe these multiples are too low for a high-quality industrial franchise with 
leading market share, improving end-market exposure, and meaningful operating leverage. The listed 
parent company also trades at a discount to the market value of its 65% stake in Precision Tsugami 
China, effectively ascribing little value to its net cash position and Japan and India operations. In addition 
to liquid cooling, Tsugami has longer-term optionality from India, where it is expanding capacity, and 
from potential future demand related to humanoid robotics. While we continue to monitor risks 
including capacity constraints, cyclicality in automotive orders, FX translation, and potential new 
competition, we believe the stock offers an unusually attractive combination of quality, growth, and 
valuation. The investment is off to a strong start and was one of our top contributors in April.

Appendix 1: The End of US Exceptionalism? 
In our first quarter 2025 letter, we discussed a possible regime change for global stock markets following 
a prolonged period of US equity market outperformance, which had pushed US equity valuations to a 
historic premium relative to emerging markets. We have revisited this analysis and note that Asian 
emerging markets have extended their outperformance to 29% relative to the S&P 500 over the past 16 
months (Figure A1). Despite this outperformance, the valuation discount has not narrowed meaningfully 
over the past year and currently sits at 33%, materially wider than its long-term 25% discount (Figure 
A2). We believe Asian emerging markets are well positioned for continued outperformance despite 
near-term headwinds from Middle East energy supply disruptions.
