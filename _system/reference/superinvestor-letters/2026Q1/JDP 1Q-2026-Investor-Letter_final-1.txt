In the first quarter the fund was down 15.1% net to investors. This compares to the S&P 500 which 
was down 4.3% assuming dividends had been immediately reinvested back into the index.  
In the 2025 annual letter I described how market sentiment was deteriorating on AI destruction 
fears and consumer softness. Although some concern was valid, especially around the most interest 
rate sensitive sectors, 4Q earnings and guidance were broadly strong. For obvious geopolitical 
reasons sentiment has now moved from cautious to extreme fear, leading to the S&P 500's worst 
quarter since 3Q 2022.1 
What has not changed is the prospects 
for durable, growing businesses.  
The doomsday narrative today can 
sound persuasive, but similar to the 
20% market drop in early 2025 after 
the “Liberation Day” tariff 
announcement, investors once again 
have an extraordinary buying 
opportunity. 
The first quarter in 2026 began with a continuation of AI-disruption scare that started in software 
and quickly spread. Investors first sold enterprise software and IT, then moved on to insurance and 
commercial real estate brokers, data and information services, trucking, media, private credit, 
shipping, freight and anything adjacent to those sectors. Even Spotify wasn't immune from the 
selloff even after a very bullish 4th quarter. 
Then the war in Iran broke out, oil prices spiked, causing further selling based on a wide range of 
bearish scenarios.  
The Iran war is a far more serious event than tariffs but, does that mean stocks are intrinsically 
worth less every day that oil trades up?   
The structure of both the U.S. economy and the S&P 500 has changed dramatically over the last 50 
years. For example, in the 1970s, the S&P was dominated by energy-intensive industrials, autos, and 
heavy manufacturing with input costs linked to oil and commodities. The US was also dependent on 
imported oil.

Today over half of the S&P 500's value is either big technology and / or intellectual-property-driven 
businesses where the primary inputs are people and compute-related expenses. Even our 
manufacturing businesses today are much more efficient than in the past.  
The U.S. is also now a net-exporter of energy. Higher oil prices drive US energy earnings and 
spending, which helps offset the impact of higher prices on the economy (Texas booms).  
More importantly, in a world of slowing growth, investors will ultimately pay a premium to own 
secular winners that can keep compounding when most can't.   
Our job is to separate what feels scary from what actually threatens long term intrinsic value. To do 
that consistently we underwrite new and existing investments using what we call the Survivor and 
Thriver Investment Criteria. This four point criteria acts as both filter and guardrail.  
Mistakes are part of investing, but I find that having a North Star helps minimize the distraction from 
whatever market rotation is fashionable in the moment. The criteria also helps reinforce the 
conviction to hold, or even double down on our best ideas in rough markets.  
The Survivor and Thriver criteria: 
1. A business model that is adaptable and relevant in tomorrow’s economy. 
2. Durable pricing power protected by a growing competitive advantage. 
3. Capital allocation and balance sheet strategy that supports and widens the company’s moat. 
4. Clear alignment of interest between management and equity owners. 
Possibly the most impactful criteria on long-term returns might be number 3, capital allocation and 
balance sheet strategy that supports the company’s moat. The other criteria are important, but if 
there is one common link among the highest returning stocks in history there was a strong CEO 
willing to invest aggressively in growth that catapulted the company's flywheel when it did not make 
sense to the market.  
The ability for a large business to reinvest most of its cash flow at incremental returns above its cost 
of capital for many years is very rare, and is the most powerful driver of long term value creation.  
Companies that are reinvesting aggressively today to meet demand are especially out of favor. 
Although the market has been skeptical on AI infrastructure spend, growth spending generally is 
being punished. Amazon is one of the best examples. The stock now trades for the lowest valuation 
in its history as punishment for announcing an additional $200 billion in spend supporting AWS, 
automation and robotics.2 Amazon is selling closer to a distressed valuation than a juggernaut 
positioned to be one of the biggest beneficiaries of AI over the next decade.  
There is a big difference between investing to meet high customer demand and spending on 
maintenance capex to preserve existing sales. All else equal, we would much rather own a company 
with a long runway to reinvest 100% of its gross profits at high incremental returns than a company 
with little left to do with its cash other than pay taxes and return it to shareholders.

Looking back at many of the most successful businesses in history such as Starbucks, Costco, Apple, 
Netflix or Nike, you will find periods when management doubled down on growth instead of 
maximizing after-tax earnings to make the P/E look good, even when the stock was out of favor.  
This often resulted in the valuation of these companies looking expensive in terms of screenable 
financial metrics, but very cheap to the investor who could figure out earnings power. 
Conversely, if you look back at many of the biggest stock failures it is common to see management 
that refused to invest behind change or new demand out of fear of getting fired after a stock price 
decline, or fear of destroying the legacy cash cow. 
In summary, if the economy continues to slow, companies with the most insulated growth runways 
should emerge even more valuable. From that perspective we are positioned well for a strong 
recovery once the market gets back to business. 
Update on Caesars Entertainment (CZR) 
In late March we exited Caesars Entertainment (CZR) after reports that the company is in talks to sell 
itself to Tilman Fertitta or Carl Icahn for a fire-sale price. Over our fund's history we have had several 
positions taken from us via buyouts at valuations far below a reasonable valuation. In the case of 
Caesars, I was especially disappointed after reading that CEO Tom Reeg is reported to be part of the 
buyout. Reeg is someone I had grown to admire and respect. I had believed he could stick out the 
stock's drawdown without taking advantage of minority investors. 
It is not uncommon to see cheap stocks get taken private especially after long drawdown periods. 
Late last year a smaller Vegas casino operator stock called Golden Entertainment announced a 
buyout by its controlling shareholder family. The deal was so egregious it sounds nearly unbelievable 
if explained out loud. Andrew Walker from the podcast YAV did an episode on the Golden 
Entertainment buyout titled Shining a Light on Golden Entertainment's Wealth Transfer that is 
worth a listen for anyone curious how these things can happen. The Golden buyout set a precedent 
for an absurdly low Caesars bid that could be accepted by public stockholders. We kept a <1% 
position in out of the money calls just in case the bid improves before a deal is approved by gaming 
regulators. Moving on..  
New position: Mercado Libre (MELI) 
Mercado Libre (MELI) is a classic Survivor & Thriver business but that we rarely see on sale. MELI is 
the dominant e-commerce, fintech platform and logistics provider in Latin America, ahead of 
Amazon, Shopee and smaller regional competitors. MELI is run by founder-operators who are 
aggressively reinvesting in the business for the next decade. Similar to Amazon in the early 2000s, 
using a P/E ratio to assess MELI's earnings power is a bad idea. That said, at an equity valuation of 
$85 billion or $1,600 per share, we acquired our stake for less than 1x GMV and ~18x estimated 2026 
earnings.3 Unlike US or European with a similar valuation and market position, MELI should be able 
to continue growing north of 25% annually for years ahead, making our entry price very attractive. 
MELI is currently in a significant macro-driven drawdown, made worse by Iran war and sell-off in 
emerging market stocks. Prior to the recent 40% decline, the stock had also not fully kept up with 
fundamental growth and geographic expansion in my opinion, especially in Mexico.

MELI operates across 18 countries with large market share in Brazil (36%), Argentina (55%) and 
Mexico (27%). MELI is a remarkable success story with revenue having grown over 55,000% since its 
IPO in 2007. The company is still growing over 30% annually.  
The three things that jump off the page for me about MELI are  (1) the durability and runway of its 
ecommerce growth, (2) the profitability trajectory of its fintech business built on top of its 
ecommerce business, and (3) management's willingness to continue reinvesting for growth years in 
advance and experiment with new products and offerings.

MELI's tailwinds 
Ecommerce penetration in Latin America is approximately half the level of the US, UK, and China. I 
see no reason why the region should not reach similar penetration levels over the next decade. 
MELI's market has ~650 million people and a median age in the late 20s to early 30s. As this young 
population moves more of its spending online, MELI is positioned to capture more share across 
ecommerce and banking.  
As we know from a past Brazilian investment, Latin America is generally under-banked and interest 
rates on credit can be north of 100%. Fewer than 20% of Mexicans and only about 40% of Argentines 
have a credit card; in Mexico only about half the population has a bank account. In Brazil, four legacy 
banks still control most of the credit issuance.

Mercado Pago has already become one of the largest digital financial institutions in Latin America. 
Fintech is MELI’s fastest-growing segment, compounding at over 40%  with payment volume growing 
30%+ annually. Assets under management in savings and investment products more than doubled in 
2025 as users move deposits out of legacy bank accounts into Mercado Pago.  
MELI's credit business is less known but becoming the company's growth driver. The company uses 
its consumer database and payment transactions to underwrite risk. In Q4 2025 alone Mercado Pago 
issued 3 million credit cards.  
Credit is only one layer on a broader financial services stack. Mercado Pago offers QR payments at 
physical merchants, debit and credit cards, consumer and SME loans, buy-now-pay-later, insurance 
products, wealth management, and payroll tools. Each new product increases revenue per user and 
deepens engagement. A merchant who adopts MELI for acquiring often takes working capital loans. 
A consumer who pays with Mercado Pago is cross-sold credit cards, savings, and insurance. The 
resulting data flywheel feeds back into better underwriting and personalization.  
In summary MELI represents a proven compounder operating in a part of the world with strong 
demographic and economic tailwinds. Not only does the stock seem very oversold, but the 
company's exceptional quality could lead to it recovering much faster than other emerging market 
peers. 
Closing thoughts 
Our goal is to invest in businesses that we would buy more of when they are down, and sell stocks 
when the valuation far exceeds a reasonable estimate of the business's potential.
