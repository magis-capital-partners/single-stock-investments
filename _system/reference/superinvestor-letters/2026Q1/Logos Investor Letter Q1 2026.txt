Industry Overview 
The Maturation of an Industry: From Promise to Proof 
What occurred in biotechnology over the past several years was not just a bear market into a recovery. It was a structural 
transition from promise to proof. The prior cycle rewarded possibility itself: platform narratives, mechanistic hypotheses, 
and preclinical ambition often commanded valuations untethered from clinical maturity. The correction that followed was 
not simply about rates and risk appetite. It was the reassertion of biological reality. Programs without differentiated science 
lost capital and relevance. Companies built around durable data survived the purge, and in many cases emerged stronger. 
The most important fact about biotechnology today is one almost no one is talking about: the public universe is structurally 
better than it was at the last peak. Five years ago, the median dollar of biotech market cap sat behind a preclinical or Phase 
1 asset, and the weighted probability of clinical success was roughly 25%. Today, >75% of the sector sits at Phase 2, 3, and 
commercial with a >50% weighted probability of clinical success. Approximately 40% of current XBI constituents already 
have approved drugs, with a credible path to roughly 80% by 2H 2027. The index price has yet to reflect that maturation. 
Q1 2026 confirmed the pattern in clinical outcomes. We expect this pattern of dense, high -conviction late-stage data flow 
to continue through 2026 and 2027.

Closing the Healthspan-Lifespan Gap 
Apart from quarterly returns and capital-markets dynamics, the most consequential development in biotechnology in 2026 
is what the industry is now positioned to address. The average American lives for approximately seventy -nine years and 
spends the final f ourteen in declining health, accumulating cancers, cardiovascular events, and neurodegenerative disease 
at rates that climb nearly vertically after age sixty-five. This is the healthspan-lifespan gap, the single largest unmet need in 
modern medicine, and it is where the most consequential science of the next decade is being directed.

The curves above reflect lived reality, not statistical artifact. Cancer incidence climbs from near zero in early adulthood t o 
nearly four thousand cases per hundred thousand by age eighty. Cardiovascular and neurodegenerative disease follow 
similar trajectories, each accelerating precisely at the moment when the practical experience of being a person should be at 
its richest. The fourteen-year gap between healthspan and lifespan is the lived experience of essentially every family in the 
developed world, and it is what biopharmaceutical innovation is now positioned to compress. 
For the first time, many of the biological pathways and diseases that defined prior generations of therapeutic frustration are 
beginning to look tractable. Across oncology, cardiovascular disease, immunology, neurology, metabolic disease, and rare 
genetic disorders, advances in chemistry, biologics, gene editing, RNA therapeutics, and precision medicine are allowing 
the industry to target mechanisms that, until recently, sat beyond the reach of modern drug development. 
What is striking is not simply the emergence of new technologies, but the growing consistency with which those 
technologies are now translating into meaningful late -stage clinical outcomes. Mechanisms once viewed as scientifically 
inaccessible are increasi ngly producing validated human data, regulatory pathways, commercial franchises, and, most 
importantly, tangible improvements in patient outcomes. The cumulative effect is that biotechnology is no longer operating 
at the edge of theoretical promise alone; it is increasingly operating in a phase of real clinical realization.

Rent the Uncertainty, Own the Transformation 
If there is a single sentence that captures how we think about portfolio construction at Logos, it is this: in biotechnology,  
everything ultimately comes down to whether you should rent the biology or own it. Historically, we described this as 
“event” versus “compounder,” but renting versus owning better captures the mentality behind the decision. 
You rent exposure when the opportunity is primarily catalyst -driven: the science is promising but not yet fully proven, the 
market inefficiency is tied to a specific inflection point, or the range of outcomes remains unusually wide. Renting is tactical, 
participating in asymmetry while preserving flexibility. Ownership is different. You own biology when conviction deepens 
with time rather than diminishes, when repeated datasets reinforce the mechanism, and when the company begins to 
resemble a future franchise. These are what we historically called “compounders.” Ownership means we have crossed the 
scientific threshold: we have validated the mechanism, the safety profile, the competitive moat, the regulatory glide path, 
and the commercial opportunity. The gr eatest long-term returns in biotechnology are generated not by perfectly trading 
events, but by recognizing early which companies deserve to be owned through multiple chapters of maturation. 
Q1 2026 produced a series of clinical readouts that, in our view, reflected something broader than isolated company 
successes. United Therapeutics reported Phase 3 TETON-1 data in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis with the largest treatment 
effect yet observed  in the disease. Revolution Medicines delivered survival data in metastatic pancreatic cancer that 
represented a major validation for the broader RAS field. Cytokinetics reported the first positive Phase 3 study in non-
obstructive hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, a disease with no previously approved therapies. At Biogen, continued progress 
in disease-modifying neuroscience therapies reinforced the growing reality that even historically difficult areas of medicine 
are beginning to yield meaningful advances. What stood out was not simply the success of individual studies, but the breadth 
of clinically meaningful progress emerging simultaneously across pulmonary disease, oncology, cardiovascular medicine, 
and neuroscience. 
We rent before the catalyst because that is when the risk is highest and the dispersion widest. Once the catalyst delivers, the 
question changes from whether the science works to how the asset compounds. Most managers do not lose money in this 
sector because they lack insight; they lose because they own the wrong assets through the wrong phases, or they sell the 
right assets too early. Discipline about the rent -versus-own distinction is the most underrated source of edge in 
biotechnology investing.

and most durable beneficiaries of the AI cycle. The market has not yet fully reflected this in equity prices. 
Three principal reasons. First, healthcare is by orders of magnitude the largest end market in the world to which AI can be 
meaningfully applied: a $5.3 trillion industry, with biopharmaceutical R&D alone exceeding $250 billion in annual 
spending. Modest improvements in productivity, hit rates, or development timelines compound into very large absolute

The AI inflection in drug discovery is operational 
Drug discovery has historically been a probabilistic search problem, with approval probability for any individual molecule 
of approximately 4% and idea -to-clinic timelines of three to seven years. AlphaFold and its successors solved structure 
prediction. Biology-native foundation models are now addressing protein- protein interactions, conformational dynamics, 
and combinatorial binding. Generative chemistry models propose molecules satisfying multiple constraints simultaneously. 
Leading platforms demonstrate idea-to-clinic timelines of twelve to twenty-four months with substantial cost reductions at 
the discovery and preclinical stages. 
In the past twelve to eighteen months, this has moved from research narrative to operating reality at the largest 
pharmaceutical companies. In January 2026, Eli Lilly and NVIDIA announced a co-innovation AI laboratory backed by up 
to $1 billion of joint investment over five years on NVIDIA’s BioNeMo platform. Anthropic launched Claude for Life 
Sciences in October 2025 and acquired Coefficient Bio in April 2026 in an all -stock transaction valued at approximately 
$400 million, signaling that frontier model la boratories see life sciences as a core vertical. Roche disclosed deployment of 
more than 3,500 NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs. Sanofi, Novo Nordisk, AbbVie, AstraZeneca, GSK, Pfizer, and Bayer have all 
announced material AI partnerships. In NVIDIA’s 2026 State of AI in Healthcare survey, 74% of pharma and biotech 
respondents reported active AI deployment, with 46% citing drug discovery among their top ROI use cases. 86% indicated 
AI budgets would increase in 2026. Healthcare is deploying AI at more than twice the rate of the broader economy. 
“The next wave of drug discovery will be designed, not discovered.”  
 Jensen Huang, NVIDIA 
Huang’s framing captures what is happening. Historical drug discovery was, in essence, high-throughput screening: throw 
molecules at a target, see what sticks. AI is replacing that paradigm with rational design. The implication is not just faster 
discovery but a different cost structure for innovation: as AI compresses the failure rate at discovery and preclinical stages, 
the economics of innovation shift toward the developer. 
Why biotechnology is structurally well-positioned to benefit 
A reasonable concern is that AI could eventually compress returns in biotechnology the way it has in some software 
categories: if molecules become easier to find, do they become cheaper to own? We do not believe so. Patents grant 
monopoly economics on a successful drug for the better part of a decade. Regulation creates the highest barriers to entry of 
any major industry. Reimbursement systems generate inelastic demand for therapies that meaningfully extend life. Major 
end markets, oncology, rare disease, cardiovascular, neurology, are non-cyclical and tied to demographic factors that 
compound. Biotechnology also exhibits the lowest cross-sector correlation in the equity market, approximately 0.32, below 
utilities, energy, and consumer staples. When AI layer s onto this structure, the result is not commoditization of output but 
compounding of input: more candidates, more rapidly, against more validated targets, with higher probability of technical 
success, all landing inside the same protected moat.

Concentration, Cohesion, Conviction 
We are sometimes asked why Logos has remained intentionally small. The answer is not that we underestimate scale. There 
are many exceptional firms in biotechnology with tremendous resources and differentiated networks, and we have learned 
from many. But bi otechnology is a field where competitive advantage does not increase linearly with size. The most 
important edges come from speed of interpretation, concentration of thought, and the ability to move decisively when 
conviction is highest. Biotechnology outcomes are often driven by a surprisingly small number of critical variables: patient 
selection, dose intensity, biomarker strategy, trial design, translational validity, or a single overlooked aspect of mechanism. 
The challenge is rarely a lack of information; it is determining which variables actually matter before consensus forms. 
Increasingly, this is where data science and artificial intelligence have become deeply important to our process. Over the 
last several years, we have invested heavily in building internal analytical frameworks capable of modeling clinical 
outcomes across thousands of parameters simultaneously: enrollment dynamics, historical placebo behavior, biomarker 
enrichment, adverse-event patterns, dose modifications, mechanistic comparability across prior studies, and statistical 
assumptions embedded within trial de sign. More recently, we have begun migrating substantial portions of this analytical 
infrastructure onto modern large language model architectures and AI -assisted workflows. The results have been 
remarkable. Tasks that previously required extensive manual synthesis across fragmented scientific literature, conference 
presentations, protocols, and regulatory documents can increasingly be analyzed, cross -referenced, modeled, and stress -
tested with dramatically greater speed and depth. 
Importantly, we do not view AI as replacing scientific judgment. We view it as amplifying the productivity of concentrated 
expertise. The advantage does not come from possessing more raw information than others. It comes from structuring, 
filtering, interrogating, and acting on information more effectively while retaining rigorous biological and clinical intuition 
at the center of the process. 
A compact, specialized team is extraordinarily effective in this environment because communication is direct, accountability 
is clear, and conclusions translate into action quickly. Our structure lets us remain selective, wait for opportunities where 
asymmetry is truly compelling, and participate in structured financings, crossovers, and specialized situations less practical 
for substantially larger pools of capital. The firm is built around a simple philosophy: concentrated expertise, disciplined 
conviction, patience, intellectual honesty, and a willingness to act decisively when the odds are materially mispriced.

Capital markets: selective reopening, disciplined pricing 
The capital markets backdrop continued to improve in Q1 2026. The biotech IPO market had its largest quarter since 2021 
by aggregate proceeds: approximately $1.7 billion across roughly six offerings, with three companies raising more than 
$300 million each. April extended the trend, with Kailera, Avalyn, Hemab, and Seaport collectively raising approximately 
$1.5 billion, each pricing above range with first-day pops. This is meaningfully different from 2021. That year, ninety-three 
biotech companies went public, mostly at preclinical or Phase 1 stage, in a window that functioned without selectivity. The 
2026 window is doing something else: the companies clearing have been late-stage, product-focused, or built around clinical 
narratives institutional investors can underwrite, while platform -only IPOs (Generate, Eikon, Aktis, Agomab) have either 
struggled in the aftermarket or trade below issue. The market is performing the work of pricing differentiation. 
The headline story in biotech capital markets is that recent IPOs have underperformed. The headline is wrong. When the 
2023 to 2026 vintages are stratified by what has happened to the underlying assets, the picture is binary: companies whose 
lead programs delivered positive material readouts have generated meaningful returns; companies whose programs failed 
have not. The good- news / bad -news split for the post -2022 cohort is roughly 50/50, compared to 70/30 in the wrong 
direction for the 2020 to 2021 vintage. The IPO market is behaving as it should, pricing data as it arrives. Selection and 
entry point matter more than vintage timing, and the discipline of investing into clinical inflections rather than thematic 
enthusiasm is exactly what the current market rewards.

On the leadership question, headlines emerged as we finalized this letter suggesting the administration may move to replace

progressively more demanding standard. The gap between commodity innovation and genuinely differentiated science is 
widening, which is broadly favorable for the opportunity set ahead. 
The China question: from threat to expansive 
We recently spent time across Asia. The dominant theme in nearly every discussion was the accelerating rise of China as a 
force in biotechnology R&D. The speed, ambition, and scale of investment are impossible to ignore, and our views have 
evolved meaningfully over the last several years. The ecosystem once considered China a competitive threat. We now see 
it as expansive. Most Chinese biotech companies are oriented toward the China market and license rights to Western drug 
manufacturers to run the expensive global trials and access the higher pricing regimes in Europe, Japan, and the U.S. through 
royalty structures. In years past the directionality ran the other way: BeOne (formerly BeiGene), Zai Lab, and Everest 
Medicines were the names licensing Western d rugs into China. The relationship has reversed. Western pharma is the 
licensee, and Chinese companies with early proof -of-concept data are the licensors. That shift has opened the addressable 
market, accelerated innovation, and enabled us to invest into ne arly every newly formed company structured around early 
Chinese POC data with global ex-China rights. 
Hong Kong-listed biotech companies have outperformed the XBI by approximately 29% over the past twelve months. 
Cross-border licensing transactions involving Chinese assets have increased materially, with several deals above $1.3 billion 
in upfront and milestone consideration. Western pharma companies (J&J, Roche, Novartis, AstraZeneca, Merck, GSK) have 
all completed multiple licensing or M&A transactions with Chinese counterparties during this period. Intellectual property

Lessons from 1999: Biotech in an Age of AI Excess 
It is worth situating the present moment in a longer arc. We have written above about the structural forces inside 
biotechnology: the maturation of the public asset base, the patent -cliff imperative, the operational shift in AI’s application 
to drug discovery, and the valuation reset that has already occurred. The natural question from any thoughtful allocator is 
how those forces compare to historical setups. The most instructive parallel, in our view, is not the 2015 biotech peak or the 
2021 cycle. It is 1999. Not because the present resembles 1999 in biotech, but because it resembles 1999 in everything else. 
The popular memory of 1999 is the dot-com boom. Less remembered is that biotechnology ran its own parallel mania at the 
same time, driven by the race to sequence the human genome. Human Genome Sciences traded above $100 per share. 
Celera, Millennium, Incyt e, and Abgenix became household names among generalists. Biotech mutual funds returned 
roughly 200% in the year ending March 2000. Then, on March 14, 2000, Clinton and Blair issued a joint statement on open 
sharing of genome data, investors panicked over patentability, and the genomics bubble collapsed alongside the broader 
Nasdaq. Some companies retained only 20% to 30% of their summer 1999 valuations. The hangover was structural and

The genomics thesis was not wrong. It was early. The science took fifteen years to translate into approved products, and 
when it did, it produced mRNA vaccines, CAR -T therapies, gene editing, and the GLP -1 franchise. The point that matters

Why This Cycle Is Different from 2021 
A reasonable concern, particularly for investors who lived through the 2021 biotech peak, is whether the current optimism

itself, without selectivity. Ninety-three biotech companies went public that year, the majority at preclinical or Phase 1 stage. 
The 2026 environm ent is the opposite. The IPO window has reopened with discrimination. Late -stage, product-focused 
issuers price above range; platform -only stories trade below issue. The capital base is institutional rather than retail. The

not isoform-specific inhibitors aimed at single m utations, but pan- RAS(ON) inhibitors addressing the broader oncogenic 
signaling architecture. Daraxonrasib is the first such molecule to deliver a positive Phase 3 outcome. The hazard ratio of
