Regarding individual winners and losers, our biggest winner in the quarter was biotech company, 
Dianthus Therapeutics (+1.5% net*). Dianthus is a small- and mid-cap biotech company developing a 
potential best-in-class, pipeline-in-a-product asset for treatment of immune-mediated diseases of 
high-unmet-need. Their lead program, claseprubart (DNTH103), is a long-acting, subcutaneous 
(subQ) C1s inhibitor designed to combine high efficacy with superior convenience and safety relative 
to existing therapies. This combination of features should enable Dianthus to capture meaningful 
share in large, under-served markets such as generalized myasthenia gravis (gMG), chronic 
inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy (CIDP), and multifocal motor neuropathy (MMN). 
 
Mechanistically, claseprubart is differentiated because it selectively inhibits the “classical” 
complement pathway, while sparing other complement pathways (i.e. the lectin and alternative 
complement pathways). We know other approved but more broadly acting complement therapies are 
associated with increased risk of serious infections (such as meningitis), leading to black box 
warnings and the need to vaccinate before initiating treatment. For example, Soliris and Ultomiris are 
multi-billion-dollar complement-targeted drugs that are less selective and each have black box 
warnings. By contrast, Dianthus’ classical-only inhibition has clinical precedent suggesting a cleaner 
safety profile. This could translate into a meaningful commercial advantage, particularly in chronic 
indications where long-term safety, tolerability, and ease of use are critical. 
 
Claseprubart is being developed as a subcutaneous, auto-injector-based therapy with infrequent 
dosing (targeting every 2-4 weeks), as compared to existing therapies that often require IV infusion 
or weekly administration such as IVIG. Thus, a convenient and well-tolerated subcutaneous option 
could drive rapid adoption, especially if efficacy is competitive, as we expect it to be. 
 
Dianthus has generated compelling Phase 2 data in MG; meaningfully de-risking the Phase 3 
program, which is underway. MG has potential to become a $10-15 billion therapeutic category over 
time, and claseprubart could become a highly competitive biologic option in this disease. CIDP is 
also emerging as an attractive and increasingly de-risked indication for claseprubart based on the 
company’s recent interim analysis in CIDP , plus other clinical data produced by a competing asset 
within the same class as Dianthus.  
 
Despite the recent approval of argenx’s efgartifimod (an anti-FcRn antibody), the CIDP market 
remains ripe for disruption by novel entrants. CIDP could become another $2 billion+ peak sales 
indication for Dianthus. Both the MG and CIDP pivotal trials will likely read out in the 2028 timeframe. 
Dianthus is also conducting a Phase 2 trial for claseprubart in MMN, another condition for which 
IVIG is the entrenched standard of care, with significant limitations. MMN Phase 2 data expected in 
the second half of the year should be a meaningful catalyst for the stock if positive.  
 
Another compound from Dianthus, DNTH212, provides a second, earlier-stage but potentially high-
upside asset with a different mechanism of action compared to claseprubart. While still early, 
DNTH212 adds optionality beyond the complement pathway and opens an even wider range of 
diseases for the company.

Shopify was our biggest loser on the long side in the quarter (-1.3% net*). Shopify is a cloud-based 
platform that enables businesses of all sizes to create and manage online stores, process payments, 
and sell across multiple channels. A small clothing brand, for example, could use Shopify to launch a 
website, accept credit card payments, manage inventory, and sync sales with Instagram and a 
physical retail location, all from a single dashboard. Beyond small businesses, Shopify also powers 
major global enterprises; in 2025, Estée Lauder joined as a major new customer. 
 
We built a position in Shopify in 2025 primarily for two reasons. First, we believed Shopify would 
keep winning large enterprise customers like Estée Lauder due to its product innovation edge over 
competitors. Second, we saw it as a company well-positioned to benefit from the rise of AI. Shopify 
sits behind many mission-critical merchant workflows, including online storefronts, checkout, 
payments, tax, fraud tools, inventory management, and fulfillment integrations, even when 
merchants use third-party providers for some functions. 
 
This made us optimistic about the emerging concept of agentic shopping, where an AI agent 
autonomously handles the entire buying process on your behalf, finding the best product and 
completing the purchase based on your instructions and preferences, without you ever opening a 
browser. Crucially, those purchases still require the same kind of commerce backend that Shopify 
already provides for millions of merchants. More AI-driven shopping could therefore mean more 
volume through Shopify-enabled infrastructure and thus more revenues as Shopify earns revenue 
from subscription fees, payments, and other merchant services tied to activity on its platform.  
 
However, in early 2026, as the market came around to our long-held view that AI is a 
transformational technology, any company susceptible to disruption has sold off, even when the 
evidence was thin. Unfortunately, Shopify got caught up in this maelstrom and was labeled an AI 
loser. Investors worried that platforms like OpenAI’s ChatGPT, Anthropic’s Claude, or Google’s 
Gemini could eventually own the entire shopping experience, including discovery, checkout, 
payments, and customer data, cutting Shopify out of the equation. There was also concern that AI 
coding tools would significantly lower the barrier for startups to build competing commerce platforms.

We believe this AI loser narrative overlooked how deeply embedded Shopify already is in the 
commerce stack. Merchants rely on Shopify not just for an online storefront, but for payments, tax 
compliance, fraud detection, inventory management, and fulfillment integrations, representing years 
of operational workflows, data, and third-party connections that cannot be replicated overnight. An AI 
platform like ChatGPT may own the discovery layer, but merchants still need a backend to run their 
business. For merchants deeply integrated into Shopify, the switching costs are onerous. A merchant 
would have to replace a large and complex operating layer, including checkout, payments, tax, fraud 
tools, inventory, order management, and fulfillment integrations, for hypothetical competitors that 
have not proven themselves. For a small or mid-sized company, a botched migration that takes 
down their store or breaks their checkout for even a few days could mean lost sales that could 
seriously upend their operations.  
 
Lastly, early signs of agentic commerce were underwhelming and OpenAI shifted away from native 
in-chat checkout in March. The market viewed this as a sign that agentic shopping may be further off 
than previously expected and that Shopify merchants may not see a pickup in demand as early as 
bulls anticipated. However, we believe this was a sign that revealed how difficult the whole e-
commerce process is and highlights the value of Shopify’s Agentic Storefronts tool, which allows 
users to check out on a familiar online store vs. ChatGPT.  
 
We may revisit buying Shopify in the future, but for now we are sitting on the sidelines. Even if our 
long-term thesis proves correct, fighting the AI-loser narrative is likely an uphill battle as AI 
innovation continues to accelerate.

resale marketplaces. This business model is attractive to both sellers and buyers, and The RealReal 
possesses operational moats that are not hindered by AI chat-enabled shopping. In contrast to the 
bears, we think The RealReal stands to benefit from AI as it has substantial physical operations—
authenticating and processing products for listing—which are made more efficient through

At present, three themes have piqued our investment interest: AI, space, and humanoids. We have 
discussed AI extensively in prior letters, so we thought it would make sense to discuss space and 
humanoids this quarter.  
 
Space is emerging as one of the most compelling public market investment frontiers, driven by a 
convergence of falling launch costs, accelerating commercial demand, and increasing strategic 
importance to governments. The cost to put a kilogram into orbit has declined by more than 90% 
over the past two decades, unlocking entirely new business models across communications, earth 
observation, and in-space services. As a result, the global space economy—estimated at roughly 
$500-600 billion today—is widely expected to exceed $1 trillion over the next decade. Satellite 
communications, alone, represent a ~$200-300 billion opportunity, driven by broadband connectivity 
demand in underserved and mobile markets, while earth observation data is evolving into a ~$20-30 
billion market with high-value applications in defense, climate, agriculture, and insurance. Launch 
services, historically a bottleneck, are scaling into a ~$20-40 billion market as cadence increases 
and new entrants drive competition. Beyond these established segments, emerging categories such 
as in-orbit manufacturing, space-based solar power, and cislunar infrastructure could each represent 
tens of billions in incremental total addressable market over time. Importantly, the sector is 
transitioning from government-led to commercially driven, with private capital and public equities 
increasingly funding growth. This shift is creating a broader and more investable universe of 
differentiated companies across the value chain. In our view, space today resembles the early days 
of the internet: capital intensive upfront, but with the potential to enable entirely new layers of 
economic activity over the long term. 
Humanoid robotics is one of the most compelling secular themes we are tracking. Just as generative 
AI is reshaping knowledge work, general-purpose humanoids stand to do the same for physical 
labor, across industrial and warehouse automation, commercial services, and eventually the home. 
What distinguishes humanoids from traditional industrial automation is generality: a single platform

We see a genuine technological inflection underway, not incremental progress. Foundation models, 
advances in reinforcement learning, simulation-based training, and steep cost declines in actuators, 
sensors, and compute have converged to make general-purpose physical AI both technically and 
economically viable. A single hardware platform can now be trained to handle many tasks in many

We think the most attractive way to express this theme is through a combination of pure-play 
humanoid developers and differentiated suppliers underpinning their supply chains. Humanoid 
developers offer the most direct exposure to commercialization and scale, while select supply chain 
participants provide attractive, platform-agnostic exposure regardless of which systems ultimately 
prevail. We are particularly focused on suppliers with durable competitive advantages in components 
that are less likely to be vertically integrated—such as force and torque sensors, precision actuators, 
and key subsystems—and we continue to deepen our research as the ecosystem evolves.
