FUND OVERVIEW            
Phorcys Opportunities I, LLC is an investment partnership focused on generating superior, tax-advantaged 
total returns across all market cycles through investments in high yield and distressed asset -backed 
municipal bonds.  The Fund invests opportunistically in middle-market credits in the U.S. and its territories 
by aggregating secondary market issues, as well as to a lesser extent, taking controlling interests in certain 
high-yield or distressed credits.     
 
PERFORMANCE COMMENTARY           
The Fund’s outperformance in 1Q 2026 was driven by a combination of realized gains on appreciated 
positions, proceeds from restructurings and asset -level dispositions, and markups on securities acquired 
through forced or technical selling. Key contributors  included GSMS 2015- GC34 B, an opportunistic 
investment in a  CMBS office exposure acquired at a significant discount to market, and Ocean Capital, 
where underlying CEF NAVs continued to recover. We also monetized a substantial portion of the New 
Hope, TX – Cardinal Bay bonds at prices modestly above evaluated levels. Additional upside was captured 
through the conversion of the IL Finance Authority – Lutheran Life bonds, which continued to gain value 
following the conversion. 
 
Quarterly detractors were largely technical in nature rather than driven by underlying credit fundamentals. 
Capital Trust FL - Palm Bay modestly detracted due to a payoff value that came in slightly below its 
evaluated price, while a handful of other positions experienced minor pressure attributable to market 
volatility and trading dynamics.

1Q2026 Overview 
Municipal bonds entered 2026 on a strong footing, supported by favorable technicals and solid investor 
demand. Both investment‑grade and high‑yield Bloomberg municipal indices posted gains in January, with 
high yield slightly outperforming. That momentum c arried into February, lifting year‑to‑date returns to 
+2.20% for the Bloomberg Municipal Bond Index (LMBITR) and +2.67% for the Bloomberg Municipal 
High Yield Index (LMHYTR), as high‑yield municipals again led performance.  Overall, the market 
remained well supported by strong investor inflows despite elevated issuance levels.   
 
This strength reversed sharply in March, as municipal bonds experienced one of their most significant 
monthly drawdowns in recent years. LMBITR declined 2.32% for the month while LMHYTR lost 1.90%, 
marking their worst performance in nearly three years and snapping a multi -month streak of positive 
returns. The selloff was driven primarily by rising U.S. Treasury yields amid escalating geopolitical 
tensions in the Middle East and higher energy prices, which reignit ed inflation concerns and pressured 
global fixed income markets. 
 
Market technicals further amplified the move. Elevated new -issue supply, ETF outflows, seasonal tax -
related selling, and weak reinvestment demand created a temporary supply- demand imbalance. With the 
market overbought heading into a seasonally weaker perio d, municipals became increasingly vulnerable, 
resulting in a rapid repricing across the curve. Intermediate and long-dated bonds underperformed, driving 
a meaningful widening in municipal-to-Treasury ratios and improving relative value.

Near‑term pressure may extend into April as seasonal technical headwinds persist. Reinvestment demand 
is likely to remain muted early in the month, partly due to tax‑related selling, although more than $30 billion 
in expected reinvestment flows from maturi ng bonds should provide incremental support as the month 
progresses. Meanwhile, primary issuance remains elevated following several years of record supply, further 
contributing to continued near‑term volatility.  
 
It is important to note that periods of market dislocation tend to expose structural weaknesses in lower -
quality or poorly underwritten credits. Our strategy and credit selection process reinforce the importance of 
discipline when investing in project -backed credits. During the quarter, several high- profile municipal 
credits continued to experience material stress, including Brightline Passenger Rail Florida, which entered 
restructuring discussions following significant shortfalls in ridership and revenue p rojections, and the 
American Dream megamall in New Jersey, where litigation surrounding property tax assessments has 
directly impaired bondholder cash flows. These situations highlight the risks embedded in projects with 
optimistic underwriting assumptions, limited margin for error, and structures that lack sufficient collateral 
and bondholder protections. 
 
Similarly, sectors that have historically attracted capital based on growth narratives rather than sustainable 
cash flow generation continue to show signs of strain. The recent bankruptcy filing of the Georgia 
ProtonCare Center underscores the challenges associated with highly levered, specialized healthcare assets, 
where revenues have proven insufficient to support substantial debt burdens despite stable operating 
activity. In addition, emerging structures such as Bitcoin- backed municipal bonds  - lacking t raditional 
security features and reliant on volatile collateral  - further illustrate the degree of risk -taking that has re -
entered certain segments of the market. We avoided exposure to these situations, as our investment 
approach emphasizes downside protection, durable cash flows, and structural control, particularly in  
environments where capital market conditions can shift rapidly. 
 
Despite the severity of the March drawdown, the Fund's performance was cushioned by several liquidity 
events that materialized during the quarter. The selloff has effectively reset valuations, bringing year -to-
date performance closer to flat. In our view, this repricing presents a more compelling entry point -  yields 
are now more attractive on both an absolute and tax- equivalent basis. For patient capital, the recent 
weakness is beginning to pay off, with improved income and value -add opportunities emerging  across 
municipals and other asset-backed credit instruments.

ILLINOIS FINANCE AUTHORITY REVENUE BONDS  
LUTHERAN LIFE COMMUNITIES OBLIGATED GROUP 
Series 2019A Issue Size: $153,360,000 
The Series 2019A Bonds (the “Bonds”) were issued to finance and refinance four facilities operated by the 
Lutheran Life Communities Obligated Group (the “Borrower”) across Illinois and Indiana (the 
“Communities”), totaling 1,103 units across independent living, assisting living, memory care, and skilled 
nursing. The Bonds were secured by a gross revenue pledge of the Obligated Group and first priority 
mortgage liens on the Communities. 
  
In February 2025, the Borrower filed for Chapter 11 protection in the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the 
Northern District of Illinois (the “Court”). The Court subsequently approved the engagement of Raymond 
James (the “Broker”) to market the communities. Following an extended process, the Trustee and Borrower 
reached a Plan Support Agreement (the “Plan”) with the backing of the bondholder ad hoc group. The Plan 
provided for a balance sheet restructuring, rather than a liquidation, with the goal of preserving the operating 
platform. The Bankruptcy Court confirmed the Chapter 11 Plan in March 2026.  
 
Pursuant to the Plan, the holders of the Bonds were subject to a mandatory exchange on March 26, 2026. 
On the Exchange Date: 
• The principal amount of the Series 2026A Bonds (the “New Bonds”) equaled: 
o 100% of the outstanding principal of the Bonds, plus 
o accrued and unpaid interest through the effective date, with any unpaid portion capitalized 
into the new principal balance. 
• In addition, bondholders received a final interim cash interest distribution covering accrued interest 
for the final pre-exchange period. 
  
We have long believed that a bankruptcy filing was unnecessary and likely the result of bad  advice aimed 
at taking advantage of market conditions to impose a cramdown on bondholders. We are encouraged that a 
more constructive outcome ultimately prevailed and expect to exit the position as the market overhang from 
the restructuring dissipates. We currently hold ~$2 million of the New Bonds, which were acquired in the 
high 60s and are currently trading in the low 90s.

CAPITAL TRUST AGENCY 
REVENUE BONDS (TUSCAN GARDENS OF PALM COAST PROJECT) 
Senior Series 2017A Issue Size: $35,520,000 
Senior Taxable 2017B Issue Size: $4,030,000 
Subordinate Series 2017C Issue Size: $2,725,000 
Taxable Junior Subordinate Series 2017D: $1,500,000 
The Series 2017 Bonds (the “Bonds”) were issued to provide a loan to Tuscan Gardens of Palm Coast 
Properties, LLC (the “Borrower”) to finance the development and construction of Tuscan Gardens of Palm 
Coast, a 130-unit assisted living community (the “Project”) located in Palm Coast, Florida. The Bonds were 
secured by a gross revenue pledge, a debt service reserve fund, and a mortgage on the Project. The 
community opened in November 2018. 
 
The credit weakened quickly following construction, as the Borrower failed to reach stabilized occupancy 
within a reasonable timeframe and ultimately defaulted in 2020. In January 2023, U.S. Bank (the “Trustee”) 
accelerated the debt and initiated foreclosure proceedings in Flagler County Circuit Court (the “Court”). A 
court-appointed receiver, William G. King (the “Receiver”), was installed in May 2023, and subsequently 
replaced the existing manager with AgeWell Senior Living. 
 
To provide support for the Project during the receivership, an ad hoc group, including the Fund, provided 
a priming note for $2MM at a 15% rate (the “Priming Note”), to preserve operations and fund the sale 
process. 
 
The Receiver engaged Continuum Advisors (the “Broker”) and, following a broad marketing process, 
selected Fortress Investment Group (the “Buyer”) with a winning bid of $29 million. After repayment of 
the priming loan and satisfaction of remaining trustee and receivership expenses, net proceeds available to 
bondholders totaled approximately $27.32 million. 
 
Recovery on the Senior Bonds was ~69 (~43 cents of principal and ~26 cents of interest). The Fund 
held ~$8.7 million at an average cost of roughly 48.
