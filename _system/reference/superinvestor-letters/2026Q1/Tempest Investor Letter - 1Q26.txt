Navigating the waters ahead.   
As we anniversary one of the more eventful periods since our inception, we’re left reflecting on yet 
another quarter that offers enough content to fill several letters. Our aim here is to focus on the areas 
most relevant to our investment mandate, while acknowledging that a great deal is still dynamically 
unfolding around us. 
 
As tech investors, there is plenty to remain excited about. Despite the noise, we continue to believe 
Generative AI (“GenAI”) represents one of the most consequential infrastructure buildouts of our 
investing lifetime, with the “plumbing” of the stack still actively being laid across chips, networking, 
power, cooling, and the growing orchestration layer required to make these systems more useful. That 
constructive long-term view remains central to our dual mandate: to stay invested behind the 
disruptive businesses we believe can most benefit from such an innovation storm, while also respecting 
that innovation supercycles rarely advance in a straight line and often pull forward destabilizing left-tail 
risks along the way. 
 
As tail hedgers, we’ve often enjoyed the conversational exercise of brainstorming potential left-tail 
events. Some are more entertaining (alien invasion), some are more financial (foreign exchange crises 
and credit cycles), and nearly all are destabilizing. This quarter presented one finally being stress tested: 
the shuttering of one of the world’s most important geopolitical chokepoints amidst a war. We may 
have also witnessed the fuse being lit on another potential crisis in the realm of private credit, a possible 
consequence of tremendous debt built up after a multi-millennia anomaly of low interest rates. 
Additionally, the specter of perhaps the most dramatic tail risk of all rose considerably since we last 
wrote, that of computational innovation advancing quickly enough to threaten the security architecture 
upon which many of our critical institutions rest. 
 
We’re certainly not alone in observing this wider field of uncertainty, as evidenced by market 
participants’ persistent appetite for downside protection. That self-recursive demand for hedging 
creates a vexing predicament for hedgers themselves. Fear gets reflected in higher implied volatility, 
which raises the cost of protection, but with each passing day that volatility under-realizes, the value of 
that protection declines (i.e. the market moves +/- <100bps versus expectations for it to move 
considerably more). If the market continues to move less violently than the options market fears, put 
buying becomes an exercise in absorbing the slow bleed of premium and time decay. 
 
We’re reminded of 2022, when vol under-realized while the market drifted lower, a condition often 
described as “spot down, vol down.” As we’ll circle back to later in this letter, that presents a unique 
challenge for tail hedgers, and one we welcome as an opportunity for differentiated execution. The 
open question is whether this period ultimately rhymes more with 2022, when many put option buyers 
were left watching premium evaporate, or with early 2020 and April 2025, when fear’s potential energy 
finally went kinetic. Hindsight will eventually reveal the answer. Until then, the only certainty is that it 
won’t play out exactly like prior episodes, just as no two storms are alike.

duration of the disruption is best left to the energy and supply-chain specialists. We also don’t intend to 
become overnight experts on the matter; we’re not sending an analyst to gather firsthand intelligence 
from a speedboat cruising the Persian Gulf anytime soon. For us, we’ll be paying attention to any 
earnings implications, as the Gulf exports a considerable amount of the products that account for the

Given that the scale of this energy disruption is often compared to periods before most contemporary

October 1973 Yom Kippur War and subsequent oil embargo. While there was no famous single-day 
crash, no “Black Monday” or “Black Tuesday,” the S&P 500 ultimately fell 44% in the year that followed, 
despite the embargo ending by March 1974. As with all bear markets, there were multiple culprits 
sharing blame, and several that rhyme for today’s audience: inflation proving stubborn even as growth 
rolled over, currency instability after the breakdown of Bretton Woods, Asian trade tensions, a 
concentrated equity market (“the Nifty Fifty”), and the promise of a new technological productivity 
wave that didn’t arrive quickly enough to alleviate the bear market burdens (Intel’s first microprocessor

For us, that only sharpens the importance of returning our focus toward the center of our concentric

Tech obsolescence risk is a constant concern and opportunity for us as tech investors. It is often the bull 
case for the present and the bear case for the future, demanding constant evaluation of current 
product cycles and management execution, while preserving the humility to recognize that disruption 
eventually comes for those who once defined themselves as the disruptors. As public market investors, 
liquidity not only affords that humility, it demands it. If we are wrong, we tend to feel the pain sooner

quickly. 
 
The risk of obsolescence may partly explain why many asset-light tech businesses historically resisted 
maximizing near-term profitability at the expense of future innovation. Net cash balance sheets and 
larger costs than presently needed (R&D and capex) drove an under-realization of present success as 
gauged by profit maximization and optimized capital structures. Yet such conservatism served a 
longer-term strategic purpose of preparedness for whenever the next technological paradigm arrived. 
This is where Andy Grove’s defining Silicon Valley mantra rings loudly in the ears of CEOs: “only the 
paranoid survive.” 
 
At times that paranoia may have gone too far, as some management teams preserved optionality only 
to destroy capital pursuing fruitless efforts. As such, we witnessed an inevitable reaction function that 
emphasized cost cutting, price raising, and saddling asset-light software businesses with debt, 
potentially ignoring Grove’s warning out of hubris or naivete. It was a risk we identified years ago when 
observing the convertible debt binge of unprofitable growth software businesses in 2021 and the 
subsequent public market reckoning in 2022. What we found notable in the years that followed was 
how private equity and private credit continued to execute on that anti-Grove reaction playbook at 
even greater scale. That dynamic helped inform some of the hedges in our portfolio, particularly where 
we believe pressure in private credit could still spill over into broader high-yield fragility. 
 
As for our long portfolio, we were not immune to such software valuation turbulence, despite our

Last quarter we wrote about agentic AI as a harbinger of creative destruction for many software 
businesses, while also detailing how we were attempting to catch a few babies being thrown out with

role in the future digital infrastructure of AI is underappreciated.

Reporting from our shore.  
To contextualize the rapid shifts in AI, we find value in contemplating how these new technologies and 
business models differ from previous cycles. A hallmark debate of innovation is that of an open versus 
closed ecosystem. For instance, in the recent SaaS boom, the overwhelming financial spoils went to 
closed or “managed” ecosystems like Microsoft, CrowdStrike, Adobe, ServiceNow, etc. Even highly 
successful open-source projects eventually had to convert to managed services to create a sustainable 
business model (MongoDB, Elastic, GitLab, Databricks, etc.), which accelerated the degradation of the 
underlying open-source project and widened the gap between closed versus open. 
 
We believe most business customers were happy to pay the premium for managed services for several 
reasons. First, the technical burden of self-managing the open-source technologies was considerable 
and often a distraction. Customers instead chose to pay a software vendor to host, patch, upgrade, and 
integrate the tools; best encapsulated by the brutish aphorism “one throat to choke.” Secondly, as 
businesses struggled with IT talent shortages, the opportunity cost of expending finite engineering 
resources on operational projects instead of revenue generating initiatives dramatically increased. Third, 
the feature/functionality gap widened as software vendors kept the “killer features” behind their 
paywalls. Finally, the total cost of ownership (“TCO”) of open proved comparable, or even higher, than 
closed when considering the myriad of additional self-directed responsibilities (implementation, 
integration, security/compliance, etc.). As a result, software vendors were able to leverage significant 
scale benefits to compress the price for customers, which made it economical for even smaller 
businesses to adopt their digital tools. 
 
As we look at how AI technologies at the model layer are being consumed and monetized, we notice 
some interesting parallels and divergences. Unlike the prior paradigm, we don’t believe business 
customers are choosing closed models like Claude or ChatGPT because of a reduction in operational 
burden, as they are mostly deployed via Amazon Bedrock or Azure AI Foundry, just like open models 
Qwen, Llama, and DeepSeek. Talent shortages are also less relevant, as autonomous agents make 
intelligence and productivity abundant, driving down the opportunity cost of engineering initiatives 
because customers can just spin up more agents/compute to do more work. 
 
We believe the decision on open vs. closed is currently made based upon the feature/functionality gap 
of the models as seen through: 1) step-function improvements in the underlying model capabilities and 
2) rapid productization to make the models easy to use across a variety of business cases (most 
popularly coding). The separation distance gained by closed models is turbo boosted by the models 
becoming increasingly recursive, with the latest versions using their newfound capabilities to make 
themselves better at an accelerated rate. Not to be left in the dust, we’ve seen the open ecosystem 
generate their own innovations, notably distillation (popularized by DeepSeek) and the creation of 
OpenClaw (formerly Clawdbot) as an autonomous AI agent. Validation of these efforts is evident in the 
defensive response of the closed model players, such as tightening their terms of service, restricting 
access, and pricing adjustments—all steps that further close the ecosystem.

We find recent pricing changes particularly interesting, as the economic model of using LLMs is vastly 
different than cloud software. Consider that power users of AI could consume a near infinite amount of 
compute as they deploy concurrent agents running individual tasks, which could break the traditional 
unit economics of a subscription model. This wasn’t an issue in SaaS because the natural limits of daily 
human productivity limited a user’s ability to consume too many CPUs in a workday. Now with AI, we’ve 
seen Anthropic recently change its enterprise pricing model to more heavily weight total consumption 
over subscription. This invites a bigger question as to how the demand side reacts because now a 
variably metered bill will be attached to any exploratory endeavors. This gives open-source models a 
new vector of attack: if they cost up to 1/100 per million tokens vs. the most expensive proprietary 
models, then the TCO argument starts to flip in favor of open-source. If the customer value proposition 
of the models is to enable iterative and often meandering consumption to eventually arrive at the right 
conclusion, then it’s critical to have the lowest possible variable cost.  
 
With so much change in the AI infrastructure stack, we continue to monitor several developments on 
the horizon. One area is the growth of domestic open source LLMs, such as Meta’s Superintelligence 
program and its advantage in extracting economics from Meta’s existing assets, thereby allowing them 
to potentially more freely open source their models. Another is following the pace set and distance 
maintained between the closed and open models, especially as newer generations are trained on 
improved hardware. We’re also keenly focused on the construction and adoption of open ecosystems in 
AI-native start-ups, all in pursuit of the single-employee billion-dollar company. We continue to “hold 
reality lightly” amidst this dynamism and we’re eager to update you with our thoughts throughout. 
 
A convex smile, welcoming volatility.   
The quarter unfolded across three distinct regimes, each of which tested our hedging architecture 
differently. January saw markets flirt with all-time highs, while underneath the surface, the AI-driven 
disruption narrative matured from theoretical to existential for the software sector, driving the IGV 
software index to its worst quarterly drawdown since 4Q 2008 and its worst relative drawdown ever, 
exceeding the dotcom bust and GFC. As for VIX, it continued its peaceful slumber, hovering in the mid-
teens while single-stock carnage raged on. February then introduced new tariff uncertainty and private 
credit woes to the picture. Then came March, when the geopolitically theorized became reality 
regarding the Strait of Hormuz and what the IEA has characterized as the largest supply disruption in 
the history of the global oil market. Finally, VIX levitated to an intraday high of 35 on March 9th, its 
highest in nearly a year, and the S&P 500 fell more than 8% from its January highs across five 
consecutive losing weeks, the longest such streak in nearly four years. 
 
It was amidst that backdrop that we encountered the dynamic known as "spot down, vol down." While 
the VIX rose in absolute terms during March, implied volatility consistently overpriced realized moves 
throughout the quarter. The vol risk premium (“VRP”) measures the gap between what options markets 
charge for protection versus what actually materializes in daily moves, and it hit the 98th percentile as 
measured by the spread between VIX and the S&P 500 one-month realized volatility. We believe the 
“Rule of 16” helps illustrate this dynamic: divide the VIX by 16 (a shorthand for the square root of 252 
trading days) to estimate the implied daily move. With VIX averaging 26 through March, the options 
market was pricing in ~1.6-1.7% daily swings, yet the S&P was frequently delivering sub-1% daily moves. 
For tail hedgers, this is a particularly vexing environment. You own insurance against a hurricane, yet 
what arrives is a persistent drizzle that slowly soaks you through. Each day the storm under-realizes, 
time decay gnaws at the premium paid, creating the slow bleed that separates disciplined hedgers 
from those who capitulate. 
 
This dynamic is not dissimilar to 2022, another period when put option buyers watched premium 
evaporate as the market ground lower without delivering the climactic, high-velocity selloffs that make 
tail hedges sing. The critical difference is that in 2022, few investors owned protection, which made the 
eventual pain more widespread but less expensive to insure against. In 1Q 2026, the opposite was true: 
investors came into March running a record amount of negative S&P 500 put delta, and despite recent

option expiration and monetization, it still stayed at the 95th percentile versus history. When everyone is 
hedged, the hedges themselves paradoxically dampen the very volatility they're designed to protect 
against. The act of monetization reflexively drives dealers to buy futures back that were sold against 
their puts, blunting the free fall during each successive move lower. 
 
This is where we think the volatility risk premium deserves respect, rather than blind participation. Over 
long periods, index options tend to price more uncertainty than markets ultimately realize, which is why 
short-volatility and income-oriented strategies keep reappearing on stage in new costumes. In calmer 
regimes, selling premium can look suspiciously like fixed income with better optics. Yet the carry is not 
free; it is a slow accrual of nickels in exchange for periodic encounters with a freight train. Lower realized 
volatility invites leverage, leverage compresses volatility further, and that compression in turn reinforces 
the illusion of stability until some sufficiently forceful shock or crowding unwind breaks the loop in 
dramatic fashion.  
 
Meanwhile, the dispersion regime we chronicled last quarter intensified to historic extremes.  The gap 
between single-stock and index realized volatility reached a two-decade high, with one-month single-
stock realized vol sitting at the 90th percentile, while index-level realized vol hovered near 20-year 
medians. While software names were ground zero for the destruction, semiconductor exposure surged 
in the opposite direction, with the recent ceasefire-driven snap-back producing a 23% weekly gap in 
semis versus software, the largest such weekly move in over 25 years. This level of internal violence, 
masked by a market where the S&P 500 finished the quarter down a modest 4-5%, is exactly the type of 
environment where index-level protection offers limited solace while the constituent parts are being 
violently rearranged. 
 
For Tempest, the implication is not that every squall deserves a full hurricane response, nor should every 
spike in implied volatility be chased with indiscriminate put buying. This reinforces our preference for 
keeping the smile convex through a more deliberate mix of actively managed structures: hedges that 
can monetize abrupt left-tail stress, defined-risk expressions that reduce carry drag when the vol risk 
premium is rich, and a continued emphasis on protecting against the possibility that index calm can 
coexist with violent idiosyncratic damage beneath the surface. In a market where structure increasingly 
feeds momentum, and momentum increasingly feeds structure, the tape is making more of its own 
weather. While we do not know which headline, flow, or feedback loop will matter most, we believe that 
the system continues to look more path-dependent, more reflexive, and more combustible than a 
placid index chart would imply. That is enough to keep us humble, active, and as always, welcoming 
volatility. 
 
Optics for the edge. 
We first encountered LightPath Technologies (“LPTH”) as we began expanding our work on defense 
tech over a year ago, building upon our longer-term investments in municipal defense companies such 
as Axon (“AXON”) and Motorola Solutions (“MSI”). That work brought us into closer contact with several 
of the drone and fixed-camera systems we saw in action at the IACP conference in 2023, and it 
reinforced a broader view we’ve held for years: drones are a natural extension of the Edge / Internet-of-
Things, a physical manifestation of compute moving outward from the cloud and into the field. In that 
sense, LPTH appealed to us not simply as a defense name, but as a picks-and-shovels supplier to a 
world where AI increasingly pushes sensing, imaging, and decision-making closer to the point of action. 
That is especially relevant in defense, where a stodgy old industrial base is beginning to yield to a new 
generation of cheaper, smarter, and increasingly autonomous systems. 
 
At its core, LPTH is an optical components manufacturer that is transforming from a commoditized lens 
supplier into a vertically integrated provider of infrared imaging assemblies, subsystems, and full 
camera solutions, with an increasing focus on defense and security end-markets. As we see it, LPTH sits 
at the intersection of two product cycles: the migration away from legacy germanium-based infrared 
optics toward its proprietary BlackDiamond materials, and the company’s verticalization from selling 
components into selling complete imaging systems. Both should benefit from the proliferation of

BlackDiamond into becoming a more strategic supplier to defense primes and new leaders like Anduril.  
 
What initially drew us in was the combination of timeliness and strategic relevance. LPTH’s

In our opinion, while LPTH’s advantage via the BlackDiamond germanium alternative is compelling, the

domestic supply chains, and avoid the friction of managing a fragmented vendor base for mission-
critical components. Once designed into a defense platform, those programs can also become quite 
sticky, as requalification can be costly, slow, and operationally disruptive. We believe that combination 
of timely material differentiation and growing system-level relevance gives LPTH the potential to evolve 
from a component vendor into a more strategic partner. 
 
Recent execution has helped sharpen that view. Over the last several quarters, LPTH has shown 
meaningful acceleration in revenue, backlog, and profitability, alongside a rising contribution from 
cameras and sub-systems. Backlog has ballooned, with an increasing share tied to higher-margin 
systems, while defense has become a much larger portion of the business. We also note the expanding 
set of opportunities tied to domestic security, border security, naval systems, and missile programs, all 
of which support our view that LPTH is benefiting from a broader sensor and imaging arms race, rather 
than relying upon a single isolated contract. 
 
The risks are real and merit constant attention. First, the moat around BlackDiamond may prove more 
temporal than permanent, as substitute materials or competing approaches could emerge faster than 
expected, particularly as more capital and attention flow into this portion of the defense supply chain. 
Second, execution still matters enormously: margins may remain noisy as LPTH ramps capacity, 
integrates acquisitions, and competes for share, while management’s promotional instinct and appetite 
for M&A introduce the usual risks of overextension, dilution, or operational missteps. In short, we believe 
the window of opportunity is attractive, but it must still be converted into durable design wins, scalable 
manufacturing, and lasting customer relationships. 
 
A book with legs.  
While Christopher Nolan’s film Oppenheimer deservedly rekindled broad interest in Kai Bird and Martin 
Sherwin’s American Prometheus, that narrative was by design centered on the man. Richard Rhodes’s 
The Making of the Atomic Bomb is centered more squarely on the technology itself, which is why we 
believe a deeper exploration of the text is timely. To many historians and World War II buffs, this 
acclaimed 1986 classic is already canon. To those immersed in the present Generative AI moment, it 
reads more akin to a field guide. 
 
Rhodes expertly captures how transformative technology rarely arrives in a straight line, nor does it 
emerge from a single genius in isolation. It is born from overlapping advances across fields, institutions, 
and personalities. The road to the bomb ran through physics, chemistry, mathematics, engineering, and 
ultimately industrial-scale manufacturing. The current AI wave is likewise not merely a software story, 
but the recombination of semiconductors, networking, data infrastructure, power systems, and 
neuroscience-adjacent research on cognition. One of the enduring lessons of the book is that when 
several disciplines begin compounding upon one another at once, progress can feel sudden, even if the 
groundwork was laid over decades.

Rhodes also reminds us that disruptive technology is almost never morally clean. German chemist Fritz 
Haber’s work famously helped feed the world through industrial fertilizer, yet the same scientific arc 
also powered explosives and chemical warfare. The atom would later reveal a similarly bifurcated path: 
extraordinary civilian promise paired with destructive power on a previously unimaginable scale. That 
duality feels highly relevant to AI. We are again living through a period where the same underlying 
technology can plausibly expand productivity, accelerate scientific discovery, improve defense 
capabilities, and compress costs, while also heightening surveillance, cyber risk, labor disruption, and 
geopolitical instability. The same breakthrough can nourish civilization and threaten it. 
 
Another reason the book feels so timely is its portrait of “big science” becoming an organized power. 
Fear that Nazi Germany might reach the bomb first accelerated a vast coordination effort across 
universities, private industry, and the state, eventually becoming the Manhattan Project. The GenAI 
buildout is obviously not a Manhattan Project in the literal sense, but it shares several familiar hallmarks: 
strategic urgency, enormous capital needs, power constraints, and the blending of commercial 
ambition and state interest. As technologies grow more consequential, they stop being mere research 
stories and become national capacity stories. 
 
We also appreciated Rhodes’s treatment of openness, secrecy, and the long shadow of fear. Danish 
physicist Niels Bohr understood early that atomic knowledge would transform geopolitics and argued 
that freer scientific exchange was essential for peaceful cooperation, while the security state naturally 
preferred secrecy and strategic advantage. AI today confronts a related tension: open-source versus 
closed models, global collaboration versus national champions, and the uncomfortable reality that once 
a powerful technology escapes the lab, it becomes difficult to contain. Fears, rational or irrational, can 
also lead societies to suppress the broader civilian promise of disruptive technology. Atomic science 
eventually opened a path toward abundant low-carbon energy, yet public fear and regulation sharply 
constrained how fully that promise was realized. The irony is that AI’s growing hunger for power is now 
helping reopen that very conversation. One of the twentieth century’s most feared technologies may 
yet enjoy renewed relevance because of one of the twenty-first century’s most exciting. 
 
If we were to distill the lesson for the present GenAI moment, it would be this: the most important 
technologies are rarely linear in their development, clean in their ethics, or evenly distributed in their 
benefits. They emerge through overlapping breakthroughs, are accelerated by fear and competition, 
absorb extraordinary quantities of capital, and then leave society arguing for decades over what should 
have been built, who should control it, and how much risk is tolerable in exchange for progress. That 
seems a rather good description of where we are today.
