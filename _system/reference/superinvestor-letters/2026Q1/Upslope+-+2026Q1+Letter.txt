MARKET CONDITIONS – A STRANGE GAME  
 
Early Q1 saw a sharp snapback in relative performance of defensive stocks. Ironically, by the time the war 
in Iran unfolded, defensives had run so hard that they sharply underperformed in a volatile macro 
environment typically conducive to good performance. While implications of the war are serious on a human 
and macroeconomic level, “serious” is not the right word to describe how financial market s processed 
events. Investors seemed far more interested in “Tweet trading” than any traditional fundamental analysis.  
 
When it comes to navigating big macro events – e.g. wars, pandemics, recessions – Upslope’s process is 
simple. First, determine whether the event is truly material or short-term noise. If the former, the next step 
is to q uickly identify offsides positioning in the portfolio . This means reducing outsized exposures to key 
risks – not eliminating them. The goal isn’t to bet on a specific outcome (e.g. quick end to the war or a 
drawn-out conflict ), but to move into balance so that the next breaking development or market -moving 
Tweet doesn’t matter much to the portfolio . While paying close attention to big picture shifts  is essential, 
predicting and acting on daily news flow and associated ups/downs in markets is not Upslope’s game. “The 
only move is not to play.” 
 
As is typical during stretches of heightened volatility, Upslope made quite a few adjustments to the portfolio 
in Q1. Exited positions mostly fell into two categories: theses that had already played out (e.g. Sandvik) 
and sales aimed at concentrating the portfolio into higher conviction ideas with overlapping exposure (e.g. 
exiting Japan Exchange while holding CME and MarketAxess). Over time, this approach has evolved into 
more of a mainstay of Upslope’s strategy: purchase a somewhat related group of stocks and narrow them 
down to fewer, higher-conviction positions over time. More details on these changes and new additions to 
the portfolio are below.

Key Exits 
 
The Fund exited Sandvik (SAND-SE, Sweden-based provider of mining, metal-cutting, and other industrial 
equipment and tools) and Holcim (HOLN-CH, Switzerland-based, global cement, aggregates and building 
materials company) due to full valuations and theses generally having played out. 
 
The Fund also exited Japan Exchange Group  (8697-JP, largest financial exchange operator in Japan ), 
Booz Allen (BAH, defense/security consulting firm), and Bio-Rad (BIO, clinical diagnostic and life sciences 
products). While each situation was unique, the broader theme was one of concentrating the portfolio on 
other existing ideas and “battles.” For Booz, the stock has come under pressure due to AI worries and 
political risk. While not the only portfolio company exposed to AI risk, Booz was the lowest conviction holding 
exposed to it and exiting enabled Upslope to add to higher conviction positions (e.g. FTI Consulting).

conditions. This feels like the right market environment to own businesses like these, as they are unusually 
well positioned to take advantage of macro volatility . Most are also too boring for the current crop of

United States (Canada, Sweden, and Finland – apparently Colorado’s lack of winter this year is affecting

Appendix C: Terminology 
Core Longs : Higher “quality” businesses (defined as low cyclicality, clean balance sheet, obvious and durable 
competitive advantages) managed with less valuation sensitivity (i.e. typically won’t exit a Core long solely because of 
valuation) and assuming a multi-year time horizon. 
Tactical Longs : Traditional “value” investments of more modest quality  (reasonably or very cyclical, currently 
experiencing operational, competitive or financial challenges) managed with greater valuation sensitivity (i.e. typically 
begin to exit if shares approach full valuation) and assuming a shorter time horizon (typically 6-18 months). 
Starter Longs: Generally smaller, undisclosed longs that fall into at least one of the following groups: 
1. Ideas where I’ve completed enough work to establish a toehold, but still have more to do. 
2. Companies facing obvious short-term challenges where I believe the near-term path in shares is likely lower, 
but the stock is ‘cheap enough’ and outright attractive over the longer-term.  
3. ‘Counter-shorts’ – higher-beta longs with good risk/reward that trade more in-sync with Upslope’s shorts on a 
daily basis and can be used to manage overall net long exposure more effectively and tax-efficiently. 
Alpha Shorts: Individual company short positions. 
Hedges: Diversified index hedges (either long or short).
