Monitoring Our Software Investments in the AI Transition 
I have recently been asked by some of you how I monitor our software investments given the rapid 
pace of change driven by AI, so I wanted to share my thoughts on the topic. My framework has 
three key elements that I revisit on an ongoing ba sis when evaluating both existing positions and 
potential new investments.  
First, the founder/CEO's ability to attract and re tain top engineering and product talent. In an 
environment where the half-life of any given technical advantage is shrinking and the best AI talent 
is highly mobile, the quality of the team a company can recruit and retain is, I believe, one of the 
clearest leading indicators of which businesses can successfully navigate the AI transition. Second, 
structural business model advantages allowing a business to monetize AI without cannibalizing its 
existing revenue base. As the ag ent economy emerges and software increasingly does the work 
rather than enabling humans to do it, businesse s with pricing already tie d to a percentage of 
customer revenue, transaction volume or premium volume — such  as Guidewire and Procore — 
should be direct beneficiaries. In addition, platforms with multiple expansion vectors beyond seats, 
such as ServiceNow and its ability to orches trate workflows across departments, systems and 
emerging agentic use cases should also benefit.  Third, AI product ve locity and tangible 
monetization signposts that demons trate AI is accelerating the underlying business rather than 
filling holes from a potentially eroding core business. Specifically, a company where AI revenues 
are growing rapidly while overall revenue is decelerating creates a "leaky bucket" where AI is not 
offsetting structural headwinds in the legacy  business. Adobe and Workday are two recent 
examples I have studied closely, where AI revenues are growing meaningfully but overall organic 
growth continues to decelerate implying I believe growing pressures in the core franchise. 
Concerns on the second and third elements of the framework were a key factor behind our exit of 
Adobe in 2Q 2024. In the case of Workday, all thr ee elements raised con cerns which drove my 
decision to exit in 2Q 2025. 
In contrast, I am focused on owning businesses where AI is genuinely accelerating the underlying 
franchise. The signposts I monitor on an ongoing basis include new product introductions, AI

Portfolio Activity 
As of March 31, our 5 largest positions were DSV (~13% of Partnership assets), Guidewire (~12%

During the quarter, I also continued to add to  our Mercado Libre (“ MELI”) position, which I 
originally initiated in the second half of  2025. I have monitored an d admired MELI for over 6 
years, with the key factors that had prevented MELI from qualifying as a West Hall investment 
until now primarily tied to valuation and insufficie nt margin of safety alongside the deliberately 
higher bar I have set for an emerging markets-based business relative to US/European based ones. 
 
Historically, three factors have  kept me away from EM-based businesses: transparency and 
culture, where the gap versus US peers is often material; structural market issues that make a given 
business model less attractive in EM than in the US (the cautionary exam ple I have used being 
wireless towers in Indonesia ear lier in my investing career); and geopolitical risk. After a lot of 
reflection and diligence, I am now confident that MELI clears all three hurdles. 
 
On transparency and culture, I would place MELI  ahead of most US peers across disclosure, 
alignment and capital allocation discipline. My St anford GSB network has also been a uniquely 
rich source of primary diligence given the deep  alumni presence in and around the company. On 
business model, the integration of marketplace, propr ietary logistics, payments and credit creates 
a moat that is better than the US comparable rather than worse — I think of MELI as an evolution 
of our prior Shopify thesis with an even deep er moat anchored in the physical logistics and 
payments flywheel. On geopolitical  risk, MELI's diversification across 18 countries and 25-year 
track record of navigating events like the multiple Argentine peso devaluations point to a level of 
resilience few EM businesses can match. The recen t and ongoing share pric e weakness provides

Mercado Libre (MELI: Nasdaq): ~11% of Partnership assets 
I view MELI as the dominant digital ecosystem in Latin America and the primary toll-taker on the 
powerful secular shift from offline to onlin e commerce for 650+MM peopl e, with regional e-
commerce penetration in the low teens or roug hly a decade behind the US. MELI effectively 
combines an Amazon-like vertically integrated marketplace (logistics, retail and advertising) with 
a PayPal-like fintech and credit franchise (Mer cado Pago and Mercado Cred ito), creating a self-
reinforcing flywheel where each service strengthens the others. Over the past 12 months the share 
price has been significantly impacted by near-ter m competitive concerns from players such as 
Shopee and Amazon as well as potential macro uncertainty from the upc oming 2026 Brazilian 
election. I estimate that MELI currently trades at ~ 30x P/E multiple for a business that I expect to 
increase its EPS by close to 5x over the next 5 years.  
 
Moat: I believe MELI benefits from a deep and wide ning moat rooted in network effects, brand, 
high switching costs and a proprietary logistics and payments infrastructure that simply cannot be 
replicated economically by sub-scale competitors. MELI is the regional leader with an estimated 
well over 30% share of Latam e- commerce, supported by the larg est proprietary e-commerce 
logistics network in the region, which now handles ~96% of ite ms shipped and 75% delivered 
within 48 hours. This physical network I believ e is not only a competitive moat, but increasingly 
a powerful AI advantage. MELI’s  combination of proprietary lo gistics infrastructure, 80MM+ 
monthly active fintech users and real-time transaction data across 18 countries creates a foundation 
for deploying AI at a pace and scale that our diligence suggests is ahead of many U.S. technology 
peers. The applications alrea dy span advertising personalizati on, credit underwri ting, customer 
service automation and seller productivity tools. On the fintech side, Mercado Pago generates a 
rich and proprietary dataset on consumer and merchant behavior  (purchase patterns, location, 
repayment history) that pure-play  competitors lack. This data a dvantage fuels superior credit 
underwriting in a region where less than 10% of the population has a credit card and roughly half 
remains underbanked — a popula tion effectively ignored by the traditional banking system. 
Importantly, the moat is being reinforced by th e rapid scaling of the high-margin advertising 
business, which is currently ~2.5% of gross merchandise value (“GMV”) in 4Q25 (vs. 2.0% a year 
earlier) with estimated 70-80% EBIT margins. I estimate that MELI's advertising business has 
multiple years of rapid growth ahead and I expect its share of GMV to increase by almost 3x over 
the next few years and reach comparable share of GMV as Amazon’s ad business. 
 
Market: I expect MELI’s blended addressabl e opportunity across commerce, payments, 
advertising and credit can grow at roughly 20% CAGR for at least the next five years. First, the e-
commerce opportunity rema ins underpenetrated, with Latin Am erican e-commerce still at only 
14% of total retail and, almost a decade behind the U.S (at ~30% penetration). Second, the region 
remains predominantly cash-driven and structurally underbanked, with less than 10% credit card 
penetration vs. 60%+ in the US, supporting total payment volume ("TPV") growth meaningfully 
ahead of e-commerce growth. Third, the credit oppor tunity remains in early inning s, with the 
population of structurally underbanked Latin Amer icans effectively ignor ed by the traditional

Management: Founder Marcos Galperin co-founded MELI with two Stanford GSB classmates in 
1999, with GSB alumni well-represented across the founding team and senior leadership. After a 
multi-year evaluation process, Marcos transiti oned to the Board as of January 1, 2026, and Ari 
Szarfsztejn (previously head of E-commerce) took over as the new CEO. Marcos continues to own 
~7% of MELI stock and the broader group of directors and officers alongside Marcos collectively 
own over $US7Bn of MELI stock. Marcos re mains actively involved focused on AI-driven 
innovation across the platform, long-term strategy and capital allocation, preserving the company's 
culture, and supporting Ari's transition. The feedback I received on Marcos and Ari and entire team 
has been exceptionally strong an d consistent on integrity, l ong-term orientation and capital 
allocation discipline. What further underpins my conviction is this team's capacity to suffer. MELI 
nearly went out of business in 2001 when some of its private inve stors tried to shut the company 
down during the dot-com crash, and the experience forged a capital allocation discipline that has 
defined the company ever since: Marcos cut the burn rate by 10x, secured a decade of runway and 
vowed to never again make the company vulnerabl e to someone else's d ecision about whether it 
would survive. That same resilience has carried MELI through Amazon's entry into Latin America, 
multiple Argentine currency crises, the COVID disruption — each time emerging with a stronger 
franchise and deeper moat. I believe MELI remains one of the most sought-after employers in the 
region, giving the company a meaningful advantage in attracting and retaining top engineering and 
product talent. I have also been impressed with management's transparency in their engagement 
with investors, including detailed quarterly shar eholder letters and podcasts  a s  w e l l  a s  a  v e r y  
knowledgeable and accessible IR team. Finall y, I view MELI's comp ensation philosophy as 
excellent, with minimal stoc k-based compensation and key management team members 
participating in a cash-settled, share-price-indexed Long-Term Retention Program ("LTRP") that 
aligns incentives with shareholder value creation without creating ongoing dilution similar to many 
of its US peers.  
 
Variance: The bear case on MELI seems predicated on the view that intensifying competition from 
Shopee and Amazon, particularly in Brazil e-commerce, has forced the company into a defensive 
investment cycle that may keep margins structurally depressed with no clear path back to historical 
profitability. I recognize that near-term margin s are under pressure, with  1Q26 EBIT margin of 
~7% well below where I expect this business to earn over time, and competition in Brazil remains 
intense. However, Brazil e-commerce is a highly attractive market and MELI is investing while 
simultaneously showing immediate  growth acceleration and what a ppear to be highly attractive 
incremental returns. Over the last two quarters, MELI has delivered startup-like growth rates at 
$35Bn+ revenue scale, with tota l revenue growing 49% year-over- year in 1Q26, the fastest pace 
6 
 
in four years. Importantly, Brazil currency-neutral revenue growth accelerated to 39% in 1Q, while 
items sold increased 56%, unique buyer growth r eached the fastest pace in five years and NPS 
scores hit record levels acro ss core markets. At the same time, the underly ing unit economics 
appear to be improving, with Brazil unit shipping costs declining 17% year-over-year in 1Q26, an 
acceleration from the 11% decline in 4Q. Consensus increasingly seems to be treating MELI as a 
“dead money” stock until investors can see evidence that current margins represent the trough. As 
I have discussed with many of you over the years, this is precisely the type of setup the West Hall 
approach is designed to exploit: a great business being marked down because reported margins are 
temporarily depressed while intrinsic value continues to compound. Based on the company’s 
disclosures, our own work, and the pattern of prior MELI investment cycles, I believe the current 
margin pressure is temp orary rather than structural. I conti nue to underwrite steady-state EBIT 
margins approaching 20%, above the mid-teens level embedded in consensus expectations, and I 
expect margins to begin improving toward the e nd of 2026 as the investments initiated in mid-
2025 mature. 
 
Pre Mortem: I believe my thesis on MELI would be wrong if the competitive dynamics in Brazil 
e-commerce shift from the current pattern of rational investment by scaled players into a prolonged 
value-destructive subsidy war that permanently  compresses marketplace unit economics. A key 
indicator for this risk would be sustained deterioration in unit economics which is the opposite of 
what we are observing currently. In addition, as credit grows from roughly 20% of total revenues 
today toward 30%+ over the next several years, investors may increasingl y assign a bank-like 
multiple to that portion of the business, cr eating ongoing multiple comp ression even as EPS 
compounds at close to 40% annually — this risk is reflected in our Base Case exit multiple of 20x 
P/E which I view as conservative for this leve l of growth but appropriate given the evolving 
revenue mix. A key mitigant to these risks I believe is the flexibility of MELI's business model — 
management has been explicit that it can "dial margins up" at any time by moderating the pace of 
investment, and the company's de monstrated track record of na vigating two prior investment 
cycles as well as multiple Argentine currency crises gives me confidence in the team's ability to 
adapt. Our Downside Scenario is intended to capture these risks in addition to the possibility of a 
weaker macro environment across Latin America. 
 
Valuation: Based on our Base Case, I e xpect MELI to grow revenue  by well over 3x to over 
$US110Bn by 2030, with EBIT margins reaching low teens. Our Base Case implies that MELI 
will compound EPS from ~$39/share in 2025 to a lmost $200 EPS in 2030. At current prices, I 
estimate we own MELI at ~8x cash P/E 4-5 years out for a business I expect to compound EPS at 
40+% annually over this period. Based on an exit multiple of 20x P/E, which I view as conservative 
for a business of this quality and growth profile, I expect MELI share price to roughly 3x over the 
next 5 years. Importantly, in our Downside Scenario which assumes ongoing margin pressure from 
sustained competitive investment and growth deceleration, I still expect MELI to generate mid-to-
high single digit IRRs reflecting what I view as meaningful downside protection at current prices.
