I chose the name “Crestbreak” as it reflects the moment when a wave reaches peak energy and begins to change 
trajectory. The firm’s investment approach centers on capitalizing on similar inflection points in business 
fundamentals and market expectations. 
Crestbreak’s objective is to deliver superior long -term returns through disciplined risk management and 
differentiated research across US and non-US public equities using a long/short strategy. 
Crestbreak expects to formally launch by the end of Q4 2026. 
The Global Opportunity 
Global equity markets are not uniformly efficient. While US large -cap equities are widely followed and often 
reflect consensus expectations, many international markets remain less researched, less institutionally crowded, 
and more fragmented. 
Crestbreak primarily focuses on what it defines as “alpha markets”, which are currently East Asia, Western 
Europe, and select emerging markets including LATAM and India. These are environments where positive 
structural changes, attractive valuations, and l imited research coverage  create outsized opportunities for 
fundamental investors. 
Crestbreak will also invest in the United States, which provides a different dynamic and opportunity than core 
alpha markets . Within the US market, the influence of passive flows, systematic strategies, and short -term 

positioning has led to more frequent dislocations around earnings events and periods of speculative activity. 
Price often reflects positioning rather than underlying business trajectory, which creates its own opportunity. As 
market participants compress ar ound shorter time horizons, duration itself has become an alpha driver in US 
equities. 
The portfolio reflects this view by design. It is focused on market exposure where research is rewarded and 
supported by strong long -term tailwinds, wide dispersion, and fundamentally driven pricing. Conversely, 
Crestbreak intends to maintain a more hedged position in efficient markets, such as the US, where returns are 
driven more by factor rotation, episodic speculation, and capital flows. 
Why Now? 
Crestbreak believes there is a structural convergence underway across global equity markets. The S&P 500 
trades at roughly 21x forward earnings, a meaningful premium to its long-term average, and the top 10 holdings 
account for nearly 40% of index weight. Additionally, US policy predictability has deteriorated significantly as 
fiscal uncertainty and governance volatility have become more characteristic of emerging markets that investors 
typically avoid. 
Meanwhile, many international markets are moving in the opposite direction. Despite having improving 
fundamentals, both international developed and emerging markets continue to trade at substantial discounts 
to the US, at approximately ~16x and ~12x forward earnings respectively. Governance reforms in Japan and 
South Korea, a return of liquidity to previously under -owned markets such as LATAM, and defense and 
technology investment across Europe are creating durable earnings  catalysts that are not yet reflec ted in 
company valuations.  In 2025, non-US equities outperformed US equities by over 15 percentage points in dollar 
terms, the widest margin of non-US outperformance in over a decade. 
Crestbreak believes this convergence is not a short -term dislocation but the early stages of a multi -year re-
rating. The portfolio is designed to capture this dynamic on both sides of the book.  
Investment Strategy Overview 
Investment Sourcing Framework 
The strategy integrates both top-down and bottom-up elements, with security selection serving as the primary 
driver of returns. Top -down analysis is used to identify environments where expectations, liquidity, or 
governance cycles may be shifting. These ob servations help prioritize where research effort is directed, but do 
not dictate capital allocation. Bottom -up underwriting determines whether companies within those 
environments offer attractive asymmetric risk-reward profiles.  
In practice, most ideas originate from bottom-up analysis. When an industry or company undergoes a structural 
shift such as margin inflection, consolidation, or technological/regulatory disruption, Crestbreak evaluates which 
individual security best expresses that thesis, across geographies, to identify the most asymmetric opportunity. 
These principles allow flexibility without relying on macro views. 
 
 
Stock Selection Process 
The core of Crestbreak’s philosophy is a “rate of change” framework. Markets continuously price businesses 
based not only on their current earnings power, but on the trajectory investors believe those earnings will follow. 
Growth may be perceived as accelerating, decelerating, stabilizing, or deteriorating. The opportunity arises when 
reality differs materially from the perceived trajectory. Crestbreak seeks to identify and forecast catalysts where 
the underlying rate of change, whether improving or worseni ng, is meaningfully different from the market’s 
consensus view. 
On the long side, this often involves businesses that are misunderstood, structurally misclassified, or temporarily 
misperceived. A durable compounder may be viewed as having significantly slowing growth. A company 
undergoing operational improvement or dem onstrating a sustained inflection in revenue growth may still be 
valued as a lower-quality asset. In such cases, returns are driven not only by earnings revisions but by multiple 
expansion as investor perception changes. 
The same framework applies on the short side. Certain companies may report stable or improving metrics, yet 
closer examination reveals structural deterioration in underlying economics, earnings quality concerns, or 
governance misalignment. Turnaround narra tives may be embraced by the market despite weakening 
competitive positioning or limited financial resilience. When consensus assumes stabilization or growth and the 
true trajectory is deceleration or decline, the downside risk can be substantial.  
For longs and shorts, the most attractive opportunities are rarely those that are universally recognized as the 
best or worst businesses. When a stock is widely celebrated and well understood, expectations are typically 
embedded in the price. Similarly, wh en a stock is left for dead or it is obvious that the 
management/fundamentals are fraudulent in nature, the stock reflects this reality. As the adage goes, “if it’s 
obvious, it’s obviously wrong.” Crestbreak instead focuses on situations where it believes perception and reality 
are misaligned. 
Research Approach  
The strategy relies on a research process designed to produce differentiated forecasts. The process includes 
primary diligence across suppliers, customers, competitors, former employees, regulators, and industry 
specialists, supported where appropriate by proprietary and alternative data. 
While US markets are highly competitive, research intensity varies meaningfully across international markets. In 
many regions, coverage remains more dependent on sell -side research and management access, which can 
reinforce home market bias and create larg e blind spots. Crestbreak applies a global diligence lens, reaching 
out to industry experts in various time zones and locales, helping to identify inflections that may be less visible 
within single-country research frameworks. 
Expert networks and alternative data have meaningfully expanded the industry’s information set. In many cases, 
however, these tools are widely used and can become incorporated into expectations quickly. Crestbreak views 
these inputs as part of the mosaic, but not as the primary source of edge. 
The firm prioritizes independent sourcing of expertise through direct outreach, engagement at industry -level 
conferences, and development of proprietary or less crowded datasets. The objective is to supplement broadly 
available information with differentiated perspectives that are harder to replicate. 
Independent research is not about accumulating data, but about informing forward looking signals tied to non-
consensus shifts over the next 18 -24 months. This time frame is intentionally selected: long enough for 
operational shifts to meaningfully impact valuation, yet short enough to retain signal reliability. 
Three Sources of Alpha 
The following three scenarios show how Crestbreak’s research approach generates alpha.  
Global Information Gathering – Overearning EU Gambling Service Provider  
A European technology company provided live dealer infrastructure (computer vision, payment rails, and 
software interfaces) to online gambling operators globally. During the COVID driven acceleration of online 
gambling, the company's earnings roughly quadr upled and its stock followed. As real -world entertainment 
normalized, the sustainability of that growth profile came into question, and the company exhibited 
characteristics that would flag as a potential short candidate: a business priced for sustained double digit growth 
facing multiple headwinds. 
The analytical edge in this situation was not in identifying the deceleration. The edge came from assembling a 
mosaic of hard -to-source, geographically dispersed information that most market participants lacked the 
infrastructure to gather. 
The company's key operations were concentrated in Eastern Europe for cost efficiency. Local language reporting 
documented labor unrest at the company's facilities, including reports of strikes, workplace violence, and alleged 
operational malfeasance. Separ ately, publicly accessible social media groups associated with the company's 
workforce suggested that a meaningful portion of employees were not actively working, directly contradicting 
management's characterizations of operational stability. The implication was straightforward: cost inflation was 
structural, not transitory, and the company's margin profile was more fragile than assumed.  
With these revenue and cost headwinds rising simultaneously, the company transitioned from the 
aforementioned double digit topline growth to stagnation, while its margin profile eroded steadily. The 
valuation multiple contracted accordingly. 
Durational Edge – Terminal Value Myopia in LATAM E-Commerce 
In the late 2010s, Latin American e -commerce penetration stood at an estimated 3 -5% of total retail sales, 
dramatically below the global average of approximately 15%. Outside of Amazon, only a handful of platforms 
in Brazil had built the logistics infrastr ucture required to serve the region at scale. Among them, one company 
stood apart: it possessed the most developed fulfillment network in Latin America and what was widely regarded 
as the highest quality management team in the industry. 
The bull case was straightforward: a dominant platform in an underpenetrated market with a long structural 
runway. The more nuanced question, and the one that created the investment opportunity, was whether the 
market would ever give the business credit for that runway. 
Consensus models consistently anchored terminal value assumptions to recent growth trends rather than to the 
structural penetration opportunity. Each time quarterly growth decelerated sequentially whether from a slight 
revenue miss or margin pressure from incremental investment, the stock sold off sharply as though the 
penetration ceiling had been reached. Skeptics questioned whether unit economics could work for low -value 
items in the region, arguing that commission fee pressure and subsidized shipping costs made the model 
structurally unprofitable. 
The edge was in the willingness to see through periods of acute pessimism driven by quarterly noise in a 
business where the relevant time horizon was measured in years, not quarters. Latin American e -commerce 
penetration has since reached an estimated 12–15% of total retail. 
Insight Divergence – Failed US Medtech Business 
A US medical device company, backed by individuals with a history of promotional capital markets activity, 
developed a product to replace traditional skin graft procedures for burn treatment. The device biopsied a small 
sample of a patient’s healthy skin, processed it into a paste -like suspension, and applied the material to the 
wound site. On the strength of this concept, the company reached a market capitalization of several hundred 
million dollars. 
The company hosted open investor facility tours that revealed blacked -out windows, an absence of 
manufacturing equipment and negligible production activity. Notably, at least one attendee interpreted the 
sparse facilities favorably, characterizing the business as "incredibly capital-light." The opposite conclusion could 
also be reached: there simply wasn't a business. 
That moment crystallized the insight at the core of this example. The same set of observable facts can lead to 
diametrically opposite conclusions depending on the framework through which they are processed.  
Eventually, Wall Street caught on to the lack of commercialization. Revenue remained negligible for an extended 
period. Following management turnover and eventual delisting, the business filed for bankruptcy.  
Portfolio Construction and Risk Management 
Portfolio Construction 
Long positions are underwritten to achieve targeted 25% annualized returns over a 2-3 year horizon, with upside 
optionality for select investments to potentially double over that period. Short positions are generally evaluated 
over a 6–18 month time frame, with targeted downside of 50–100% depending on the nature of the thesis. The 
long and short books are constructed with a view toward maintaining a balanced duration profile across the 
portfolio.  
The portfolio is expected to operate within a typical gross exposure range of approximately 120–160%, with net 
exposure flexibly managed between 30-60% depending on the opportunity set and risk conditions. The strategy 
maintains diversification across coun tries and industries while allowing capital to concentrate in higher 
conviction situations within defined limits. 
On the long side, position sizing reflects conviction, liquidity, and downside asymmetry, with the portfolio 
typically comprising 20-30 positions and average exposures of approximately 300 -500 basis points. Individual 
positions are subject to defined conce ntration limits and are actively managed as fundamentals and valuation 
evolve. 
Given the asymmetric risk profile of short selling, individual short positions are sized conservatively and 
monitored closely for concentration risk. The short book is expected to comprise approximately 40-50 positions 
across varying conviction levels, including higher conviction shorts typically initiated at 150 basis points or 
greater and smaller tactical positions generally initiated at approximately 50 basis points to help manage 
portfolio exposure. 
Similar to longs, short position sizes are actively managed within defined concentration limits to prevent 
disproportionate impact on portfolio returns. Adverse price movements or developments that challenge the 
original thesis will prompt formal re-underwriting and risk reassessment. 
At Crestbreak’s current scale, the portfolio will operate well within the strategy’s estimated capacity of $2 -3 
billion, allowing full expression of the firm’s highest -conviction ideas across international SMID cap markets 
without market impact.  
Risk Management 
Crestbreak defines risk as the probability of permanent capital impairment. Volatility is monitored closely as a 
signal of shifting conditions and potential changes in risk, rather than as risk in isolation.  
Systematic exposures (country, industry, growth, value, momentum, size, etc.) are monitored but not 
mechanically neutralized. Country and industry represent two of the most significant embedded factors in global 
equity investing and are incorporated directly into the research funnel.  
Liquidity risk is also explicitly considered. The portfolio emphasizes securities where position size can be 
adjusted responsibly under normal market conditions, and exposure is calibrated to avoid forced selling during 
periods of stress.
