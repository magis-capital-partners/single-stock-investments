Market Update
Global equity markets sold off sharply and experienced elevated volatility in March, as investors re-priced inflation 
and growth risks linked to the Iran conflict. Most indices ended March in negative territory, with Europe (SX5E down -
9.14%) and Asia (Nikkei down -12.68%) suffering more than the US (S&P down -4.98%) reflecting their heavier 
dependence on imported energy. Credit conditions tightened noticeably, especially in high yield and lower rated 
corporate debt, as higher oil prices lifted inflation expectations and pushed government bond yields higher. Markets 
moved from pricing one further ECB cut this year, to now expecting two hikes. Energy intensive and cyclical sectors 
were hit hardest, while energy and defense stocks outperformed. Oil was the main transmission channel between 
geopolitics and global markets. Brent crude surged dramatically in March, jumping from around $70 per barrel pre-
war to above $100, and at times briefly spiking close to $120, amid fears of supply disruption and ongoing shipping 
constraints through the Strait of Hormuz.
Hedging 
In the chaotic market movements at the start of March as the war began, we thought some parts of  the market were 
slow to react. Volatility pricing in particular was muted for several days, so we took the opportunity to increase our 
SX5E put option hedges and materially reduce portfolio Gap beta to  0.17 (from 0.32 at the start of the month) whilst 
maintaining a reduced but positive MTM beta of 0.04. 
After the War
Protecting the downside is a vital component of generating absolute returns over the cycle and whilst we continue 
to play an active “defense”, the long term “offensive” playing field in opportunistic credit now looks different than it 
did at the start of the year, and in a good way. As the ceasefire in the Gulf nears the two-week deadline and the 
Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, the likely impact on European economies is becoming starkly clear. The 
IMF highlighted a number of Europe  specific vulnerabilities in its April briefings, all of which are compounded by an 
ongoing energy crisis: (i) Energy dependence: higher oil and gas prices feed inflation and squeeze real incomes; (ii) 
Weaker buffers: limited fiscal space in several European economies; (iii) Structural drag: slower productivity growth 
than the US or parts of Asia; and (iv) Trade exposure: heavier sensitivity to global trade slowdowns and supply chains. 
These events will have a significant impact on European growth. Morgan Stanley have cut their 2026 EU GDP growth 
forecast from +1.2% to +0.5%*. This is based on an expectation that flows through the Straight of Hormuz return to 80% 
of normal by the end of April. We think this is optimistic given it is now mid-April and a deal is still not within reach. In 
a more pessimistic scenario where the Straight remains closed for months, Morgan Stanley expects the EU to enter 
a recession with GDP falling -0.2% in 2026.
At the same time, markets have returned to all time highs with Eurostoxx 600 (SXXP) trading at a 15.6x 2026 
consensus earnings. However consensus earnings growth of 10% already looked optimistic pre-war and, with 
consensus GDP downgrades likely (the question just being their scale), we think the risks are currently skewed to the 
downside. If we instead assume only flat earnings (as has broadly been the case over the last 3 years), the forward 
earnings multiple is 17.3x which is at the very top for European markets. We therefore remain well hedged, adding 
only selectively so far and stand ready to take advantage of a correction should this more negative scenario realize.

Portfolio Update
In response to the evolving geopolitical landscape, we initiated alpha short CDS positions in three airlines during 
March. As a result of the conflict in Iran, we saw a growing risk that constrained fuel supply would translate into 
higher input costs, operational inefficiencies, and ultimately pressure on margins and capacity. We also established 
a short credit position in an Auto-Cars/Light Trucks company that we believe is particularly exposed to the rising 
fuel prices resulting from the conflict. Over the course of the month, we increased our credit position in an electric 
distribution company, building on activity from February, as well as adding to our chemicals (diversified) company 
loan position. We also increased our bond short in a tannery company. We exited our credit position in an oil field 
services company and closed a short credit position in a chemicals (specialty) company after both reached price 
targets. Additionally, we reduced our credit exposures across several names including a short CDS position in a 
chemicals (specialty) company and long credit positions in a water company, a travel services company and a 
finance (other services) company. Overall, we actively reduced LMV to 113% to ensure dry powder is in place and 
ready to deploy into new investments as opportunities arise. 
March performance was led by portfolio hedging activity, which contributed +57bps gross (+45bps net). The 
second-largest contributor was our credit position in an oil exploration and production company, adding +49bps 
gross (+38bps net). Performance was supported by the amend-and-extend refinancing agreed at the end of 
February, alongside tailwinds from rising oil prices. Management indicated that cash flow could potentially double 
by 2026 should oil prices remain around $100 per barrel. Our (well hedged) equity position in an oil exploration and 
production company was our third largest contributor, adding +22bps gross (+17bps net), supported by strong Q4 
2025 results, highlighting reduced capex and improved cash flow generation. In addition, the technical overhang on 
the stock eased materially following the completion of share sales by its two largest shareholders. The position 
also benefited from macro tailwinds linked to higher oil prices amid the Iran conflict. Our long equity position in a 
finance (other services) company was the fourth largest contributor to the performance this month, adding +20bps 
gross (+15bps net). We are private on this name. Rounding out the top five contributors was our long credit position 
in an applications software company, which added +19bps gross (+15bps net). This reflected a recovery from the 
prior month’s software-driven sector sell-off, rather than any company-specific developments. 
Moving on to detractors, our long credit position in a medical labs and testing service company was the largest 
detractor this month at -76bps gross (-61bps net) and we will provide a fuller analysis in next month’s letter. Despite 
both companies reporting strong February results, our equity positions in two medical nursing homes were 
negatively impacted by a broader macro-driven sell-off in French markets, as well as implied real estate valuation 
pressure from rising French government bond yields. One medical nursing home equity position was the second-
largest detractor at -54bps gross (-44bps net), while the other equity position was the fourth largest detractor at -
38bps gross (-31bps net). Our credit position in this name remained relatively unchanged. We expect these equity 
moves to reverse as market conditions stabilize and both companies continue to execute on their value creation 
strategies. A meaningful recovery has already been observed in early April. A finance (other services) company 
was sandwiched between the French care homes, coming in as the third largest detractor. In the absence of any 
material company-specific news, our credit and equity positions detracted -42bps gross (-34bps net), reflecting 
broader market weakness. Finally, our equity position in a commercial bank (non-US ) detracted -22bps gross (-
18bps net), driven by the selling pressure across equity markets during the month.

15. S HARP E  RATIO  This statistic is computed by 
subtracting the return of the risk-free index (typically 91-
day T-bill or other cash benchmark) from the return 
of the manager to determine the risk-adjusted excess 
return. This excess return is then divided by the standard 
deviation of the manager. A manager taking on risk, 
instead of investing in cash, is expected to generate 
higher returns and Sharpe measures how well the 
manager generated returns with that risk. It is a 
measurement of efficiency utilizing the relationship 
between annualized risk-free return and annualized 
standard deviation. The higher the Sharpe Ratio, the 
greater efficiency produced by the manager. For 
example, a Sharpe Ratio of 1 is better than a ratio of 0.5. 
(Source: eVestment)

manager. A positive alpha indicates that a manager has 
produced returns above the expected level at that risk 
level, and vice versa for a negative alpha. Alpha is the Y 
intercept of the regression line. (Source: eVestment)
