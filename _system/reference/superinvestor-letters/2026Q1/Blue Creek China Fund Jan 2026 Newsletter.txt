AI Anxiety Syndrome
Over the past month, global equity markets have been dominated by a wave of
anxiety around artificial intelligence.
Following earnings releases, the major U.S. hyperscalers — Amazon, Google,
Microsoft, and Meta — significantly raised their 2026 capital expenditure guidance.
Combined AI infrastructure investment is expected to reach USD 600 billion,
triggering market concerns over declining free cash flow. Investors fear a classic
prisoner’s dilemma: an AI arms race that risks inefficient capital allocation and
potential overinvestment. As a result, share prices of all four companies corrected
sharply, with Google experiencing a relatively smaller drawdown due to its more
complete large-model ecosystem. At the same time, enthusiasm surrounding AI
agents intensified volatility. The rapid rise of Openclaw’s personal agent, alongside
Anthropic’s announcement of legal-industry agent solutions, sparked a sell-off in
enterprise SaaS stocks. Markets began pricing in the possibility that AI could reduce
labor demand across service industries, thereby compressing software spending.
Extreme sentiment led to irrational moves. A micro-cap company valued at just USD
2.85 million claimed its AI model could reduce empty truck mileage, yet this
announcement wiped nearly 20% off the stock of logistics leader C.H. Robinson, a
company with decades of industry expertise and substantial AI investment. Ironically,
the small company’s prior core business was karaoke equipment.

In China, several large AI models were launched ahead of the Lunar New Year.
ByteDance’s Seedance 2.0 video model drew particular attention, demonstrating the
ability to generate multimodal AI videos approaching Hollywood-level quality via
prompts — with capabilities some observers believe rival or exceed leading U.S.
systems. Zhipu’s GLM-5 showed programming and agent performance comparable
to advanced closed-source models. Meanwhile, markets await the release of
DeepSeek V4. In contrast, companies perceived as lagging in model competitiveness,
including Tencent, faced noticeable valuation pressure.
The clearest beneficiaries of the AI narrative remain upstream infrastructure
players — storage leaders such as SK Hynix, semiconductor manufacturers such as
TSMC, and energy equipment suppliers directly leveraged to AI data center
expansion.
Amid widespread anxiety, a more rational perspective is necessary.
Due to GPU constraints, Chinese internet giants are investing less aggressively
in AI infrastructure compared with U.S. hyperscalers. However, Chinese engineering
teams have demonstrated an ability to optimize under resource limitations,
producing highly competitive open-source models. Tencent’s measured “late-mover”
strategy in AI development is consistent with its long-standing pragmatism. As
domestic open-source ecosystems mature, Tencent is likely to integrate
best-of-breed capabilities, particularly through deeper collaboration with DeepSeek,
to close model gaps. In consumer AI, ByteDance’s Doubao chatbot currently holds a

leading position. Other platforms are expected to differentiate rather than replicate
chatbot strategies. Alibaba continues advancing Qwen within its e-commerce
ecosystem. Tencent is building Yuanbao around its social graph and is positioned to
deploy AI agents through its Mini Program ecosystem to empower merchants and
services.
Unlike the mobile internet era — where incremental users carried near-zero
marginal cost — AI interactions require substantial token consumption and energy
expenditure. For Tencent, scaling a mass-market chatbot to hundreds of millions or
even a billion MAUs would entail enormous ongoing compute and maintenance
costs with uncertain monetization, implying structurally low ROI. AI competitive
dynamics therefore remain fluid, shaped as much by ecosystem fit as by model
capability.
AI’s disruption to China’s traditional software sector may prove more muted
than in the U.S. China’s SaaS penetration remains relatively low, and enterprise
willingness to pay for standardized software is limited. With the proliferation of
open-source AI, leading companies across industries are more likely to internalize AI
functionality to improve efficiency and reduce operating costs. Among our portfolio
holdings, industry leaders are already accelerating R&D investment in AI integration.
2026 is likely to mark an inflection point for AI applications, with novel use
cases emerging across sectors.
Recent weakness in China’s internet sector reflects not only AI-related

sentiment but also broader structural factors. First, Alibaba’s continued investment
in near-field retail has intensified losses among major players, weighing on
heavyweight stocks such as Alibaba, Meituan, and JD.com and contributing to
temporary capital outflows. Second, regulatory developments under the platform
antitrust framework have renewed investor caution. Investigations into exclusivity
practices, combined with policy pressure on gig-economy platforms like Didi and
Meituan to enhance worker protections, have revived concerns about tighter
constraints on platform economics. Even unverified rumors — such as potential
increases in gaming tax rates — have been sufficient to trigger sharp market
reactions.
Despite persistently weak domestic demand data, property transactions in
tier-one cities have shown signs of recovery. The Shanghai government’s RMB 80
billion program to acquire older small apartments has further contributed to a
gradual shift in real estate sentiment. In an environment characterized by
heightened emotion and narrative-driven volatility, we remain focused on
undervalued industry leaders with resilient business models, strong balance sheets,
robust cash flow generation, and proactive — yet economically disciplined — AI
adoption strategies.

Fund Strategy
 Investment philosophy: research-driven fundamental-oriented value investing to pursue long-term
above-average returns.
 Investment criteria: the fund invests in companies with 1) a strong business model, 2) a strong
management team, and 3) strong corporate governance.
 Due diligence system: the fund adopts a “360 degree” due diligence approach, which includes interviewing
management, customers, supply chain, competitors, regulators, and industry experts.
