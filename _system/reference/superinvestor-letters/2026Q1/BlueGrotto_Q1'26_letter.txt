Update on TSMC 
We added to TSMC in late March (we own TSMC partially through the ADR but added to our position in the 
Taiwanese listing on swap).  The most significant takeaway from recent industry intelligence is that the non-TSMC 
leading-edge market is actively shrinking. While the previous year began with optimism that Intel and Samsung 
could provide viable alternatives, that sentiment has evapora ted. Both competitors remain mired in yield and 
execution issues; Samsung’s 5nm yield struggles pushed customers toward TSMC , and their 3nm Gate -All-
Around transition has failed to gain traction. Furthermore, early Process Design Kits from Intel and Samsung are 
being viewed by the industry as incomplete versions compared to TSMC’s polished releases. Simulations are not 
matching physical reality  and Intel’s recent cancellation of its foundry event is interpreted as a clear signal of a 
lack of traction. Currently, virtually all meaningful leading -edge design wins are flowing to TSMC, leaving them 
with no functional competitor in the 3nm and 2nm generations. 
Historically, TSMC was perceived as being cautious with pricing to ward off competition, but with that threat now 
neutralized, the pricing floor for next-generation wafers has shifted dramatically. While 3nm wafers were estimated 
at approximately $28,000, intelligence suggests that 2nm prices are climbing toward $40,000, with specialized 
nodes involving backside power potentially reaching $47,000. These nodes are critical for high -performance 
compute and the latest AI hardware from companies like Nvidia. For these high-end chips, the logic wafer cost is 
becoming a smaller percentage of the total bill of materials due to the rising expense of HBM4 memory. This gives 
TSMC immense room to hike prices without breaking the customer's business model, as the industry increasingly 
acknowledges that the foundry deserves these premiums for its reliability and technical execution.  
Despite macro headwinds in PCs and smartphones where volumes are down significantly, TSMC is positioned to 
grow revenue by 30% to 40% this year. This decoupling from consumer electronics is driven by a surge in AI chip 
design starts and the high utilizatio n of advanced fabs. Under new leadership, the company is maintaining a 
disciplined approach to contracts, protecting base volumes while renegotiating "upside" or emergency capacity 
requests at premium spot rates. AI -assisted design tools are also allowing startups to reach tape -out faster, 
creating a surge of new customers who have only one viable manufacturing partner. TSMC is even successfully 
commanding higher pricing for US -based wafers from its Arizona fabs, turning a geopolitical necessity into a 
margin-accretive offering for customers who require domestic production. 
TSMC’s dominance extends beyond the wafer into infrastructure and the broader ecosystem. Capital expenditure 
is expected to scale from $55 billion in 2026 to over $60 billion in 2027. Because competitors are failing to yield, 
TSMC must pick up the slack for the entire AI industry, essentially forcing them into massive investments for which 
they are guaranteed to be paid. As the company focuses its primary capital on high -margin logic production, it is 
strategically utilizing partners as pressure valves for advanced packaging needs. This allows the firm to maintain 
control over the silicon photonics and CoWoS roadmap while focusing its own facilities on the most complex 
manufacturing tasks. Overall, we see a business with a critical technology position in the highest growth portions 
of the market, delivering 30%+ revenue growth along with operating margins expanding from 30-40% a few years 
to now solidly above 50%. Despite this strong financial profi le, the stock is only valued at 17x consensus 2027E 
EPS. 
Updated View on CACI International 
If you would have told me we would go to war with Iran, I would not have expected CACI International to de-rate.  
CACI International is a national security technology company currently misclassified by the market as a generic 
government IT services provider. At roughly 16.5x FY27 earnings, the stock trades at a services multiple despite 
a fundamental transformation into a defense technology business.  
CACI International is a $9.4 billion national security technology company currently priced like a generic 
government staffing firm, but it is poised for a significant valuation re -rating as a Defense Technology leader , in 
our view. This shift is expected to be catalyzed by a change in its GICS code following the recently completed 
$2.6 billion acquisition of ARKA Group. A decade ago, CACI was 80% services, relying on labor hours and cost -
plus contracts with no competitive moat. Tod ay, the mix has flipped: 60% of revenue comes from technology, 
featuring proprietary hardware and software sold commercially at margins 300–500 basis points above the legacy 
services business. This structural transformation is backed by a $34 billion backl og, a 6 -year average contract 
duration, and a recompete win rate exceeding 92%. 
Page 5 
The largest product business in the portfolio is SIGINT; in layman’s terms, sensors detect a specific radio 
frequency fingerprint, which can identify exactly what type of device is transmitting , whether it's an adversary’s 
radar station, a specific type of drone, or an encrypted radio.   Recent channel checks reveal that SIGINT is a 
$1.3–1.4 billion business growing at 10% annually , which is a critical figure we don’t think is well understood by 
the market. When combined with electronic warfare and counter-UAS, this segment generates more EBITDA than 
the entire services segment despite having nearly three times less revenue. Because one dollar of this high -end 
product revenue generates seven times the bottom-line contribution of a dollar of services revenue, earnings per 
share are projected to grow from approximately $26 to $40 in just three years even with modest total revenue 
growth. 
The counter-UAS line, anchored by the Merlin system, is the fastest -growing part of the portfolio at roughly $300 
million today with a 25% annual growth rate. Beyond hardware sales, Merlin carries a powerful recurring revenue 
component through its signal f ingerprint library, sold on a maintenance model at approximately 15% of the unit 
cost annually. This creates a SaaS -like revenue stream of $45 –90 million today that grows automatically with 
every new deployment. This library represents an insurmountable co mpetitive moat; it contains 20 years of 
classified operational radio frequency collection from global combatant commands. A new competitor cannot 
simply buy or replicate two decades of battlefield signal data, which is essential for accurate AI classificat ion and 
threat defeat.   As the DoD increasingly utilizes AI, we think the proprietary data CACI owns and generates 
represents a significant AI-advantaged asset. 
The acquisition of ARKA Group completes this technological architecture by adding geospatial intelligence 
(GEOINT) to CACI’s dominant SIGINT capabilities. ARKA adds $700 million in full -year revenue and provides a 
multi-domain sensing architecture covering air, land, sea, and space. Crucially, ARKA brings the only "agentic" AI 
platform authorized for classified networks , a certification that creates an 18 -month barrier for any new entrant. 
This integration allows CACI to provide an AI -orchestrated fusion of signals and imagery that the government 
previously had to assemble manually. By owning this sensing and processing su bstrate, CACI positions itself as 
a complementary partner to orchestration platforms like Palantir or Anduril, rather than a competitor.  
The budget environment acts as a massive tailwind, with the defense budget tracking toward $1 trillion and 
supplemented by $300 billion in procurement -focused reconciliation funding. This funding flows directly into 
CACI’s core strengths: counter -UAS, border security, and space -based sensing. Recent contract wins, such as 
the $306 million Defense Logistics Agency order for financial modernization and a $287 million Army IPPS -A 
modernization contract, highlight CACI's role as the indispensable partner for non-discretionary federal mandates. 
As higher -margin products scale and ARKA is integrated, CACI is on a trajectory to compound EPS at 18% 
through FY29. With an estimated FY29 EPS of $49, a 20x multiple suggests a price target of $980 within three 
years, offering significant upside as the market begins to value the company as a national security technology 
company and not a DOGE threatened services business. 
Discussion on Portfolio Management  
We are bulls on the adoption and diffusion of AI and hence most of the companies supporting the build out of the 
infrastructure to support it.  However, with a fairly large portion of the portfolio dedicated to these businesses, 
which are also trading with “the momentum factor,” it is difficult to not face sharp ramps and drops in the portfolio 
associated with our long book.  We think owning these names, especially with a layer of valuation discipline and 
judgement attached to which businesses we own, should result in strong returns going forward.  However, it also 
may result in months like March, especially after periods where the portfolio has done well for several months in 
a row. 
While we tried to adapt our short book moderately for what we somewhat anticipated after a strong run in the long 
book, it is difficult to find shorts where we have significant catalysts that are tied to the above factors (at least for 
the time being).  Adding additional complexity, software stocks seem to be all of a sudden trading with extreme 
counter correlation to AI stocks (we’ll lovingly call this the Citrini factor from what happened this quarter), and we 
were short several software stocks that traded to very low RSIs in the month of March/early Ap ril.  We adapted 
on the fly to this by covering a number of software stocks that flushed down based on these mechanical 
basket/factor trades in the market.

Final Thought  
We are very interested to see how companies use AI to generate new businesses or advantage their existing 
business.  So far, it seems like corporations outside of big tech companies have largely used it to lower costs.  
While companies like Anthropic has been shipping new products that are ramping fast, we haven’t seen a lot of 
interesting new products introduced by companies that are not “AI native.”  Ad tech remains an interesting area 
where especially incumbents like Meta and Google are ramping new AI products that help drive advertiser ROIs.  
C.H. Robinson has been utilizing agents to both bid for new business and help drive logistics  routing.  Akamai, a 
new position, should benefit from both an inference at the edge network they are building plus bot management 
that helps secure companies from the proliferation of agents.  That being said, we are looking for compelling use 
cases of enterprise adoption and would encourage clients and friends of the firm to send us examples if you see 
them.  We hope everyone has a wonderful summer.
