Y2 Capital Newsletter – March 2026 
March 2026 Performance and Market Insights 
Y2’s net return in March was -1.71%, in a month when MSCI China declined 7.70%, MSCI APAC drew down 13.00%, and the HFRI 
Emerging Markets China Index fell 3.11%. Our average net exposure in the month was 27.73%. Year-to-date through March 2026, 
the fund is up 8.84% net, outperforming MSCI China (-8.94%), MSCI APAC (-0.01%), and the HFRI Emerging Markets China Index 
(+1.40%), while maintaining an average net exposure of 29.8 0%. Our focus on core investment pillars such as Self-Sufficient 
Fuels, Reliable and Affordable Power, Robust Resources Supply and Recycling , and Tech-driven Global Competitiveness helped 
the portfolio navigate a market that was forced to reprice geopolitical risk, energy shocks, and growth expectations 
simultaneously, particularly in March. In doing so, we have avoided treating the oil shock as a blanket reason to own energy and 
instead focused on businesses that can either pass through higher energy and logistics costs or help customers reduce them, 
while being cautious and selectively short where higher feedstock costs lower earnings rather than support them. 
Against the backdrop of a weak global market, Chinese  equities were similarly weak in March but fared relatively better than 
many global and regional markets. The Hang Seng Index fell 7.2%, and the Hang Seng Tech Index declined 9.7% . A-shares 
continued to benefit from lower geopolitical sensitivity and greater exposure to policy-aligned real-asset sectors, in line with our 
focus on renewable energy, power, resource, and technology – onshore indices were better shielded, with the CSI300 falling 6.0% 
and ChiNext down 4.3%, although the majority of the drawdown occurred in the second half of March.  At the same time, higher 
energy prices can also help alleviate China’s entrenched deflationary pressures, while low inventories in parts of the indust rial 
chain could support a near-term pickup in restocking activity and selected areas of real-economy demand. 
Elsewhere in the global market, March was defined by a violent global de-risking as escalating Middle East tensions rippled across 
asset classes, pushing crude up by 51.3% during the month. Despite a sharp reversal on the final day of the month, US equities 
still finished lower, with the S&P 500 Index down 5.1%, while Asia broadly sold off, led by Korea’s KOSPI at -23.2%, Japan’s Nikkei 
225 at -14.8%, and Taiwan’s TAIEX at -12.7%. 
Y2 Views – Resilience, Energy Security, and Supply Chains 
We remain conservative on supply-chain disruptions and are tilting the portfolio toward companies that are either less exposed 
to critical bottlenecks or positioned to benefit from the re-wiring of global trade. Recent developments highlight why  – two of 
Zambia’s key copper smelters are preparing for unusually long maintenance shutdowns at a time when sulphuric acid supplies 
are already tight, and several brokers now warn that this could materially constrain regional copper and cobalt production if  
sustained. We are seeing similar strains in other critical metals , such as nickel, where policy changes and higher input costs in 
Indonesia, the largest producer, are forcing some newer processing plants that produce battery-grade nickel to scale back output. 
In fertilizers, India and other lower-income Asian economies are facing higher costs and potential rationing risks because th ey 
rely heavily on the Middle East for both fertilizer and the gas used to produce it domestically, prompting governments to consider 
subsidy and allocation measures to protect food security.  
We are also seeing pressure build in higher-value inputs that matter for technology, manufacturing, and air travel. Recent industry 
research now flags disruptions to the supply of industrial gases and specialty materials used in semiconductors and medical 
equipment, with prices rising sharply as inventories are drawn down. Airlines have begun adding fuel surcharges to tickets to 
offset higher energy and logistics costs, while broader inflation in chemicals and plastics is starting to work its way down the

Given these dynamics , we prefer companies with stronger pricing power, more domestic or diversified demand, and clearer

For example, on energy security, our focus on opportunities arising from the current shock reflects both logistical constrain ts 
and the repricing of physical risk. Vessel traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has fallen by roughly 90% from pre-conflict levels, 
as many shipowners and crews are unwilling to accept the sharply higher war-risk insurance premia and tighter policy terms 
now required, even though such cover remains technically available in specialist markets. This is already pushing up delivered 
prices for oil, gas, fertilizers, and key chemicals (notably sulphuric acid), strengthening the economic case for electrifica tion, 
efficiency upgrades, and more robust power and transport infrastructure. At the same time, we remain constructive where tight 
supply meets resilient demand, particularly in rare earths and other strategic materials central to electric vehicles, renewa bles, 
industrial upgrading, and defense. 
Portfolio Actions – Positioning for Energy Shocks and Real‑economy Stress 
Consistent with these views, we have been increasing exposure to a selection of companies that directly benefit from higher and 
more volatile energy costs, as well as the push for more resilient infrastructure. The common thread across these positions i s a 
focus on leveraging electrified transport, smarter power infrastructure, and services that help households and businesses 
manage energy and logistics shocks, rather than on reliance on a cheap and uninterrupted fossil-fuel supply. 
Our positioning in the EV OEM space is a good illustration. For most of last year, we were net short the segment due to fierc e 
competition among brands in China, but we turned net long in March.  Our largest investment here now is in a major domestic 
player undergoing a brand and volume turnaround, supported by a refreshed product line-up, improved perception among mid- 
to high-end Chinese consumers, and a pipeline of new model launches that broaden its addressable market. At the same time, its 
battery-swap network is becoming more attractive as fuel prices rise and urban drivers place a premium on shorter charging 
times and a more predictable total cost of ownership, which we believe can support better utilization, higher service 
attachment/brand loyalty, and improved lifetime economics per customer. 
Alongside this, we own select names across the electric-bus and commercial-vehicle ecosystem, grid and power-management 
equipment, two-wheel electric scooters, mobility platforms, and charging infrastructure. These businesses sit at different points 
along the electrification and energy-efficiency chain, but all stand to benefit from corporate and policy decisions to reduce energy 
intensity, diversify away from vulnerable supply routes, and invest in more robust transport and power networks. In contrast, in 
areas such as integrated oil and refining, where the market often assumes that higher crude prices are an automatic positive, we 
have been cautious and selectively short, as higher feedstock costs, regulated pricing, and margin pressure can lead to earni ngs 
downgrades and potential misses to the street’s high expectations in the current environment. 
Looking ahead, we continue to see attractive opportunities across our core pillars, even as the macro and geopolitical backdrop 
remains complex. While select parts of technology and other growth segments are seeing genuine earnings upgrades, recent 
market strength has also been supported by higher risk appetite and a greater tolerance for unresolved risks around Iran, energy, 
and supply-chain disruption. We also think investors may be too quick to assume that the eventual outcome will resemble prior 
episodes of policy brinkmanship . Unlike last year’s trade negotiations, this time President Trump is dealing with a far less 
predictable counterparty in Iran amid an active military conflict, which broadens the range of possible outcomes and makes them 
harder to predict. In this environment, we continue to prioritize risk-reward over chasing near-term performance, keeping net 
exposure deliberately low and letting stock selection, rather than beta exposure, drive outcomes – even if that means more limited 
participation in periods when indices are strong. We believe this risk-reward discipline, combined with our focus on the 6

Director and Head of China at Sculptor Capital ( “Sculptor”, formerly “Och-Ziff”). During his 13 years with Sculptor,
