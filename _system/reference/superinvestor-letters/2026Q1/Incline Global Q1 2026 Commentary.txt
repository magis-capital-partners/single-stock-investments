Market Commentary 
 
From a macro perspective, we are monitoring developments related to five key macro factors impacting the 
stock market: 1) the pace of economic growth, 2) interest rates, 3) moderating inflation, 4) tariffs and 5) the 
war with Iran. 
 
 
Pace of Economic Growth 
 
The Atlanta Fed publishes a weekly real-time GDP tracker called “GDPNow” which currently forecasts that US 
real GDP growth was 1.3% in Q1.2 This is an acceleration relative to the 0.7% posted in Q4, although modestly 
below trend relative to GDP growth in prior quarters with 4.4% posted in Q3 and 3.8% posted in Q2. Growth in 
consumer spending has somewhat moderated, though still positive, while corporate investment remains 
strong. We believe that GDP growth should provide a stable backdrop for corporate revenues and, if 
inflationary costs can be managed, we expect that it will also result in continued solid corporate earnings in 
Q1. While a recession could materialize in the future, the economy is currently showing consistent positive 
GDP growth – we expect that numerous stimulative factors could result in meaningfully stronger than trend 
GDP growth and S&P 500 returns in 2026. 
 
We believe US companies with global operations will see a tailwind to foreign earnings  from favorable Y/Y 
currency comparisons. The US dollar has strengthened since Q4, but remains well above prior year levels, as 
evidenced by the Euro near its 3-year high at $1.15 (+6% Y/Y) at Q1 end, and the broader dollar index (DXY) 
down 4% Y /Y. This should be a continued tailwind and provide upside to US global companies’ foreign 
earnings. We will continue to monitor these economic trends and the potential impacts on future GDP growth. 
 
 
Interest Rates  
 
Q1 saw an uptick in US interest rates. The US 10-Year Treasury yield started the year at 4.17% and finished Q1 
up slightly to 4.32%. Current market-implied expectations of 0-1 rate cuts for 2026 are down from expectations 
of 2 cuts as of early January. We believe the market is underestimating the likelihood of future rate cuts. CPI 
now remains only modestly elevated (driven largely by backward-looking housing inflation discussed below). 
Core CPI has declined over the last year  from 3.1% to 2 .6%, while employment, the other half of the Fed’s 
dual mandate, has weakened. 
 
While rising fuel prices and commodity disruptions caused by the closing of the Strait of Hormuz pose a 
potential risk, we expect energy prices will fall as traffic through the Strait normalizes and moderating shelter 
will be a tailwind to lower inflation . Chairman Powell has indicated that the Fed intends to look through 
immediate inflation spikes caused by rising energy prices, and w e expect improving core-inflation prints will 
support additional rate cuts by the Fed. As core inflation continues to decline, lea ving nominal rates 
unchanged is a de facto hike in the real interest rate. Even after the recent rate cut, the Federal Funds rate 
remains ~1.0% above Core CPI (see following chart), firmly in restrictive territory.

US Tariffs 
 
The Supreme Court ruled that the tariffs levied under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act 
(IEEPA) were unconstitutional in February, overturning the tariffs. The administration has pivoted to alternative 
tools, implement ing a temporary 15% import surcharge under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 . The 
administration is now exploring the use of more permanent tools, including expedited Section 301 
investigations targeting unfair trade practices across several countries . The end result is that the 10-15% US 
global tariff level remains intact.

War in Iran 
Since U.S. and Israeli strikes in Iran began on February 28, global energy markets have experienced significant 
volatility, with Brent crude oil rising from ~$72 per barrel pre -conflict to ~ $120 per barrel in March . With the 
announcement of the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, oil has fallen back to near $85-$95. Energy volatility 
and geopolitical uncertainty also created a dislocation in equity markets in March, with individual stock 
reactions that were much more extreme than the S&P 500’s ~8% decline w ould imply. The recent ceasefire 
announcement and progress on a diplomatic solution have resulted in the equity markets fully reversing 
March losses in April. 
 
We believe the US administration is actively seeking a swift resolution to the war, as evidenced by the recent 
high-level diplomatic mission to Pakistan. Elevated fuel prices will be a drag on economic growth abroad 
and have already caused fuel shortages in parts of Asia. However, the U.S. position as a net energy exporter 
materially dampens the domestic economic impacts and a near-term resolution to the war should allow for 
the fundamental strength of the U.S. economy to continue. Additionally, the Federal Reserve has indicated 
that it will likely look through the near -term inflation spikes caused by the con flict, viewing them as transitory 
rather than warranting a policy response. We believe stable economic growth and an accommodative Fed 
are supportive of continued US equity market strength. 
 
 
Despite potential headwinds, we have a very positive outlook for above trend GDP growth in 2026 due to a 
number of stimulative effects: 1) the US tax cuts that took effect in March/April, 2) further interest rate 
reductions as we pass the Iran war related price shocks, 3) continued AI investments and resulting productivity 
improvements, 4) capex growth due to US reshoring . While 2025 saw strong performance for the Fund , we 
have been more active than usual in refreshing the portfolio to position the fund to take advantage of these 
trends. We believe our long portfolio remains highly “spring-loaded” with great current entry points that we 
took advantage of during periods of uncertainty.

Dave & Buster’s (PLAY)  
 
Dave & Buster’s is a leading operator of high-volume amusement-based restaurants. The company operates 
under two brands: Dave & Buster’s (“D&B”) and Main Event. D&B caters to adults and families, and has 179 
stores across 43 states, Puerto Rico, and Canada. Main Event is a family-oriented brand that has 64 locations 
across 22 states, mostly in the southern U.S. PLAY is also expanding internationally, a new avenue of growth 
with a long runway. At year end , PLAY had four  international D&B stores, had finalized three international 
store openings for FY 2026, and had an additional 35 stores in its pipeline, a fraction of  the 200 -unit D&B 
international opportunity. 
 
PLAY’s new CEO has taken a comprehensive approach to turning around the business. The renewed focus 
on culturally relevant and immersive games, re- allocation of marketing dollars across channels to increase 
efficiency, and optimization of store layout to drive higher spending has stabilized SSS. However, this turning 
point was not communicated aggressively on the recent Q4 earnings call . We believe management was 
conservative given the call occurred before Easter weekend, historically D&B’s most accurate si gnpost for 
the year. With the data now in, it appears Q2 will be the inflection point for positive SSS  and we believe the 
FIFA World Cup will be a significant further catalyst – accelerating foot traffic and driving SSS growth well 
beyond current expectations. 
 
Recently, we traveled to PLAY’s headquarters in Dallas, TX, and met with the company’s management team. 
We were impressed with both Mr. Tarun Lal, CEO, and Mr. Darin Harper, CFO. Both shared a clear vision, 
strategy, and execution plan, as well as their de ep understanding of the business and field in which the 
company operates and gave us further confidence in the position.
