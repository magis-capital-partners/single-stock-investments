The general idea of our investment letter (or any investment letter) is to take what is happening and 
succinctly explain it to investors.
The problem this past quarter has been that the pace of geo-political and technology change has been 
so dizzying that it has been hard to do that – perspectives look outdated quickly.
January and February were dominated by fears of what GenAI could do to whole sections of the 
economy while March and April were dominated by the implications of the Iran war. As we move through 
May, we are now seeing both themes play out at the same time as the dizzying infrastructure build out for 
AI accelerates in conjunction with escalating energy inﬂation. The indexes have rallied overall but the rally 
has been conﬁned to AI infrastructure and energy while the rest of the market stagnates.
We have little exposure to either of these winning themes. Our names have idiosyncratic risk factors 
that are unusually divorced from broader market factors. While we suffered in the ﬁrst quarter, the 
latest earnings period, which occurred at the end of April and early May, was positive, and we are now 
up meaningfully on the year through May 19th.  
Our overall perspective on AI has not changed. We believe AI is going to change the world, but we 
are reluctant to bet on speciﬁc outcomes with speciﬁc companies. Historically, new technology 
infrastructure builds (railroads, electricity, broadband) dramatically alter the lived human experience 
but also tend to leave investors with massive impairment as the technology gets overbuilt.
While we are reluctant to call AI stock winners particularly as the whole sector goes parabolic, we have 
not been reluctant to focus on leveraging AI in our own processes.
The start of this year was spent immersing the whole team in Claude Code. We trained agents on 
data sets to give us signals when businesses are inﬂecting positively or negatively. We have built an 
intelligence gathering tool that collects all ﬁlings and research reports as well expert transcripts and 
data on a particular company and then trains itself to be an expert on that company. We can then have 
a conversation with that agent and have key questions answered succinctly.
We believe the capabilities of this technology are extraordinary and any fund (or person for that 
matter) that is not learning how to use these tools will be left behind.  
As we enter the summer, we will continue to look to lever these new AI tools for research. We remain 
mindful that there are elevated risks in the overall economy with energy inﬂation and bubble-like 
valuations in certain sectors. That said, the productivity boom with the deployment of these new tools 
is growing. How that productivity boom unfolds relative to these other risks will determine how things 
go from here.
LQDA: 
Liquidia reported their quarter earlier this month. The roll out of Yutrepia has only grown stronger 
with weekly patient adds increasing to 94 a week from 82 and the reported revenue being $130mm 
versus consensus at $115mm.
6 There was nothing in the report that surprised us - just further 
conﬁrmation that Yutrepia is becoming the prostacyclin of choice. This was, however, news to the 
market as the stock rallied aggressively following the report.
As the launch of Yutrepia goes from strength to strength, we project LQDA to annualize a billion of 
revenue before the year is over (likely by this fall) and do north of $10 in EPS next year (versus a $57 
stock price today).
7 
As some further valuation context, Verona had what was considered one of the best launches of 
all time in 2024 with Ohtuvayre and was bought for $10 billion within three quarters of that launch. 8   
LQDA has exceeded that launch every quarter by +30% and yet even after this recent rally trades for 
a 40% discount to Verona.
9 
As always, the persistent discount is due to the litigation overhang from Judge Andrews who has 
yet to rule on the bench trial from last June. Our view of the case remains the same and that is that 
LQDA likely wins and the longer we see no decision the less likely it is that Andrews would look to do 
something that disrupts the status quo for commercial parties and patients. We have opined on it so 
many times in prior letters and in public pitches that a full re-hash again here is redundant but happy 
to discuss further with investors.  
ROOT: 
ROOT had a quarter that we thought fully con ﬁrmed the thesis that we pitched late last year. Yet, the 
market barely noticed.
ROOT is a car insurance carrier that by virtue of its technology stack that it built from the ground up 
can embed itself with car sellers. This is a capability that eludes other insurance carriers because 
their technology stacks are antiquated and cobbled together. With access to customer at the point 
of sale, ROOT can grow policies in force without having to ramp up marketing dollars.

We saw that play out this past quarter. ROOT beat on GAAP EPS ($2.09 versus $0.59) and they did 
so by growing policies in force 9% year over year while at the same time reducing their marketing 
expenditure by 50%.
10   
ROOT’s differentiated approach to customer acquisition is being proven out in real time but it 
is being obscured by a negative macro environment as the rest of the car insurance industry is 
aggressively competing on price.
The car insurance industry has fat years and thin years and we are in the middle of a thin year period 
where operating ratios will compress as competition ramps. While the macro may be a headwind, 
ROOT should grow through this period and prove out its differentiated position in the market as a 
partner of choice to car sellers. When the pricing environment gets less competitive, ROOT will be 
ramping policies in force (PIF) and earnings at an operating ratio that we believe will far exceed the 
rest of the industry and the stock will re-rate multiples of where it is currently trading.
