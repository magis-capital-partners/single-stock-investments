AI & Memory Exposure 
 
Google's TurboQuant has generated significant investor attention. Can you provide an update on the fund's current gross 
and net exposure to AI and memory, your reaction to the TurboQuant announcement and whether it represents a 
structural shift in demand similar to DeepSeek?  
Google’s TurboQuant is a “new” compression algorithm that meaningfully improves the efficiency of LLM inference by 
compressing the amount of KV cache memory needed for each “token” of compute. We say it is “new” because while 
the paper was published in 2025, the underlying concept is not new and KV cache memory per token has been 
following a compression roadmap that has already been incorporated by AI model companies such as OpenAI and 
Anthropic. In light of this, we have already included these efficiency gains in our supply and demand models for HBM, 
DRAM, and NAND.  
There are a few important nuances here worth noting. First, the efficiency improvements are non -linear in a negative 
way, meaning each successive generation of model yields diminishing KV cache memory gains. Second, and more 
importantly, we subscribe to Jevons Paradox and believe as memory becomes cheaper and more available, it gets used 
up immediately. Increasing the context window of AI agents drives more demand for those agents, not less memory 
consumption, as increased context and “state” is one of the main drivers of model capability. The short answer is: we 
don't think TurboQuant matters meaningfully to the thesis. 
On the question of which storage types are actually affected, TurboQuant is relevant only to HBM/DRAM and NAND, 
given its connection to the KV cache and GPU architecture. It has nothing to do with HDDs. AI capex, despite what the 
narrative suggests, is not a driver of HDD demand. The use of agents is stressing the infrastructure of non -AI data 
centers and accelerating the data creation trend that has existed in the cloud for years. That is what drives HDDs.

Short-Side AI Positioning & Tech Equities 
 
How are you thinking about building short exposure to complement the long memory book? Which thematics are most 
interesting on the short side related to the AI infrastructure buildout?  
There are parts of the AI infrastructure buildout where we believe supply will overwhelm any reasonable estimate of 
demand, and those are our favorite kinds of shorts. Electrical equipment and cooling are a good example as there are 
dozens of new entrants, and both gross margins and multiples are at peak. Layered on top of that is the concept of "rip 
and replace" as you start to lap data centers built five years ago, the upgrade cycle creates its own headwinds for 
incumbents.

We also see meaningful risk in speculative data center builds, projects where developers construct everything except 
the servers, only to find there isn't enough memory to run the chips needed to complete those facilities. The supply 
chain is not as ready as the construction pipeline assumes. 
 
How are you thinking about the bear case for memory durability across HDD, NAND, and HBM, and the key indicators 
you're monitoring for risks such as improving model efficiency or a slowdown in AI capex?  
 
We have had a tracker for some time that helps us understand current supply and demand for AI chips. W e track the 
rent of old chips (H100s) and new chips (Blackwells) and do a projected return (to see if capex will continue). Prices 
should decline as chips come on, such that they are replaced after about five years. They are expected to follow a 
normal depreciation curve. If prices go down quickly below the curve, it can tell you that capex may slow down. If 
prices go above the curve, it will tell you that returns are good and may continue or increase.  The problem with this is 
that a lot of the rent could be coming from companies that don’t get a return on that rent. Thus, what is most 
important (and most difficult and maybe impossible) is to track and understand how useful AI is.   
 
Fortunately for us (well not so far as none of them have worked), we have found companies related to AI capex that 
have massive amounts of competition coming and trade at high multiples (see above).  
 
The bear case for memory durability would be a massive change in AI architecture (don’t see that coming, but we are 
constantly looking). Or a massive increase in supply like we see  in other parts of the data center build out.  HBM 
capacity is what the companies are adding to, as aggressively as they can. The ability to ramp capex is limited near 
term by “clean room” capacity and long term by ASML (the lithography company) capacity. The supply ramp is thus 
known for the next four to five years due to these constraints.  
 
NAND capex hasn’t ramped at all. The main reason is that the producers of NAND (except for Kioxia/San Disk JV) also 
produce DRAM/HBM and are using clean room and capex dollars for that instead. Kioxia/San Disk we believe are 
waiting for volume and price commitments to add significantly (it was only a year ago when NAND was in the shitter).  
 
For HDD’s, the two companies are not adding any capacity (no new boxes). They are adding more content per box and 
raising prices. We are monitoring any changes to this behavior.  We do not believe that new entrants can come into 
this industry due to the IP (both tech and process) that the companies have. China would already be in this business if 
it wasn’t so (China produces no HDDs).  
 
Do you believe tech equities can sustain further upside without aggressive Fed rate cuts, or is a real bear market possible 
even in a strong AI growth environment? 
 
We don’t want to pontificate on macro and guess on what would happen to tech multiples in the face of Fed rate cuts . 
What we do know is that there are names in the AI ecosystem that are trading at very high multiples on expanded 
margins, with competition coming which could potentially lead to bottlenecks in demand growth. That's where we're 
focused on the short side.

Energy, Iran & the Strait of Hormuz 
 
Since the early March update, how has the ongoing Iran conflict affected your energy positioning across oil, LNG, and 
gas? What are your near-term views on the acute pricing implications of an extended Strait of Hormuz closure, and how 
do you think about the ripple effects on global supply chains including petrochemicals, fertilizer, and Asian refinery 
capacity across a 2-, 4-, and 8-week closure scenario? 
 
We have the supply and demand fully modeled across oil, LNG, and gas under various Strait of Hormuz closure 
scenarios. The math is fairly straightforward: three months of no oil moving through the Strait and we reach scarcity -
level inventories. That said, even before the ceasefire, oil was finding its way out. A VLCC carries approximately two 
million barrels, and you only need seven of them moving to effectively balance the oil market (you don’t have to clear 
the entire Strait of 60 ships each way per day to clear the oil market). 
On the permanent damage front: one LNG train and a small refinery have been affected. Absent further damage, the 
vast majority of supply will come back when peace is restored. It is also worth noting that the very large oversupply in 
oil markets in 2025 made this buffer longer than it would have been had we entered with normal inventories.   
We can walk through the 2-, 4-, and 8-week scenario analysis in detail, but the short version is: the shorter scenarios 
are manageable with inventory draws and rerouting; a three-month scenario starts to involve meaningful price 
appreciation to demand destruction levels and supply chain dislocation across petrochemicals, fertilizer, and jet fuel 
which is not priced in. The economies of the world can not handle permanent dislocations like the first four weeks of 
this war.  
 
We did the above work on supply and demand immediately after the war started. We have then observed  the various 
prices of oil and inventory draws of oil and its derivative products. The observable draw is ~5 mm bls/day, when the 
above math would say it should have been ~12 mm bls/day.  So, it’s been almost 10 weeks, and prices aren’t to the 
moon.  So, we believe that the price action plus inventory draw tells anyone who is watching that perhaps 3 or 4 ships 
a day have been coming out undetected. 
 
Broader Commodities & Inflation 
 
Do you see elevated commodity prices including non-energy markets like agriculture and metals leading to a structurally 
higher inflation environment?  
 
No, we do not believe elevated commodity prices lead to structurally higher inflation in the current environment. 
Higher energy prices act like a tax on the economy in that they slow demand. Unless accompanied by very loose 
monetary policy (and real rates are rising, not falling), the more likely outcome is deflationary pressure, not inflation. 
This is the same logic that applies to tariffs –  they are a tax, and taxes are not inflationary in the structural sense.  
 
How has increased volatility affected your view on Alcoa? 
 
Alcoa is more nuanced. Supply has been semi-permanently removed from the market due to the war, which is a 
bullish structural setup. But a deteriorating demand environment driven by high oil prices would be a headwind, and 
we are currently above the cost curve, which limits downside protection. If the war ends with supply offline for 12 to 
18 months, Alcoa (and copper more broadly) should perform very well. An extended conflict paired with a severe 
recession would be the bad outcome here.

How are you thinking about the consumer discretionary space (particularly RV names) in a higher oil price environment?  
 
Any exposure we have to the consumer discretionary space is in cyclicals like RVs. They would be affected by higher 
oil prices, but more so by higher interest rates. The two RV companies that we own are facing horrible current 
conditions. Volumes were at multi year lows (and we think ~40% below trend and ~20% below replacement due to 
attrition of the RVs), and multiples were at historic lows (and are now back to historic lows). WGO has traded at a 
range of 0.4x to 1.0x P/S. It was at 0.5x P/S at year end 2025, rallied to 0.6x P/S in the beginning of 2026, and now is 
back to 0.4x P/S since the start of the war. If sales come back to trend or replacement, the multiple of sales should at 
least double. Volume recovery plus multiple recovery would get you “plus a lot” for very good companies in an 
industry that has very high ROIC and structural long-term growth.  
 
Opportunity Set & Portfolio Positioning 
 
Where are you finding the most asymmetry in market pricing today? What areas of the cyclical tracker are at peak or 
trough, and how are you expressing those views in terms of gross and net exposure? Where do you see the best 
risk/reward looking forward including areas like trucking and housing/building products that appear to have low 
current exposure? 
 
We see the most asymmetry in US-based cyclicals that were at trough, rallied and then re-troughed in March. Many of 
these names had rallied in January and early February as PMIs crossed above 50 for the first time in three years, then 
gave it all back in March as the macro environment deteriorated. Part of why March was painful for us was that these 
positions were running relatively unhedged as we believed we were at that inflection point in the US cycle and 
positioned accordingly. They are now back at trough (and some at distressed levels), and that is where we have been 
adding risk: trucking, housing, and building products, in particular. The math on those names at current levels is 
compelling, and we are expressing that view through increasing nets to things at trough (the cyclical tracker tells us 
what to do).  
 
Risk Management & Team Through Volatility 
 
In an environment where sector and thematic moves have been particularly violent and correlated, how are you 
managing gross and net exposure?  
Gross and net management in a highly-correlated, volatile tape comes back to process. When everything is moving 
together, the temptation is to react to price rather than fundamentals, and that is exactly when the discipline of our "if 
ands" framework matters most. We slow down, run the scenarios, and ask what would have to be true for the thesis to 
be wrong. That is how we keep the team anchored. 
 
The historical parallel we've drawn on most in this environment is COVID, specifically the lesson around patience. In 
2020, we took on a lot of risk ahead of the vaccine approval and could have waited, sized more conservatively, and 
still made nearly the same return. That experience is very much present in how we're thinking about positioning 
through the current dislocation. We know what the "what ifs" look like. Our job now is to wait for the facts to be 
confirmed before we size aggressively, and not to let the noise of violent moves override the math.  
 
How do you keep a relatively young team anchored to the math and models during periods of extreme market dislocation, 
and are there historical parallels you've drawn on to frame the current environment?  
 
It’s true that we have a young team, but Aaron and Chase have now been through COVID and Liberation Day, and I feel 
that they (and I) are getting better at this every week. It’s not quite dog years, but the learning is fast at Maple Rock –  
we are all hands on and work closely together. I am genuinely grateful for this “young” team’s energy and curiosity. It 
keeps me feeling young and motivated to teach and to keep improving myself.

What has worked well in the portfolio during the drawdown?  
On the long side, NAND and HDD memory have worked the best. Our cyclical long exposure performed horribly, and 
some of cyclical shorts directly benefited from the war and ripped in our face. The first week of this was painful. That 
week we covered most, if not all, of the shorts that ran against us and let our longs go down without doing much 
(effectively degrossing). We then added back longs at trough levels, taking gross and net higher.  
Are there any mistakes or omissions made in hindsight with respect to conflicts abroad?  
The biggest mistake we made in hindsight was ignoring the fact that two battlegroups moved within striking distance 
of Iran. The oil market was somewhat pricing it in (oil up ~15% in first two months of the year, with implied vol for 
oil futures up from ~30% to 40%), but we ignored it and stuck to our S&D. What usually happens when “something 
bad or crazy is happening” is our cyclical shorts go down with our cyclical longs and we are protected. During this 
“something crazy happening” scenario, that wasn’t the case. We should have realized the risk we were taking going 
into this event was way too high.   
How do you think about the right time to adjust S&D math for markets like oil (i.e. not overreact to short term / potential 
things normalize in a few months)? In other words, is there a point where it makes sense to adjust mid -cycle oil price 
assumptions and where you might be comfortable adding more longs and shorts?  
 
Mid-cycle oil could absolutely be adjusted. We are just not sure which way yet. WHAT IF Iran gives up nuclear 
ambitions, the “new” regime promises to play nice, and sanctions go away? Production out of Iran and Venezuela 
could ramp meaningfully, and Russia could easily ramp without sanctions on top of tha t. WHAT IF this war turns 
south again? You take away Middle East supply growth and add a security risk premium . We think you have to take 
mid-cycle oil higher. 
It would be very hard for us to underwrite to any of this with conviction at this time, which is exactly why it is better 
to do other things that are easier! We will let the facts come to us. When the math is clear enough to size, we will size. 
Until then, we stay disciplined and patient.
