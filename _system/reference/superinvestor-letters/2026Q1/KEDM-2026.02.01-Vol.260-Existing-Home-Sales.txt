Housing Update
Let’s go back to the housing market of 2021. A year into Covid, we were still stuck at home flattening the 
curve (or at least flattening our couch cushions). Businesses closed, and people lost their jobs. Whoever 
was fortunate enough to keep their job was told to work from home. To keep us from protesting on the 
street, we were all given stimmies, which most degenerates gambled into the hottest SPAC. Surprisingly, it 
worked. We were all flush with cash and bored out of our minds.
In addition, J Pow took interest rates to 0%, thereby kick-starting the hottest housing market since the 
GFC. With mortgage rates at 2.7%, we could suddenly afford our wives’ dream house. And even if you 
didn’t buy a bigger house, spending so much time at home made you aware of all the squeaky flooring, 
the decking that needed upgrading, the windows that were beginning to look old, or the doors that didn’t 
sufficiently block the noise made by your children playing around the house while you were trying to mute 
them during a Zoom call.
As you’ll remember, we were big housing bulls at KEDM. We were invested across the supply chain: 
distributors of building materials such as Builders FirstSource (BLDR), manufacturers of windows such as 
Cornerstone, or siding and OSB such as Louisiana Pacific (LPX). Basically, we owned anything that could be 
nailed to a house and marked up.

Looking at the graph for US housing starts (single fam + multi-fam), the market saw a massive supply gap in 
the decade following the GFC. The market had been underbuilt by 3 million to 5 million houses, depending 
on who you listened to. Population growth meant a steady demand of 1.6 million new houses per year, 
plus whatever was necessary to fill the supply gap.
Source: Koyfin
Rapid inflation, followed by the 2022 rate-hike cycle, spoiled the party. Yes, there were plenty of people 
willing to move out of their parents’ basement, but the issue was affordability. Mortgage rates peaked 
just over 8% in 2023. Landlords could expect to make a 3-4% net yield from renting out their house or 
apartment, while the Cost of Debt was a good 3-4% higher. Renting made financial sense, as long as you 
could convince your wife to put off the dream house for another couple of years.
Figure: 30-year mortgage rates (white) versus US 10-year government yields (orange).

Look, Davos was supposed to be the moment where Trump pumped the US housing market, before 
everyone got carried away and turned it into a week to make fun of the Europeans. Housing affordability 
is clearly a priority going into the mid-term elections. But let’s look at what Trump can actually do.
Replacing Powell with a strawman who will quickly lower the Fed Funds Rate affects short-term rates, 
while mortgages are priced off longer-term rates. The spread between government bonds and mortgages 
is easier to influence simply by increasing demand. While Trump does not (yet) control the Fed’s purchasing 
of mortgage-backed securities, he is clearly trying to get the same result by instructing Fannie and Freddie 
to buy $200b worth of MBS. Judging by the recent dip in spreads, it is having some effect.
Is this enough to re-awaken the housing market? We wouldn’t be so sure. Housing turns slowly like a 
cruise ship, not like a meme stock. It takes homeowners a while to be realistic about their home’s price 
because Zillow once told them it was worth more.

The easier call is that existing home sales have bottomed and are normalizing. Look, if you’re locked into 
a 3% mortgage and your wife wants to buy a much larger, nicer house that you’ll have to take out a 
6.5% mortgage for, you’ll probably be able to tell her off for a couple of years. But that argument only 
works for so long. Furthermore, as we go further into this downturn, the number of people with 3% 
mortgages declines. Just this January, the number of people with a >6% mortgage exceeded those with a 
<3% mortgage for the first time since 2021. 
Source: Realtor.com, FHFA National Mortgage Database
In a normal year, roughly 5 million people used to buy an existing home. This briefly peaked around 6 
million during Covid, but it has since fallen to 4 million. This has impacted many industries. Mortgage 
originators saw earnings disappear overnight. Mortgage origination is a subject worthy of its own write-up 
someday. For now, fundamentals look good, but it’s hard to argue that Rocket (RKT) or Pennymac (PFSI) 
aren’t pricing in at least some recovery yet (even after PFSI blowing up this Friday).
Source: Koyfin

But there are more industries impacted. How about furniture? Sales of mattresses are below 2009 levels, 
which is not bullish for domestic bliss. Similar to houses, you can only postpone the purchase of a mattress 
for so long. The number of companies in the furniture supply chain is enormous: Leggett & Platt (LEG), 
Somnigroup (SGI), Sleep Number (SNBR), just to name a few. They all come with their own problems and 
limitations. But maybe the macro tailwind will be strong enough to offset the fact that manufacturing 
furniture in the US is a low-value-add business?
How about real estate brokerage? Don’t get us started on the excessive commissions they charge, but at 
the end of the day, these are a play on existing home sales. But you can get as diverse as you like. Self-
storage and U-Haul trucks have had a tough 3 years. How about their supply chain: Janus Int (JBI)? The 
possibilities are endless.
As is always the case in finance, calling the exact bottom is hard, but housing is clearly a top priority for the 
Trump administration, and even without an immediate recovery, existing home sales seem to be slowly 
recovering back to the 5 million range.

Spin Notes:
• Flagging Versant (VSNT) and Comcast’s recent spin. The stock is down roughly 30% since the 
spin, on classic spin dynamics, a ‘melting ice-cube’ narrative, but (we think) mainly due to 
index selling (it isn’t in the S&P500). They own CNBC and some networks. Yes, it’s indeed a 
melting ice-cube, but with a $4.7bn market cap, roughly $2bn net debt, and $1.0-1.5bn free 
cash flow generation per year, the ‘bet’ here is that you will get your money back in 5-6 years. 
Imagine if FCF stabilizes.
• International Paper (IP) decided to break up and spin off its European packaging business . 
This is just hilarious. This looks like a badly timed, high-risk move (and the market clearly 
agrees), piling a complex separation on top of an ongoing cost-out while also integrating assets 
that are being carved out. Management probably feels compelled to do it because Europe is 
structurally weak, fragmented, and a drag on the portfolio, even though the company basically 
bought the assets just a short while ago. It really looks like they’re selling at the bottom after 
buying at the start of a down-cycle. Just the wrong moment to do a spin. But what is bad for IP 
might be good for us. Let’s see what the spin action will be. Tbd.
• The Hexagon AB (HEXAB SS)  spin-off is on track for H1 2026 with an investor day in March. 
The SpinCo, which will be named Octave, will be listed on the NYSE as a pure-play, high-growth 
SaaS, while the RemainCo will retain its industrial measurement and autonomous technology 
activities. We love cross-border spin-offs and will be keeping an eye on this one.
• Becton, Dickinson (BDX) will spin its Biosciences & Diagnostics division with a Feb 5 record date 
and immediately merge the SpinCo into Waters via a Reverse Morris Trust. BDX shareholders 
will retain their BDX shares and receive Waters stock, representing roughly 39% of the combined 
company, while BDX will receive approximately $4bn in cash. Activist Starboard is involved.

Investor Day Notes:
• Brookdale (BKD) hosted its investor day, prior to which it announced its 2026 guidance. The 
event focused on the attractive backdrop and on how their renewed focus on occupancy should 
drive profitability. The latter point seems so obvious that it’s surprising how long the previous 
management team resisted. Nothing revolutionary, but the market is slowly beginning to view 
this as a compounder rather than a distressed asset play.
• Western Digital (WDC), a memory HDD player, is holding an innovation day on Feb 3. Memory 
has been on a tear, and it’s hard to imagine today that WDC was trading at a single-digit 
multiple on below normalized margins only 7 months ago.

IPO Notes:
• Last week’s KEDM highlighted IPOs, so it’s a good moment to flag the recent listing of 
Czechoslovak Group (CSG) on Euronext Amsterdam. CSG is a (very) rapidly growing defense 
company. Think artillery, armored vehicles, radars, small-caliber ammo, etc. The shares 
popped over 30% on IPO, with CSG now sporting a €33.6bn market cap. Despite the move, we 
estimate the company is trading at ~17-19x forward EBIT, with EBIT growing 20%+. Pretty much 
all European peers are trading in the 20-40x range, and many are smaller and not growing 
as rapidly. And with Europe waking up on its defense spending, we easily see 10+ years of 
tailwinds from increasing budgets. 
• We almost feel like apologizing for writing this up, but an interesting trade is an interesting trade. 
SpaceX is now rumored to IPO at a $1.5tn ‘valuation’ , probably somewhere in the summer. 
And this number keeps going up. It’s LaLaLand, we know. But that’s why it’s interesting. Why 
couldn’t this become $3tn, or $10tn on a limited free float?? It’s insanity, but we’re seeing 
a lot of that today. And we’re here to flag potentially interesting trades, even if they don’t 
make sense on a fundamental level. So, who owns SpaceX? Of course, Cathie Wood. ARK 
Venture Fund (ARKVX) owns a TON, and the price doesn’t seem to track the SpaceX prices 
we’re reading about in the markets. And if you prefer more liquid positions, there’s always AST 
SpaceMobile (ASTS), which owns ~3%, basically covering its entire market cap.
Bankruptcy Notes:
• Both FAT Brands (FAT) and Twin Hospitality (TWNP)  (the RemainCo and SpinCo of the Jan 
2025 spin-off, respectively) filed for Chapter 11 this week. Restaurants are a tough business, 
and consumers are tapped out. Remember TGI Fridays and Hooters?

Newsletter Short Notes:
• We always thought that the bull case for Carvana (CVNA) was that it was a total fraud. Gotham’s 
short report apparently still came as a surprise to the market, sending shares down 20%. They 
argue that CVNA’s earnings were heavily dependent on gain-on-sale profits from transactions 
with related-party DriveTime, which is highly levered. Gotham accessed DT’s financials through 
a FOIA request. Similar allegations were previously made by both Hindenburg and Kerrisdale.
13D and Activist Notes:
• Zennor AM has been buying Appier Group (4180.JP) en masse since the big stock correction 
back in October. They’re now at roughly 5%, and we don’t see them stopping anytime soon. 
Appier is an AI SaaS company focused on AdTech and MarTech solutions. Growth has been 
more than decent, with operating leverage and cash flow generation now kicking in as well. 
The shares corrected on slightly lower guidance and AI fears. The latter might be exaggerated; 
on the contrary, we imagine they are at the forefront of the AI trend. ~10x forward EV/EBITDA 
for 30%+ EBITDA growth p.a. over the medium term doesn’t seem much. We know what 
Zennor thinks.
• Take a look at all the buying over at Foster Electric (6794.JP). Asset Value Investors recently 
disclosed a ~5% position, and the number one shareholder, Axium Capital, continues to add 
as well (now at ~16%). Foster is having a challenging 2026 (FY March) with some pressure on 
the topline. But the company has been managing its margin and cash flow generation pretty 
well. It also has a strong balance sheet with roughly 80% of its market cap in current assets, net 
of total liabilities. Trading <1.5x forward EV/EBITDA net of the latter. We get why they like it.

• The fight is back on at CoStar (CSGP). Third Point is back at it , hammering the company’s 
push into residential real estate (through Homes.com). TP mentions misallocation of capital, 
ignored agreed-upon reforms, and calling the CEO an empire builder. The standstill agreement 
has now expired, and TP will push for new directors and a refocus on its strong commercial real 
estate franchise, while exploring a sale (or shutdown) of Homes.com. CoStar fired back, saying 
TP misunderstands the strategy and that Homes.com is essential to its broader data platform. 
The fight is causing volatility in the shares, which can always be good for a trade.
• Irenic Cap is targeting SPS Commerce (SPSC), pushing them to do a strategic review, i.e., sell 
the company. SPS had a horrible 2025, with loads of profit warnings. Suppliers absorbed 
higher tariff costs rather than passing them through to retailers, which drove tighter cost 
scrutiny and increased down-sell pressure. On the customer side, smaller accounts shut off  
trading-partner connections to save money or because they had no recent order activity, 
which directly hit the company’s pricing model. Several large enablement campaigns tied to 
retail relationship-management programs were also pushed into 2026. In short, not good. But 
growth has been decent (though largely due to acquisitions), and margins and FCF generation 
remain strong, all of which is expected to continue to improve. There are quite a few SBCs, but 
not many open market sales. Meanwhile, the shares are down 50%, and SPS is buying back 
stock en masse.
Merger Notes:
• UK food wholesaler Kitwave (KITW.L) announced an acquisition proposal from OEP Capital 
Advisers at 295 pence in cash. The premium was 33.5% and valued the company at ~10x TTM 
FCF, but the announcement came concurrent with strong interim results, which would have 
likely pushed prices higher regardless. Some long-term shareholders are unhappy with the 
deal, and we wouldn’t be surprised to see heavy resistance, potentially leading to a price 
increase.
• Billionaire Křetínský is making a (friendly) move to take control of Fnac Darty (FNAC). He looks 
ready to gear up again. To note that he also owns a roughly 29% in Dutch postal legacy operator 
PostNL (PNL) and 25% in French parcel lockers solutions provider Quadient (QDT). We know 
Křetínský likes 100%; perhaps these two are next on the list?
• Spire (SPI) recently confirmed that it is in talks with Bridgepoint and Triton regarding a takeover. 
Both have experience in healthcare and the NHS, and certainly the firepower to buy the whole 
business (not just strip out the property, which regularly came up with Spire). While shares 
were up on the news, they’re not even back at the levels of a few months ago, when Spire 
came out with a rather large profit warning. A few large shareholders own almost 50%. Fwiw, 
analysts covering the stock mention ‘at least 300p’ to be a serious offer, which would still be 
roughly 50% upside.

Strategic Alternatives Notes:
• Strong pressure from its main shareholder, Kawa, pushed Orion Properties (ONL)  to launch 
a strategic review process to unlock value, including a sale. Orion also struck a cooperation 
agreement with Kawa, which will withdraw its notice to nominate five directors. Pretty clear 
what’s going on here. Orion is trading at 0.2x BV; clearly, the market is not believing the value 
reflected in the books, but this also creates the opportunity.

launched a full strategic alternatives process to unlock value that it says isn’t reflected in the

much growth priced in, which makes sense given the headwinds, but this is a cyclical industry. 
And a decent selling price should reflect that.

Privatization Notes:
• In terms of rummaging through the dirt, we’ve added Uniper (UN0.DE) to the watchlist. Still 
very much seen as a state-owned rescued zombie company (for all the good reasons), but 
they are steadily moving more into core European energy infra. Cold winters keep reminding

Germany is required to sell its stake under the EU rescue terms, making reprivatization the 
real catalyst. Add a new gas-to-hydrogen plant with contracted returns, and here is some good

Tender Offer Notes:
• Lands’ End (LE) recently pulled off an interesting move. The company agreed to put its brand 
IP into a joint venture with WHP Global in exchange for $300m in cash , which will let it wipe 
out its term loan and meaningfully de-risk the company. WHP will handle global licensing and 
brand expansion, while Lands’ End continues to run the core DTC and B2B business. Lands’ End 
will own 50% of the JV, with the optionality to swap that stake into equity in WHP down the 
line. WHP plans to launch a $100m tender offer. WHP Global will commence a tender offer for 
up to $100 million of Lands’ End shares at $45 each (compared to roughly $18 today).
Other Interesting ED Action (but that doesn’t have a separate monitor):
• Broadcaster STV (STVG UK) is having a tough time. Resilient top-line revenues, in large part due 
to acquisitions, and continued margin pressure. The advertising market remained soft, with ad 
revenues down around 10% and profits well below last year’s levels. Management’s message 
has basically been “hold on until 2026.” Sure. As a result, the share price got clobbered, given 
little visibility, and the market lost patience. But this one might be interesting this year. The 
FIFA World Cup will be a major catalyst for advertising. STV is also seriously cutting costs to 
protect margins. In short, 2025 was a bad year driven by macro pressures, but the company is 
positioning itself for a cyclical rebound, with several major upcoming triggers.
• The Brazilian stock market is booming, so this one might be worth a look. Companhia 
Siderúrgica Nacional (CSN)  is trying to sell its steel operations . The idea is to shift capital 
toward mining and infrastructure, which are seen as more stable and higher -margin, while 
steel remains under pressure from imports. CSN is also planning to sell control of its cement 
arm and a minority stake in infrastructure assets. The company is reported to be looking at 
R$15-18bn in asset sales. That’s quite a bit on a roughly R$14bn MC and to R$52bn EV. Add to 
that that the remaining business will probably deserve a higher multiple.
