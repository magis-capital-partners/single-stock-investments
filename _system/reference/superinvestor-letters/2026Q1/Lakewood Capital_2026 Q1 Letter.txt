While Verisure’s business has demonstrated consistent growth over decades, some 
investors appear concerned that AI -enabled technologies may pose a long-term threat.  
We think this view does not fully appreciate the stickiness of Verisure’s business or the 
way in which it has navigated past concerns.  First, Verisure has continued to grow 
uninterrupted for over a decade despite worries that “do-it-yourself” offerings from tech 
giants like Amazon and Google would erode its market share .  Moreover, in many of its 
core markets, regulations require alarm systems to be professionally installed and 
monitored, creating a meaningful barrier to entry for new competitors lacking Verisure’s 
12,000-person sales and installation workforce.  Rather than being disrupted by AI, we 
expect Verisure to benefit from the implementation of AI, potentially enhancing the 
company’s ability to filter false alarms, reduce costly guard dispatches, and improve both 
customer service and marketing efficiency.  Over time, these improvements should 
strengthen unit economics and further widen Verisure’s competitive moat.

FDJ United (Long) 
 
Back in our 2019  annual letter , w e discussed our investment in FDJ, the monopoly 
operator of France’s national lottery system, following its IPO.   At the time, we believed 
the market underestimated a significant margin expansion opportunity and the company’s 
strong, consistent cash generation .  Over the next several quarters, the business 
substantially outperformed expectations , and we subsequently exited the position 
following a more than 150% gain in the share price a little more than 18 months after the 
IPO.  Since then, the core French lottery business has continued to perform well, but the 
stock has traded poorly following the company’s late 2024  acquisition of gaming 
operator K indred for €2.5 billion, which has meaningfully underperformed due to 
regulatory challenges in several European countries.   With these acquired assets now 
stabilizing at a lower level and the French lottery business continuing to excel, we think 
the current valuation is highly attractive with a 10% free cash flow yield and an 8% 
dividend yield, and we have reacquired a position in the stock. 
 
We believe the discounted valuation is the result of an outsized focus on the acquired 
Kindred business, which repre sents less than 20% of company earnings.  Kindred 
operates online betting and gaming markets across Europe , and since the acquisition, 
many governments increased the level of gaming taxes and instituted other regulations 
that have pressured profits across the online gaming industry.   Since 2024, gaming tax 
rates were increased in Sweden, the Netherlands, Romania, and the United Kingdom, and 
furthermore, the Netherlands and Belgium add ed regulations meant to promote safer 
gambling, including limits on player deposits and reductions in marketing bonuses for 
new accounts.   As a result, we estimate that Kindred’s EBITDA contribution will fall 
30% below its 2024 levels.  Although predicting the direction of future regulations is 
difficult, many players have since moved to unregulated black markets, which leads to 
both worse oversight and lower tax revenue , making it less likely that governments will 
implement another round of broad tax increases in the near future.

Amrize (Long) 
 
Last summer, Swi ss building materials company Holcim spun out its North American 
business into a new publicly -traded company called Amrize.  As Amrize’s stock 
languished in the immediate aftermath of its spin-off, the fund established a position at an 
attractive valuation relative to peers and its underlying business quality.  Since 2018, the 
legacy Amrize business, while part of Holcim, grew annual revenues and operating 
income by 4% and 9%, respectively, benefitting from a portfolio of scaled assets with 
strong pricing power.   Despite this impressive historical performance, including the 
company’s excellent cash conversion and history of steady margin expansion, Amrize 
continues to trade at a sizable discount to peers, which we think largely reflects the 
recency of the spin and the company’s prior history as a European -listed stock.  As the 
business continues to deliver solid results and execute on its meaningful growth 
opportunities, we believe the company’s valuation can improve , and we see significant 
upside in the shares.  The management team appears to share our view , as the CEO has 
purchased $61 million of stock on the open market since the spin and eight other 
executives and directors have purchased another nearly $4 million collectively. 
 
Roughly 80% of Amrize’s EBITDA is generated from its Building Materials segment, 
which primarily supplies cement and aggregates.   Pricing power is high with average 
annual price increases over the past decade of nearly 5 % for cement and 6% for 
aggregates.  Existing cement plants benefit from significant barriers to entry, most 
notably stringent regulatory and permitting limitations given the carbon -intensive nature 
of cement production.   While several existing cement plants have increased production 
capacity in recent years through brownfield expansions, the U.S. has not built a new 
cement plant since 2009.  At the same time, there is no viable substitute for cement as the

EBITDA multiple, Amrize stock would be worth roughly $90 per share in two years 
inclusive of dividends, nearly 50% higher than the current price. 
 
RadNet (Short) 
 
Last year, the fund initiated a short position in RadNet, the largest operator of outpatient 
imaging centers with more than 400 facilities across eight states.  RadNet also operates a 
small artificial intelligence division (5% of EBITDA) that purports to develop radiology 
software solutions, which has been pieced together through a series of acquisitions over 
the last few years.  The prevailing market narrative positions RadNet as an AI winner, 
benefiting from enthusiasm around generative AI and healthcare technology, with shares 
trading at 40x our estimate of operating income.  We believe this optimism is misplaced .  
The core imaging center business, which generates nearly all of the company’s revenue 
and earnings , is structurally weak and generates a lackluster return on capital , and 
RadNet’s AI offerings are limited in differentiation and scale.

RadNet’s core imaging center business, accounting for more than 95% of revenue and 
earnings, is an undifferentiated, capital -intensive business characterized by robust 
competition, moderate returns on invested capital , and poor free cash flow conversion.  
The company’s 400+ facilities compete with more than 6,000 imaging centers nationwide 
offering largely commoditized services that require few unique capabilities to operate.  
Volume is heavily dependent on referrals from large health systems, many of which 
operate their own imaging centers and prioritize internal capacity.  Each facility requires 
a substantial investment in MRI and CT equipment with finite useful lives and significant 
maintenance costs and must rely on radiologists who are in chronic short supply and 
possess meaningful bargaining power with providers.  Unsurprisingly, RadNet’s returns 
have been persistently mediocre with returns on invested capital of less than 15% in 
2025.  Despite these modest returns, RadNet shares trade at nearly 8x tangible invested 
capital.  Cash generation has also been anemic as the company has generated cumulative 
free cash flow of less than $300 million over the last decade versus its current market 
capitalization of $4.5 billion. 
 
While bulls often point to RadNet’s headline revenue growth (roughly 10% annually 
since 2015), this growth has been materially supported by aggressive acquisition activity 
and ongoing growth capex of questionable economic return.  Even the company’s 
reported same -center revenue growth, which has averaged 6% per year since 2019, is 
misleading.  Management excludes underperforming non -consolidated joint ventures 
from the calculation and routinely removes weak facilities from the same -center base by 
merging or restructuring dozens of centers each year.   Between 2020 and 2024, RadNet 
opened 74 centers, but closed 97 centers.  Curiously, management stopped disclosing 
gross center openings and closings in 2025.  
 
Much of the market’s enthusiasm centers on RadNet’s AI segment, which management 
claims will transform radiology workflows and generate a new, high -margin licensing 
revenue stream.  We believe this narrative is dramatically overstated.  The AI segment 
contributes less than 4% of total revenue and operates at a meaningful loss, though these 
losses are obscured by heavily adjusted non -GAAP disclosures.  Moreover, we estimate 
that roughly half of the segment’s reported revenue is not AI -related at all.  Instead, 
RadNet includes sales of its legacy back -office radiology software, used primarily for 
administrative coordination and image archiving, which is largely consumed internally 
and has seen limited third -party adoption in the face of superior offerings from GE and 
Philips.  The remaining AI products consist mainly of several cancer screening 
technologies acquired over the last couple of years for approximately $270 million, 
which now compete against a growing cohort of well -funded startups with significantly 
stronger technical talent.  Notably, more than 40% of AI segment revenue comes from 
intercompany sales to RadNet itself. 
 
As investors reassess the sustainability of the AI narrative and refocus on the economics 
of the core imaging center business, we believe RadNet’s valuation could compress 
materially.  At 10x heavily adjusted EBITDA, still a premium to the high -single-digit 
multiples at which the company traded historically and where hospitals trade, RadNet 
would be worth approximately $35  per share, implying over 40% downside from current

GFL Environmental (Short) 
 
Over the years , we have had success shorting highly-valued roll-ups, as investors often 
mistake the acquisition-driven revenue and profit growth for underlying value creation.  
We particularly like hunting for this set-up in industries that generally command elevated 
valuation multiples, as companies can use roll -up strategies to buy lower -quality 
businesses at optically lower valuations and hope the public markets will value those 
earnings at the same multiples as higher quality peers.   We believe GFL Environmental 
fits this mold perfectly, as the company increased its debt load to “diworsify” its business 
over the last few years within the generally well -regarded waste management space.  We 
see substantial downside to shares as the lack of fundamental value creation becomes 
increasingly obvious over time. 
 
Founded in Ontario in 2007, GFL consolidated the solid and liquid waste markets  in 
Canada, building an enviable collection of interconnected assets across the nation .  
Similar to Waste Management or Republic Services in the U.S., we believe these 
Canadian assets generally have the pricing power and durability that come with high 
market share and unique landfill access.   
 
However, following its success in Canada, GFL pushed south into the much-larger, more-
mature, and more -competitive U.S. market.  Using debt, private equity, and eventually 
IPO proceeds, GFL went on a massive acquisition spree, buying disparate assets in 
markets spanning from Michigan to Texas, growing revenues from C$3.5 billion in 2019 
to C$6.6 billion in 2025 and adjusted EBITDA from C$825 million to C$2.0 billion over 
that timeframe.  Despite this enormous revenue and EBITDA growth, we believe GFL’s 
acquisition binge has created minimal value for shareholders.  GFL spent a net amount of 
roughly C$4 billion on M&A and growth capital expenditures to increase underlying 
operating profit by C$420 million since 2019, representing a meager 8% post-tax return 
on its invested capital.  Another way to view this limited value creation: annual free cash 
flow has been flat for the last five years despite the company doubling adjusted EBITDA, 
with GFL generating just 10% of its current market capitalization in cumulative adjusted 
free cash flow over the last six years.  
 
As is often the case with roll-up strategies, we believe this stark disconnect between 
growth and value creation ties back to the low quality of GFL’s acquired assets.   Our 
research indicates that many of the acquired U.S. businesses are small waste hauling 
companies ( i.e., “a few guys and some trucks”) rather than the scaled landfill-backed 
hauling and distribution assets owned by its U.S. peers.  This translates to higher capex, 
lower pricing power, and lower return on invested capital  over time.  We calculate GFL 
generated a 9% after-tax return on invested capital in 2025 versus larger peers in the high 
teens.

figures or cash generation.  However, we find several of these adjustments highly

Kratos Defense & Security Solutions (Short) 
 
In recent quarters, defense drones have become one of the latest market crazes as the 
Department of War declared it will unleash “drone dominance.” Earlier this year, we 
initiated a short position in Kratos Defense & Security Solutions , a U.S. -based defense 
technology company with exposure to hypersonic missile components, solid rocket 
motors, unmanned systems, and satellite communications, among other areas.  At the 
time, the company’s enterprise value had reached roughly $20 billion, reflecting the 
market’s unbridled enthusiasm for a company that has generated just $20 million of 
average annual EBIT over the past decade.  Despite the company’s underwhelming 
historical financial performance, investors are valuing the stock at a current multiple of 
roughly 100x EBITDA while established prime defense contractors with stronger product 
portfolios and better cash generation trad e at more reasonable mid -teens EBITDA 
multiples. 
 
We view Kratos as a classic “story stock,” where the narrative surrounding a new era of 
warfare has overshadowed the company’s underlying economics and more modest 
growth prospects.  Although management has guided to high -teens to low -20s revenue 
growth through 2027, sell -side expectations increasingly reflect “blue-sky” scenarios in 
which revenue expands 3 x to 4x to approximately $5 billion by 2030.  While many of 
these analyses highlight potential scaling of the company’s Valkyrie tactical drone 
platform and the hypersonics business, it appears analysts are simply trying to invent 
potential financial projections that could possibly justify the company’s stretched 
valuation.  By contrast, we believe a closer examination of these programs suggests such 
outcomes are unlikely. 
 
The company’s Valkyrie drone program first flew back in 2019 , and, despite years of 
management promotion and anticipated contract wins, we estimate the program is 
currently generating less than $50 million in annual revenues, implying fewer than five 
units delivered annually.  While Kratos recently announced a Valkyrie -related contract 
with the U.S. Marine Corps , the reality of this “win” is somewhat less impressive, as 
Northrop Grumman is the actual prime contractor on the program and Kratos  is simply 
serving as a subcontractor.  Further, the cumulative revenue of this program is likely to 
be around $120 million over several years, not particularly material in the context of a

cumulative free cash flow of approximately $250 million, while its share count has 
increased 23-fold.  The company’s long -term financial profile has more in common with 
a garden variety stock promotion than it does with the next big defense contractor.
