namely meaningful concentration in nicotine pouches, for profit education, housing and building

long exposure and value arbitrage represented 31.77%. By March 31, 2026, those figures had moved 
to 48.44% and 48.68%, respectively.

Throughout most of 2025, TPB ran an accelerated version of its old Stoker's playbook, using regional 
salespeople to gain distribution for FRE in smaller chains. To the extent the company won placement

By paying for premium placement and a higher number of facings in large retail chains, TPB can  
increase its brand visibility and enhance the necessity that smaller retail chains that don’t currently

This is important because according to the National Association of Convenience Store Owners

is achieved in the large chains, TPB will be able to accelerate its penetration of the smaller operator 
stores where TPB can earn much higher margins with no slotting fees or concessions paid.

fees in a high-volume nicotine pouch store. If they pay for six slots, that totals $1,200 in slotting fees.

Market Commentary 
 
The defining feature of the current macro backdrop is no longer simply the path of inflation, Fed 
policy, or even AI related capital spending , though those remain material considerations, but the 
closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the physical inventory math that flows from it. In our view, the 
oil market has likely passed a tipping point, with a disruption on the order of 11 to 13 million barrels 
per day. This can either be compensated for through inventory draws or outright demand destruction. 
For certain technical reasons, given the size of the disruption we believe that inventory will be drawn 
down to their limit and ultimately demand destruction will have to occur through market pricing. That 
is a materially different setup from the more familiar  conflict pattern in which crude merely trades

The physical market appears to have moved beyond the first buffer  of excess oil on the water , with

The second point, and the one we think the market still underappreciates, is that reopening the Strait 
would not immediately normalize the system. Even in a relatively benign scenario, tanker transit times, 
offloading delays, vessel turnaround, and the nee d to empty Middle Eastern onshore storage before 
production can fully restart imply a much longer healing process. Put differently, the closure creates a 
lagged inventory disruption, not merely a spot price problem. Once enough barrels fail to arrive at 
end markets, the resulting draws continue well after the headline event itself begins to resolve. 
 
Global crude exports are already running materially lower, and the apparent surplus outside China 
looks increasingly illusory. Asia is particularly vulnerable because their refineries are especially 
dependent upon Middle East production that flows through the strait, and reduced product imports 
and refinery throughput can create product scarcity before crude inventories are fully exhausted. 
Europe faces a similar issue but on a lag. In that environment, the market can move from skepticism 
to urgency very qu ickly once physical inventory declines, especially in products like jet fuel.  The tail 
outcomes of an extended closure are so severe and binary  that the market may struggle to fully 
discount them given the incentives of almost all parties, including Iran’s main political ally China, are 
to find a resolution. 
 
America enters this episode from a stronger domestic position than prior oil shocks, with far higher 
production relative to consumption and a much smaller share of household income devoted to 
gasoline. Even so, the United States is not insulated from global pricing, and it remains central to 
balancing the system through exports, inventory releases, and refinery utilization. If commercial crude 
stocks move toward operational minimums later this year, policy responses that today seem remote, 
including tighter controls on product or crude exports, become easier to imagine.

For markets, the implication is straightforward. The base case may not be a quick return to the benign 
setup many investors expected coming into the year. Even a deescalation wouldn’t  restore the prior 
equilibrium because time, inventories, and shipping capacity cannot be recovered instantly. At best, it 
would leave the world with lower inventories, a more fragile energy supply chain , and a higher

in 2020 was 9 million barrels a day. The longer this event persists the more likely that a similar amount 
of demand destruction will have to occur to balance the market. The question is not whether we will 
see disruptions but how severe they will be and how quickly they are ameliorated.

A Step Change in Visibility 
Historically, subsystem suppliers have had 6 months of order visibility at best. This leads to over -
ordering, and increasing WFE intensity during upcycles, but severe underperformance during 
downcycles as channel inventory from the over -ordering gets burne d off. It’s now clear that the 
subsystem cycle bottomed in Q4 2025 and is now in what may prove to be its largest upcycle yet. 
Recent indications from both the subsystem suppliers and their semi -cap customers suggest an 
unprecedented surge in demand and expansion in backlog.  
 
During ICHR’s Q4 2025 earnings call on 2/9/2026, their CEO noted that orders are “very solid in 
the second half compared to what you would normally see walking into the year” and “you hear it 
from our customers directly, they're talking about what they're seeing in 2027. I would say that our 
view on 2027 is very similar to what they say.” A recent UBS note summarizing meetings from a bus 
tour of various semiconductor companies stated that “AMAT/LRCX/KLAC providing multi -year 
BOM visibility to sub-system suppliers to avoid constraining fab build-outs, with AMAT in particular 
pushing suppliers to add capacity and build inventory.” 
 
Semi-cap demand is currently limited by available clean room space at fabs, since semiconductor 
production equipment can only operate in these specialized environments where the air is recirculated 
every 6-12 seconds, among other standards, and fabs take 2-3 years to build. We believe unconstrained 
WFE demand would be over $200 billion, versus our current 2027 estimate around $170 billion. KLA 
Corporation (Nasdaq: KLAC), a producer of semiconductor inspection equipment, recently held an 
investor day in March during which they projected WFE of $200 billion by 2030, an amount that “will 
still massively underserve all the data centers that have been announced.” 
 
There’s also been reporting on an all -hands meeting at Google in November attended by Sundar 
Pichai, where the company’s head of AI included a presentation slide on AI compute demand that 
stated, “we must double every 6 months.” That scenario is unlikely to happen because there isn’t the 
capacity to serve it, but this likely creates a prolonged cycle, accompanied by unusual amounts of order 
visibility for the subsystem suppliers that, while possibly undeserved, could serve to elevate the 
multiples these stocks will trade at. 
WFE Intensity Moving Structurally Higher Especially in Memory 
Moore’s law, an observation that that the number of transistors on a chip doubles every two years, 
began to plateau a few years ago and will soon hit the limits of physics. To obtain increasing 
performance with each subsequent generation of semiconductors,  advanced processes and 
workarounds are necessary that ultimately result in progressively more complicated procedures during 
chip fabrication. This means more equipment is required for the same number of chips produced over 
time, meaning WFE intensity (semi-cap equipment sales as a percentage of semiconductor end-market 
revenue) should secularly grow faster than total semiconductor sales which are in a secular growth 
trend of their own.
