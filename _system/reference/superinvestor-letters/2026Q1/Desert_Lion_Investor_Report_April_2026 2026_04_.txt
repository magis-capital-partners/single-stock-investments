Desert Lion Capital Fund returned approximately +4.8% net for the month of April, compared

I recently had a discussion with one of our investors about rising energy prices, and how that could 
influence emerging market economies and investment return prospects. The topic is likely to be 
at the forefront for some of our other investors and readers, so in this month’s letter I am sharing 
a summary of the key insights flowing from that conversation. 
 
Energy prices and Emerging markets 
With the conflict in the Middle East staying unresolved, energy prices remain elevated. How could 
this affect emerging market economies, and more specifically, return prospects from South 
African equity markets? 
Exploring the negative impacts first: 
• Higher energy prices mean higher input costs.  
• Higher input costs lead to higher inflation, making things less affordable, causing lower 
consumption, which is drag on economic growth. 
• For countries with disciplined monetary policy, higher inflation will be countered with 
“higher than pre-war rate path” rates, which means higher discount rates and lower equity 
valuations. 
Within the category of emerging markets, we find countries that are resource or commodity rich, 
and countries that are less so. We also find countries that have their own energy sources, and 
countries with limited own energy sources that are very reliant on energy imports.  
• Countries that are very reliant on energy imports are more likely to be impacted than those 
that are less reliant on imports.  
• We can stratify import relying countries further by those which are reliant predominantly 
on Middle Eastern energy imports via the Strait of Hormuz, and those that are importing 
energy via alternate sources and routes. 
Then there is the global context: 
• The fragility of geopolitical cooperation and global supply chains have been exposed. 
There’s renewed push and investment to develop own sources of supply and onshore 
production in pursuit of increased self-reliance. Onshoring is the new theme.  
• AI, compute, and datacenters require massive investment in energy generation, storage 
and distribution.

• All of which are constructive for demand for commodities.  
• The commodity complex finds itself at the lower end of the capital cycle, characterized by 
low- or underinvestment for several years. Supply response to higher prices is restricted 
by the physics of developing and bringing new supply online, which will be slow.  
• Developed economies are running unprecedented debt levels. It is increasingly clear that 
the preferred playbook is to run it hot and favor nominal growth.  
• The consequence is gradual debasement of global fiat reserve currencies. Gold’s 
resurgence is testament to global reserves’ reallocation to a hedge against debasement. 
Given the above setup, where gold leads, the rest of the commodity complex eventually follows. 
Therefore, emerging economies that are resource rich are more likely to be in a position to offset 
the impact of energy imports with the surplus from commodity exports.  
Where then does this leave South Africa? 
• SA is an importer of crude and refined products.  
• However, South Africa is a net exporter of coal and also has coal-to-liquid production 
capability.  
• Roughly half to two thirds of SA’s crude oil imports are sourced from African countries 
like Nigeria and Angola. 
• SA is very rich in resources and runs a trade surplus when commodity prices are elevated.  
• The South African central bank is very conservative and follows a disciplined approach to 
monetary policy. Inflation is in check at around 3%. With bond yields around 8.5% and 
the repo rate at 6.75%, positive real rates are high. Discount rates are already high, and 
correspondingly, equity valuations are attractive.

have diversified and less risky access to energy, that follows a disciplined monetary policy 
approach, and has low starting equity valuations. South Africa ticks all the boxes, and the equity 
market is well positioned for superior returns, albeit likely in a volatile fashion.
