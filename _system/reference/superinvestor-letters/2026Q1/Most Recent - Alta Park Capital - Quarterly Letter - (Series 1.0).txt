QUARTERLY COMMENTARY AND MARKET OBSERVATIONS 
The ﬁrst quarter of 2026 was less about direction and more about dispersion and sector rotation, 
further complicated by the emergence of the Iran War late in the quarter. On the surface, the 
numbers look straightforward: the NASDAQ declined approximately -7.1% in Q1. Beneath that, 
however, the composition of returns tells a much more important story.  
The Mag7 cohort declined approximately -11.3% in Q1, materially underperforming NASDAQ, 
down -7.1%, while the equal-weighted NASDAQ (QQQE) declined approximately -3.7%. This a de-
rating of the largest cap stocks, not a broad-based technology unwind.  
The drivers of this shift were relatively clear. NVIDIA (NVDA), after an extended period of strong 
performance, began to face increased scrutiny not around demand itself, but around the second 
derivative of that demand and the returns generated on hyperscaler spend. At the same time, 
Microsoft (MSFT), Alphabet (GOOGL), and Amazon (AMZN) all faced increasing investor focus on 
the magnitude of AI-related capital expenditures relative to near-term monetization. Meta (META), 
while continuing to invest aggressively, was not immune to similar questions around incremental 
returns on capital. Collectively, these dynamics drove most of the underperformance in the 
NASDAQ. This is a dynamic that we anticipate will persist, and the days of “set it and forget it” 
portfolio of the same seven stocks will remain challenged for the foreseeable future. The 
combination of a massive AI-driven platform shift, questions on ROI, and the emergence of new 
disruptors signal a change in equity leadership and an environment, where stock selection and 
dispersion will play more signiﬁcant roles going forward.  We anticipate that ﬁnding new ideas, 
driven by deep research, will surface diƯerentiated ideas, better performance, and alpha in the 
short to medium term. 
The risk of AI-driven disruption was the most important theme in Q1. This narrative had taken hold 
in 2025, as the market had already begun to rotate out of software and into semis, but this move 
accelerated sharply in Q1 2026, driven largely by the rapid evolution of AI capabilities, particularly 
around coding and agentic workﬂows. 
The emergence of Claude Code and subsequent product releases from Anthropic marked an 
important inﬂection point. While these technologies had been building throughout 2025, their 
capabilities became increasingly tangible to investors late in the year and into Q1. Claude Code 
had its “ChatGPT moment” with the launch of Claude Code for Web in October and the launch of 
Opus 4.5 in November. This snowballed with the open-source OpenClaw agent launch around 
Thanksgiving, which demonstrated the capabilities of AI agents, followed by the release of Claude 
Co-Work in January of this year. The combination of dramatically improved coding performance, 
agent-based workﬂows, and broader accessibility began to shift the narrative from AI as a 
productivity tool to AI as a potential substitute for labor. That transition materially accelerated 
concerns around the durability of traditional software business models. 
At the same time, the hardware scarcity theme continued to strengthen. Following a period of 
consolidation in late 2025, we used the pullback in memory-related equities in December as an 
opportunity to increase exposure across HDD, NAND, and DRAM, with positions in Western Digital, 
SanDisk, and Micron (MU). After CES and into Chinese New Year, pricing trends accelerated

THE EVOLVING AI LANDSCAPE 
 
HYPERSCALER CAPEX: THE MARKET’S BIGGEST TENSION 
 
One of the clearest issues in the market today is the sheer magnitude of AI spending and the return 
on that capital outlay. Hyperscaler equities sit at the center of this debate. Revenues remain strong, 
but free cash ﬂow is not. 
As the chart below highlights, trailing four-quarter revenue for the major hyperscalers is growing at 
approximately 16%, and cash flow from operations at roughly 25%, yet free cash flow has declined 
approximately -32% year-over-year as capital expenditures have accelerated significantly faster 
than revenue or operating cash generation. This is not a typical investment cycle – it is a step-
function increase in spending. Investors are no longer debating whether demand is real – they are 
debating whether the returns on this spend will arrive fast enough to justify the compression in 
near-term free cash ﬂow and the growing reliance on external ﬁnancing.

Amazon said it expects approximately $200B of capex in 2026, up from $131B in 2025, while 
Alphabet guided to $175–185B of capex in 2026. Oracle (ORCL), meanwhile, has become one of the 
clearest examples of both the opportunity and the anxiety embedded in this cycle: the company’s 
backlog and RPO continue to surge, but investors have become increasingly focused on the size of 
its AI infrastructure commitments and the ﬁnancing required to support them. In aggregate, 
consensus expectations suggest that large-cap technology companies will spend approximately 
$630 billion in 2026, rising to nearly $800 billion in 2027, with a substantial portion of their operating 
cash ﬂow being reinvested back into infrastructure. The scale of this investment is unprecedented.  
The drivers of this investment are clear – the leading frontier labs are racing to secure enough 
capacity as they jostle for dominant position, in eƯect creating an arms race where compute could 
be the sole determinant of which lab maintains the lead. At the same time, AI inference workloads 
are scaling, which require massive incremental compute, storage, networking, and power 
infrastructure. The competitive dynamic among hyperscalers has eƯectively become a race to build 
capacity fast enough to meet demand, while simultaneously attempting to establish durable 
positioning within the AI stack.  
We remain in the phase of the cycle where the industry is eƯectively building the factory. At the 
same time, the nature of demand is evolving. What began as a training-driven cycle is increasingly 
shifting toward inference and continuous compute, as AI systems move from point-in-time queries 
to persistent, multi-step workﬂows. This transition increases both the duration and intensity of 
compute demand. In parallel, additional layers are forming across the stack – encompassing 
orchestration, monitoring, governance, and security – required to support production-scale

Importantly, the system remains constrained not by demand, but by inputs. Power availability,

SOFTWARE: THE MODEL IS BEING REWRITTEN 
 
Software is now reﬂecting what was previously a theoretical debate. As we laid out in our Q4 letter, 
the traditional SaaS model is under pressure across its core drivers – seats, pricing, expansion, and 
diƯerentiation. More fundamentally, AI is shifting software from monetizing access to monetizing 
output. This implies fewer users are required to generate the same output, driving higher 
productivity per employee and ultimately a re-thinking of pricing models. Incumbent software 
companies are faced with the classic Innovator’s Dilemma, and we believe we are in early innings 
of self-imposed disruption as companies like Salesforce (CRM) and SAP (SAP) enter a period of 
business model evolution. Additionally, early data points suggest that AI-native companies are 
achieving signiﬁcantly higher revenue per employee than prior generations of software business 
models. Competitive intensity is increasing rapidly. New entrants are attacking incumbents both 
from above, through intelligence layers, and from below, through simpler, AI-native products.  
 
We are frequently asked “are we there yet” on software. To date, much of the drawdown has been 
driven by multiple compression and concerns around terminal value, rather than broad-based 
estimate revisions. We believe that as AI adoption progresses, earnings pressure will follow, though 
the magnitude and timing will vary signiﬁcantly by sub-sector and company. While there will 
undoubtedly be winners, we believe the path forward for many software companies will be more 
challenging than in prior cycles as they re-underwrite their business models. At current levels, a 
number of these stocks appear more likely to represent value traps rather than attractive value 
opportunities, at least in the near term. We continue to look for greater clarity around forward 
estimates into 2027 and 2028, as well as how business models evolve in response to AI-driven 
changes in demand. 
 
The outcome is unlikely to be uniform. Value will increasingly accrue to companies that control 
data, workﬂows, compliance, and distribution while others may struggle to maintain relevance as 
the competitive landscape shifts. 
 
INTERNET: CAUGHT IN THE CROSSCURRENTS 
 
The Internet sector has been hit nearly as hard as software, with material declines across the group 
during the quarter.  “Internet” has always been a loosely deﬁned category. It spans subscription 
services, gaming, marketplaces, e-commerce, travel, rideshare, advertising, and other digitally

That lack of cohesion has become more problematic in the current environment.  
 
The sector today sits in an uncomfortable position – part software, part consumer, and exposed to 
the headwinds of both. On one hand, it is subject to the same valuation pressures impacting 
software, including higher rates and concerns around long-duration cash ﬂows. On the other, it is 
fundamentally tied to consumer demand, which has remained uneven amid tariƯs, energy volatility, 
and broader macro uncertainty. At the same time, the sector continues to exhibit winner-take-most 
dynamics. A disproportionate share of economic value accrues to Amazon, Alphabet, and Meta, 
leaving the rest of the ecosystem competing for a smaller and more contested pool of proﬁts. This 
dynamic has only intensiﬁed as AI begins to reshape content creation, discovery, and advertising 
models. In addition, several subsectors face idiosyncratic overhangs. For example, Uber (UBER) 
continues to contend with uncertainty around autonomous vehicles, even as recent OEM 
partnerships have helped improve sentiment around the mobility business. 
 
Over the past several years, we observed increased investor focus on Internet equities, particularly 
as capital rotated out of software during its initial drawdown. For a time, this worked well, with 
several high-proﬁle names delivering strong returns. That dynamic has now reversed. Conviction 
across the space has been meaningfully reduced as investors reassess both business model 
durability and valuation frameworks in light of AI disruption and a more challenging consumer 
backdrop. 
 
While we have experienced some losses on the long side within this category year-to-date, it has 
also been an area where we have generated strong performance on the short side. We continue to 
believe there will be selective long opportunities that emerge over time, but for now, the sector 
remains highly bifurcated and increasingly dependent on diƯerentiation in data, distribution, and 
execution. 
 
MARKET OUTLOOK 
 
In broad terms, the Nasdaq eƯectively lost momentum around six months ago, and because a very 
small number of stocks represent such a large share of the index, conversations about what is 
“wrong” with Nvidia, Amazon, Broadcom (AVGO), or Microsoft have become almost 
indistinguishable from conversations about what is “wrong” with the market itself. Oil, credit, and 
geopolitical stress have all had large moves and have catalyzed bouts of broad-based de-risking. 
Meanwhile, Anthropic and broader AI disruption have wreaked havoc on software, producing a 
drawdown in parts of the sector that is severe enough to warrant not just reﬂection, but a 
willingness to think diƯerently about the group. The Internet sector has been damaged as well, 
stuck between software compression and consumer weakness. AI remains the number one theme 
in the market, but the ways to play it have narrowed materially, with stocks associated with 
constraints in memory, optical, and storage attracting the positive momentum and, by extension, 
the positioning risk that comes with it. 
 
We continue to view the current environment as a transition across phases of the AI cycle. The 
market has largely gotten the ﬁrst two phases correct. It has correctly rewarded infrastructure. It 
has now begun to scrutinize investment returns. Uncertainty remains greatest in the application 
layer, where durable value capture remains unclear.

Our positioning reﬂects that view. We continue to emphasize exposure to areas of the market where 
demand is visible and supported by structural tailwinds, particularly within AI infrastructure and 
supply-constrained segments of the value chain. These areas beneﬁt not just from strong end 
demand, but also from the scarcity of key inputs, which supports pricing and returns. At the same 
time, we remain selective within software and the broader application layer. We do believe 
meaningful value could ultimately be created there, but outcomes are likely to be uneven and 
increasingly dependent on execution and positioning. 
 
Stepping back, we believe the market is repricing an entire technology stack in real time. It is a 
transition from software to labor, from tools to autonomous systems, and from access to output. 
These transitions are rarely linear, and they are never comfortable, but they are often fertile periods 
for active managers willing to embrace dispersion rather than fear it. We look to take advantage of 
volatility rather than letting volatility take advantage of us. 
 
The ﬁrst quarter was less of a breakdown and more of a reﬁnement. AI demand remains strong.

in the crosscurrents. Dispersion is increasing, and in our view that means one thing above all else: 
going forward, selectivity will drive returns.
