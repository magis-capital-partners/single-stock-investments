Market Observations 
For the first quarter of 2026, we generated net returns of +12.47% which compares to +0.89% for the 
Russell 2000 and -4.98% for the S&P.  This follows 2025, a year in which we generated +33% net returns.  
We are very pleased with these results, especially in a time of moderate market returns and extreme 
market volatility.  Moreover, we believe this demonstrates that, as we have claimed before, we are in the 
very early innings of a long bull market in the materials and industrials sectors in which we focus.   
Aluminum smelting continues to be a focus for our fund and Century Aluminum (CENX) continues to be 
our biggest position.  At the end of the third quarter last year, we wrote that when commodity/material 
markets get tight, operational volatility tends to benefit prices. In other words, unforeseen events tend to 
tighten markets further.  Specifically, we were referencing the accident at Freeport McMoRan’s Grasberg 
mine in Indonesia, which took an estimated 1.7% of annual global copper supply out of the market.  Please 
see Exhibit 1 below showing copper prices around and after this mine accident. We believe there was an 
initial, more muted upward price reaction driven largely by financial participants and then a lagged, larger 
price reaction driven by the physical realities of lower copper volumes arriving in ports and at warehouses.

Similarly, we believe the aluminum market is tightening right now.  On March 28th, the Al Taweelah 
smelter owned by Emirates Global Aluminum (EGA) and the Aluminium Bahrain B.S.C. (Alba) smelter were 
both hit by Iranian missiles.  EGA has stated that restoring production at Al Taweelah will take up to twelve 
months.  The extent of the damage to Alba is yet to be announced.  The shutdown of Al Taweelah appears 
to have been uncontrolled, generally resulting in molten aluminum solidifying in the pots when the 
smelter shuts down.  Not surprisingly, this repair is extremely difficult.  Al Taweelah alone accounts for 
~2.0% of the global primary aluminum supply.  Alba is the largest single-site smelter in the world 
accounting for ~2.2% of global primary supply. 
Exhibit 2 below shows a graph of the LME aluminum price before and after the shutdowns.  It took 
approximately four months from the Grasberg copper mine closure before prices peaked 38% higher than 
where they were immediately before the accident.  If the same pattern were to repeat in aluminum, spot 
prices would reach ~$2.10 per pound early August 2026. There could still be substantial upside in 
aluminum prices over the next several months.

At Mountaineer, everyone on the investment team has deep experience in fixed income, specifically in 
distressed debt, giving us a differentiated lens with which to analyze equities in the lending space.  As all 
flavors of private lending have grown, we have marveled at the degradation of lending standards.  First, 
we saw it in the weakness of new lending documents and, more recently, simply in the poor credit quality 
of debt.  For instance, First Brands. 
Over the prior several years there has been a well-documented surge in capital flowing into private credit.2   
Just as a surge in supply in industrial markets generally leads to a reduction in prices, we believe a surge 
in the supply of debt capital has resulted in a significant reduction in underwriting standards. 
A recent example of this deterioration in underwriting standards can be found in First Brand’s 1st lien debt.  
During June of 2025, First Brands issued $250 million of 1st lien debt.  If you had invested $100 in that 
loan, it would be worth $0.01 to $0.10, a loss of essentially $100.  If had attempted to roll your loan into 
the debtor-in-possession financing, you would have lost even more. 
Amazingly, this new issue private credit first lien loan went from $250 million to zero in less than seven 
months.  Given that fact pattern, it is clear that this loan represents a dramatic deterioration in standard 
underwriting practices.  We do not think that loans like this are aberration. Rather, we believe this loan is 
emblematic of the destruction of credit underwriting standards that is rife throughout private credit.   
Finally, we offer this analysis which we believe will drive BDC performance for several quarters.  
Hypothetically, if we assume that the market believes that 20% of a BDC’s book is mismarked and should 
be marked at 70% and not at par.  This means that before any redemptions are made, the portfolio is 
worth 94 cents instead of par (80% is marked correctly at 100 cents and 20% should be marked at 70 
cents).  If we then assume an investor does not redeem and that the BDC sells only loans that are truly 
worth 100 cents, then at the end of four years the true value of the book is as little as 85 cents.  If you 
assume consistent one times leverage in the BDC through the redemptions, the four-year true value of 
the equity is in the low 70s.  As investors redeem and the BDC sells par loans, the remaining investors 
become more concentrated in loans that are mismarked.  We believe that this logic will drive increased 
redemptions for the medium term.  Moreover, any investor who lived through the financial crisis should 
be highly motivated to redeem.
