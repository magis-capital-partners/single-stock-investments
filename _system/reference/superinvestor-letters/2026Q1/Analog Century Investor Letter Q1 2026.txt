The Physical Layer Takes Command 
The deﬁning narrative of the ﬁrst quarter was the decisive and accelerating shift of economic value 
toward the physical infrastructure underpinning artiﬁcial intelligence. While semiconductor and 
hardware stocks have outperformed software since 2019 (Figure 1), the ﬁrst quarter marked a 
meaningful step-change in both the pace of hard tech investment and the visibility of earnings leverage 
across the supply chain. Memory, semiconductor capital equipment, optical networking, and power 
infrastructure all exhibit tangible bottleneck dynamics, and the market rewarded companies that can 
demonstrably relieve those constraints.

The clearest evidence of this demand was in memory. DRAM and NAND pricing, which had already been 
under upward pressure from AI-driven workloads, inﬂected sharply higher. Quarter-over-quarter pricing 
increases for the March quarter came in at 60–80%, far exceeding what manufacturers had guided to 
just months prior. This is not a cyclical inventory restock. It reﬂects a structural shortage created by more 
capable models being used for higher value inference work. Model context windows are growing by 
orders of magnitude, and this requires more real time memory and storage to support computing. 
Longer context windows enable reasoning models, which in turn produce more tokens needing storage. 
Finally, reasoning has enabled AI agents (and Claw orchestrators of agents) to run long-lived, multi-step 
workﬂows with persistent context and state retention, all driving increased memory demand.

Capital equipment tells a similar story. TSMC raised its 2026 capital expenditure guidance to $52–56 
billion, above prior consensus, and equipment makers across the board reported that customers have 
locked capacity well into the future. One leading etch equipment provider disclosed that its tools are 
fully booked through the end of 2026. The transition to sub-2nm process nodes has increased the 
number of process steps per wafer, raising demand for lithography, deposition, etch, and inspection tools 
simultaneously. Advanced packaging —  required for HBM and chiplet architectures —  has added a 
second vector of demand for metrology and process tools that was largely absent from prior 
semiconductor cycles. 
Networking: The Growing Bottleneck 
Optical networking has become a constraint on AI infrastructure growth. The conclusion from OFC, the 
optical ﬁber industry's leading trade event, was unambiguous: every new AI accelerator architecture 
comes with big step-increases in bandwidth needed for the GPU and ASIC chips to communicate with 
each other and with memory. Networking capacity for connecting AI clusters is growing more slowly 
than computing capacity, and the gap is widening. Silicon, by virtue of continued architectural 
innovation, is on a steeper improvement curve than lasers and ﬁber optics. The result is that AI cluster 
networking capex is growing even faster than compute capex. 
This has profound implications for where value accrues. Demand for 1.6 terabit-per-second optical 
transceivers is ramping in high volume now, and the ecosystem of components required to support them 
—  including the specialized semiconductors that drive optical interfaces —  is seeing supply constraints 
similar to what compute experienced eighteen months ago. More importantly, OFC made clear that 
legacy technologies like copper cabling will continue to grow even as pluggable optic units expand over 
50% annually. Next-generation architectures like co-packaged optics and optical circuit switching will 
begin gaining traction in 2027, expanding the addressable market further. The overall message was not a 
battle between competing technologies but a recognition that the TAM is large enough, and growing fast 
enough, that the industry needs more of everything. 
Co-packaged optics deserves particular attention. CPO integrates the optics directly onto the package 
alongside the compute die, dramatically reducing power and latency penalties associated with moving 
data on and oﬀ chip. Adoption is occurring sooner and at greater scale than the industry anticipated 
even six months ago, and it will favor suppliers with diﬀerentiated laser and silicon photonics technology. 
This is a theme we have been positioned for, and we and expect it to remain signiﬁcant through the 
balance of the year. 
The Agentic Inﬂection and Its Infrastructure Consequences 
NVIDIA's GTC conference and ARM's analyst day this quarter delivered a consistent and important 
message: agentic AI —  systems that autonomously take multi-step actions, browse the web, interact 
with external software, and manage complex workﬂows —  has arrived and is scaling rapidly. The 
implications for infrastructure demand are not incremental. They are multiplicative. 
 
 
4 
 
A single human interacting with an agentic system can generate ﬁfteen times or more the token volume 
of a standard chat interaction, because agents must reason through intermediate steps, verify results, 
call external tools, and maintain persistent context across long sessions. As the number of humans 
interacting with agentic systems scales, the aggregate token demand can go nearly vertical (Figure 2). 
The economics of serving this demand require new silicon architectures —  optimized for inference 
rather than training —  and new rack designs. They also require a new orchestration layer, what ARM 
described as a CPU-level function, that governs which agent performs which task and routes it to the 
appropriate computational and data resources.

Eﬃciency improvements —  quantization, mixture-of-experts, KV cache compression, distillation —  are 
real and ongoing. Token costs have fallen by one to two orders of magnitude per year for several years, 
substantially faster than Moore's Law would predict. Each successive eﬃciency gain has historically been 
met with a more-than-proportionate increase in token consumption, as lower costs unlock new use 
cases and accelerate adoption. Google’s recent announcement of TurboQuant KV cache compression 
technology, which the market initially treated as a demand headwind, is best understood in this context: 
a 6x compression improvement is meaningful but is dwarfed by the demand trajectory created by 
agentic workloads. The history of the past two years argues strongly that cheaper tokens produce more 
token usage and infrastructure demand, not less. 
Compute Scarcity Evident in Pricing 
Tightness in AI infrastructure is no longer an abstraction as it is being priced in real-time commodity 
markets. H100 GPU rental pricing on one-year contracts has risen approximately 40% since October 
2025, moving from $1.70 per GPU-hour to $2.35 by March 2026 (Figure 3). Rental prices for older GPU 
architectures should decrease as more eﬃcient GPU architectures start producing cheaper tokens.  
Blackwell Ultra GB300 NVL72 delivers up to 50x higher throughput per megawatt and up to 35x lower 
cost per token versus the Hopper platform. But, we are seeing the reverse. On-demand GPU capacity

This pricing dynamic is a real-time conﬁrmation of what hyperscale capital expenditure announcements, 
semiconductor capital equipment order books, and memory pricing all suggest: we are not near a peak 
in AI infrastructure investment. We are in the early stages of a multi-year build that is being executed 
with a sense of urgency that has no modern parallel in the technology sector.  
The market remains skeptical despite this evidence. The leading computing chip makers, Broadcom and 
Nvidia, trade close to the market’s forward PE multiple. TSM, the near monopoly manufacturer of their 
silicon, trades just below the market. These companies are growing revenue 40-90%, leaps and bounds 
above the average S&P 500 business. Optics stocks like Lumentum and Ciena do not appear cheap at 60x 
forward earnings, but with revenue for those companies growing high double digits and earnings 
growing several fold, looking out just two years the stocks are much more reasonably priced. Memory is 
in a class of its own with multiples for Micron and SanDisk in the low to mid-single digits given cyclicality 
in those businesses. We continue to see large opportunity holding shares of AI infrastructure companies 
as high earnings growth alone will produce sizable returns even if the market does not price in that 
growth persisting.

become unusually favorable for disciplined long/short investing. Realized dispersion across individual 
equities has reached levels not observed in over a decade —  by some measures, the implied volatility 
spread for the average S&P 500 constituent approached levels last seen during the ﬁnancial crisis (Figure

The sources of this dispersion are structural, not transient. Markets are genuinely uncertain about the 
pace and distribution of AI's economic impact —  which industries will be disrupted, which will be 
ampliﬁed, and on what timeline. This uncertainty creates recurring cycles of compression and release: 
factor pairs push to historical extremes as the market prices one narrative, then snap back violently 
when that narrative is challenged. We experienced several such episodes during the quarter, including 
sharp rotations that bore no relationship to fundamental news, driven instead by hedge fund de-grossing 
and retail deleveraging. In each case, the dislocations created attractive entry and exit points for 
positions we had conviction in on fundamental grounds. 
Geopolitical uncertainty, ongoing tariﬀ dynamics, and the emergence of AI as a genuine labor market 
variable add further layers of complexity that the market will continue to process unevenly. We expect 
this environment to persist. The combination of high single-name dispersion, recurring technically driven 
dislocations, and a technology landscape that is producing genuine fundamental surprises at an 
accelerating rate is, in our view, close to an ideal environment for the strategy. We are well-positioned to 
continue generating alpha from it.
