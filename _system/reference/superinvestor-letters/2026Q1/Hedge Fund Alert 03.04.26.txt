Eisler Chief Shifts Focus to Family Office
Having liquidated his namesake multi-strategy opera -
tion, Eisler Capital founder Edward Eisler appears to be 
turning his attention to his family office.
Last month the family office, Jersey-headquartered 
EE Holdings, made a follow-on investment in a nuclear 
energy startup. Eisler initially invested in the company in 
September.
In January, Eisler updated his LinkedIn profile to reflect 
his role as chief investment officer of the family office. 
Meanwhile, Nick Karangizi, formerly senior legal counsel 
at Eisler Capital for nearly five years, is now EE Holdings’ 
general counsel and chief operating officer.
Records show Edward Eisler formed EE Holdings in 
2021, though it wasn’t registered with the Jersey Financial 
Services Commission until mid-2024. 
The registration document lists Karangizi as an EE 
director and Eisler Capital employee, with no mention of 
his family office roles as general counsel and COO. That 
suggests the relationship was formalized more recently. 
Indeed, Karangizi also updated his LinkedIn profile in Jan-
uary to reflect his standing at the family office. It’s unclear 
if the operation has other employees or if it will be hiring.
Edward Eisler didn’t respond to a request for comment.
EE Holdings has made two investments in the nuclear 
energy startup, Deep Fission of Berkeley, Calif. The family 
office in September was part of a group that invested a total 
of $30 million to finance a reverse merger with Surfside 
Acquisition Inc., with plans to take the company public 
under the Deep Fission name.
Then on Feb. 10, Deep Fission announced it had raised 
$80 million via a stock offering of 5.3 million shares priced 
at $15 per share. EE Holdings was among the investors. 
Another was Mark Tompkins  of venture capital shop 
Montrose Capital, which led a previous financing round 
for the company.
Eisler Capital began unwinding in September, cit -
ing soaring staff costs and disappointing performance. 
Edward Eisler, a former Goldman Sachs partner who had 
co-headed the bank’s global markets division, founded his 
firm in 2015 as a global-macro hedge fund shop. In recent 
years, however, he sought to transform the firm into a 
multi-strategy operation.
To do so, he needed top talent, and he went after it. By 
2024, operating costs at Eisler Capital reached nearly $500 
million, a sum that included massive signing bonuses and 
big salaries. The spending proved unsustainable. More -
over, it revealed the costs to investors, who remained on 
the hook for bonuses even as Eisler unwound.
Eisler finished 2025 down 14.3%, in part reflecting the 
dilution of any gains because of make-good payments 
on bonuses and other costs. Some limited partners also 
were irked by Eisler’s plan to keep 100% of proceeds from 
licensing its proprietary risk technology. Multi-strategy 
rival Verition Fund Management subsequently acquired 
a license to use the technology, called Photon, Bloomberg 
reported on Feb. 24. 
Ex-Jackson Square PM Preps Own Firm
A portfolio manager who led a large-cap growth-ori -
ented equity fund at long-only, concentrated investment 
manager Jackson Square Partners  is readying his own 
hedge fund operation.
Billy Montana is raising capital for Skybreak Capital’s 
debut, slated for later in the first half of the year. Montana 
is expected to invest the majority of his own net worth 
alongside money raised from limited partners.
Montana is an 11-year veteran of Jackson Square, a 
manager of private funds and mutual funds that peaked 
at $25 billion and wound down its investment operations 
in December, at which time Montana departed to form 
Skybreak. Jackson Square was managing around $1 billion 
at yearend 2024. The Larkspur, Calif., manager’s growth-
equity strategy formed the blueprint for Montana’s own 
firm, which will likewise manage a concentrated, high-
conviction, long-only program. 
Montana was the lead portfolio manager on the Jackson 
Square Large-Cap Growth Fund for roughly three years, 
during which time the mutual fund generated an annual -
ized gain of 33% and was ranked a top-performing fund in 
the large-cap growth category. The S&P 500 Total Return 
Index returned 24.1% on an annualized basis over the 
same period.
Despite its name, the large-cap growth fund Montana 
oversaw at Jackson Square invested in companies with 
market caps as low as $3 billion. He also contributed ideas 
to the firm’s other offerings, including a small- and mid-
cap growth fund. The firm’s SMID- and large-cap growth 
funds were merged into San Francisco-based Spyglass 
Capital’s Spyglass Growth Fund in the fourth quarter.
Skybreak aims to profit from idiosyncratic stock selec -
tion powered by deep, fundamental research. Its strategy 
applies a strict value-discipline to the growth-stock uni -
verse, targeting high-quality disruptors and compound -
ers benefiting from secular tailwinds and high returns 
on incremental capital, with a long runway for growth. It 
plans to invest opportunistically during what it describes 
as moments of unfounded skepticism or overlooked posi-
tive fundamental change.
The Los Angeles firm believes the stage is set for its 
strategy, given the historic valuation gap between smaller-
cap and large-cap stocks; the impact of growing dispersion 
among mega-cap technology stocks; the rise of artificial 
intelligence dividing the market into winners and losers; 
and shrinking holding periods.
“We’re launching at a moment when passive investing 
and factor strategies are hitting their limits and idiosyn -
cratic stock-picking is starting to matter again in a way that

The firm’s Skybreak One offering will run with a higher 
concentration – 12 to 15 high-conviction positions – than 
Montana’s previous firm, as Skybreak is not subject to the 
same diversification requirements as a mutual fund. The 
fund is expected to invest across market capitalizations 
and across all sectors in U.S.-listed companies, including 
young, fast-growing businesses and dominant, well-estab-
lished businesses.
Skybreak intends to hold a position as long as the busi-
ness continues to reinvest at attractive rates, the oppor -
tunity remains asymmetric and the valuation isn’t being 
driven by hype.
With its long-term horizon, the firm says it will attempt 
to benefit from both time and sentiment arbitrage, buying 
in times of elevated skepticism and holding through peri -
ods of recovery, when other capital is impatient.
“Most funds do one or the other – they’re trading around 
sentiment or they’re closet indexing and passively hold -
ing for a long time, ” Montana said. “We want to do both 
simultaneously and in an entirely unconstrained manner 
to maximize returns. ”
Montana joined Jackson Square shortly after its launch 
in 2014. He previously was an associate in TPG Capital’s 
North American buyouts group and an investment-bank-

Emerging-Markets Fund Opens to LPs
Variis Partners, which focuses on equities in emerg -
ing markets, has kicked off its first formal marketing 
campaign following a year of solid results that have con -
tinued into 2026. 
The London operation, which began trading in Octo -
ber 2023, introduced itself to investors at the Global Alts 
Miami event hosted by iConnections  and the Managed 
Funds Association  in Miami Beach last week. Variis 
executives – including Leila Kardouche,  chief invest -
ment officer and portfolio manager, and Jamie Carter,  
a managing director – are planning to attend the iCon -
nections/MFA Global Alternatives event in New Y ork in 
June.
Variis, which is in dialogue with 250 institutional 
investors mainly in the U.S., is making progress landing 
on consultants’ recommendation lists.
Variis’ only vehicle, Variis Partners Emerging Markets 
Fund, has $50 million under management capitalized by 
friends and family, along with the firm’s four partners.
The fund gained about 8% year-to-date through most 
of February following a 27.35% advance in 2025. That 
compares with a more than 14% return for the MSCI 
Emerging Markets Index in the January-February period 
following a 30.6% rise last year for the benchmark.
Variis runs a concentrated portfolio of 20 to 30 posi -
tions, with a maximum 10% of assets in a single stock. 
The firm emphasizes deep fundamental research and will 
typically hold investments for multiple years, targeting 
mispriced securities with durable growth prospects. The 
median market capitalization of its holdings was $39 bil -
lion at end of 2025.
Emerging-markets equities surged in 2025 as a weak -
ened dollar eased financial conditions across developing 
economies, spurring renewed interest in markets that 
had lagged their counterparts in developed nations for 
a decade. Technology-heavy markets including China, 
Taiwan, India and South Korea – Variis is significantly 
invested in Asia – benefited from investor demand for 
artificial-intelligence-related semiconductor companies.
As of Dec. 31, China accounted for 27.6% of the Variis 
portfolio, followed by Taiwan at 20.6%, India at 15.3% 
and South Korea at 13.3%. The fund also held positions 
in Brazil, Indonesia, Malaysia and Poland.
Top holdings at the beginning of the year included 
Samsung Electronics, SK hynix, Delta Electronics  and 
Nu Holdings.
As part of its strategy, Variis looks beyond the 1,196 
constituents in the MSCI Emerging Markets Index, con -
tending that some of the most compelling businesses are 
not included in the benchmark. Indeed, the firm’s port -
folio contains only 10 of the biggest 35 members of the 
MSCI index.

Small-Business Lender Seeks U.S. LPs
Toronto-based small-business lender Kilgour Wil -
liams Capital is making its first broad marketing push in 
the U.S., the focus of its $250 million financing operation.
The firm, whose assets are up from up from about $100 
million in 2023 and whose strategy has an annualized 
return of 14% since its launch in 2018, has taken a num -
ber of steps to increase its investor base in the U.S.
Following an appearance at the iConnections Global 
Alts conference in Miami Beach last week, the firm is 
planning to attend two to four more U.S. conferences this 
year. Also in recent months, Kilgour Williams arranged 
to be custodied on the Schwab Advisor Services  plat -
form to make itself available to customers of wealth man -
agers that use that venue. And the firm’s U.S. fund is now 
also listed on iCapital Marketplace,  where another set of 
wealth advisors and family offices may reach it. Finally, 
Kilgour Williams also signed with a firm that is introduc -
ing it to potential U.S. investors.
Kilgour Williams’ investment approach is to make 
many investments – currently more than 1,000 – to a 
diversified set of Main Street and primarily U.S. busi -
nesses. Led by founders Colin Kilgour  and Daniel 
Williams, the firm believes it has capacity to manage 
$1 billion. Its borrowers include operations such as gas 
stations, grocery stores, professional services providers, 
hotels, healthcare practices and facilities, construction 
businesses and small manufacturers.
The firm issues loans in amounts ranging from $75,000 
to $500,000, with an average of about $125,000, and for 
terms lasting from six to 24 months. The loans are secured 
by the businesses themselves and they carry interest rates 
that are comparable to that of retail credit cards – roughly 
20%. Kilgour uses its own risk-and-return parameters to 
select among its potential borrowers that it sources from 
a number of platforms, of which its key provider is Biz-
2Credit.
Kilgour Williams’ U.S.-domiciled offering, the KiWi 
Alternative Income Fund, makes monthly distributions 
to investors and targets for returns of 12% to 15%. How -
ever, it’s hit higher than that in each of the last two years.
As measured by the U.S. feeder, the strategies’ returns 
grew in the years following the coronavirus pandemic 
and as the Federal Reserve  raised rates multiple times in 
2022 and 2023. The fund gained 15.7% last year, 16.7% in 
2024 and 16.9% in 2023. That followed returns of 13.5% 
in 2022 and 15.6% in 2021.
The fund’s one single-digit year was in 2020, the first 
year of the pandemic, when it produced an 8.5% return 
with two monthly losses of 0.1% each. Those represent 
the strategy’s only monthly losses. Before that, the firm 
returned 11% in 2019 and 13.9% in 2018, the strategy’s 
first year.
After deploying more than $1 billion in its small-
loan strategy since 2018, principal losses total about 3% 
annually.
The push for U.S. investors follows changes the firm 
made in 2023, when it formed a Bermuda-based mas -
ter feeder structure and set up feeder funds into that for 
investors from Canada, Israel and the U.S. But the U.S. 
remains a very small portion of the firm’s overall assets, 
and until now it’s sought that capital based on personal 
relationships and referrals. (The firm’s also setting up 
with a distribution partner in Brazil and it now has some 
investors based in Singapore.)
Kilgour Williams launched its strategy, now managed 
via the KiWi Alternative Income Fund, in January 2018 
when it was part of a broader strategy that it has since 
narrowed. The firm formed in 2007 to advise Canadian 
investors on $2 billion of restructured asset-backed 
loans.
In 2001, Kilgour founded and led another firm, Effi-
cient Capital Corp.,  that provided factoring capital, a 
business that was bought by Coventree Capital  in 2006. 
Williams’ resume includes former roles as chief invest -
ment officer of Dundee Bank and head of credit portfolio 
management at National Bank of Canada. 

Among the 153 participants – including recruiters – in 
Hedge Fund Alert’s annual industry outlook survey for 
2026, 110 noted that they were using AI in at least some form, 
and another 12 planned to begin integrating or increasing 
their use of AI this year. The respondents largely agreed on 
whether AI would make analysts better (it would) but were 
divided on whether it would also make their jobs obsolete. 
Fifty said they expected AI would make analysts better but 
obsolete, while another 49 said their jobs were safe. Fifteen 
respondents predicted AI would eliminate analyst roles.
Recruiters who Hedge Fund Alert talked with over the 
past month also are seeing an interest in credit strategies; 
a return to work for previously remote workers; stronger 
efforts to retain top performers; and a continuation of 
products for retail investors, lowering investment thresh -
olds for some smaller-pocketed investors to sit at the table 
that for years was occupied by high rollers. And every 
recruiter said they were busier than they were last year.
SEC chair Paul Atkins, who has been in office for just 
under a year, has been warm to what has been termed 
the “retail-ization” of the private fund industry – opening 
funds to a wider audience.
“ Almost every fund that I know is launching some form 
of ’40 Act strategy, whether it’s ETFs, BDCs, mutual funds, ” 
Colabella noted. “They are all trying to get into the retail 
space. So the expansion of their teams, both on the invest-
ment and back-office side, is booming to make sure that 
they have the right staff and experts in those areas to help 
build out those strategies. Much of it is new territory for a 
lot of these funds. ”
Likewise, John Lane,  co-founder of executive-search 
firm Landing Point  in New Y ork, has found additional 
competition for legal and compliance talent because of 
the SEC reporting and disclosure requirements associated 
with potentially complex ’40 Act retail-focused products.
“On the legal side, we’ve seen real meaningful hiring 
growth coming from traditional managers that are moving 
into more retail-oriented registered products like BDCs, 
index funds or interval funds, ” he said. “They’re competing 
for the same limited talent pool. ”
Landing Point also continues to see hiring demand in 
the growing family office space, a result of the maturing of 
the asset-management industry.
“Y ou have a lot of managers who have been running 
firms for 20, 30 years, sometimes more than that, and they 
have acquired significant personal wealth in that time 
period, ” Lane said. “Sometimes it is a retirement-type play, 
where they are stepping away from their funds and they are 
just managing this. Other times, they are continuing to run 
their fund, and they are setting up a separate vehicle and 
bringing in CIOs to run that. ”
Landing Point also continued its emphasis on placing 
consultants for project-specific work at hedge funds for 
six-month or 12-month hiring periods. The firm’s consult-
ing practice grew 98% year over year and now accounts for 
40% of its revenue.
“It’s more of an augmentation on hiring and hasn’t been 
replacing permanent hiring – our permanent hiring prac -
tice is up 27%, ” Lane said.
The era of work-from-home following the global pan -
demic appears to be over, though some firms continue 
to offer perks unheard of before the pandemic, such as 
“Remote August” or five weeks of “work from anywhere” 
built into the year.
“Some of my smaller funds are still three days or four 
days [in office], but the vast majority, everyone’s back, ” Jud-
son said.
On the hedge fund side, Judson said, most of the hiring 
activity is in long/short strategies.
“We have seen a fair number of mid-level hires on the 
hedge fund side in credit, ” she noted. “But the vast majority 
of what is out there is on the private side. ” 
Colabella noticed a quiet trend: the sudden removal of 
veteran chief compliance officers.
“The biggest trend we saw in 2025 was the amount of 
firms that were upleveling their current chief compliance 
officers with new talent, ” she said. “Individuals who had 
been at the seat for eight to 15 years being confidentially 
replaced to upscale the profile with somebody else. That 
happened a lot. They felt that these people were very reac-
tive, versus proactive, and given the changes in both reg -
ulatory landscape as well as expansion of strategies, they 
wanted a sharper, kind of hungrier person in the seat. ”
The high cost of retaining top-tier talent in part claimed 
at least one big multi-strategy firm in 2025. When it 
announced it was closing in September, Eisler Capital  
listed those expenses among its reasons for shuttering.
Anthony Rodriguez, who oversees the global-market 
and front-office recruiting business at Atlas Search in New 
Y ork, said firms are still willing to pay big to attract super-
star performers.
“That’s definitely part of the story, ” he said. “But firms 
are also smarter now about how to get talent to stay. ”  
Similar to the star professional athlete who is getting 
ready to test the free-agent market, a hotshot portfolio 
manager can get a multi-year contract to stay with the cur-
rent team, albeit sometimes with a longer non-compete 
period.

Benchmark Plus suffered a black eye in recent years: A

risk limits and lying about its holdings. Anavio, which moved 
to close its fund roughly a year ago, denied the claims. The 
two firms settled the matter on undisclosed terms in January. 
“The settlement does not reflect any determination on the 
merits of the claims but rather acknowledges the practical 
limits on recovery arising from the Anavio defendants’ finan-
cial circumstances and the insurance policy limits, ” Bench-
mark Plus said in a statement to Hedge Fund Alert.
No source directly linked the redemptions to the Anavio 
investment, though one said it “didn’t help. ”
Rather than retool the Institutional Partners fund’s strategy 
to adapt to a diminished asset base of perhaps 10% of the fund’s 
assets, Benchmark Plus opted instead to shutter the vehicle. 
But the closure of that main offering has forced reallocations 
among the firm’s smaller fund. That’s because both funds are 
believed to have invested in the same underlying hedge funds, 
and the firm is no longer able to meet investment minimums 
on at least some of the underlying funds. What’s more, returns 
would be eaten up by magnified expenses.
At issue with the Anavio investment was its allocation to 
a company called Canadian Overseas Petroleum via the 
Anavio Equity Capital Markets Fund. Benchmark Plus, which 
constituted the bulk of the fund’s assets, said it expected the 
vehicle to make investments in small, liquid debt and equity 
securities in companies in Europe and Asia. Anavio, however, 
ultimately put 92% of the fund’s capital into the Canadian oil-
exploration company, which filed for bankruptcy in March 
2024, Benchmark Plus alleged. 
Hedge Fund Alert  parent With Intelligence reported 
in February 2025 that Benchmark Plus’ loss shook the con-
fidence of at least one pension consultant, NEPC Research, 
whose clients comprised 40% of Benchmark Plus’ asset base 
at that time.
“While it appears that the underlying fund [Anavio] was 
not transparent and potentially misled the firm regarding the 
size of the underlying position, NEPC Research believes that 
this situation does call into question the fund’s underwriting 
process for existing managers from both an investment due 
diligence and operational due diligence perspective, ” NEPC 
wrote in a memo to one of its clients at the time, the Balti-
more County Employees Retirement System. 
“NEPC Research feels that while the manager did try to 
take action to mitigate the risk of this one underlying fund to 
the overall fund, the size of the allocation to this manager in 
the first place seems questionable, ” the consultant wrote.
At least three employees have already exited Benchmark 
Plus – which reported eight employees, including five invest-
ment professionals, in a March 28 regulatory filing – and two 
more are believed to be on their way out over the coming 
months, leaving partners Robert Ferguson, Stephen West-
phal and Steven Carroll to make a go of it.   
Benchmark Plus aims to produce market-neutral port -
folios by deploying capital to underlying managers through 
commingled funds and separate accounts across a variety 
of strategies, such as long/short equity, statistical arbitrage, 
closed-end fund arbitrage, fixed-income and activist. The 
firm can then combine that market-neutral product with 
derivatives linked to individual benchmarks, such as the S&P 
500 Index, the Russell 2000 Index or the price of gold. Inves-
tors in such offerings will then receive the returns from the 
hedge fund portfolio plus the return of the benchmark after 
expenses.
The version of the Institutional Partners fund that offered 
only the market-neutral hedge fund portfolio vehicle gained 
9.9% last year through November after dipping slightly, about 
2%, in 2024 – one of just three down years in more than two 
decades. Over that span, it generated 4.1% on an annualized 
basis and a 0.9 Sharpe ratio since it launched in 2004.
The hedge fund-only version of its vehicle that targets 
wealthy individuals, Benchmark Plus Partners, meanwhile, 
produced a 6.5% annualized return from January 1998 
through November 2025, with a Sharpe ratio of 1.45. In 2005, 
that fund, which is now under $100 million, managed $515 
million. The fund returned 12.1% in the first 11 months of 
2025 and finished 2024 roughly even.
Benchmark Plus is owned by Ferguson, Westphal, Carroll 
and Scott Franzblau, who retired in 2020. 
Westphal, who has been with the firm since 2012, previ-
ously was a vice president of equity finance at BMO Capital 
Markets. Ferguson is the firm’s chief investment officer, while 
Carroll serves in the dual roles of chief financial officer and 
chief compliance officer. He first arrived at Benchmark Plus 
in 2005 as a hedge fund operations analyst. 
