Dear Investor: 
The Sandglass Opportunity Fund delivered a strong +5.61%1 net return in January, driven by broad -based gains. 
Corporate positions led the way, fueled by strong performance in Allied Gold converts (and equity) and the 
anticipated emergence of Azul Airlines from bankruptcy.  Sovereign exposures also contributed meaningfully, with 
solid advances in hard -currency positions (Lebanon and Venezuela) and our diversified local -currency holdings. 
The month's modest losses stemmed primarily from short -risk positions, notably select Brazilian corporate 
credits— which short positions played out favorably for us in February (details to follow in next month's update). 
As outlined in our recent Investors Table Presentation (attached in last month’s newsletter), both Venezuela and 
Lebanon stand out as high-convexity, idiosyncratic opportunities for 2026.  In Venezuela, the dramatic January 3 
capture and exfiltration of former President Maduro by U.S. special forces marked a pivotal shift in longstanding 
U.S. efforts to bring the country “in from the cold .” The Administration's pragmatic approach— tolerating 
continued interim chavismo governance— prioritizes near-term stability, reducing risks of violent elite power 
struggles over national resources. The PSUV appears poised to seek White House accommodations that revive 
economic activity and bolster its position ahead of eventual elections. While formal debt restructuring talks 
remain distant (pending at minimum an OFAC license), the prospective economic rebound should drive 
meaningful value accretion for bondholders in the interim. We maintain a balanced portfolio across the range of 
claims - Venezuela vs PDVSA, high vs. low PDI – to avoid premature high-conviction bets on the claims’ relative 
merits. Sandglass plans a Caracas visit in April. 
In Lebanon, our team continues frequent on-the-ground assessments of recovery prospects for this long-defaulted 
sovereign, now advancing toward sovereign and banking-sector restructuring to restart the economy. Top-down, 
the case is compelling: major global players (U.S., France, Saudi Arabia, Qatar) remain committed to Lebanon's 
stability and unification, with readiness to fund reconstruction. Sustaining this support hinges on preserving the 
fragile Israel ceasefire and progressing Hezbollah disarmament — outcomes so far mixed but better than feared. 
Looming U.S.-Iran tensions will test these dynamics. Bottom -up, Lebanon must finalize IMF -compliant fiscal and 
bank-restructuring frameworks to secure a program and unlock donor aid. Spring elections could delay progress, 
though a postponement to enable technocratic focus appears probable. Compared to Venezuela, Lebanon's 
recovery offers attractive gains from current levels but lower absolute upside, but with a clearer, shorter, and less 
uncertain path forward.

Late January brought the announcement of an all- cash acquisition of our core mining holding, Allied Gold, by

fully exited the position. Timing proved fortunate: subsequent gold price and miner weakness— sparked by market 
interpretation of President Trump's Kevin Warsh Fed Chair nomination as hawkish — allowed us to redeploy 
proceeds into two other high-conviction names at more attractive levels.
