exacerbate the burgeoning economic weakness. As we have regularly noted, we view employment data as the most 
important indicator to monitor due to the  dominant role consumer spending plays in the economy. In March,

energy prices over the last several weeks ha s added to our concerns about the economy. Nationwide , gas prices

closure of the Strait of Hormuz could cause a significant negative shock to the world’s economy. At least for now,

Investment Review: Visa and Mastercard interchange antitrust opt-out litigation claim
4  
 
In March, we received a final settlement payment for  our long position in a Visa  and Mastercard interchange 
antitrust opt-out litigation claim. This was a highly profitable investment for the Funds and illustrates one of the 
fundamentally oriented investment areas in our rate- of-return portfolio. Within the rate -of-return sleeve of our 
portfolio, we seek opportunities to invest in idiosyncratic situations where outcomes are not driven by, or highly 
correlated with, the equity market. While many of our sub -strategies are arbitration-oriented, there are also a 
number of other areas that involve deep fundamental analysis. Litigation claims, such as the Visa and Mastercard 
opt-out claim, are an example of one of these areas, especially when we believe the risks are concentrated around 
the amount and timing of collecting payment rather than the validity of the claim itself. 
 
The opt-out claim we purchased was bought in partnership with a claims trading firm with which we have built a 
+5-year relationship and which had previously helped us source several similar opportunities. The claim arose from 
the 2005 antitrust class action lawsuit against Visa and Mastercard in the Eastern District of New York, which was 
brought by U.S. merchants who accepted Visa- and Mastercard-branded credit and debit cards. The lawsuit alleged 
that the card networks violated antitrust law by fixing interchange fees, imposing anti -steering rules, and 
maintaining network rules that suppressed competition. After yea rs of discovery, pre -trial litigation, and failed 
settlement agreements, in 2018, several of the country’s largest merchants chose to “opt out” of participating in 
the class action lawsuit. “Opt -out” merchants hired their own counsel and pursued higher individual recoveries 
relative to the broader class.  The remaining claims were subsequently referred to as “opt -in” claims, and we 
continue to have exposure to this class of claims through other investments made by the Funds.  
 
We acquired our “opt-out” claim from one of these merchants in a privately negotiated transaction that closed in 
January 2025. This situation was highly opportunistic as the merchant was undergoing a corporate sale and wanted 
to monetize its claim quickly. This dynamic allowed us to acquire the claim below the levels at which other opt -
out merchants had recently settled with Visa and Mastercard. 
 
Our research made us optimistic that we could reach a settlement agreement significantly above where we 
purchased our claim. First, we believed Visa and Mastercard were motivated to avoid a public trial that would 
scrutinize their highly profitable busines ses and could potentially lead to treble damages. Second, Visa and 
Mastercard’s disclosures in their SEC filings showed they had settled similar opt-out claims well above our purchase 
price. The companies’ disclosures also showed a clear trend of increased opt-out settlement activity over time, and 
our research also suggested settlement levels had been increasing. Third, we interpreted a summary judgment ruling
