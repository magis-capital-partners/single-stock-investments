It seems each time we send out a quarterly letter a major economic or geopolitical event 
requires commentary. This quarter is no exception. The U.S. and Israeli military strikes on 
Iran currently get top billing, which pushes the ouster of President Nicolás Maduro from 
power in Venezuela by the U.S. in January to the background. 
 
While the strategic and political motives for each event might seem opaque and controversial 
to some, the obvious connection that interests us as investors is each country’s role in the 
global supply of oil and other commodities. Opinions vary widely among  informed and 
thoughtful observers of the impact on the availability and price of exports from the Mid dle 
East over the next year or more. Many are shocked that oil is not priced much higher in the 
futures market and are very concerned that constrained supply of related commodities such 
as sulfur and fertilizer could become a crisis.  Regardless of the short and intermediate 
volatility in the price of commodities, we are confident in our framework that just like water 
always finds its level, commodity prices always revert to their long-term cost of production. 
 
We tend to believe the market efficiently discounts all potential macroeconomic outcomes 
for the obvious first and second order shock effects, though undoubtedly there will be 
surprises along the way. We take seriously the possibility that the supply disruption has yet 
to fully impact on the global economy, akin to COVID in February of 2020. As of today, the 
strength in the stock market implies that the spike in energy prices is unlikely to send the 
world into a recession. Since we do not attempt to make macro calls, that is a conclusion we 
are willing to absorb. 
 
We will wrap up this introduction section by making two points, the first from a very long -
term perspective and the second that’s more immediate and practical. 
 
1) The U.S. actions in Iran and Venezuela likely mark more datapoints in the 
deglobalization trend. While we remain steadfastly bottom -up, fundamental 
investors, we cannot help but observe the breakdown of the prior world order that 
led countries to become dependent on imports for critical supplies, including energy. 
It seems obvious to us that we are moving into a low er-trust environment where 
countries and regions will implement policies to become more self -sufficient. Data

CONTRIBUTORS 
Shares of Intrepid Potash (IPI)  increased by 54% in the first quarter of 2026. In early 
March, the company reported strong profitability  for its fourth quarter 2025 compared to 
losses in the prior year. This earnings improvement was driven by strong volume and price 
growth in Intrepid’s Trio segment as well as solid potash prices. During the first quarter, the 
price of potash in the U.S. corn belt increased by 4% to $485 per ton. In March, Intrepid also 
announced that it had collected an $8 million deposit on the possible sale of its Intrepid South 
acreage in New Mexico’s oil fields. (In early April, the company announced the completion of 
that sale for $70 million in cash.) This cash infusion further strengthens the company’s 
already strong balance sheet. 
 
In January of 2026 we exited our position in Endeavour Silver (EXK)  after a 43% price 
increase in the month of January. We began investing in silver miners in October of 2023 
when the price of silver was $21 per ounce. We were drawn to the commodity because the 
silver market began running a supply deficit in 2021 and silver miners required much higher 
silver prices in order to incentivize production increases. At that time, we purchased shares 
of MAG Silver (MAG) near $10 per share. In May of 2025, Pan American Silver (PAAS) 
announced that it would acquire MAG Silver in a stock deal that valued MAG shares near $20 
per share. We then sold the shares of MAG Silver but remained bullish on silver even though

DETRACTORS 
 
Shares of uranium miner Encore Energy (EU) declined by 27% in the first quarter of 2026. 
In March, the company reported fourth quarter uranium production of 139,000 pounds 
which was a sequential decline from the 227,000 pounds of uranium production in the third 
quarter. The company said that the f ourth quarter production decline was a result of 
temporary variability in ore grades. For the full year, Encore’s total uranium production 
increased from 264,000 pounds in 2024 to 705,000 pounds in 2025. The uraniu m market 
remains structurally short of supply. Encore should benefit as it increases uranium 
production into the tight uranium market. 
 
Shares of TrueBlue (TBI)  decreased 14% in the first quarter.   In February, TBI reported 
mixed results with revenue exceeding estimates. However, mix negatively impacted 
margins.  Revenues grew 5% organically as management noted stabilizing demand trends 
with reduced customer hesitancy to hire. Current trends in the ASA staffing index remain 
encouraging and support management’s statements. The ASA staffing index tracks weekly 
changes in temporary employment. In September, the index moved into positive territory 
for the first time since late 2022 —and has remained positive since —finishing the fourth 
week of March up 5.1% year over year. TBI remains significantly undervalued and we are 
confident TBI can effectively manage through the cycle and achieve our normal  earnings 
estimate. 
 
Shares of Standard Motor Products (SMP) decreased 5% in the quarter, a move that looks 
disconnected from recent fundamentals.   In February, SMP reported results which exceeded 
market expectations, with organic sales growing 4.5% YoY with all three segments 
contributing. The Nissens acquisition continues to deliver, tracking ahead of estimates with 
continued market share gains in Europe. EBITDA margins expanded by 130bps YoY on 
continued operating efficiencies. Guidance was in- line with expectations, with p otential

During the first quarter we initiated a position in Avnet Inc. (AVT). Founded in 1921, AVT is 
one of the world’s largest electronic component distributors. A substantial portion of AVT’s 
business centers on semiconductor distribution in industrial and auto end markets. After a 
two-year inventory glut in lagging edge semis, demand is beginning to improve as 
inventories are worked down. European and North American demand was hardest hit over 
this period; however, both geographies are showing early signs of improvement. Europe and 
North America carry the highest margins and improving demand is forecast to drive both 
margins and normal earnings higher. 
 
In the first quarter we also initiated a position in Seadrill Limited (SDRL) . The company 
owns and operates offshore drillships and semi- submersible rigs which they contract to 
global oil and gas producers for use in oil and gas extraction. The company has 12 modern 
rigs (~15% market share) and a strong balance sheet. As a result of a deep industry 
downturn from 2014 to 2021, the number of global offshore floater rigs declined by more 
than 50%. In addition, the drillship industry has become highly consolidated. With the 
recently announced acquisition of Valaris by industry leader Transocean, the top three 
players will own almost 90% of industry ships. Demand for offshore oil and gas production 
is set to increase as major oil producers look to increase reserves which have declined in 
recent years. This demand increase into the limited supply of offshore drillships should 
result in higher day rates and earnings for owners of these assets like Seadrill. 
 
We exited five positions during the period. Endeavour (EXK) and Kennametal (KMT) were 
mentioned above. Two others also represent the successful culmination of our investment 
thesis for each – NexGen (NXE) and Vishay Precision Group (VPG). 
 
The fifth , Scholastic Corp. (SCHL) , represents an illustrative misjudgment. We first 
purchased the stock pre- COVID on the expectation that a new CFO could lead efforts to 
improve the company’s cost structure and permanently lift margins. As mentioned in past 
letters, we are much more discerning when it comes to  an investment thesis that relies on 
company-specific, internal execution and would not buy that stock today under similar 
circumstances.

shut down. As the market leader in the publishing and distribution of children’s literature, 
SCHL relies on its in-school book fairs and clubs for a significant portion of revenue. Earnings 
collapsed, and we made the correct decision to stay in the stock until schools reopened and 
fundamentals recovered. Our thesis, however, continued to embed structural margin

We began this letter with the Iran strike and the Maduro ouster because they are the kinds 
of events that appear to create an unusual amount of uncertainty but actually repeat on a 
regular basis. The world will continue to produce surprises — geopolitical, economic, and 
otherwise. The objective of our investment philosophy is not to forecast the outcomes of 
these shocks but to own companies that are resilient enough to absorb them and, where 
possible, positioned to benefit. Balance sheet strength and durable earnings power are most 
valuable when the future is least clear. During our career that has been a common 
environment, and it is the environment for which our process was built.
