For most of human history, there was little opportunity to compound wealth in any
meaningful way. Economic growth was nearly nonexistent, surplus capital was scar
and living standards improved little across generations.
What changed was not simply that people became smarter or worked harder. It was

The math of compounding is simple: initial capital × (1 + annual rate of return) ⁿ , wh
n is the number of years. Each variable matters, but time is what gives compoundin
its exponential power. Given enough time, even a relatively modest rate of return ca
turn a small initial sum into something very large. The longer compounding continu
the more the results become concentrated in the later years.
Benjamin Franklin understood compounding well enough to leave long-dated
bequests to Boston and Philadelphia in his will, structured to earn interest over
generations. But even Franklin might have been surprised by what public equity
ownership eventually made possible. His £1,000 gift in 1790 was roughly equivalent 
$4,400 at the time. At an 8% annual return, that initial sum would have grown to mo
than $300 billion by 2025. Few investors have a 200-year horizon, but the example
illustrates how dramatic the effects of compounding can become over time.
That is why Warren Buffett made over 99% of his wealth after what most people wo
consider retirement age. That is not some strange exception. It is simply what happ
when capital compounds for long enough.
A 10% annual return over a century is a remarkable outcome, but in real time there 
major obstacles that stand between investors and the benefits of compounding.
One is that there is no law that says progress must occur continuously or smoothly.
Future returns depend on businesses continuing to solve problems, create value, an
grow their earnings power. Because future returns are uncertain, investors can neve
know for sure whether a new risk is temporary or a sign that something more
fundamental has changed. That uncertainty can tempt even long-term investors to
prefer the apparent safety of cash or bonds, even at the cost of giving up much of th
benefit of owning productive assets.

The last decade is a good example. The S&P 500 returned an exceptional 14% annua

A low-cost index fund is a sensible default option. It gives investors ownership of a
broad cross-section of productive businesses while helping them avoid many of the
errors that plague active management.
That matters because wealth creation in markets is highly uneven: a small number o
companies account for a very large share of long-term returns, while most stocks
underperform the market and many produce negative lifetime returns. Indexing
ensures investors own the next Apple, Amazon, or Google before the market fully
reflects the value those businesses will ultimately create, even though it also means
owning many companies that decline or disappear.
This skewed outcome is not surprising in a knowledge-creating economy. Most
companies do not discover fundamentally better ways of serving customers, and eve
those that once did often stagnate as new competitors emerge with better ideas. Th
few that continue finding better ways to solve important problems can create

The purpose of the Saga Portfolio is to invest in businesses that appear unusually w
positioned to create substantial long-term value, but whose share prices still do not
fully reflect that future earnings power. The goal is not to depend on macroeconom
forecasts, avoid drawdowns, or use leverage to chase extraordinary returns. It is to b
selective and invest where the gap between price and value appears widest.
If that analysis is directionally correct, the portfolio should outperform not by
predicting short-term market movements, but by owning more of the businesses th
drive an outsized share of long-term wealth creation. Active management can make
sense for investors willing to put in the time and effort required to identify better
opportunities, understand them thoroughly, size them appropriately, and hold them
through greater volatility. Given the power of compounding, even a modest edge ca
produce a materially different long-term outcome over time.
A steep decline in a stock price can reflect a real decline in business value, but it do
not automatically mean something is broken or permanently impaired. Rather, it is 
prompt to reexamine the thesis and ask whether the market’s criticism is correct,
exaggerated, or mistaken in light of the business’s underlying economics and
competitive dynamics.
The clearest current example in the Saga Portfolio is The Trade Desk, which is facin
the greatest market skepticism and has experienced one of the largest drawdowns
among our holdings. The Saga Portfolio first bought shares in 2017 based on the the
that programmatic ad buying would grow, demand-side platforms (DSPs) would
consolidate into a small number of scaled winners, and one of those winners would 
an independent platform. More than eight years later, the main elements of that the
have largely been borne out. The Trade Desk emerged as the leading scaled
independent DSP, while revenue, earnings, and market value grew substantially.
