Returns and Exposure Summary  The Q1 drawdown should be understood in the context of the portfolio's positioning. We entered the year with high exposure to AI infrastructure and related themes, including agentic AI, the emerging CPU bottleneck, optical interconnect, India datacenter/electrification beneficiaries, and select China AI/cloud opportunities. In March, many of these areas sold off together despite improving fundamentals. • The sharp April rebound supports our view that the selloff created an attractive entry point. As of the April monthly estimate, the Fund was up approximately +22.23% month-to-date, with estimated year-to-date performance recovering to approximately +9.92%.  • Indian equities were a large source of the April recovery. Meesho contributed almost 300bps and our India datacenter/electrification basket contributed another approximately 300bps. This was achieved despite the Indian market remaining down roughly 9% in USD terms at the end of April, while the Fund was up roughly 9% year-to-date on an estimated basis. We think this relative resilience is important: our anchor market has been a headwind, not a tailwind, and foreign investors remain meaningfully underweight India. Any normalization in flows should create additional room for catch-up. • The balance of the April recovery was broad-based across our core AI infrastructure and cloud exposures, with Intel, Terawulf, Soitec, Alphabet and Amazon among the other meaningful contributors. The rebound was not a single-stock bounce, but a recovery across the Fund's key themes: AI infrastructure, compute bottlenecks, hyperscale cloud, datacenter power, and the private-public crossover book. • We remain deliberately positioned away from the most crowded expressions of AI. We continue to believe the best risk-adjusted opportunities are not simply the obvious GPU beneficiaries, but the structural bottlenecks and second-order beneficiaries that the market is still underestimating: CPUs, datacenter power, interconnect, colocation, India electrification, and China AI localization. Return Attribution Q1 was characterized by a broad risk-off move across technology, intensified by concerns around AI disruption, software deflation and the West Asia conflict. This impacted both longs and privates. However, we believe the quarter reinforced the importance of our investment framework rather than invalidating it. • Our work on software deflation and agentic AI led us to avoid or reduce exposure to parts of software and IT services that we believe are structurally vulnerable. The market is beginning to recognize that many software companies and staffing/payroll ecosystems are exposed to AI-driven automation, lower incremental software pricing, and lower demand for certain categories of white-collar labor. • Our research increasingly points to the CPU as an underappreciated bottleneck in the agentic AI stack. Consensus entered 2026 focused almost entirely on GPUs and memory. We believe that view is incomplete. As autonomous agents scale, the CPU becomes critical because agentic workloads generate massive I/O, orchestration and instruction-feeding requirements. If CPUs cannot keep pace, expensive GPUs sit idle. • Intel became a core expression of this thesis. Our checks with supply-chain partners, packaging experts and foundry specialists increased our conviction that Intel is doing the right things technically and commercially. Multi-core agentic CPUs could command materially higher ASPs than legacy CPUs, while Intel's EMIB and Foveros packaging technologies are becoming more strategically relevant as chipmakers search for alternatives in advanced packaging. • Our India datacenter and electrification basket continued to validate the Fund's process. We built this theme from the ground up by mapping demand, mapping supply-chain beneficiaries, and validating the thesis through on-the-ground diligence. We estimate India datacenter capacity can scale from approximately 1.3GW today to roughly 8.5GW by 2030, creating a large opportunity across civil construction, power equipment, cooling, interconnect and IT infrastructure.

Outlook on AI Our outlook is organized around the same AI investing framework we have used internally: structural bottlenecks with enduring pricing power; full-stack control across hardware and software; and deflation engines that compress costs at scale.• The agentic AI transition is the most important shift we are underwriting. Today, token consumption is still constrained by humans interacting with copilots and chatbots. The next phase removes that constraint. Humans will define objectives, while swarms of autonomous agents execute workflows. Once that happens, compute demand can become nonlinear because software will be running software, agents will be calling other agents, and enterprises will begin to deploy digital labor at scale.• This is why we are focused on what breaks inside the datacenter when autonomous agents scale. GPUs remain important, but CPUs, networking, power, cooling, interconnect and orchestration software become increasingly critical. The best investments will be the companies that either control these bottlenecks or enable hyperscalers to compress cost per unit of intelligence.• China remains an important part of this opportunity. We believe China's AI ecosystem is entering a self-reinforcing phase: low model costs, high adoption, integrated cloud platforms, local chips, and strong application-layer experimentation. China may not lead in frontier datacenter training, but it can lead in applied AI, edge AI, physical-world automation, and low-cost inference.• At the same time, India is becoming an equally important AI infrastructure market. The Indian market has been treated as an afterthought in global AI, but we believe that is changing. India's datacenter capacity is scaling rapidly, hyperscalers are committing capital, and the government is increasingly focused on AI infrastructure, power reliability and datacenter policy.• The public-market implication is significant. India's legacy IT services sector has historically represented the country's technology exposure. We believe the next cycle will look different. Software deflation and AI automation will pressure parts of legacy IT services, while capex will rotate toward datacenters, power equipment, cooling, interconnect, semiconductors and digital infrastructure.The Q1 drawdown was painful, but we believe it created a compelling opportunity. The April recovery reinforces our conviction that the portfolio is exposed to real, improving fundamentals. We remain focused on owning companies where the delta to consensus is large, where our diligence creates a differentiated view, and where the market is underestimating the durability of the growth and pricing-power cycle.Thank you again for the privilege of managing your capital.
