The REB Emerging Markets Financials Fund LP (“The Fund”) returned +2.57% during April, underperforming both the 
MSCI Emerging Markets (“EM”) and the MSCI EM Financials Index by 1214 and 272 basis points, respectively. Last 
month’s jaw-dropping performance in the core EM index was yet again driven almost entirely by semiconductor 
stocks, with the “Big3” EM semis (i.e. Taiwan Semiconductor, Samsung Electronics, and SK Hynix) up +17.2%, +29.1%, 
and +51.6%. Though the melt-up in semis has been a global AI-driven phenomenon – the Philadelphia Semiconductor 
Index appreciated +37% last  month – the euphoria has been particularly pronounced in EM , as the Big3 alone 
account for over 24% of the MSCI EM Index, 1 a collective weight that has increased by more than +60% since June 
of last year.2 As we said in our February investor letter, “in the short run, these situations always get more painful 
and more ridiculous than we could have ever imagined.” 
 
REB’s EM financials strategy has beaten MSCI’s core EM index rather handily in the five years leading up to 2026, so 
we’re not going start complaining about the perverse procyclicality of cap-weighted index construction in a passive-
dominated investment world. That said, it is preposterous that Taiwan Semi is both the largest index weight in the 
MSCI EM Growth Index (14.6%) and the largest weight in the MSCI EM Value Index (7.9%).

With that in mind, the biggest mistake REB could make right now is focusing too much on the parabolic equity price 
moves outside of our financial sector mandate, and not enough on the opportunities and risks emerging within our 
USD 2.2 trillion dollar asset class. 4 On that front, it needs to be acknowledged that we also underperformed our 
financial sector benchmark by 272 basis points during April, bringing our YTD underperformance to 202 basis points. 
Much of this underperformance can be attributed to our zero weight in Korean financials, which rose +11.7% in April, 
largely in sympathy with the +34% gain in the Korea’s big board KOSPI Index.5 The April bounce in the MSCI Korea 
Financials Index was the best across the entire EM financial sector.  
 
REB believes the ongoing re-rating in South Korea’s financial sector is almost entirely attributable to spillover from 
the wall of money flooding into the local memory sector, rather than any sort of sustainable uplift in the profit 
generation capacity of the former. While it is true that the margin-amplified retail trading binge on local AI/memory 
stocks has been a boom for Korean banks’ investment and securities divisions, investors do not typically ascribe a 
higher multiple for such windfalls in non-core fees and gains, as they are inherently unsustainable. But even a never-
ending boom in the memory cycle would do little to mitigate the structural challenges for Korea’s over -leveraged 
and over-regulated banking industry [CHART 1].6 This is perhaps best evidenced by the fact that, despite the recent 
assist from their investment and securities divisions, the overall profitability of Korea’s banking sector remains 
moribund, with the sector-wide return on assets for 2025 at 62 basis points, which is bang in- line with the 10-year 
historical median.7  
 
While our zero weight in South Korea has been the primary culprit of our YTD underperformance vs. our financial 
sector benchmark, there have also been some material performance detractors in the book that have exacerbated 
the situation. At the top of that list are the Fund’s Indonesian investments, which have been a performance albatross 
since the first quarter of 2024. Most of the Fund’s recent losses in Indonesia have been concentrated in Bank Rakyat 
(BBRI IJ), which cost the Fund 39 basis points worth of absolute performance in April and 90 basis points YTD. This 
P&L destruction has led REB to ask itself, are we missing something?  
 
As discussed in last month’s investor letter, REB did reduce exposure to Indonesia by roughly ~240 basis points during 
early March as it became clear that the local market remains vulnerable to shortages of various energy -related 
commodities, despite the country’s status as a net energy exporter. That said, REB has clearly underestimated the 
extent to which Prabowo Subianto’s particular brand of “strong man” fiscal populism has damaged investor 
enthusiasm for the market. REB does see encouraging signs of late that Prabowo is learning from his early missteps, 
with the recent clean -up of the MSCI kerfuffle showing a new sense of urgency and pragmatism in maintaining a 
capital-friendly environment for foreign investors.
