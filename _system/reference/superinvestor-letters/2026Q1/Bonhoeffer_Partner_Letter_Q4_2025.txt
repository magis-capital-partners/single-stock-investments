SELLING TOO EARLY AND HOLDING TOO LONG 
I am constantly seeking to improve my process by examining what has worked both personally and for 
other investors I respect.  I have examined the cases over the past five years in which  I have sold stocks 
too soon ,  sold to prevent losses, or held positions too  long.  The positions held to o long fall in two 
categories, one where the focus has been quantitative cheapness versus other trends and the other is 
cyclical businesses with futures that are driven by forces out of management’s control. We have sold all 
of our statically cheap firms without longer term positive trend s and/or shareholder friendly 
management.  This is one reason for the large capital losses in this year’s K-1. These accounting losses do 
not tell the full story of the economic value of the current portfolio. On the flip side , should the fund 
succeed in holding winners for longer periods then the realized accounting gains would be reduced on 
future K-1’s. 
When making the hold or sell decision, the most challenging cases are frequently cyclical businesses . In 
our case, we have a situation in which the longer-term trend of increased demand for housing exists, but 
shorter-term risks of higher interest rates and building restrictions still exist. We have identified a 
segment that is capital light and has operational leverage that should benefit from the flow of building 
materials to build homes, however, housing starts have recently declined to the lows of the great financial 
crisis. Builder’s First Source has higher margins today than after the great financial crisis due to increased 
scale and scope. 
The stocks I sold too soon had less cyclicality than I originally thought. These included  Wilhelm 
Wilhelmsen (a RoRo shipping firm), Millicom (a LatAm telco firm) and Buzzi Unicem (a cement firm).  My 
thoughts on the shipping firm was the recovery was over but the n it continued and given the oligopoly 
Wilhelm Wilhemsen  competes in, the returns may be sustainable. For Millicom, there was a change in 
management that I underestimated in terms of the amount of runway and value they created over time. 
For Buzzi,  I underestimated the extent the company could increase sales and profit over the next 5 years. 
Fortunately, most of the sales  (ten in total)  resulted in re -investing in higher return opportunities .         
 
PRIVATE CREDIT RECKONING   
Private Credit has begun a reckoning with some poor underwriting . This has largely been  due to 
originating loans  based upon fund raising levels versus a required rate of return.  Some investors in these 
new interval funds had a duration mismatch between their investment time horizon and the maturity of 
the underwritten loans  (typically 3 to 5 years) .  Most Business Development Companies ( BDCs) 
originated a large number of loans over the past two years as competition for leveraged finance boomed. 
These loans were originated with lower interest rates and looser covenants than in previous years , 
leading to lower returns on equity.  
Sixth Street Specialty Lending (TSLX), a BDC with excellent underwriting, actually reduced the size of its 
portfolio over the past two years. This discipline is illustrated in TSLX’s portfolio metrics that reflect 
lower non-performing loans, higher return on equity, and higher portfolio interest coverage compared 
to the average BDC.   
Many of the interval funds  (such as Blue Owl and Blackstone) have had to gate redemptions due to 
higher-than-expected redemption requests.  This may cause the selling of some loans to meet these 
redemption requests. Another possibility is if credit line advance rates are reduced to the point where 
there are forced sales to meet lower advance requirements.  TSLX has the dry powder to purchase these 
loans as well as  originating new loans as spreads widen.  Another factor driving future redemptions is 
the discount between publicly traded BDCs to interval funds with similar or the same sets of loans.  
In addition, in the broadly syndicated loan market, a more liquid loan market for larger borrowers, TSLX 
can purchase loans and lever them up via securitization financing with its recently announced joint 
venture with Carlyle. This will provide more portfolio diversity by investing in larger and higher quality 
loans and levering them to reach TSLXs target net investor returns of mid-teens returns on equity. This 
plays to TSLX’s strength as their underwriting process was developed while underwriting commercial 
loans. 
 
 
  
CASE STUDY: MERITZ FINANCIAL (138040.KS) 
Meritz Financial is a health insurance and security brokerage company who uses insurance float  and 
brokerage liabilities (via deposits and debt) to finance a Korean real estate and fixed income portfolio. 
The health care insurer has scale generating 4% re turn on assets (typical of other large healthcare 
insurers) and the brokerage firm has a specialty in restructuring Korean real estate.  Meritz has a 
decentralized business model where it can finance premium growth or contraction in its business lines 
and geographies. Meritz has strategically acquired investment banking teams to expand its target 
markets. Meritz has implemented management equity compensation plans ahead of other Korean 
investment banks thus attracted the best talent. 
The investment portfolio is an important part of the profit and book value growth of Meritz.  The portfolio 
(₩124.2 trillion) primarily consists of bonds (47%), loans (47%) and equity (3%).  In 2025, this portfolio 
generated returns of 3.1% per year.  The insurance segment bonds are duration matched to the insurance 
liabilities.  The liability side of the balance sheet includes float (₩32 trillion), debt (₩72 trillion), deposits 
(₩15 trillion) and common equity (₩11 trillion).  The 2025 insurance profit of 8% resulting in net cost 
of float of -4.4% per year (-profit/portfolio value) and the 2025 cost of debt and deposits is 2. 3%. The 
resulting total cost of liabilities of 0.6%. The equity leverage is 11x (portfolio/common equity). Using the 
2025 return on investments of 3. 11% times leverage (11x) minus liabilities/equity time the  cost of 
liabilities of 0.6%, investment banking income which offsets G&A costs  and a 25% tax rate results in a 
RoE 21%. 
Meritz has four levers for earnings growth: 1) increased healthcare insurance market share ; 2) more 
efficient operations; 3) more investment banking fee income and 4) repurchasing stock. Management is 
selective in the firms and teams they acquire. When they can’t find a suitable acquisition, they can invest 
in their fixed income portfolio.   
Meritz has a long runway for growth in its investment banking business and invest ing insurance float, 
deposits and debt into its fixed income portfolio. Float has increased by 4% per year and deposits by 42% 
per year over the past 5-years. This has resulted in a five-year EPS annual growth rate of 35% and 28% 
book value and dividend growt h rates. The insurance portfolio is about 40% with remaining 60% 
associated with brokerage segment.   
Industry Segments 
Meritz competes in primarily the healthcare insurance and brokerage business in Korea.  Meritz 
competes primarily in the long -term healthcare insurance market. This coverage is supplemental 
insurance as Korea has public healthcare insurance. The supplement al coverage is provided by large 
property and casualty insurance firms such as Samsung Fire and Marine, DB Insurance, Hyundai Fire and 
Marine. and Meritz Fire and Marine.  These competitors have a 75% market share.  The insurance liability 
duration is about 10 years so policies generate a large amount of float.  The insurance market is growing 
in line with Korean GDP, 1% to 2% per year. Meritz F&M has the highest return on equity of the insurance 
providers and has been taking market share over time. 
Meritz is the largest real estate security brokerage business i n Korea. It has expertise in restructuring 
distressed real estate.  Meritz brokerage business has provided high yielding investment for its insurance 
float with a 100 bp premium to other Korean insurance providers.  
Given the amount of latent value buried in Korean Chaebols (large, family controlled business 
conglomerates in South Korea) , there is a large pipeline of corporate actions for investment banks to 
facilitate. Meritz has one of the best employee and shareholder aligned compensation systems, with long-
term vested equity incentives to attract the best investment banking talent. The brokerage business has 
access to low-cost deposits and other forms of leverage.  
Sources of growth for Meritz include organic growth in their insurance and brokerage business and 
acquisition growth in their brokerage segment by purchasing banking teams . Organic growth in these 
segments is expected to be 3% annually,  with any other growth coming through share repurchases. 
Meritz operations have become better over time, as disciplined investment strateg ies and share 
repurchases have yielded rewards. The 5-yr trailing return on equity has increased from 5.8% in 2013 
to 22.5% in 2024. The drivers included increased health insurance market share, consistent insurance

2025, a 5% CAGR. The investment leverage was increased from 10.0x to 11.0x between 2023 to 2025.

    Management and Incentives 
Meritz’s management team has developed a brokerage team M&A engine and operationally efficient 
firms in the insurance and securities brokerage industry. They perform M&A when targets are available 
at the right price, and return capital via buybacks when there are not opportunities to invest organically 
or buy investment banking teams.  Buybacks have been in the 5-6% range over the past two years. 
The compensation for the management team is 30% base and 70% incentive compensation. Options are 
granted after 3 to 4 years of service and in the years thereafter. In addition, deferred cash bonuses are 
tied to total shareholder returns and return on equity metrics. The top three executives have 
compensation of 5% of net income. The CEO and executives currently hold 55% of common shares with 
the Chairman holding 54%. Since 2018, 1.2% shares outstanding per year were issued to employees.

Valuation 
 
The key to the valuation of Meritz is the expected growth rate. The current valuation implies an earnings 
decrease of 0.4% in perpetuity using the Graham formula ((8.5 + 2g)).  The historical 10-year earnings 
growth has been 17% per year including acquisitions and the current return on equity of 21%.  
A bottom-up analysis based upon market growth rates of  Meritz’s markets’ (insurance and re-insurance) 
results was used to estimate an organic growth rate of 4% for Meritz, 2% GDP growth plus 2% for more 
M&A activity as Chaebols unwind. This does not include any future acquisitions. Incorporating 
operational leverage and buybacks results in a 13% EPS growth rate. Historically, Meritz’s EPS growth 
rate was 28% per year including three acquisitions over 10 years. If we assume no acquisitions over the 
next seven years, a forward EPS of 7% growth per year is conservative. Using a 7% expected growth rate, 
the resulting current multiple is 23x of earnings, while Meritz trades at an earnings multiple of 9x. If we 
look at insurance comparables in the US, which are larger but have slower growth prospects , they have 
an average earnings multiple of 13x. If we apply 13 x earnings to Meritz’s estimated 2026 earnings of 
₩15,322, then we arrive at a value of ₩199,316 per share, which is a reasonable short-term target. If we 
use a 7% seven-year growth rate, then we arrive at a value of ₩324,653 per share. This results in a four-
year (assuming growth will be reflected in Meritz’s price over the next four years) IRR of 31%.

Risks 
The primary risks are: 
• slower-than-expected M&A transaction and restructuring growth in Korea; 
• lower-than-expected growth in Meritz’s insurance markets; and 
• a lack of new investment opportunities (new IB teams) coupled with higher stock prices making 
buybacks less accretive. 
Potential Upside/Catalyst 
The primary catalysts are: 
• higher-than-expected M&A transaction and restructuring growth in Korea;  
• faster growth in Meritz’s insurance markets; and 
• increased local scope or purchase of local scale in new markets.
