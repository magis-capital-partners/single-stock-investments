Reflections on the offshore energy trade
This week, Transocean (RIG) announced its intended acquisition of Valaris (VAL), thereby further 
consolidating the offshore drilling space. At KEDM, we have been involved in offshore energy since both 
Noble Corp. (NE) and VAL emerged from bankruptcy in 2021. With clean balance sheets and a slowly 
recovering energy market, we believed both companies were well-positioned to benefit significantly if 
offshore energy were to eventually recover from its decade-long bear market.
In the 3 years that followed UDW day rates recovered from roughly $200k to $505k, which was still well 
short of the $681k booked by the Transocean Deepwater Pathfinder in 2013.
In mid-2024, we were all caught off guard when a shortage of FPSOs, further down the energy value chain, 
created a bottleneck. The industry hit a pause, and tender activity came to a halt. Rigs had to get sent back 
to the Canary Islands to get warm-stacked, while some outdated semi-submersibles went straight to the 
scrap yard.
We exited our position in 2025, and while we picked the bottom and offshore perfectly, our exit could have 
been timed a little better.

Our concern was that the 2024/25 pause in offshore activity would extend until 2026 (it did) or maybe 
even 2027 (it might). Furthermore, low utilization rates tend to go hand in hand with lower day rates 
(day rates fell to $380k). VAL was looking to have 7 of 13 offshore rigs off contract by the end of 2025 and 
would need to get more aggressive on pricing. RIG had been disciplined in putting all its rigs to work, in 
part forced by its high leverage, while VAL failed to understand that it is a price taker, not a price setter.
Modern 7g or 8g rigs are also becoming more efficient, with drilling speeds having tripled since the last 
cycle. Could we see a situation where we need fewer rigs to do all the work, similar to the permanent drop 
in the US onshore rig count?
With day rates down into the high $300k, and oil floating around $70, we are genuinely surprised to see 
offshore having performed as well as it has.

Industry insiders still expect global Offshore spending to recover in 2027. However, this spending is still 
pending an FID, and any project slippage would move this further into the future.
Figure: Tidewater’s projections of offshore spending
Reflecting on the acquisition by Transocean, it’s a win-win-win. Valaris shareholders get to cash a quick 
premium of roughly 31%, paid in RIG shares. RIG gets to deleverage its balance sheet, cut back some 
redundant overhead, and show its shareholders accretion on EV / rig. RIG has a track record of doing 
acquisitions funded with shares, which are effectively hidden capital raises (remember Ocean Rig?).
Meanwhile, a less leveraged RIG can be less aggressive in bidding on new work, and it can now potentially 
retire some of its 6th-generation rigs, which should benefit the entire industry.
This brings us to today, where the major drillers are all trading at low-double-digit FCF multiples. That’s 
quite rich for companies with depreciating assets and a cost of debt of >8%. It is still cheap on steel value, 
especially relative to today’s replacement cost, but at least some recovery is priced in. We think the long-
term trend remains intact, but we’ve moved on to other trades.

Refiners
As we’d described in our September 2025 (Vol # 241) write-up on refiners, we are now expressing our view 
on oil & gas through refiners. Demand for refining capacity tracks oil consumption, which means it grows 
roughly 1-2m bbl per year. But supply growth should be constrained in the second half of this decade. 
Meanwhile, most refiners trade at a fraction of replacement cost.
The US large-cap plays (VLO, MPC) on this theme have performed well since the write-up.
Figure: VLO and MPC stock performance since the KEDM write-up on refiners
Crack spreads have been volatile, but the overall direction is up. They have benefited from the fact that 
Russian refineries are not drone-proof (see the spike in November) as well as strong diesel demand from 
Europe.

A potential return of Venezuelan crude further benefits the high-Nelson-complexity US Gulf Coast refineries, 
which are well equipped to handle the much cheaper Venezuelan crude. The real benefit happens when 
Venezuela increases its crude production, which is probably a few years out. But S&P Global reports that 
US refiners are already processing low hundreds of kbpd, while they historically processed 500-800 kbpd. 
Venezuelan crude can easily add a few dollars to the crack spread.
Figure: US crude oil imports from Venezuela  
Source: Bloomberg
But with all the cyclical factors impacting crack spreads, it’s easy to lose sight of the longer-term trend: 
new refining capacity is falling off a cliff starting this year. As we explained earlier, this supply forecast 
includes refineries in Nigeria (Dangote) and Mexico (Dos Bocas) that are unlikely to reach their nameplate 
capacities.

We fully expect crack spreads to remain volatile in the short term. Peace between Ukraine and Russia, or 
at least a halt to Ukraine’s drone attacks on Russian facilities, would be bearish. And an end to Russian

difficulty Russia faces in maintaining its refineries without access to the required spare parts.
We’re here for the long-term trend, and we would welcome any short-term dip.

KEDM Kliff Notes
Inflection Notes: 
• Trump’s EPA terminates Obama’s 2009 “endangerment finding” declaration, which determined 
that CO₂ and other greenhouse gases endanger public health and welfare and provided the 
legal basis for regulating greenhouse gas emissions under the Clean Air Act. This is the ruling 
that Ford (F) and General Motors (GM)  were waiting for, and which we highlighted in last 
year’s automotive write-up.
Spin Notes:
• An Indian $29bn market-cap, diversified natural resources and energy group, Vedanta (VEDL 
IN), updated on the demerger. The company is in the process of demerging into five specific 
commodity companies to basically eliminate conglomerate discount. The listing of the entities 
is planned for this March, and each shareholder will receive 1 share in each listed entity. The 
main business units are cyclical, yet the company as a group has had strong profitability. Worth 
looking at.

Investor Day Notes:
• DSM-Firmenich (DSFIR NA)  just experienced a one-two punch combo to the face. It finally 
sold its volatile ANH unit , but while the price (€2.2bn + €0.5bn earn-out) was pretty much in 
line with expectations, cash will come in much later than previously guided, and the €500m 
buyback is also lower than expected. Making it all worse, this Thursday, management pretty 
much messed up the results, failed to provide any guidance, cut the call short, and stuck to 
unrealistic medium-term targets. But a clear catalyst could be the upcoming investor day in 
March. We’re quite sure it will be a bullish story. Might make for a good trade.

CEO Turnover Notes:
• GEO Group (GEO) founder Zoley is stepping back into the CEO role to lead the company through 
this transformative period. Both CXW and GEO have corrected somewhat, partially due to the 
political backlash against ICE and the $10bn DHS still not being approved (the 4-year $75bn 
ICE funding through the OBBB is still available). GEO’s guidance for 2026 was also very soft (it 
assumes an 8% contribution margin), but this is partly due to startup expenses as it reactivates 
new facilities. Were they sandbagging, or are new contracts just not as profitable? The real risk 
is the Dems taking over Congress after the midterms.
• CSL (CSL AU)  is having a difficult year. The shares recently dropped to multi-year lows on 
deteriorating results, largely due to lazy management. And indeed, the CEO has recently been 
kicked out, paving the way for some kitchen-sinking, followed by asset sales, and we suspect 
also the divestment of the struggling vaccines unit. In the meantime, there’s uncertainty, which 
means opportunity. Too early perhaps, but good to keep this one on the watchlist.
SPAC Notes:
• Talking about SPACs, Reservoir Media (RSVR)  is interesting. While the shares have only 
gone sideways since their initial derating in 2021, Reservoir has quietly been doing its job: 
acquiring music publishing and recording rights and steadily growing revenues and earnings. 
The company has a simple rinse-and-repeat model with tons of visibility. So 13x EV/EBITDA for 
>40% EBITDA margins and ~70% FCF conversion (pre-music right purchases) does not seem 
much. And there’s plenty of inorganic growth potential left. Irenic probably agrees and has 
been increasing its stake (now over 9%). Expect more action.
Newsletter Short Notes:
• Kyndryl (KD) sold off 54% after announcing a CFO change as well as delayed financial filings 
pending an accounting review. This follows a public short report by Gotham City Research in 
March of last year, accusing the company of inflating key financial metrics. With a lot of ‘dumb 
stuff’ in correction mode, it is worth scanning the newsletter short monitor for some alpha on 
the short side.
• Not a Newsletter Short, but interesting nonetheless. Solvay (SOLB BB)  has clear short-term 
potential in our view. It’s still holding up decently in bad markets, but that’s mainly due to its 
8.5% dividend. End markets remain horrible, and 2026 is going to be another challenging year. 
We don’t think that the company’s cash generation will be sufficient to sustain its dividend. 
The upcoming FY results might make for a good moment to cut it, but that would not be well 
received by the market.

life. Good recent EPS growth on rising interest income, falling funding costs, and a steadily 
improving net interest margin. Deposit growth is picking up (mainly commercial deposits),

Merger Notes:
• UniFirst (UNF) is once again in play as Cintas recently renewed its long-running effort to buy 
the company. UniFirst confirmed it is now actively reviewing Cintas’ latest $275 p/s offer. While 
the Croatti family’s control and dual-class structure mean nothing can happen without their 
approval, Cintas has been pushing for years, and the current discussions feel more serious than 
past attempts. We might see an offer of >$275 p/s, which, honestly, would be a good deal.
• We’ve highlighted InPost (INPST NA) a few times; calling this company will be taken out. And 
indeed, on Monday, the company accepted an offer from a consortium of major shareholders 
(PPF, the CEO, Advent) and, surprisingly, FedEx. And while the spread to the €15.6 p/s offer is 
only ~3%, it’s still a decent place to park some cash for a few quarters. The chances that the 
deal breaks are basically zero, with the consortium holding roughly 50% and nobody wanting 
to return to the pre-deal Allegro worries.

Strategic Alternatives Notes:
• A reminder that Array Digital Infrastructure (AD)  is liquidating a lot (all?) of its assets and 
returning cash. It already sold its wireless business to T -Mobile for $4.3bn, paying a $23 p/s 
dividend back in August and a $10.25 p/s dividend in January. More deals monetizing its 
spectrum are most probably coming, incl. its C-band spectrum within 12-18 months, ahead 
of the 2027-28 auction window. What will remain is a rather large tower portfolio, which we 
could see divested in the future, given its low implied valuation.
Tender Offer Notes:
• Yext (YEXT), which recently dropped massively after its CEO withdrew its $9 p/s offer, launched 
a tender offer to purchase $180m of its shares  (~27% of the market cap). The price range is 
$5.75 to $6.50 (current share price: $5.52), running until March 12.
Other Interesting ED Action (but that doesn’t have a separate monitor):
• Webjet (WEB AU) recently dropped 30% after the company disclosed a tax audit in its Spanish 
subsidiary. The shares recovered a bit from then but remain very much below the levels of a 
few weeks ago (roughly $4.75). The headlines are scary, but the Spanish subsidiary is tiny! The 
business represents ~3% of the group’s activity and contributes something like $1-2m in EBIT 
per half-year. It really seems immaterial. BIG overreaction. h/t to our friend Jeremy Raper for 
flagging this one.
• You don’t often find stocks that go sideways while the company is undergoing big changes. But 
Global Dominion Access (DOM SM)  does. DOM has been reshaping itself into a much more 
asset-light services platform. Revenues have been quite steady over the past few years; as a 
result, margins have improved to record levels. And said revenues are now largely recurring. 
They’re also selling assets for €175m, which will push them into net cash by the end of the 
year. At <5x EV/EBITDA, we see potential management buyout rumors.
• ICON Plc (ICLR) traded down 40% after reporting the results of an internal investigation into its 
accounting practices, which could lead to a 2% restatement of revenue. The earnings impact is 
likely larger than 2% (10% of EBITDA if you assume a 100% drop through margin), but this feels 
like an overreaction. The $9bn EV is now well below the $12bn they paid for PRA only 4 years 
ago. CROs are seen as potential AI losers by the market, so buyer beware.

 Fertilizer 11/12/2022
After nearly a decade of fertilizer prices in the doldrums, prices for potash, phosphates, and nitrogen hit near all time highs in 
March and April driven by increased energy costs, geopolitical conflicts, and supply chain and logistical challenges. Prices have fallen 
in recent months as the supply picture begins to look less dire and demand destruction sets in. With several projects to increase 
capacity in both potash and phosphates, and falling natural gas prices and the reopening of nitrogen fertilizer production facilities in 
Europe, fertilizer prices may continue to fall from what are currently extremely elevated levels.
