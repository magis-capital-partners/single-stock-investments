Let’s Make a Deal In our last letter we discussed our plans to build an internal AI-powered system to supercharge our investment team.   We thought we would share some of our early learnings and our new approach to achieving these goals.  Our Process and the Original Build We think of fundamental long/short equity investing in four sequential stages: idea generation, due diligence, the investment decision (where valuation and sizing live), and ongoing diligence on positions already in the portfolio. Our initial design for Garth (our proprietary internal AI system) was holistic. We set out to replicate the Broad Bay analytical process inside the system and have AI augment every stage, with the goal of supercharging our investment team. 
 What We Learned Working through the initial build, we quickly identified where Garth was going to need substantial work, and the most important shortcoming surfaced in valuation. Garth’s outputs were systematically biased toward mean reversion. Companies trading at depressed multiples relative to their own history would screen as cheap; companies at elevated multiples would screen as expensive. The framework had no view on whether those valuation gaps should close or widen. The system kept guiding us toward classic value traps. Pattern recognition over historical data tends to produce outputs that look like the historical mean, which is the opposite of what a fundamental investor needs. Finding industries or companies that will look very different in the future than in the past is probably the single biggest defining feature of our entire investment process, and it’s not something today’s AI is very good at.   Working Backwards Rather than continue to replicate the front part of our process, we have refocused Garth on the backend of it: position monitoring and ongoing due diligence. AI is a natural fit for this stage of the work because computer brains are particularly good at one specific thing humans struggle with, which is staying dispassionate as new data points enter a thesis. The AI does not feel the sting of a position moving against it. It does not protect a publicly committed view from disconfirming evidence. It does not let a thesis curdle into an identity. Those are the failure modes that may quietly hurt fundamental investors, and they are structurally absent within AI. From this starting point we plan to work backwards through our process, building proprietary AI-driven research tools that feed into ongoing diligence first, then extending those tools into the investment decision and due diligence stages over time. Idea generation will be the last stage we touch, but we think by working backwards we can ultimately get there in a more streamlined manner that will have lasting positive impacts.

Building Bayes Bayes’ theorem is the formal rule for how a rational agent should update beliefs as new evidence arrives: a prior view, adjusted in proportion to how informative the new evidence is, becomes the updated view. Essentially this is how position monitoring and ongoing due diligence is structured by constantly evaluating new data points against existing beliefs. The classic example of this is the “Monty Hall” problem, in which the game show host offers the contestant a chance to switch guesses when a bad outcome is revealed. There’s a scene with Kevin Spacey explaining this concept in the movie “21” which is based on Ben Mezrich’s book Bringing Down the House. (The book is great; the movie is awful.) The simplest description of the system we are now building is as follows. For every position in our portfolio, a thesis will be defined. Garth will ingest this framework and continuously evaluate the company against it. Earnings transcripts, expert calls, management changes, management meeting notes, SEC filings and news stories will all get fed into the system, and the job of the AI will be to decide which of these are  relevant to the thesis we have and surface them for the analyst.   This is essentially Bayesian updating at scale and with a lot less human bias. Every position is a prior, every new data point is evidence, and the discipline of investing is the discipline of weighing new evidence against the prior. The positions that hurt fundamental long/short investing are generally ones where there are thesis creeps, or the world changes and we don’t adjust quickly enough.  We think starting Garth here will be valuable for our current day-to-day lives and also give it a great foundation to scale into other aspects of our process.  The Human Brain and the AI Brain The table below summarizes how we now think about the division of labor across the cognitive tasks that matter for fundamental investing. Capability Human Brain AI Pattern recognition Deep, experience-shaped intuition; identifies subtle anomalies in familiar territory. Vast breadth across many sources; surfaces statistical patterns no human could process at scale. Emotional response to evidence Can be subject to anchoring, motivated reasoning, sunk-cost effects, and thesis-as-identity drift. Dispassionate; no ego attachment to a thesis; processes new evidence without psychological friction. Calibration May be overconfident on easy questions, underconfident on hard ones, but capable of genuine doubt. Sounds equally confident whether right or wrong; cannot reliably flag its own uncertainty. 
Judgment on appropriateness Strong; can argue why a multiple should compress or expand based on regime, structure, or behavior. Currently weak; defaults to mean reversion, which is the wrong default for fundamental investing. Creativity and origination Originates contrarian theses from constraint, lived experience, and lateral thinking. Recombines existing patterns; current technology rarely produces a genuinely novel insight. Bayesian updating Capable in principle but potentially subject to bias from emotion and identity; failure mode is failing to update at all. Mechanically strong at updating against pre-defined criteria; failure mode is updating on noise.

Press Your Luck The launch of Operation Epic Fury on February 28th reshaped our outlook on the U.S. consumer in a matter of weeks. We do not pretend to be geopolitical experts, and we have no edge handicapping the path of the conflict from here. We do, however, think the oil market has been too quick to assume that a settlement returns prices to where they were before the strikes. Even if the conflict were resolved tomorrow, the refinery capacity that has been damaged or destroyed will not be returned online overnight. The repairs will take many months at a minimum, and the more severely damaged assets will be out of service considerably longer. Our base case is higher oil for longer, and by extension higher gasoline prices. The impact on the U.S. consumer has been muted thus far in 2026, but this is largely a function of timing. Tax refund disbursements have run materially heavier this year than last as a result of the OBBBA (no tax on tips, etc.). After analyzing daily tax refund data from the IRS, we estimate roughly $50 billion of incremental refund cash will have arrived in consumer bank accounts through April relative to the same period in 2025.  This money has hit just as gasoline prices have moved sharply higher, and, as of late April, the cumulative net impact on the consumer is a positive $36 billion. We worry that this windfall is fleeting. In 2025, gasoline prices were lower than 2024 throughout the year, providing the consumer with roughly $29 billion of tailwind from cheaper fuel. At the current spot price of $4.04 per gallon held flat for the balance of the year, the same calculation flips to roughly $100 billion in headwinds in 2026.  There have been many shocks to the consumer since the great Covid stimulus days such as SNAP reductions, Student Loan payments resuming, tariff related inflation, etc.  However, at current prices, this will be the largest direct negative impact to the U.S. Consumer in the past 5 years.

Shop ‘Til You Drop  In the first quarter of 2026, Broad Bay initiated a long position in VF Corporation, owner of iconic fashion franchises including Vans, The North Face, and Timberland. Years of brand mismanagement and poor capital allocation have eroded the business considerably, leaving many investors convinced the company is structurally impaired. We disagree. In our view, VFC presents a potentially transformative turnaround opportunity where the market underappreciates the impact of leadership change, early brand inflections, and quiet growth across the rest of the portfolio.  The story starts with Bracken Darrell, appointed CEO in July 2023. Investors who have followed Darrell's career will recall his decade at Logitech, where he transformed a stagnant hardware business into a high-single-digit grower while expanding operating margins from 3% to 15%. He has wasted no time applying that same playbook at VF. One of his first major decisions was to exit non-core, loss-making brands like Supreme and Dickies and refocus the company on its core franchises. Darrell has also built a powerhouse team around him, bringing in former Spotify CFO Paul Vogel, former Lululemon Chief Product Officer Sun Choe, and Tiffany Beers, co-creator of the Nike Air Yeezys (who followed Darrell from Logitech). The most consequential near-term opportunity for VFC is the Vans turnaround. The brand is pursuing a deliberate reset: closing over 150 unprofitable DTC locations, destocking value-focused wholesale accounts, and rebuilding brand relevance through product innovation. Early signs have been encouraging. Search trends and website traffic recently turned positive for the first time since 2019. We believe Vans is also well-positioned if broad consumer pressure persists this year. Legacy styles average just ~$70 at retail, a relative bargain to trendy peers like New Balance, Birkenstock, and Salomon, which typically retail for $100–200. This value proposition is capturing attention, and a recent post comparing a $995 Prada sneaker to a $60 Vans Authentic went viral on X/Twitter. Beyond Vans, the rest of VFC’s portfolio provides a stable and increasingly compelling foundation. The North Face continues to expand into new categories, with footwear growing double-digits across every region and management actively expanding the brand beyond its winter-centric attire. Timberland continues to release new colorways, collabs, and products in adjacent categories like boat shoes. These brand revivals serve as an instructive roadmap for Vans and reinforce our confidence in management. Finally, Altra Running, a performance running shoe brand, is currently VF's fastest-growing franchise, on track to reach approximately $250M in revenue this year. We view Altra as a hidden gem within VF’s portfolio and first came across it at a conference several months ago. At the time, we were struck by how an emerging brand with little national awareness was already showing clear signs of market share gains. While we don’t embed significant upside from Altra in our forecasts, we're encouraged by what it says about VFC's ability to cultivate emerging brands and grow the business generally.   We believe VFC can achieve its profitability targets by fiscal year 2028, which could drive considerable upside to current consensus forecasts.  One of the things that always catches our eye at Broad Bay is a potential turnaround story where the market has conflated prior mismanagement with permanent brand impairment, and we believe VF Corporation fits that description well. Gross attribution, alpha, and contribution analyses do not reflect the deduction of fees and expenses that an investor has paid or would have paid. To understand the overall effect of fees, please refer to the fund’s estimated net return in comparison to the fund’s gross attribution and gross rate of return figures.
