March 2026 Commentary 
Dear Partners and Friends,
Desert Lion Capital Fund returned approximately -0.7% net for the month of March, compared 
with -17.4% for the JSE All Share Index (J203). Year to date, the Fund has returned +6.5% net, 
compared with -4.8% for the JSE All Share Index (J203). 
The JSE experienced a broad selloff, led by the heavyweight precious mining stocks. Returns in 
USD were further diluted due to exchange rate weakness on the part of the ZAR as the market 
adopted a risk off approach in the midst of the Middle East conflict. The Desert Lion Capital Fund 
portfolio benefited from our position in Sasol (I wrote about Sasol in our February 2026 letter), 
while the rest of the stocks in the portfolio held up just fine. This performance further supports 
the Fund’s portfolio characteristics and investment strategy as communicated: 
 The Fund represents a concentrated portfolio of our best ideas selected through a bottom-
up approach, based on a research driven and value-oriented investment process.
 Although the Fund is concentrated, it is diversified in the composition of factors and 
variables driving the performance of the underlying portfolio constituents. 
 On a portfolio level, the Fund is benchmark agnostic and has low correlation to indices. 
 Ultimately, the Fund aims to deliver index beating returns, with the nature and drivers of 
those returns being differentiated and lowly correlated from the index or large fund 
alternatives. 
Quite simply put, Desert Lion Capital Fund offers exposure to a portfolio of companies that cannot 
be accessed through conventional products or solutions. It presents a genuinely unique value
proposition. 
We expect the returns of the Fund to continue to be more volatile, but not riskier, than those of 
the alternatives. The combination of superior growth fundamentals and lower valuation multiples 
presents an appealing setup of lower risk thanks to margin of safety, and higher prospective 
returns from the combination of growth and the optionality of multiple expansion to fair value. 
We expect that returns will come in chunks. 
In this month’s letter, I will briefly touch on one of our top 6 positions that we have gradually built 
over the past year. 
iOCO (JSE ticker: IOC) 
iOCO is an IT services provider based in South Africa. It was previously known as EOH. 
The predecessor, EOH, has a tumultuous history. Founded by Asher Bohbot and listed on the JSE 
in 1998, EOH grew into one of South Africa's most celebrated IT companies through an aggressive 
acquisition strategy, absorbing over 270 businesses and growing revenue from R59 million in 
2001 to R15 billion by 2017. The share price surged from around R0.70 in 2001 to more than R170 
by late 2016, making it an institutional darling and a fixture of virtually every major SA equity 
portfolio. 
   
www.desertlioncapital.com    |    investorrelations@desertlioncapital.com  
The unraveling began in 2016–17 when media reports surfaced alleging that EOH had secured 
government contracts through bribery and corruption. Forensic investigations revealed rigged 
bids, payments to subcontractors for no work done, over invoicing, and payments to politically 
connected middlemen. Corporate governance was virtually absent, with no anti-corruption 
policies, no internal audit committee, no head of risk, and excessive authority concentrated 
among a few directors. Bohbot resigned as CEO in mid-2017, and the share price collapsed.  
Stephen van Coller, appointed in 2018, inherited a business needing a R3.2 billion rescue facility. 
He overhauled the board, implemented governance reforms, cooperated with authorities, and 
sued former executives for R6.4 billion. 
Fast forward to 2024, and we find a cleaned-up company with good governance and controls in 
place, and sustainable revenue in the 25% to 30% GP margin range. Enter Rhys Summerton, the 
guiding hand behind Argent Industrial’s transformative capital allocation and growth trajectory, 
as an active shareholder. 
Under the stewardship of Rhys Summerton (CEO), Dennis Venter (active shareholder), and 
Ashona Kooblall (CFO), the company rebranded as iOCO in December 2024 and embarked on a 
new path of value creation and sustainable growth. 
The strategy was based on three stages: 
1. Cost rationalization: Management identified significant inefficiencies in the business and 
took the hatchet to unnecessary or bloated costs to get to a more optimal cost structure. 
2. Decentralization: Through a combination of radical autonomy, ruthless accountability, 
and aligned incentivization, each business division was positioned for growth with its own 
CEO. 
3. Capital allocation: A focus on cash generation, reducing debt to conservative levels, share 
buybacks at discounts, and earnings enhancing acquisitions. 
We monitored their implementation closely and gained increasing conviction as they made 
successful progress. The strategy is clearly working, as can be gauged from the financial 
performance metrics in the charts on the following pages.

iOCO strikes us as a classic mispricing. Due to the checkered past, it has fallen off everyone’s 
radar, and no one is looking at it. We have an advantage in monitoring a universe of stocks in the 
unloved and inefficient part of the market. We believe we also have an informational advantage 
through the combination of our experience and institutional knowledge of the market, our on-
the-ground presence and network, and engaging with management and relevant parties to get 
direct, unfiltered information. 
At around R4.20 per share, iOCO stock is trading at a PE of about 8 times and a FCF yield of about
14%. We expect earnings and FCF to grow at decent double digits for the foreseeable future. Add 
further earnings per share enhancements from share buybacks and accretive acquisitions, plus 
the distinct possibility of M&A at group level, and we think the setup is quite compelling.
