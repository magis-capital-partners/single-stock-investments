in most cases, whoever is right about the future prospects of a company will be rewarded14.

Current Investment Conditions & Selected Investment Outlook 
There are contrasting extreme narratives to the two major investment themes (private credit and artificial 
intelligence) we discussed at the start of this letter: 
• Private credit:  Some fear that “private credit is a bubble that could lead to another financial crisis” 
while others think that “private credit ecosystem is very healthy” and the fears are led by “mass 
hysteria.”  
• Artificial intelligence : Likewise , there are many who believe that advancements in artificial 
intelligence could spiral out of control to the point where there could be mass unemployment, while 
at the same time, many others believe that artificial intelligence may be an overhyped bubble, have 
very little practical use and represent an enormous waste of capital investment.  
While it would be nice to opt out of having a view on these two major arguments, their ramifications span 
many public and private investments, so we are forced to give the matter some thought.  Our humble view 
is that the answer to these questions is difficult to know on private credit and nearly impossible on artificial 
intelligence, but likely lands somewhere in the middle of the most extreme outcomes.   
 
That said, we believe it may be possible to identify potential winners and losers in both categories based on 
track record, capital allocation, management quality, valuation and other factors.   
 
There is very little doubt that the next few years for private credit are likely to be much more difficult than 
the past.  The vast sums of private credit capital raised have almost certainly led to relaxed credit 
underwriting standards, as these unregulated firms attracted both retail and institutional investors with a 
unicorn marketing pitch of high returns and virtually no volatility as traditional banks were sidelined with 
much more onerous capital requirements and regulatory scrutiny after the financia l crisis.  This delayed 
credit risk combined with the industry’s abnormally high exposure to software companies at risk of 
technological disruption could likely lead to far higher default rates than prior experience.  In addition, there 
are Johnny-Come-Latelies that either overpaid18 to get in the game, have no experience managing credit 
through a downcycle ( Cliffwater) or have inferior business structures (such as publicly traded business 
development companies19).  For firms that are not adequately diversified or well-capitalized, it is possible 
that many could go bankrupt.  We estimate that some firms such as Prospect Capital and PennantPark 
Investment (PNNT) may end up having little equity value, while others such as Blue Owl Technology 
Finance (OTF) may survive but will have to cut their dividends  as their reliance on “payment-in-kind” 
interest reveals that these companies do not actually earn enough  cash to fund their stated distributions.

That being said, if there will be one man left standing in private credit, we believe it will be Apollo Global 
(APO).  Apollo has pioneered the use of captive insurance capital (with its formation of Athene many years 
before peers belatedly followed the same path), has a long history of deftly shorting credit (including some

Apollo will not be immune20 to challenges in credit markets, we believe that Apollo will make more money 
from turmoil than they will lose and is priced at an attractive valuation.   
 
Perhaps the most notable aspect of private credit is its illiquidity, with most private credit funds having five 
percent quarterly liquidity provisions.  At first glance, this appear to be a flaw worthy of skepticism and 
criticism, because if the valuation marks for private credit are right  (as they claim) and the loans are first 
lien secured instruments, there should be no reason why investors who ask for their money would be barred 
from receiving it.  However, this provision is a feature, not a bug, of the system.  Silicon Valley Bank and 
Lehman Brothers would never have failed if their depositors and prime brokerage clients , respectively, 
were limited to only five percent withdrawals per quarter.  It is precisely this protection that prevents a “run 
on the bank” for private credit and it is for that reason that we do not see the inevitable and imminent 
challenges in private credit leading to a widescale, systemic financial crisis.   
 
We do not have to go far back in financial history to find an analogy.  Blackstone’s flagship private real 
estate fund ( “BREIT”) was the poster child for the rise and fall of non-traded real estate trusts, growing 
rapidly from retail investors  to peak at $6 8 billion in net asset by the end of 2021 .  Every seasoned real 
estate investor knew that BREITs net asset value was wrong when it claimed to grow by 8% in 2022 while 
the publicly traded REIT index fell by more than 20%.  When BREIT investors tried to cash out, they were 
“gated” in December 202 2 and could not redeem more than five percent of their funds per quarter.  
Blackstone even went so far as to give a large institutional investor a sweetheart deal that guaranteed it an 
incredible 11.25% return in an effort to shore up confidence among investors.  BREIT’s future performance 
has been a shell of its past, with three-year annual returns of 3.0% compared to its 13.1% annualized return 
since inception prior to its 2022 redemption gate. What was the result?  Today, BREIT still has $55 billion 
in net assets and has begun thumping its chest again.  Had BREIT honored unlimited redemptions in 2022, 
it is unlikely the fund would be in business today.   
 
In conclusion, we believe that (in aggregate) private credit will generate lower returns than in the past and 
likely see its fundraising slow , but it is unlikely to cause widespread financial turmoil.  One important 
impact, we believe, is that traditional banks will benefit from the retreat of some of the ir largest, most 
aggressive competitors and that is one reason we are net long traditional banks and net short private credit.

Gross Long and Short Profit and Loss Contribution is calculated by taking the dollar amount of profits or 
losses (excluding fees and operating expenses) attributable to long and short positions respectively and 
dividing by the total dollar amount of profits or losses for the period before fees and expenses.  Return on 
Capital is calculated by dividing P/L Contribution by Average Exposure.  Please note that this is designed 
to show the portion of the total P&L coming from long and short investments and does not show the return 
that an investor would have received from just the long portfolio or just the short portfolio. Hypothetical 
performance is performance that was not actually achieved by any portfolio managed by Steamboat.  The 
numbers shown under Return on Capital may be deemed to be hypothetical because they are returns divided 
by exposure and not the actual returns achieved . (Alternatively, Return on Capital may be deemed to be 
simply another measure of performance, because it is the actual calculated return on capital of the portfolio.) 
In addition, projected, targeted or potential returns on individual investments (which are included in some, 
but not all quarterly letters) are hypothetical. There are significant risks and limitations in using hypothetical 
performance in making investment decisions. Upon request we will promptly provide sufficient information 
to enable recipients of this document to understand the risks and l imitations of using such hypothetical
