Earned Conviction 
 
At the end of Q1 2026, roughly 65% of Fund capital was focused in our top 10 public long 
investments (the largest was Magnite Inc. at 8.9%). While concentrated, this focused style is 
emblematic of how we invest. O ur style is to develop deep conviction to take a reasoned 
stance that current market price is incorrect and should improve in the future to our benefit.  
 
One of our favorite sayings is “Fundamental investing works because it does not work all the 
time.” Said plainly, stock prices often deviate from company fundamentals for many reasons: 
macroeconomic perceptions, asset flows, style factors, and industry sentiment. Taking 
advantage of these natural deviations is a primary drive r of our strong track record since 
inception (Note: annual track record net of fees and expenses since inception is on page 15). 
 
The majority of this letter will unpack two top 5 sized long investments, both of which we 
added to during the first quarter. The first, Magnite, Inc. ( NASDAQ:MGNI), is our largest 
position at quarter end. The second, Driven Brands ( NASDAQ:DRVN), is timely example of 
the non-linearity inherent with fundamental conviction investing.

To level set, Magnite, Inc. (“Magnite” or the “Company”) ( NASDAQ:MGNI) is a leading 
independent sell -side platform which sells advertising inventory to video, audio, mobile, 
desktop display, and connected TV advertisers. The crown jewel business is connected TV: 
Magnite operates the technology stack for 29 of the top 30 streaming companies (only missing 
YouTube as a wholly owned Google subsidiary and related sell -side ad tech platform )  to

Is Magnite’s Multiple Contraction Reasonable? 
 
Over the past few pages, we have been discussing Magnite as an individual business and 
stock. Yet that conversation is only part of the story. Publicly traded stocks transact within a 
dynamic ecosystem including industry peers and competitors, each of which are subject to 
investor sector perception changes.

Herein lies the two reasons Magnite shares have declined in price over the past two quarters. 
The following discuss es the two “strawman” arguments regarding Magnite share weakness 
and why we believe differently:  
 
(1) The Trade Desk Weakness (89% correlation): While Magnite is the largest 
independent sell-side advertising technology provider, The Trade Desk (NASDAQ:TTD) 
is the marquee buy-side advertising technology provider. Formerly a Wall Street darling, 
The Trade Desk overplayed their commercial hand during 2025 which caused a sharp 
rebuke by customers and Wall Street (The Trade Desk shares fell 68% during calendar 
2025). The Trade Desk’s recent efforts to assuage customers and Wall Street did little 
to stem the pain with shares trading down another 40% in Q1 2026.

The short version of why this occurred is: The Trade Desk (with take rate >2x Magnite’s) 
attempted to assert further industry dominance by pushing technological changes which 
customers neither desired nor valued. Concurrently, competitors closed the tech  and 
value add gaps versus The Trade Desk and thus earned market share. 
 
Much of The Trade Desk’s recent stock price decline was a lofty starting valuation (41x 
NTM free cash flow at 9/30/25) which left little-to-no room for operational missteps or 
weakness in Wall Street’s perception. Conversely, Magnite’s starting valuation (20.8x 
NTM free cash flow at 9/30/25) was quite reasonable given continued growth 
expectations of ~12% per annum.  
 
The Trade Desk valuation has declined to 23x NTM free cash flow as of 3/31/26.  The 
perceived best in class ad vertising technology business declining so significantly has  
weighed on industry sentiment . We believe the price and valuation decline was partly 
responsible for Magnite’s languishing stock price which ended Q1 at just 9.6x NTM free 
cash flow. This multiple is far too punitive given Magnite’s steady drumbeat of customer 
wins translating into double digit free cash flow growth. In fact, we believe T he Trade 
Desk’s struggles have been a tailwind for Magnite’s business as advertising agencies and 
other DSPs have further leaned on Magnite to increase return on investment for 
advertisers. For a business continuing to grow and deliver recurring free cash flow 
growth, a mid-teens multiple of free cash flow is more reasonable over time. For Magnite 
this would equate to a stock price of $20 to $25 (potential 6 5% to 100% stock price 
appreciation over the next few years).  
 
(2) Software Industry Weakness (94% correlation): Artificial intelligence is on the tip 
of investors’ tongues these days  as advancing large language model functionality 
threatens to reshape many industries. Perhaps the industry most in jeopardy is software 
given the thinking : “AI will simply code companies new software more applicable and 
tailored to their specific market vertical and niche. Then one company’s AI agents will 
simply conduct business with the other company’s agentic AI and render legacy software 
superfluous.” 
 
Applying this line of thinking towards the advertising technology industry, the ultimate 
bear case would be: “advertising sellers and buyers can simply find each other’s agentic 
AIs out in the open  instantly and transact without a middleman.” In this case, neither 
The Trade Desk nor Magnite would have a role in selling advertising impressions. While 
an interesting theoretical exercise, we think this hypothetical is overly simplistic and 
misses key elements of how advertising is both sold and delivered. 
 
Since the dawn of time, products have been bought and sold via marketplaces. 
Regardless of physical or digital form, marketplaces today serve as the primary effective 
place to (1) specify what product is being sold, (2) agree on a price, and (3) deliver the 
product to the b uyer. Applied to the digital advertising landscape, sell side platforms 
such as Magnite play a vital role in all three areas: 
 
• Aggregation and Ad Targeting: Digital ad buyers expect a mix of fine-tuned 
demographic targeting  and broad -based reach . Magnite plays a role in 
assembling both a wide range of prospective ad supply that has been built over 
many years and also provides many unique ad vertising inventory via exclusive

partnerships with top tier publishers. Given the fragmented nature of the media 
landscape, very few publishers (other than some “Magnificent 7” such as Google, 
Meta etc.) have the scale that would make it economically feasible or attractive 
to build their own agentic advertising technology stack. Rather, a substandard 
platform would lack the above attributes Magnite supplies to buyers. 
 
• Efficient Pricing and Delivery: Magnite’s platform allows nearly instantaneous 
real time bidding (far less than one second). The marketplace demands this 
speed and Magnite’s platform provides the requisite platform for buyers and 
sellers to quickly transact. Magnite has nearly two decades of experience and 
now processes nearly 1.5 trillion advertising requests per day.  
 
• Infrastructure and Data : Over the years, Magnite has spent hundreds of 
millions of dollars building out the Company owned infrastructure and 
technology to be able to deliver on the needs for ad sellers and buyers. The 
combination of data, unique relationships, and infrastructure would make it 
difficult for any individual publisher to eliminate SSPs without a material 
detraction in performance. In actuality, we believe Magnite will be able to 
leverage artificial intelligence to reduce its own costs to serve advertisements 
which will benefit both their customers and their own margins. We believe these 
relationships are much stickier than the current valuation implies. 
 
In aggregate, Magnite’s marketplace platform provides demonstrable value for both ad 
buyers and sellers. In fact, artificial intelligence could be a positive driver for Magnite. 
AI driven advertising auctions require a marketplace (i.e. platform) on which to 
exchange. Magnite plays a vital role here as the largest vendor independent of media 
companies or Google  (biased towards their own inventory and/or auction platforms to 
the detriment of advertising buyers).

share repurchase authorization  and management has noted that they believe current share

Accounting Inconsistencies 
 
In early February 2026, Driven Brands released financials for Q1 to Q3 2025 including 
resegmenting to show their auto glass segment (catalyst #3 from our original memo). The 
stock was trading around $16 per share and we were anticipating a solid 2026 guide on Q4 
earnings in late February. 
 
However, during Driven’s audit closing process, accounting inconsistencies arose leading the 
Company to delay the delivery of both their Q4 earnings and (more importantly) their annual 
10-K filing . The market reaction was deserved and swift: shares declined ~33% to finish 
February at $11.0 per share. 
 
Clearly, this was a negative development. However, each accounting related situation is 
unique and requires precise analysis to determine the magnitude of the issues.  To start, 
Driven’s audit is being performed by PricewaterhouseCoopers, a reputable “Big 4” firm.  
 
PricewaterhouseCoopers did not resign upon this announcement this year after signing off on 
the last two Driven audits in a timely manner. Secondly, Driven’s CFO, Michael Diamond, 
remains in his role (been since Summer 2024). These two items dramatically decrease the 
probability of outright fraud.  Recall, private equity firm Roark owns over half this business, 
which further diminishes the probability of core-level malfeasance. 
 
Parsing the accounting comments further to assess materiality, we believe the accounting 
stems primarily from the divested car wash businesses (albeit other segments also involved). 
Additionally, the time period in question was predominately 2023 and 2024.  Stepping back, 
the car wash issues during 2023 were the primary reason Driven shares traded down to levels 
we believe materially undervalued the businesses’ true value and thus what created to long 
investment opportunity for GrizzlyRock. 
 
Frankly, 2023 and 2024 were the time frames we focused on when making the investment. 
Our focus has always been on Driven’s 2025 to 2027 financial performance lead by Take 5 Oil 
Change.

GrizzlyRock Re-Underwriting 
 
Given the tectonic news and resultant share price change, we re -underwrote Driven Brands 
wholistically during the quarter. Here is what we see today: 
 
• Take 5 Oil Change: This stellar business continues to grow store count, system wide 
sales and EBITDA double digit rates year over year (EBITDA +10% to 15% year-over-
year for Q1 through Q3 2025). Same store sales have continued to grow in the mid -
single digits through Q3 2025 and our franchise conversations reveal broadly 
consistent brand performance. This is all in line with our original 2024 underwriting. 
Take 5 Oil Change is ~2/3rd of total EBITDA and provides the majority of intrinsic value 
today and future value creation. 
 
• Franchise Brands: Usually a steady segment of franchised paint and collision brands 
continues to hover around flat unit count and same store sales. EBITDA was weak mid 
2025 yet that was largely related to shaky consumer confidence for lower affluence 
customers. We think this segment should stabilize towards flat EBITDA over the next 
few quarters. Regardless, the franchised business nature ensures Driven’s prodigious 
free cash flow from this unit remains solid. 
 
• Auto Glass: Financials from this nascent segment have only been released for Q1 
through Q3 2025 with limited management commentary over the past few years. 
Therefore, it’s hard for us to confidently comment about this segment’s cadence today. 
During our original underwriting, we saw a clear #2 United States competitor to 
industry bellwether Safelight with tremendous potential over multiple years. More to 
come on auto glass as Driven’s public disclosure resumes this year. 
 
• Balance Sheet:  Since the car wash troubles emerged Summer 2023 , Driven’s 
management has been consistent in their deleveraging focus. Their goal has continued 
to be net debt leverage of less than 3.0x EBITDA by 2026 year-end. Our math indicates 
this remains on pace via (1) car wash divestiture debt paydown and (2) Take 5 Oil 
Change related EBITDA growth.  Low financial leverage broadens the aperture of 
potential investors (including GARP minded long -only investors). At l ess than 3.0x 
leverage with extremely low-cost franchisee securitized debt, Driven’s growth 
algorithm should shine through to double digit annual free cash flow growth which is 
highly attractive to many investor types.

us to acquire materially more Driven Br ands shares given our years of diligence amid 
exemplarily, ongoing Take 5 Oil Change performance.

So, What is Driven’s Way Forward? 
 
Majority owner Roark successfully brought Driven Brands public in 2021 and sold po rtions of 
their stake in 2021 and 2022 around $30 per share. Since, Driven has floundered on many of 
their non Take 5 Oil Change segments. Clearly, Roark is motivated to resolve these issues 
both for their own investors as well as their public reputation. Chaotic recent events increase 
the probability of event -driven outcomes during 2026 and /or 2027. While many possible 
permutations exist, the most likely paths are as follows: 
 
(1) Separate Paint, Collision, and Auto Glass from Take 5 Oil Change  
 
Paint, collision, and auto glass services are episodic  in nature, have a relatively large 
cost, and often include an insurance claim. On the other hand, Take 5 Oil Change is a 
recurring, low ticket consumer purchase. Other than public company scale and serving 
the automotive aftermarket, there is no need for these businesses to be owned by one 
platform.  
 
Historically, private equity was a major player in auto body repair. Additionally, 
secularly growing auto glass calibration trends fit the standard private equity playbook 
well. Should Roark decide to simplify Driven Brands by divesting part or all of the 
episodic paint, collision, and auto glass business lines, we expect many bids at 
multiples far higher than Driven’s current market multipl e. A potential sale would 
further simplify and de-lever Driven and potentially lead to a near term share price re-
rating.  
 
(2) Simplify and Rebase in the Public Markets 
 
Driven’s aftermarket automotive services for United States consumers can work as a 
public company. In this scenario, Driven does not divest any more businesses and 
instead focuses on growing both Take 5 Oil Change ( continuation of current success) 
and Auto Glass Now (#2 national player on a single operating system with key, growing 
accounts in regional insurance and fleet). 
 
In our base case, the franchised paint and collision businesses remain “cash-cows” and 
provide deleveraging in 2026 and cash to accelerate share repurchase  in 2027. This 
scenario positions Driven Brands stock to work over the next couple years.
