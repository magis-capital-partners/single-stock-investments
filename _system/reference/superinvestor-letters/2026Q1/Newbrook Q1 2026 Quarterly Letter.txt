The S&P 500 fell 4.3% in the first quarter, the worst start to a year since 2022 and a sharp reversal from the 
17.9% gain posted in 2025. Markets were relatively stable through February, but the Iran conflict triggered a 
sharp repricing across oil, inflation expectations, and rates. By March, the environment had shifted to broad 
de-risking, with correlation moving to one and very few places to hide outside of energy. Unlike prior 
volatility episodes, which were largely contained within specific sectors or styles, the March drawdown was 
fast and indiscriminate outside of energy and defensives. Energy was the sole area of strength during the 
quarter, while Financials was the weakest sector. Technology, Industrials, and Consumer Discretionary all 
declined meaningfully. Long-term bond yields rose during the quarter, driven by the energy-driven inflation 
shock rather than stronger growth, with the move concentrated in March as oil surged above $100. Headline 
year-over-year CPI held steady at 2.4% through February, while core CPI printed 2.5%. Though both were in 
line with expectations, economists estimate that the March spike in gasoline prices will push headline CPI 
meaningfully higher when reported, potentially toward 3.3%, complicating the inflation picture. The Federal 
Reserve held the federal funds rate steady at 3.50%-3.75% at both of its January and March meetings, with 
Chair Powell noting that policy is well positioned to allow a wait-and-see approach as officials assess the 
economic effects of the conflict. Markets have largely priced out rate cuts for the remainder of the year, a 
dramatic shift from the three to four cuts anticipated entering the year. We believe the Fed will remain on hold 
for the foreseeable future, caught between energy-driven inflation pressures and a labor market that is showing 
mixed signs.

Macro Overview 
 
 Credit Markets – Credit is holding up better than it should have given the move in energy and rates. 
Investment-grade CDS widened from 51 to 65 bps and high-yield from 318 to 390 bps - a move that does 
not fully price in a sustained inflation shock or refinancing stress. The technical backdrop remains 
supportive, with high all-in yields and limited net issuance, though primary activity slowed in March as 
issuers pulled deals. Default rates remain contained, with S&P Global's speculative-grade default rate near 
3.0%. The area we are most focused on is private credit. Amend-and-extend activity and loose covenants 
continue to mask underlying pressure, particularly for leveraged borrowers with floating-rate exposure. 
This is a timing issue, not a resolution, and risk will increase if rates and energy remain elevated. We 
anticipate these issues will play out over years rather than months, and that this is not a systemic issue as 
most private credit vehicles have minimal leverage. Returns will be disappointing versus history, but 
largely manageable. 
 
 Housing – Housing is effectively frozen. Higher mortgage rates and elevated input costs have stalled both 
transactions and new supply. Until rates move meaningfully lower, it is difficult to see a catalyst for 
reacceleration. Existing home sales held flat at approximately 4.0 million annualized. Mortgage rates, 
which had trended to 6.25% by year-end, rose to approximately 6.7% by late March as Treasury yields 
climbed. New home sales moderated further as builders contended with elevated input costs, including 
roughly $9,000 per home in tariff-related cost pressures according to the NAHB. We maintain a cautious 
outlook, with further progress dependent on normalization of energy prices and rate stabilization. 
 
 Interest Rates – Rates are being driven by oil, not the Fed. The move higher in yields this quarter reflects 
a repricing of inflation risk rather than stronger growth. The 10-year rose from 4.17% to 4.32%, with the 
move concentrated in March as oil surged above $100 and inflation expectations reset. The 2-year rose to 
3.80% and the 30-year to 4.91%. Markets have materially repriced the Fed path. Entering the year, three to 
four cuts were expected; by quarter-end, futures implied effectively no easing, with roughly a 75% 
probability that the Fed remains on hold. The Fed is in a difficult position – policy is restrictive, but 
energy-driven inflation limits its ability to ease into a slowing growth backdrop. Rates remain more 
sensitive to commodity prices than to incremental Fed communication. The ECB underwent a dramatic 
hawkish repricing over the course of March as the Iran war drove energy prices sharply higher, with 
markets swinging from pricing rate cuts at the start of the month to fully pricing a 25 basis point hike by 
July and approximately 80 basis points of total hikes by year-end. 
 
 Employment – The labor market is weakening beneath the surface. Payrolls averaged 68,000 per month 
in Q1, though the monthly prints were noisy: January was revised up to +160,000, February declined by 
133,000 (largely due to a healthcare strike and federal reductions), and March rebounded to +178,000 as 
those workers returned. The underlying trend is softer. Over the past year, net job creation has averaged 
just 22,000 per month, federal employment is down 355,000 from its peak, and labor force participation 
has fallen to 61.9%. Wage growth has moderated to 3.5% year-over-year and hours worked continue to 
edge lower. The labor market is no longer a source of support for the economy. The combination of

 Consumer Confidence – Consumer sentiment is weakening, driven primarily by higher gasoline prices. 
The University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment Index rose very slightly to 53.3 from year-end 2025, 
despite rising concern around the economic outlook and cost pressures. The move in gasoline from 
roughly $2.80 per gallon in December to over $4.00 by late March is a direct hit to lower-income 
consumers and reinforces the K-shaped dynamic in spending. Aggregate consumption remains relatively 
resilient, supported by higher-income households and the expectation of fiscal stimulus. In our view, much 
of that incremental income will be absorbed by higher energy costs rather than discretionary spending. 
From here, sentiment will be driven largely by energy prices and the duration of the U.S.-Iran conflict. 
 
 Earnings – At the conclusion of the Q4 2025 reporting season, corporate earnings demonstrated continued 
resilience. Within the S&P 500, approximately 75% of companies exceeded earnings expectations, in line 
with the trailing pre-pandemic average. The revenue surprise rate was healthy, with 73% of companies 
beating Street estimates. At the sector level, Communication Services and Information Technology led 
with 94% and 90% of companies reporting earnings beats, respectively, while Materials posted the 
weakest showing at 52%. Year-over-year Q4 EPS growth for the S&P 500 came in at 13.2%, above the 
8.4% expected entering the quarter, marking the tenth consecutive quarter of year-over-year earnings 
growth. Information Technology and Industrials led sector earnings growth, while Consumer 
Discretionary and Energy posted the weakest growth. Market reactions to earnings were somewhat muted, 
with companies beating both revenue and EPS estimates trading +1.1% relative to the S&P 500 the day 
after reporting, just below the long-term average of +1.3% since 2010. Companies that missed both 
estimates were penalized -2.0% relative to the index, below the historical average of -2.8%. The market 
currently expects Q1 2026 EPS growth of 13.1% and full-year 2026 EPS growth of 17.4%, though these 
estimates face meaningful uncertainty given the energy-driven inflation shock and its potential impact on 
margins and consumer spending. 
 
We underperformed the index this quarter. The primary source of long-side losses was concentrated in two 
areas: our position in Apollo, which suffered from a broad repricing of alternative asset managers amid non-IG 
private credit fears - an outcome we believe was somewhat indiscriminate given Apollo’s annuity-driven and 
IG credit exposure - and a cluster of names that proved more vulnerable to AI disruption than we had initially 
underwritten, specifically Unity, Fair Isaac, and Rocket. We underappreciated the speed at which AI-native 
alternatives and shifting competitive dynamics would compress the market’s willingness to pay for incumbent 
business services and fintech franchises in a higher-rate, lower-growth environment. We eliminated Unity and 
Fair Isaac during the quarter and added to Rocket, where we believe the risk/reward has improved at current 
levels. Partially offsetting these losses were gains in companies benefiting directly from the AI infrastructure 
buildout and energy transition, including FTAI Aviation, MasTec, and copper exposure, as well as Huntington 
Ingalls, which benefited from continued defense spending momentum. Taiwan Semiconductor also contributed 
positively as demand for advanced compute capacity remained robust despite the macro volatility. The short 
book provided a modest buffer but was not sufficient to offset the magnitude of long-side weakness. In 
hindsight, we should have reduced net exposure more quickly in March as the Iran situation escalated and been 
more skeptical of AI-adjacent businesses without direct infrastructure exposure – lessons we are applying to 
current positioning.

Long Book Overview 
On March 31, our gross long exposure was 64%, a decline from 71% at year end. We eliminated Unity and 
Fair Isaac from the portfolio during the quarter, while adding Hut 8 (detailed below) and ASML. We continue 
to own aerospace names (Airbus, GE Aerospace, FTAI Aviation, Rolls Royce, and Safran) and AI 
infrastructure exposure (Hut 8, MasTec, and Taiwan Semiconductor) as our two biggest themes. Our 
framework heading into Q2 is straightforward: we want to own the companies that build, power, and enable AI 
infrastructure (the “toll roads”) rather than the application-layer businesses that risk commoditization as the 
technology diffuses. This lens drove both our additions and eliminations during the quarter and will continue 
to shape portfolio construction. We remain positioned for a soft landing as the base case but acknowledge the 
range of outcomes has widened materially given the geopolitical backdrop. We are prepared to further reduce 
exposure if the U.S.-Iran conflict re-escalates, energy prices remain elevated, or labor market data deteriorates 
beyond the current trend. 
 
Short Book Overview 
On March 31, our gross short exposure was 25%, unchanged from December quarter-end. We maintained a 
focused set of short positions where we believe fundamentals remain structurally impaired. The short book 
continues to emphasize companies exposed to AI-driven disruption, particularly within IT services and the 
payments and money transfer space where stablecoin and blockchain technologies pose competitive threats. 
We also maintain selective exposure to consumer discretionary and industrial companies facing demand 
normalization and unfavorable operating leverage, as well as technology businesses where valuations remain 
disconnected from underlying fundamentals amid intensifying competition. We remain disciplined and 
opportunistic in managing short exposure based on risk/reward. 
 
Idea Summary 
One current long position in our portfolio is Hut 8 Corp. 
 
Hut 8 Corp (HUT) 
Hut 8 Corp (HUT) is an energy infrastructure platform that integrates power, digital infrastructure, and 
compute at scale to fuel next-generation, energy-intensive use cases. Based in Miami, FL, the company 
operates approximately 710 megawatts of energy capacity under management, 330 megawatts under 
construction, and 1,230 megawatts under development across 15 sites in the United States and Canada. 
Originally known as a Bitcoin miner, Hut 8 has undergone a fundamental transformation under CEO Asher 
Genoot who repositioned the company as a power-first AI infrastructure developer with a multi-gigawatt 
development pipeline. The company spun off its legacy Bitcoin mining operations into American Bitcoin Corp 
in 2025 to sharpen its strategic focus.  
 
We believe HUT is a compelling idea for the following reasons: 
 
1. Landmark Google-Backstopped Lease Validates the Platform – In December 2025, Hut 8 signed a 15-
year, $7.0 billion triple-net lease with Fluidstack for 245 MW of IT capacity at its River Bend data 
center campus in Louisiana, with financial backstop from Google (S&P: AA+, Moody’s: Aa2). The 
lease includes a 3.0% annual base rent escalator, expected cumulative NOI contribution of $6.9 billion 
over the base term (averaging $454 million annually), and three 5-year renewal options that increase 
potential contract value to approximately $17.7 billion. Fluidstack also holds a Right of First Offer for 
up to an additional 1,000 MW at River Bend. Project-level financing at up to 85% loan-to-cost is being 
underwritten by J.P. Morgan and Goldman Sachs, significantly de-risking Hut 8’s equity commitment.

2. Strategic Partnership with Anthropic Creates a Multi-Gigawatt Growth Runway – Announced 
alongside the River Bend lease, Hut 8’s partnership with Anthropic and Fluidstack positions the 
company to develop and deliver between 245 MW and up to 2,295 MW of AI data center 
infrastructure. The partnership is structured in tranches: the initial 245 MW at River Bend, a Right of 
First Offer for up to 1,000 MW of additional River Bend capacity, and the option to jointly develop up 
to 1,050 MW across Hut 8’s broader pipeline. This is a binding commercial relationship with a leading 
frontier AI company, underpinned by blue-chip counterparties. We believe this framework is 
repeatable. Hut 8 currently has an 8.5 GW development pipeline with 1.8 GW under exclusivity and 
5.2 GW under diligence. We expect one to two additional deals of similar scale and structure to be 
announced over the next twelve months, which would represent transformative catalysts for the stock. 
 
3. Power-First Model Creates Structural Competitive Advantage – Scaling frontier AI infrastructure is, at 
its core, a power challenge. Hut 8’s model is differentiated by its ability to originate and control power 
at the site level – a capability that most traditional data center operators lack. The company’s vertically 
integrated approach encompasses site origination, power procurement, data center design and 
construction, and ongoing operations. This end-to-end control enables Hut 8 to move faster, deliver 
capacity at lower cost, and offer counterparties a turnkey solution that compresses time-to-market. In a 
world where hyperscalers are competing fiercely for available power capacity, Hut 8’s ability to source 
and deliver large-scale, permitted, grid-connected sites represents a scarce, strategic entry point in a 
capacity-constrained power market, and has a faster time to market than competitive IPP powered 
options. 
 
4. Attractive Valuation Relative to Contracted Cash Flow Potential – HUT currently trades at a market 
capitalization of approximately $5.3 billion. The River Bend lease alone is expected to generate $6.9 
billion in cumulative NOI over the base term, and the full pipeline opportunity is multiples of that. 
While significant capital deployment is required to realize these projects, the combination of 85% 
project-level debt financing, investment-grade-credit-backed revenues, and a repeatable development 
model enables Hut 8 to grow its contracted cash flow base with manageable equity dilution. To 
illustrate the unit economics: Hut 8 is deploying approximately $2.5 billion in capital to achieve a 
stabilized initial-term NOI of $454 million, representing an 18.1% unlevered yield on cost. Under the 
30-year renewal scenarios, this NOI averages to $587 million. When supported by 85% project-level 
debt and backstopped by Google’s investment-grade credit, we believe the equity-level IRR is 
significantly superior to any comparable infrastructure play in the public markets. We believe the stock 
has a path to $75 to $100 valuation as the market transitions from a spot-crypto multiple to an 
infrastructure-yield framework.

represents institutional validation for a company of this size. Management has demonstrated the ability 
to originate, structure, and execute complex transactions with world-class partners, and has laid out a 
clear framework for repeating the River Bend playbook across the broader pipeline. The American 
Bitcoin spinoff further demonstrates capital allocation discipline, allowing management to focus

6. Key Risks – Execution risk is real. Hut 8 must deliver the River Bend data halls on schedule by Q2
