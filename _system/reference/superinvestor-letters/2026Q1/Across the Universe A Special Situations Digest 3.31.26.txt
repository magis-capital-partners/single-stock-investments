What we like in Special Situations
• Terawulf (WULF), Hut8 (HUT) – Many of the converting crypto miners are trading at or around the value of their existing contracts due to shifts in the macro, with a robust catalyst path ahead for more 
deals. The CEO of WULF has been on Twitter saying he will have an announcement for his 480 MW site in Kentucky by the end of Q2. This deal would generate significant equity value relative to WULF’s  
current market cap. MS Analyst Stephen Byrd also highlights HUT as having the best terms to date of any announcement and line of site to deals at Corpus Christi (700 MW) and RB2 (1 GW). According 
to Byrd’s 15-15-15 framework, deals for either site with a hyperscaler could exceed HUT’s entire enterprise value; see: research report [MSR is OW on WULF, $37 PT; does not cover HUT]
• Devon Energy (DVN) – The CTRA acquisition brings compelling synergies (mgmt. expects pre-tax run-rate synergies of $1B by 2027) and MS Analyst Devin McDermott forecasts ~11% accretion to 
FCF/sh in the first 12 months post-close. History is also supportive - in Oil & Gas E&P transactions >$2B since 2020 (with relative market cap <1.5x), acquirers have returned over 100% on average, with 
all seven deals creating value relative to the XOP. As domestic E&Ps, both DVN and CTRA are positioned to benefit from higher oil prices amid geopolitical volatility, without facing the same production 
volatility as peers with international exposure; see: research report [MSR is OW on DVN, $46 PT]
• Brookfield Renewable Partners (BEP), Brookfield Renewable Corp (BEPC) – BEPC trades at a ~23% premium to BEP, the stock it’s tracking, due to investor constraints and preferences regarding K-
1 entities. MS Analyst Robert Kad sees the degree of the discount as hard to justify with multiple mechanisms to tighten it. First, earlier this year, mgmt. announced an ATM equity issuance program for up 
to $400mm of BEPC shares. The ATM allows Brookfield to capitalize on the premium with the net proceeds being directly used to repurchase BEP shares. Second, in what would be a more immediate 
collapse of the premium, Brookfield could consolidate BEPC and BEP into a single traded corporation. There is a recent precedent for this, with Brookfield Business Partners converting BBU and BBUC 
into one public Canadian corporation. The day of the announcement, BBUC's premium to BBU fell from 26.2% to 3.8%; see: research report [MSR is OW on BEP, $42 PT; is UW on BEPC, $42 PT]
• Aptiv (APTV) – New Aptiv (APTV) will be operating two distinct businesses, Intelligent Systems (IS) and Engineered Components Group (ECG), while Versigent (VGNT) will operate as a single segment 
business, formerly known as Electrical Distribution System. MS Analyst Andrew Percoco’s $87/sh price target on APTV pre-spin includes $17/sh for EDS on pre-spin share count (soon to be Versigent), 
and $63-76/sh for New APTV. Further, the Hyundai JV represents potential material underappreciated value for RemainCo. Corporate clarity and growth stories are both qualities investors have been 
willing to pay for, and the catalyst to begin the rerating is April 1st; see: research report [MSR is EW on APTV, $87 PT]
• Liberty Formula One (FWONK) – Mallone is in wind down mode already making moves to resolve his sprawling empire. One next logical step could be in FWONK. People want to own this business as 
evidenced by multiple reported sets of buyer interest in the past several years. Client feedback points to multiple credible and motivated buyers. FWONK is a growing, one-of-a-kind, unreplicable asset and 
those tend to trade at a premium; see: research report [MSR is OW on FWONK, $120 PT]

M&A Environment
‘Running it hot’ was the expectation coming into ’26, and what has materialized so far is strength 
in headline deal value much more than real breadth. This market is being pushed forward by a 
handful of large, strategic swings rather than a broad reopening. It’s top heavy and getting more 
so. What is notable, though, is how aligned C-suites are across both banks and corporates in 
explicitly calling a shift. Boards feel like they can execute again and are leaning in. It’s very much 
an era of “shoot your shot.” Underneath that, you’ve got real drivers: pent-up consolidation after 
the tougher antitrust backdrop of the last few years, improved rate visibility, and an IPO window 
that is reopening enough to act as a release valve. The behavioral shift matters just as much. 
Corporates are prioritizing scale and strategic growth, and the sequence has been pretty clear: 
macro anxiety drove the need to scale and build ballast, which turned into relief, then confidence, 
and now FOMO. We’re firmly in that last phase. From an event-driven lens, the bigger change is in 
how deals are getting done. Competitive dynamics are heating up. You’re seeing more overbids, 
bumps, and activists forcing processes again, even if activity hitting the tape is still concentrated in 
the largest situations. 
On the PE side, the capital is still there to support activity. Global buyout dry powder remains 
extremely high, and the broader private-capital complex is materially larger than that, with 
sovereign wealth funds showing up more frequently in consortium bids. The macro isn’t perfect, 
and geopolitical conflict isn’t helping, but it doesn’t need to be. It just needs to be stable enough. 
The real question for arbs isn’t flow, it’s composition. Does geopolitics stall what was supposed to 
be a banner year? And if it does, do we still get enough contested and complex situations to offset 
a slow start? Right now, it feels like most capital is chasing a constructive backdrop, compressing 
returns in plain-vanilla deals to the point where they’re not all that that interesting.

Antitrust Environment
As the landscape shifts in the new era of antitrust, though nothing formal has changed regulatorily, what has 
changed is the practical overlay. Reviews feel more negotiated, more remedy-oriented, and more exposed to 
politics and administrative priorities than it did a year ago. The Harvard Law M&A roundtable captured it well: 
dealmakers are increasingly operating with a mindset of “we can cut a deal with the agency” or “we’ve got a 
White House strategy.” Enforcement is still there, but it is being steered. For arbs, the framework is relatively 
straightforward. If you can underwrite a credible national-interest angle—defense supply chains, AI 
infrastructure, energy security, semis—regulatory risk may be meaningfully lower. The flip side matters just as 
much. Media, healthcare, and anything tied to consumer pricing can turn political quickly. That kind of 
unpredictability is where the spread lives. The attention of the administration seems to be the swing factor. 
The net result is wider dispersion in regulatory outcomes than we have seen in years. Some deals clear quickly 
with manageable remedies, while others get stuck depending on how the political narrative evolves. If you 
manage to escape the political spotlight, or even catch a political tailwind, acceleration appears possible. We 
have had very few second requests YTD and TGNA/NXST, SSTK/GETY, and HOUS/COMP all represent bull 
case outcomes. Timing matters. There is an incentive to get more complex transactions signed and in process 
before the midterm cycle rewrites the practical playbook again. Structurally, you want protection. Reverse break 
fees and longer outside dates should be standard for anything with real political sensitivity.

Deal Financing Environment 
Deal financing in 2026 is open and liquid at the top of the capital stack, but the underlying financing 
mix is shifting in ways that directly affect M&A execution. At the high-quality end, conditions are as 
strong as they’ve been in years, and better-quality large-cap financings are still clearing with solid 
underwriting support. The change is beneath the surface. Private credit is becoming more selective, 
and the result is renewed competition between financing channels. The choice is no longer a simple 
trade-off between certainty and cost. For sponsors, certainty is now more deal-specific than market-
venue-specific. Outcomes depend on leverage, size, structure, and timing, rather than defaulting to 
private credit as the reliable option for execution. That shift matters because private credit sits at the 
core of modern deal financing. As it becomes more disciplined, and as banks lean in, financing 
conditions are fragmenting, particularly outside the largest, highest-quality deals. The result is a 
bifurcation in M&A: strong certainty at the top end, and more variable execution risk beneath it - 
something investors cannot treat as uniform.

Sectors to Watch – Retail and Consumer M&A
• MS Analyst Jenna Giannelli expects sustained M&A momentum in Retail and Consumer sectors for 2026, underpinned by ongoing private equity engagement and lower rates. The 
US M&A market demonstrated robust momentum in 2025, with announced deal volume rising by 69% to $3.1T, with significant acceleration particularly in the second half of the 
year. Technology and Industrials were the primary drivers of this growth, yet Jenna notes that select pockets within the Consumer sector, notably retail and wholesale (across both 
discretionary and staples), outperformed the broader M&A market, even as overall consumer activity lagged.
• Jenna thinks that several key factors contributed to this trend, including a substantial reserve of private equity capital, supportive equity markets, open capital markets, and deal 
sizes in the Retail and Consumer space that remain manageable, striking a balance between scale and accessibility. These dynamics have made the sector especially appealing for 
private equity sponsors, supporting continued transaction flow. Looking ahead, while she anticipates that growth rates may moderate from recent peaks, Jenna expects M&A activity 
to remain resilient in 2026.

Sectors to Watch – Cybersecurity M&A
• 2025 included a multitude of acquisitions across the cybersecurity landscape ranging 
from TENB acquiring Vulcan Cyber and ZS acquiring Red Canary, to PANW announcing 
its entry into Identity Security and observability through its acquisitions of CyberArk and 
Chronosphere, as well as GOOGL’s $32B acquisition of leading cloud security vendor, 
Wiz. The latter three transactions highlight 1) the race to secure the best platform

• From a platform standpoint, GOOGL’s acquisition of cloud security provider, Wiz, is 
consistently mentioned throughout MS Analyst Meta Marshall’s conversations as 
investors look to understand what this acquisition could mean for other CSPs looking to 
provide more robust capabilities on top of their existing cloud infrastructure (see

observability vendor, Chronosphere, may have caught some by surprise as it skewed 
away from their historical approach of smaller tuck-ins while also marking their entry into 
the highly competitive identity and observability markets.

Sectors to Watch – Food M&A
• M&A and portfolio activity could reaccelerate as companies seek growth in a muted demand environment. With organic growth opportunities limited across much of US food in an 
environment where consumers continue to prioritize health and wellness exacerbated by ongoing GLP-1 and the MAHA movement, MS Analyst Megan Alexander Clapp expects 
management teams to increasingly look to targeted M&A and portfolio reshaping as a way to reposition toward higher-growth or more structurally attractive categories. While 
balance sheets across the group are generally supportive of smaller, bolt-on acquisitions, she remains cautious on large-scale M&A, as history suggests that synergy assumptions 
are often optimistic and such transactions are not consistently rewarded by investors, particularly in a low-growth environment where execution risk is elevated.
• Megan also would not be surprised to see continued divestment activity, building on the acceleration observed over the past year (SJM selling Cloverhill and Big Texas, CAG selling 
Van de Kamp's and Mrs. Paul's, GIS sale of its US Yogurt Business), as companies refine portfolios in response to shifting consumer preferences, potential regulatory changes, and 
the gradual adoption of GLP-1 therapies. Divestments offer a more flexible path to simplification, allowing companies to sharpen their focus on core brands while freeing up capital 
for debt reduction or shareholder returns, outcomes she believes are more likely to be favorably received by the market than transformative acquisitions.

Themes to Watch – Powering AI & the Bitcoin Miners
• MS Analyst Stephen Byrd’s latest Intelligence Factory model shows a power bottleneck between 2025 and 2028 totaling 49 GW. Even after he factors in all innovative “time to 
power” solutions, he projects that US data center developers will face a net power shortfall of 9-18 GW through 2028, equal to 12-25% of needed power. Stephen believes that 
converting existing Bitcoin mining operations to AI DCs represents an attractive proposition, especially given the time-to-power advantage Bitcoin miners can offer. In addition, 
Bitcoin-to-DC conversions look attractive due to the relatively large scale of Bitcoin mining sites combined with the declining economics of Bitcoin mining (owing to the scheduled 
Bitcoin halving, with the next one coming in 1H28).
• Stephen recently initiated coverage on two bitcoin miners at Overweight: WULF, with a high upside (~150%) to his base case price target, has a significant history of building power 
infrastructure, and has a track record of multiple repeat Bitcoin-to-DC conversion transactions with customers. In his view, the company has a viable path to significant annual 
growth in power and data center capacity, and he includes this growth potential in both his base and bull cases. Similarly, he includes additional growth potential for CIFR, which 
offers essentially the same upside (~175%) to his base case price target. For both companies, this Bitcoin-to-DC growth potential is a significant driver of upside.

Themes to Watch – Critical Minerals
• The current demand mega trends (like data centers and EVs) and the trends MS expects to see in the future (like humanoids) run on critical minerals. Moreover, power efficiency of 
chips will be key, and this is most obvious in gallium, which significantly enhances electron movement in semiconductors, increasing their power efficiency. Along these same lines, 
humanoids will drive demand for rare earth permanent magnets given their unrivaled power density, which allows for the use of small magnets to deliver movement. These trends 
represent tailwinds for heavy rare earth companies such as MP, UUUU, and LYC AU. Additionally, the MS Thematics team has created the National Security basket (MSXXNSEC), 
comprised of industries core to the administration’s focus – roughly evenly split across (a) rare earth mining, (b) uranium/ nuclear, (c) lithium, batteries, and energy storage.
• China controls more than 90% of supply; in the cases where China does not control >90% of supply, like uranium and titanium, the other key global supplier is Russia. MS Analyst 
Carlos de Alba awaits the next major milestone which will come on July 13th where the USTR and Secretary of Commerce must provide an update on how negotiations are 
progressing. President Trump has also left the door open to apply further measures under Section 232 should there be no agreements reached or if agreements are deemed 
ineffective, essentially following a “negotiate then escalate” playbook.

• Following President Trump’s announcement earlier this year that he plans to increase the U.S. defense budget to $1.5T (~50% increase over the prior year), MS Analyst Kristine 
Liwag has been fielding questions from investors about the likelihood and feasibility of this increase. When adjusting for Purchasing Power Parity, Russia, China, and Iran started 
spending more on defense than the U.S. in 2023 for the first time in modern history, with their budgets further accelerating since. In Kristine’s view, recognition of this situation could 
bolster support for a $1.5T U.S. defense budget. 
• An increase of this size would reflect a historic increase and should help alleviate skepticism around the administration's commitment to increasing defense spend. Additionally, 
Kristine has seen higher budgets support sector valuation. This is in line with one of MS Analyst Stephen Byrd’s key predictions for 2026 that the Trump Administration will increase 
military spending (with a relatively large increase in emerging areas of military capability such as drones/counter-drone defense, 3D printing, electronic warfare, cyber, directed 
energy and space). Single name beneficiaries of these trends include KRKNF, PLTR, ONDS, DDD, AVAV, KTOS, DPRO, DRS, UMAC, RCAT, VELO, and GNSS.

Clients engaging in the execution structure known as Spreading should understand that Spreading may also entail substantial commissions, because it involves at least twice the number of contracts as a long or short position and because 
spreads are almost invariably closed out prior to expiration. Potential investors should be advised that the tax treatment applicable to spread transactions should be carefully reviewed prior to entering into any transaction. Also, it should be pointed 
out that while the investor who engages in spread transactions may be reducing risk, he is also reducing his profit potential. The risk/reward ratio, hence, is an important consideration.
The risk of exercise in a spread position is the same as that in a short position. Certain investors may be able to anticipate exercise and execute a “rollover” transaction. However, should exercise occur, it would clearly mark the end of the spread 
position and thereby change the risk/reward ratio. Due to early assignments of the short side of the spread, what appears to be a limited risk spread may have more risk than initially perceived. An investor with a spread position in index options 
that is assigned an exercise is at risk for any adverse movement in the current level between the time the settlement value is determined on the date when the exercise notice is filed with OCC and the time when such investor sells or exercises 
the long leg of the spread. Other multiple-option strategies involving cash-settled options, including combinations and straddles, present similar risk.
