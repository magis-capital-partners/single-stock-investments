February 2026 Performance Commentary Fourthstone Master Opportunity Fund Ltd | 1
It was gratifying to see the fund outperform its benchmark and the broader market this February, 
with SMID-cap banks continuing to gain traction off the back of strong earnings and the short 
book performing well.  We trimmed a number of positions that were approaching their high’s 
during the past few months, which proved timely as the liquidity provided ample flexibility to re-
position the portfolio at the outset of Trump’s war in Iran.  
Our immediate response to the conflict in the Middle East was to decrease the fund’s net exposure 
through a combination of individual name shorts and increased exposure to hedges we felt would 
be most effective against exogenous risk to the U.S. economy.  We rotated into some new names 
at the start of the conflict and re-entered positions that sold off on the news.  Active trading 
around core holdings was highly additive and the impact of the conflict on fund performance has 
been minimal.  Bank valuations are back where they were in the autumn of 2025 but our outlook 
for bank EPS is flat to improving.
Geopolitical events have historically created short-term headline risk and outsize moves in share 
prices that can present opportunities to acquire quality companies at heavily discounted valuations.  
That said, any long-term disruption to the oil market would demand caution and we are cognizant 
of the short-term mindset of today’s market.  We are more willing to trim long positions when they 
appreciate even if we find them still incredibly attractive at higher valuations.  The short book has 
been highly effective YTD and we anticipate both sides will continue to contribute.  The broader 
market appears willing to digest headlines with relative aplomb to date, but we will monitor the 
Iranian conflict’s impact and take time to form an opinion about its longer-term effect on the U.S. 
economy.

We are focused on rising oil prices but the impact to banks’ loan books may be a wash as

A second topic to which most banks have little to no exposure that has been grabbing headlines 
recently is private credit, which has grown fivefold since 2008 to $2 trillion globally.  Increased 
regulation of banks following the Great Financial Crisis created a void in riskier areas of the market 
that unregulated non-depository financial institutions (NFDI’s) such as insurance companies, 
mortgage companies, private equity funds, hedge funds, broker-dealers, real estate investment 
trusts, marketplace lenders, and special purpose entities stepped in to fill.  This industry flew 
under the radar for years until last fall when fears were ignited with the high-profile blowups of 
auto lender TriColor and auto parts maker First Brands. The flames were further fanned when 
alternative asset manager Blue Owl Capital recently halted redemptions at one of their funds 
and UK mortgage provider Market Financial Solutions failed. 
Today’s concerns about private credit brought the woes of Blackstone’s private commercial 
real estate fund, BREIT, to mind.  In late 2022, fears about a potential wave of defaults in 
commercial real estate led to a surge of redemption requests at BREIT that caused the fund to 
limit withdrawals.  The wave of defaults never materialized.  By early 2024, the fund was fulfilling 
100% of withdrawals and, by January 2025, BREIT experienced a 97% reduction in requests.  
Flash forward to 2026 and BREIT has raised $1B more than investors redeemed, reversing the 3 
year outflow trend.
Time will tell how extensive defaults will be in private credit funds, but recent issues remind us 
that credit cycles have not been eliminated but rather they may play out in more opaque non-
bank lenders.  There will undoubtedly be more distress in private credit this year, however, the 
core banks we own have practically zero exposure to this market.  As discussed in prior fund 
commentaries, what little exposure banks hold to NDFI’s is in the top 25 banks in the country 
(their exposure generally runs 1-2% of total loans).  The fund held short several names related to 
private credit over the past several months and it will continue to do so when prudent.     
Our research team met several large cap banks this week and found management commentary 
on credit quality to be broadly constructive, despite ongoing market concerns surrounding 
private credit. Most banks noted that they have yet to identify systemic stress across any specific 
industry or lending vertical. With respect to private credit, Kevin Khanna, Head of Fifth Third’s 
Commercial Bank, shared this week that, “ I don’t think there’s contagion in the credit market, 
right?...when you look at the structures, the leverage points, the lack of covenant that they have 
in a lot of those transactions, that’s creating a big part of the challenge… I think time is going 
to tell us a little bit more about to what extent that is a problem. And it’s clear that it’s not just 
software related. It’s clear that there’s other sectors that are going to be impacted in the private 
credit markets… We have a different leverage point for a lot of those same sectors. We have 
different covenants that we have in place to track cash flow and how it gets deployed. So I think

Our research team met a further 70+ banks year-to-date and our constructive thesis entering 
the year was reinforced by notably upbeat management commentary.  Many executives have a 
more optimistic outlook for loan growth this year as the rate of loan payoffs, which dampened 
balance-sheet growth last year, is beginning to ease.  The vast majority of banks with whom we 
met continued to report improving credit trends, and the recent rate cut should lead to lower 
provisions relative to improved net loan growth.  The most recent Federal Reserve H.8 data, 
through February 25th, indicates 6.6% YoY loan growth for domestic banks and the most recent 
Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey (SLOOS), which asks the 60 largest banks questions about 
changes in loan underwriting standards, revealed banks transitioned from tightening to easing 
several CRE loan categories for the first time since 2021.  
Another prominent theme in our meetings was an increase in share repurchase authorizations, 
which many banks signaled should continue throughout 2026.  Most banks indicated margin 
expansion should continue this year driven by fixed asset repricing, which should provide an 
incremental lift to net interest income.  
The chatter regarding M&A was as pervasive as anything we’ve seen and we expect more 
bank deals given the number of names that trade at or below tangible book value during this 
generational right-sizing of bank regulation.
The NASDAQ Bank Index is down 3.1% YTD vs. the S&P 500 at -2.3% as of time of writing, with P/E’s 
near April 2025 “liberation day” tariff lows.  The fund is performing well, and we don’t think the
