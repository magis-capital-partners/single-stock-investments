D. Hedge-Factor h Control Logic — Deep Dive
D.1 Signal construction (per underlying i, per rebalance date t, cell 14 in code)

Inputs are daily close prices of the underlying i and 212 broad-universe underlyings for cross-sectional z-scoring.
Component features
   r_10di(t) = log( Pi(t) / Pi(t−10) )
   σ5d,i(t) = std( ΔlogPi, 5d window )
   σ63d,i(t) = std( ΔlogPi, 63d window )
   range_expansioni(t) = σ5d,i(t) / σ63d,i(t)
Cross-sectional robust-z (median / MAD) clipped at ±3
   z_r10di(t) = −1 · robust_zx-sec( r_10d·(t) )
   z_rexpi(t) = +1 · robust_zx-sec( range_expansion·(t) )
   z_compi(t) = ½ · z_r10di(t) + ½ · z_rexpi(t)
Economic interpretation of the two components
  −z(r_10d): cross-sectional short-horizon mean reversion. Underlyings that sold off most over the last 10 trading days (relative
to today's universe) get z_r10d > 0. Cohen-Diether-Malloy style short-supply crowding argues you should re-hedge when
everyone else has already shorted; this signal says lean more hedged when our underlying just crashed (re-hedge to buy
back).
  +z(range_expansion): cross-sectional vol-regime capitulation. Names whose short-term vol has spiked vs their trailing vol get
z_rexp > 0 — Moreira-Muir (2017) says inverse-variance weighting adds α ≈ 4.9%/yr on equities; here we use it the opposite
way: lean more hedged when vol is already capitulating, anticipating a volatility-selling bounce.
Both components push z_comp upward ⇒ through the sigmoid-style tilt this lowers h* (more long-underlying, less
short-underlying). That is the design intent: the hedge leg is reduced after the pair has already been beaten up, so we do not
fade the bounce.
D.2 Hedge target h* — formula & step-by-step
   h*i(t) = clip( V6_OPT2_H_BASE − K · z_compi(t), H_MIN, H_MAX )

   K = 0.05, H_MIN = 0.10, H_MAX = 1.10 (plan §10)
Regime overlay multiplies h* by a three-factor modifier before the clip:
   regime_mult = rally_mult × vix_mult × dd_mult

D.4 How h interacts with sizing (cell 17)

Hedge factor h and capital weight w are decoupled in code: h lives on the underlying leg inside each sleeve's P&L; w lives
across sleeves. Mechanically:
   sleeve_returni(t) ≈ −( r_ETFi(t) + hi(t) · r_undi(t) )
   portfolio_return(t) = Σi wi · sleeve_returni(t) − frictions
Sizing score (per pair, at start_sim):
   base_scorei = decayi0.85 / (1 + 18 · borrow_ETFi2)
   risk_penaltyi = clip( exp(−2.5 · risk_adj_normi), 0.25, 1.0 )
   risk_denomi = 1 + 3.0 · risk_adj_normi1.5
   cov_penaltyi = 1 / (1 + 0.85 · cov_contrib_normi)
   new_scorei = base_scorei · risk_penaltyi · cov_penaltyi / risk_denomi
   w_newi = clip_and_renormalize( new_scorei / Σ new_score, 0.005, 0.35 )
Economic interaction (in order of current dominance):
  Decay⋅borrow does 70-80% of the actual cross-sectional variation — high-decay low-borrow names (CLSZ, APLZ, UVIX) get
the top weights.
  Tail-risk penalty does 15-20% — it is what pulls UVIX from 21% → 14% and APLZ from 14% → 9%, because their long-run
20-day worst drawdowns are big (UVIX override uses its own history, not SVIX's).
  Covariance penalty is doing 0% today because cov_contrib_norm = 0.0 for all pairs — a visible implementation bug: the
proxy sleeve returns are padded with zeros before each pair's inception, so the sample covariance matrix under-estimates
co-movement and the normalizer washes everything out.

F. Theory Appendix (plain-English + math)
F.1 Pair mechanics — why the trade exists at all
A leveraged inverse ETF tracks −L · rund daily. Because it rebalances daily at close, its compounded return lags −L · rund,total
over multi-day paths. For any volatility path, the gap is:
   log(ETF_T / ETF_0) ≈ −L · log(P_T / P_0) − ½ L (L+1) · integral of σ² dt
That second term — the variance drain — is what we harvest. We short the ETF to collect it, and short the underlying at fraction
h to neutralize most of the residual beta from the first term. Perfect neutralization (pure variance capture) would be h = L; plan's
h = 0.5 deliberately leaves residual short-beta tilt, so the sleeve carries a mild bearish bias that gets re-used as internalized
hedge against B1/B2 longs.
F.2 Hedge-factor dynamics — why tilt h at all
Let µfwd = expected next-h-day log return of the underlying. The sleeve's hedged P&L is proportional to:
   E[sleeve PnL] ≈ variance_drain − (L · h) · µfwd
So the value of tilting h away from 0.5:
  If we expect the underlying to rally (µfwd > 0) we reduce h (less hedge) to avoid paying the rally.
  If we expect selloff (µfwd < 0) we raise h (more hedge) to capture the fall.
Empirical failure mode (v3-v5a): the 6-signal µfwd stack was systematically bearish-signed on small-cap high-vol names during
2024-2026 bull recovery, and pushed h up at local bottoms. v6 swaps the theory-driven µfwd for a data-survivor composite that
does not forecast return directly but captures cross-sectional mean-reversion + vol-capitulation — two factors Rentec-scale in
effect size. Gross alpha ≈ 0.56%/yr at sleeve; ~0.3-0.4%/yr net of frictions on 25% sleeve weight.
F.3 Borrow & friction modeling
Every day t, per short leg:
   borrow_cost(t) = |notional_short(t)| · borrow_annual / 252
   short_proceeds_credit(t) = |notional_short(t)| · short_proceeds_annual / 252
   financing_pnl(t) = credit − cost
Rebalance day adds commission + slippage linear in Δnotional. In portfolio sizing we also apply the plan's quadratic borrow
penalty:
   sizing_score = decay / (1 + k · borrow2),  k = 18 in notebook, k = 3²=9 in plan.
Quadratic (rather than linear) — because the plan's objective is risk-adjusted decay net of borrow, and a high-borrow name's
information ratio degrades super-linearly once borrow approaches the decay rate. The 2× portfolio borrow surcharge
(V6_PF_BORROW_SURCHARGE) inside each sleeve is a buffer for intra-day borrow moves + margin maintenance.
F.4 Tail-risk penalized sizing — motivation and mechanics
Raw worst-20d return on SVIX looks benign (SVIX is the long-vol side). But we are short UVIX against SVIX, and the risk is a
UVIX spike (e.g. Aug 2024, COVID 2020). The plan-level framework would size UVIX/SVIX the same as CLSZ/CLSK because
both show similar decay; the cell-17 override uses UVIX's own history (not SVIX's) to score the tail.
   tail_loss_trailing = max(0, −min( r_{20d}(s), last 1260 bdays ))
   tail_loss_full = max(0, −min( r_{20d}(s), full history ))
   tail_loss = 0.3 · tail_loss_trailing + 0.7 · tail_loss_full
   down_vol_ann = stdev(rd | rd < 0, 126 bdays) · √252
   risk_raw = tail_loss + 0.45 · down_vol_ann
   risk_adj = risk_raw · max(1, |βETF|)
   risk_adj_norm = risk_adj / median(risk_adj) across live pairs

The normalization makes the penalty scale-invariant: doubling the worst drawdown of all pairs leaves weights unchanged —
only relative tail-worseness matters.
F.5 Covariance-aware sizing — motivation and mechanics
Decay + borrow + tail-risk penalty do not account for the fact that two sleeves can have identical standalone risk but very
different portfolio-level contribution if their sleeve returns are correlated. A concrete example: if CONI/COIN, MSDD/MSTR,
MSTZ/MSTR and SMST/MSTR all move together (they do — all short MSTR-family or crypto-correlated), giving them each 10%
weight creates ~30% effective concentration in one factor.
Mechanics in cell 17:
  Build per-pair proxy sleeve returns rsleeve,i(t) ≈ −( r_ETFi(t) + H_BASE · r_undi(t) ) over the last 756 bdays before start_sim.
  Estimate sample covariance Σ of rsleeve,·.
  Shrink toward diagonal: Σ* = (1−τ)Σ + τ·diag(Σ), with τ = 0.35 — a Ledoit-Wolf-lite regularizer that stabilizes an N-small,
T-small cov.
  Compute the marginal risk contribution: MRCi = (Σ* w)i, then contribution weight = wi · MRCi.
  Normalize contributions by their median, then penalize: cov_penaltyi = 1 / (1 + γ · contrib_normi) with γ = 0.85.
  Multiply the base weight by cov_penalty and renormalize with the 0.5%-35% clip.
Why multiplicative (not additive)? An additive term can flip sign; a multiplicative [<1] keeps weights non-negative and keeps
the sizing mental model: “rank by decay, then shade down everything the penalty says is too risky or too crowded.”
Why MRC and not full mean-variance? Full MVO is brittle with N=12 and T=200; MRC gives 80% of the diversification benefit
at 10% of the numerical instability. This is the choice the AQR/Asness/Frazzini “Understanding Defensive Equity” paper explicitly
recommends for small-N portfolios.
Current implementation bug (see §A & §C). Proxy sleeve returns are NaN before each pair's inception date; they are filled
with 0.0 inside ret_cov = ret_proxy_df.fillna(0.0).tail(756).cov(), which produces near-zero off-diagonals. The MRC-based
contribution is then tiny and the median-normalizer inflates the floor, pushing cov_contrib_norm to 0.0 and cov_penalty to 1.0
everywhere. The concept is correct; the current covariance estimate is not.
F.6 Why these choices might improve risk-adjusted returns — and why they might not
  Might help: the regime H3 drawdown-cut is empirically confirmed at +17% ΔCAGR / +0.30 ΔSharpe vs base; this is a genuine

  Might help: tail-risk penalty size-downs UVIX (historically the biggest spike-risk asset we trade) while preserving its weight at
a conservative level. Even if cov_penalty is broken, tail-risk alone is ~10-15% weight shift.
  Might hurt: v6 composite tilt is empirically dead on today's panel and would burn ~25 bps/yr of turnover cost for
