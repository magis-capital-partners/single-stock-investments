As we’ve discussed in several recent investor letters, REB began positioning the Fund more defensively in the fourth 
quarter of last year, whereby an unusually high number of position exits led to: (1) a higher cash balance; (2) a higher 
mix of investments denominated in dollar-pegged currencies like the Hong Kong Dollar and the Saudi Riyal ; and, (3) 
a higher notional exposure to various index puts. After conceding some upside alpha in the preceding three months 
since November, the Fund’s more defensive posturing finally led to some meaningful outperformance in March. REB 
sees no immediate need to make any major changes to our defensive stance , as we suspect that the markets have 
yet to reach their peak pain moment.  
 
That observation notwithstanding, one of the many painful lessons that REB learned in March of 2020 is that it rarely 
pays to be a “hero” when markets are under siege during moments of global crisis.4 “Hero” portfolio managers tend 
to make aggressive portfolio adjustments during such moments, largely based on bold calls about what is likely to 
be a binary outcome. Hero tactics may work in a one-off scenario, but we’ve found that the underlying arithmetic – 
i.e. batting a perfect “1.000” on career coin tosses –  does not lend itself to sustainable replication over the long -
term. To paraphrase REB friend and ally Louis Gave, you are paying us to adapt to the Iran situation, not predict it.  
 
The tactical consequence of this “ hero avoidance” mentality is that REB finds itself using Iran -induced market 
volatility – not to mention AI doom-raking – to slowly add to existing investments that have been overly penalized, 
most notably in the beaten- up fintech corner of our book. At the same time, we’ve been reducing exposure to 
investments in markets that run the risk of being indefinitely impaired due t o energy shortages, most notably in 
Indonesia. These tactical adjustments have roughly offset each other from a funding perspective, with our cash 
balance staying relatively constant throughout March at ~10% of NAV . We plan to deploy more of this cash buffer 
in a peak pain moment where selling becomes indiscriminate and sloppy.  
 
REB returned to Bangkok last month for CLSA’s annual ASEAN I nvestment Forum, which in REB’s opinion is one of 
the better stops on the EM conference circuit. While the initial plan for the trip was to take as many meetings as 
possible with Indonesia-based corporates and research analysts in order to get a better handle on local macro risks,5 
the Iran situation rightfully subordinated all other matters to the existential risk of an energy shortage. While it 
remains true that Indonesia is , technically speaking, a net energy exporter, the world’s biggest archipelago state’s 
“net exporter” status is awkwardly balanced between exports of coal and imports of refined products, most notably 
diesel and jet fuel.  
 
While the March 2020 vintage of REB might have chosen to be a hero and double down on our bets in Indonesia in 
the hope of a swift resolution to the Hormuz-induced energy bottleneck, we like to think that the post-2020 vintage 
of REB is more circumspect. The sudden stop of an emerging market economy –  due to fuel shortages or otherwise 
– can have perilous implications for the valuations of banking sector equities, much less any real-world operational 
impacts on credit quality and/or the availability of depo sit funding. Facing such binary table stakes , REB chose to 
reduce exposure to our three Indonesia banking investments by a net 2.4%  NAV, with most of that length coming 
out of Bank Rakyat Indonesia Persero (BBRI IJ), whose weighting went from 6. 5% of NAV at the beginning of March 
to 3.8% by month’s end.
