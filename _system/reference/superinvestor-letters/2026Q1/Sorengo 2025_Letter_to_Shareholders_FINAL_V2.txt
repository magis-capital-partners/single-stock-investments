Dear Friends, 
2025 was a year of  positive change for Sorengo Partnership (the “Fund”). Performance was strong at 
+40.3% net (Class A USD) , and this has continued into the new year . This letter addresses the following 
topics: 
1) Track record – a review of the investing backdrop over the past nine years. 
2) Projections - a best guess as to what to expect in the future. 
3) Review - a brief discussion of attribution, activity and exposure. 
4) Risk management - peeling back the curtain on some proprietary techniques. 
5) Outlook - outlining a new path for the future guided by our capital cycle framework. 
The fund received net subscriptions last year and we welcomed some new investors (and enquiries have 
picked up further in 2026). While I was sad to see a longstanding investor redeem last May, I have always 
been pleased to run a strategy that can provide immediate liquidity when investors need it. This appears 
to have engendered trust and endurance. We welcome enquiries from long-term investors who seek the 
peace of mind that comes with our portfolio liquidity (perhaps to balance out illiquid holdings elsewhere 
in their portfolio?).

Growing a single strategy hedge fund is no easy business  these days, even though agility is a distinct 
advantage. We operate in the most capital -starved segment of the industry food chain, with myriad 
external factors to contend with such as over-regulation, concentration of flows to the large multi-strategy 
firms, too little risk appetite (career risk) and, ironically, simply too much money in the hands of would-be 
institutional partners.  I wrote about the state of play for emerging managers in this recent article in 
AlphaWeek. 
There are also internal factors that impede the returns of a n open-ended fund as compared to well-
funded closed-ended vehicles. These include a lack of permanent capital; a lack of structural alpha from 
investing “float” or leverage to increase returns on shareholders equity; high operating expenses at low 
levels of AUM; and cash drag from often holding excess liquidity. 
Please evaluate our results below against this somewhat hostile backdrop and our open-ended, 
unleveraged structure.

Risk Management 
 
2025 was a directional year where momentum in high conviction holdings drove results. In 
hindsight, the best decision was to do nothing. For example, had we simply left our January 31st 
2025 34% exposure to basic materials alone for the full year, this sector would have contributed 
34% to the fund ’s gross return instead of 29%. Why do I not regret leaving that money on the 
table? The reason is that, all else equal, this one sector alone would have represented 2/3 of 
fund net assets by year -end! Portfolio  and risk  management is not just about finding and 
investing in ideas with great risk/reward and forgetting about them. It is also about exercising  
a fiduciary duty to ensure minimum diversification, attractive risk/reward and balance in the 
portfolio.

In past roles and through learning from peers I have been lucky to have assimilated various risk 
management techniques. These insights inform the current framework for managing risk at the 
portfolio and individual position level. For me, risk management goes well beyond the typical 
value investor toolkit of buying with a margin of safety, diversifying, eschewing leverage and 
occasionally holding cash. Here are three rules I have found useful. 
1) One simple concept, introduced to me by a legendary investor in the mid -2000s, is 
called the wedge. If the portfolio falls 10% below the annual high -water mark, start 
cutting positions. In fact, start cutting at -8% because before you know it, you’ll be down 
20%. This investor used more leverage than I do (think 150% net long at times, as well as 
huge FX positions) , so my modified version of this is to stop adding to positions , raise 
cash where possible  and cover shorts (lowering gross exposure) while retaining some 
market risk. This approach helped me to navigate the first quarter of 2020. I reduced 
exposure in January taking cash to 25%, then covered shorts and only started adding 
to positions in mid-March. It was painful to end the quarter down 24% but boy were we 
well positioned for the rest of the year.  
The other part of the wedge is letting things run when times are good.  In certain 
backdrops (for example, in 2024) this can backfire. But v olatility above the high -water 
mark, while unnerving, is the price one pays for high return potential. 
2) At the single stock level, I have settled on a risk budgeting approach  for long positions 
(on the short side, we keep things simple with a 20% stop loss). Back in 2019 I concluded 
that the single step of selling any long position that had lost 4% of NAV would have 
dramatically improved results up to that point. So today we re-evaluate at a 2% cut-off 
with a bias toward exiting the position (though retaining the option to hold or add in 
unusual circumstances, for example if the broader market sells off hard). But 4 % is a lot 
of rope to hang ourselves with and it’s  a hard stop . There are fewer paths to penury 
than averaging down a loser without a plan , and I say that from painful first-hand 
experience. Stock picking is cyclical and we are going to get things wrong.  
3) Introducing long volatility options positions to amplify return asymmetry is a more recent 
initiative. I am looking to maximize payoff structures and minimize premium at risk. Put  
and call spreads with at least 5x payoffs are a good example. These structures also offer 
risk management flexibility. I am acutely aware how difficult making money in options 
can be. After all, one needs to be right not only on the magnitude of the move, but 
also the timing. Commissions are also high. But deployed sparingly and with the above 
discipline, it is possible to improve the potential of the portfolio with no commensurate 
increase in downside risk. 
At some unknowable point in the future , we will be tested again. If our roles were reversed, I 
would want to know that you had a plan. “The oak fought the wind and was broken, the willow 
bent when it must and survived” (Robert Jordan).  
Outlook 
The elimination of remaining precious metals holdings in early 2026 is the most consequential 
change in portfolio composition in many years. Consistent exposure to this industry during the 
bull cycle of the past decade provided valuable, if volatile, diversification. One consequence 
of abandoning this sector is that return correlation to broader equity indices may creep up. On 
the other hand, long volatility positioning, when combined with the risk management 
techniques described above, may help to curtail drawdowns  (hence my projection for 
improved annual return risk/reward).
