Introduction
Pharmacyclics was on the verge of failure. The company had IPO’d as a biotech darling in the late 
1990s, peaking at $12 per share. In a pivotal Phase 3 trial, the company’s lead drug candidate, Xcytrin, 
failed to gain FDA approval. Despite a positive clinical trend, and what many experts at the time 
considered meaningful therapeutic activity, the trial had not reached statistical significance of its 
primary endpoint. Now, in the dark days of the financial crisis, Pharmacyclics stock traded close to $1 
per share, with the NASDAQ threatening to delist the company. 
Desperate and with few other options, Pharmacyclics doubled down on Imbruvica, a BTK inhibitor 
that had shown two partial responses in chronic lymphocytic leukemia. The move represented a 
dramatic departure from its previous lines of research and was considered a long shot. 
The bet, however, would pay off. From the depths of crisis, Pharmacyclics would recover from the 
brink of delisting to a stock price of just under $15 in 2011 when Johnson & Johnson agreed to partner 
with Pharmacyclics on Imbruvica in a deal valued at $975 million. The drug went on to become one 
of the most promising new treatments for chronic lymphocytic leukemia, with Pharmacyclics stock 
hitting $261.25 in 2015 after AbbVie purchased the company for $21 billion.
 
This story of Pharmacyclics, recounted in For Blood and Money: Billionaires, Biotech and the Quest for 
a Blockbuster Drug,  is the prototypical biotech investment story. Biotechs face the constant specter 
of a failed trial, the despair of a struggling research agenda, the doubts that can send share prices 
spiraling to the verge of delisting. And they hope for and often achieve the clinical breakthroughs, 
pursuit by big pharma, and headline acquisitions that ultimately lead to outsized returns. 
Biotechs are perhaps the most promising and most heartbreaking companies in the small-cap 
investment universe. They enter public equity markets for the purest of reasons: to raise capital at 
the scale necessary to fund the enormous expenses necessary to deliver life-changing drugs. When 
they succeed, they can achieve huge valuations in a short period of time. But they fail at a rate that is 
much higher than in other industries. 
In the last 30 years, of the just over 1,000 biotechs that have reached at least $200 million of market 
cap, a whopping 67% have lost money. Of these, roughly 50% were acquired at negative total returns, 
P.  3
10% delisted and the remaining 40% are still public with a significant proportion trading below their 
cash balance representing “zombie biotechs”; failed programs where cash remains stranded instead 
of being returned to shareholders. Of the 33% that were successful, 55% were acquired and the 
remaining 45% are still public. 
In this white paper, we chart a path for how to navigate this sector. We cover the strangeness of the 
sector, the recent tribulations that have led to massive underperformance of the broader market, the 
important role played by specialist biotech funds, the factors that drive performance, how to manage 
risk, and, ultimately, a strategy for how to invest quantitatively in the biotech sector.
This is the culmination of over a year of work. We’ve built our own database of biotech specialist 
holdings and company insider disclosures. We’ve studied SEC filings, acquisitions and company-level 
financials. We’ve created a point-in-time dataset of more than 130,000 clinical trials with metadata 
allowing us to quantitatively compare individual trials and company-level research programs. And 
we’ve built signals based on these datasets that help point the way to a strategy for investing in the 
sector.
The biotech sector is difficult terrain to traverse. But consensus pessimism is what creates the ideal 
conditions to invest. The right data gives us the coordinates we need to cross the waste land. With 
a disciplined long-short strategy, we believe we can capitalize on the conjunction of change and 
science that defines biotech investing.

The high failure rate is a feature of biotech’s unique business model. Most biotech companies are, 
essentially, science projects that investors fund in hopes of discovering a revenue-producing drug. 
The sector, therefore, is dominated by companies with no revenue that raise money to fund research. 
Of companies labeled as biotech by Standard & Poor’s, 69% have no revenue, representing a full 57% 
of pre-revenue companies in the S&P Total Market Index. Companies without revenue and companies 
without profit are hard to value. It may seem easier to look away. 
But “branches grow out of this stony rubbish,” as Eliot says, and investors ignore this sector at  
their peril. 
Biotech has historically delivered returns. For the twenty years before 2020, the S&P Biotech Index 
returned 9.3% annually versus 6.1% for the S&P 500 and 7.6% for the Russell 2000. Compensating for 
the high failure rate and many losing stocks are a few acorns that grow to mighty oaks, big winners 
that compensate for the morass of mediocre or horrible outcomes.
Biotech is diversifying. Its very strangeness means biotech returns are far less correlated to the 
market than other sectors. Its average correlation to major industries is below any of the major 
industry cross correlations.

An industry that historically delivers returns, has low correlation to other investments, and represents 
a substantial portion of investments should not be ignored. And for quantitative investors, biotech has 
two very attractive attributes that make it especially useful for constructing portfolios. 
First, it has the most companies with which to build portfolios, more than any other sector. It makes 
up the largest share of the S&P Total Market Index, accounting for over 400 companies (~11%). The 
next largest sector, banks, has a little over 300 (~8%), followed by software (~5%). 
Second, because major biotech outcomes result from scientific breakthroughs and acquisitions 
from big pharma, rather than the more quotidian business cycles that drive other sectors, the internal 
correlation of stocks within the sector to one another is much lower than other industries.

Low internal correlations mean that diversification can provide more risk reduction than in other 
industries, though risk models need to be trained on biotech-specific data to capture these internal

As quantitative investors, industries with many names, low correlations to other industries and low 
internal correlations are particularly attractive. Quantitative approaches work best when they can 
benefit from diversification. Industries where there is ample opportunity to profit from shorts also 
help to diversify and isolate profitable factors. 
We find ourselves drawn to the opportunities in biotech but deeply aware of its dangers. After all, can 
a quantitative approach work in a sector with idiosyncratic successes that are not reflected in the 
financials until years after the value is known? We have spent the past year exploring this question, 
and we believe we have found a reliable quantitative guide to investing in the sector.

Inferno
How Experts Can Guide Us
As Dante wandered in confusion until Virgil appeared to lead him, so too do most investors enter 
biotech in a kind of darkness—surrounded by unfamiliar science, opaque data, and no revenues to 
anchor valuation.
In the introduction, we told the story of Pharmacyclics. What we did not emphasize was the role of 
biotech specialist funds as its guides. In 2009, when Pharmacyclics was a $1.28 stock with only early 
BTK inhibitor data, specialists such as OrbiMed, Quoque Capital, and later Baker Brothers recognized 
its potential and began buying. Within a year, at least three expert groups had invested, and a few 
years later the company was acquired for $261.25 per share: a classic example of how great science 
is often identified first, and rewarded most, by specialist investors who quickly cluster into good ideas. 
Their collective judgment shows up clearly in the data. When we sort biotech stocks by how 
heavily they are owned by biotech specialist funds—from the least owned (quintile 1) to the most 
owned (quintile 5)—a striking pattern emerges. The chart on the next page illustrates how specialist 
conviction maps directly into returns.

In general, the higher the concentration of biotech specialist funds, the better that stock performs. 
Conversely, if no biotech specialist funds own a stock (generally true for quintile 1), returns have  
been poor. 
Because biotech specialist funds typically hold positions for nearly two years—and because the 
strongest signals come when multiple specialists own the same stock—their activity shows up 
consistently in 13-F filings. As a result, even with a two-month reporting lag, the 13-F data remains 
both robust and highly informative. 
One interesting twist to our findings on specialist ownership is that 72% of specialist fund exposure is 
in large stocks. This is likely because multi-billion-dollar funds cannot just invest in small and medium 
stocks. But specialists are best at identifying return opportunities in small stocks. In the smallest third 
of biotech stocks, the stocks with the highest concentration of specialist ownership have nearly 30% 
returns in our historical data versus near-zero returns for stocks with no specialist concentration. 
The implication is that smaller funds have a structural advantage if they can focus on smaller 
opportunities.

But biotech specialist funds are not the only specialists we should pay attention to. There are two 
other sets of investors who also provide expertise: company insiders and short sellers.
Company insiders are the best positioned to understand the prospects of a company. There is a rich 
academic literature that shows that company insider transactions contain useful information about 
stock returns. This is especially true if the insider transactions are filtered for high-information trades, 
such as buys versus sales (most insiders are natural sellers), reversals of previous trade directions, 
transaction clustering, and purchases by knowledgeable parties such as CFOs.

Biotech is a challenging industry, but expertise provides a path—specialist biotech funds, insiders, 
and short sellers each illuminate part of the terrain. In Dante’s words, after guidance through the

brings investors out of the darkness and closer to the truth.

Clinical Trials as Building Blocks 
Thus by unseen decrees of smallest 
bodies the mighty world is built. 
LUCRETIUS  
“DE RERUM NATURA”
In most industries, the “smallest bodies” in question are financial statements. In biotech, they are 
clinical trials. Revenues and profits—anchors of traditional analysis—are often absent. What matters 
instead is the probability that a company’s asset will succeed in the clinic. Each trial readout is a 
binary moment where a stock can double or collapse overnight. Any serious attempt to model the 
biotech universe must therefore begin with clinical trials.
Fortunately, clinical trials generate data as structured and information-rich as financials: standardized 
disclosures, required public filings, and oversight by the FDA. But the data is scattered, incomplete, 
and not designed for investors. Clinicaltrials.gov and the WHO’s ICTRP provide raw inputs, but neither 
gives a clean, longitudinal view of what truly matters—how a company’s research program evolves 
over time.
That evolution is messy. Drug candidates change names; partnerships form and dissolve; companies 
merge, spin out assets, and license or buy pipelines. Trial registries capture these events only in 
fragments. In Lucretius’s terms, 
“The atoms move forever downward through the void—yet by a random 
swerve they meet and join. ”
To understand biotech, one must trace those swerves and reconstruct the true lineage of every 
research program.
P . 13

DE RERUM NATURA   /   P . 14
So we built that map. Over more than a year, we created a point-in-time dataset covering over 
130,000 clinical trials, drawing from registries, SEC filings, company documents, and government 
sources. This allows us to see, at any historical moment, exactly which trials are attributable to which 
company—and how those portfolios changed. We then standardized the metadata into a hierarchical 
structure that lets us quantitatively compare trials to one another, backtest hypotheses with 
historically accurate data, and measure the impact of trial activity long after completion.
From this foundation comes deeper insight. Clinical trials let us classify companies. At each point in 
time, we can define a company by the attributes of its trials—and by how similar those trials are to 
every other trial in the universe. This in turn lets us observe which clusters of biotech tend to move 
together: companies focused on similar diseases, firms at similar clinical stages, or groups pursuing 
parallel therapeutic modalities.
Rather than guessing which categories matter, we can measure them directly. By calculating how 
similar each company is to every other one (based on the compilation of its clinical trials), we create a 
peer-based similarity score. This allows us to compare a company to a weighted average of its closest 
scientific peers—an essential step in understanding momentum, valuation, and risk inside biotech’s 
unusually heterogeneous ecosystem.
With these peer groups defined, we can begin to test whether a company’s fate is influenced by the 
performance of the companies most similar to it. One natural question is momentum: do stocks with 
strong-performing scientific peers tend to outperform as well? To answer that, we sort companies 
into quintiles based on the trailing returns of their closest peers. Quintile 1 contains companies whose 
peers have weak recent returns; quintile 5 contains those whose peers have been strong. The results 
are on the next page.

Defining peer groups is also useful for risk management. To the extent that different categories 
of biotech move together or differently, we’d like to take this into consideration when building our 
portfolios. Clinical trial data allows us to build these risk categories to use in our portfolio optimization.
The natural impulse in biotech is to treat clinical trials as riddles to be solved: predict the result, 
win the stock. But quantitative investors approach them differently. Trials are not oracles; they are 
coordinates. They let us anchor each company within a broader constellation of peers—turning 
uncertainty into structure, chaos into pattern. Clinical trials do not yield prophecy, but they do yield 
perspective. As Lucretius puts it,

Across most industries, quantitative investors rely on three durable factors: quality, value, and 
momentum. High-quality firms tend to outperform, cheap firms tend to outperform, and returns 
tend to trend. Importantly, these factors are largely independent—each adds information even after 
accounting for the others. A stock’s cheapness matters even when comparing companies of similar 
quality and momentum.
But what happens when the traditional definitions of these factors simply don’t apply? In biotech, the 
usual anchors dissolve. “Quality” is typically measured using gross profits; “value” is usually measured 
using profits relative to enterprise value. Both require profits. Most biotech companies, of course, 
have none.
To build factor models that work in biotech, we need biotech-specific definitions. Fortunately, one of 
the pillars—momentum—survives. Traditional momentum does not work here, but with a modest re-
engineering we showed earlier that similarity momentum does.
Value is the next challenge. Here, the traditional metric fails even more spectacularly. On the next 
page, we show what happens when we naïvely apply the standard value factor to the biotech 
universe. Quintile 1 contains the most expensive stocks and should do the worst; quintile 5 contains 
the cheapest and should do the best. The opposite occurs.

Building the Quantitative Model
To strive, to seek, to find,

“ULYSSES”
More than almost any other sector, biotech investing is about managing risk. The high failure rate of 
companies means that there will be many losers. The implication of a high failure rate is that portfolio 
concentration is dangerous. Diversification is essential. The other implication is that the path to 
success is likely to be volatile. 
To us, shorting is perhaps the most important and least explored investment approach in biotech. 
Shorting is important because it allows us to take profit from losers and reduce the volatility typical of 
returns in biotech. 
To illustrate the importance of shorting, we first look at the performance of a long-only strategy. 
We earlier outlined the excellent performance of biotech specialist funds. Why not just invest in 
the biotech stocks most widely held by biotech specialist funds? When we look at the time series 
of returns for the top quintile of biotech concentration versus XBI, the ETF that tracks the biotech 
universe, we can see something remarkable.

A specialist strategy is very good at helping to identify where returns come from, but it has little to 
say about risk. In an industry with high company failure rates and high volatility, risk is certainly as 
important as return. It is to that challenge that we now turn.  
Shorting is dangerous and difficult. It is an approach that requires diversification and discipline 
to execute well. This is where a quantitative approach has advantages over a purely fundamental 
approach. Factors are how we transform fundamental insights into data that can be fed to 
quantitative models. Humans are good at the creative process that identifies return factors. Computer 
programs are far better at executing the statistical code that combines expected returns, correlations, 
trade costs, shorting costs and other constraints. 
To build a portfolio that takes full advantage of a two-sided return distribution (both winners and 
losers) we need more than an idea of expected return; we need to build a risk model. To do that, we 
forecast not just factor returns but factor volatility and factor correlations. 
The details are technical, but they can be thought of as predictions based on a weighted average of 
historical observations.

The optimizer will also take into account other constraints that we specify. The most important ones 
in this case are trade costs, shorting costs, and short availability. Trade costs are the direct cost of 
the trade but also the market impact costs. Smaller stocks with low liquidity will be more expensive 
to trade. Shorting costs are the costs to borrow the stock, and short availability makes sure that the 
stock is even available. 
The net impact of these constraints is that the optimizer will not be able to chase returns, long or 
short, in small, illiquid stocks. It is subject to the same constraints that we are when actually trading a 
portfolio. Factor return spreads can look very attractive when we don’t account for these costs, but 
the reality can be much different.

Biotech is a risky and complex industry. But, as Tennyson says, “Tho’ much is taken, much abides.” 
The sector follows investing logic to some degree: Cheap companies do well, expensive companies 
do poorly. Company returns cluster and move together. Higher quality companies outperform. But 
in a sector with so many losers, a long-only strategy has trouble capitalizing on this dynamic. A long-
short strategy can do this well, but it requires discipline and diversification, features that a quantitative 
approach does particularly well.

Renaissance
Why Biotech is Primed for a Second Spring 
Since December 2019, before the start of COVID, the S&P Biotech Index has returned just 4.5% per 
year while the S&P 500 has returned 15.1%. The S&P Biotech index is still down 21% from its February 
2021 peak despite retracing 36% in 2025. Over the same time period, the S&P 500 has risen 88%. 
By any measure, biotech remains in a drawdown. Rising interest rates have choked off speculative 
investment, price pressure from the government’s new powers under the Inflation Reduction Act have 
decreased terminal values, and a few high-profile trial failures for companies like Sage Therapeutics 
and Fibrogen, have increased perceptions of fundamental risk.
But, in our view, the best opportunities arise when investors withdraw capital from small, illiquid 
markets, when pessimism and forced selling drive a collapse in share prices, even as fundamentals 
and the prospects of recovery seem high. In these scenarios, small changes in investor perception 
can drive massive swings in share prices, particularly among the most illiquid small caps. 
When we put the drawdown of the biotech market relative to the S&P 500 in context with historic 
periods of comparable relative underperformance, we can see how biotech performed in the 
subsequent three years. On average, biotech saw a >60% return over the subsequent two years, a 
return comparable to the results we saw in our major study Crisis Investing.

Biotech has been through harsh winters before, and there seem to be reasons to think that spring is 
coming for the sector. 
First, there is an estimated $200 billion in annual drug revenues at risk by 2030 due to expiration of 
patents on blockbuster drugs, the largest patent cliff facing the pharmaceutical industry since the 
early 2010s. As patents expire, the market share of a drug can plummet by 80% or more within the 
first year of generic/biosimilar market entry. This puts pressure on large pharmaceutical companies to 
acquire new revenue streams to replace expiring assets.
Second, we are living through an era of tremendous pharmaceutical innovation. Think GLP-1 agonists 
for obesity, next-generation antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), radioligand therapies, and advances 
in cell and gene therapy. Indeed, 2024 marked the fifth year out of the prior seven with new drug 
approvals reaching or exceeding 50 and besting the 10-year trailing average (46.5). Of the 50 drugs 
approved in 2024, 72%  (36) were developed by small and midsize companies. This momentum has 
continued. Despite a tumultuous period at the FDA, 2025 yielded an additional 46 approvals, further 
cementing this high-output era for the industry.

Third, the widespread integration of artificial intelligence into R&D offers a long-term pathway to 
improved productivity. AI-powered platforms for drug discovery and tools for optimizing clinical 
trial design and patient recruitment have the potential to reduce development timelines, ultimately 
increasing the probability of success. According to a study by McKinsey & Company, an achievable 
average reduction of 6–12 months in drug development timelines across a portfolio will result in a net 
present value increase of $200M-$400M.
Earlier this year, sentiment regarding biotech could hardly have been bleaker. Latham & Watkins noted 
that the 2024 biotech IPO cohort was down ~50% from deal price. Tim Opler of Stifel described a 
complete withdrawal of capital by non-specialists, 
         “The generalists are gone from our market. ” 
But it is out of such consensus pessimism that great investment opportunities are born. The 
challenge is to follow the fundamentals with discipline amid speculative despair, to chart the course 
with data as our coordinates and reason as our compass. We believe the strategy outlined above can 
position us to benefit from biotech’s rebirth.
