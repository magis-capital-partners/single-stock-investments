## What this business is
Alphabet Inc. (Class C, ticker GOOG) is the non-voting share class of the same company that trades as GOOGL. **[Fact]** GOOG carries identical economic rights (dividends, buybacks, liquidation) but no voting power; founders retain control through super-voting Class B shares (`DEF 14A_20260424`). **[Fact]** The company owns Google (Search, YouTube, Android, Chrome) and Google Cloud. **[Fact]** Advertisers and enterprises pay for ad auctions, cloud compute, and subscriptions; consumers use free products that drive query volume and data. **[Fact]** FY2025 revenue was **$402.8 billion** (+15%); Q1 2026 revenue **$109.9 billion** (+22%). **[Fact]** Reportable segments are **Google Services**, **Google Cloud**, and **Other Bets** (Waymo, Verily, etc.), plus **Alphabet-level activities** (centralized AI research and development). **[Fact]** FY2025 cash from operations **$125.3 billion**, capital spending **$52.5 billion**, owner free cash flow **~$72.8 billion**; management guides **$180 to $190 billion** capital spending in 2026. **[Inference]** Owner economics depend on whether Cloud return on invested capital exceeds the capex step-up and whether Search monetization holds as user interfaces shift toward AI answers.

## Why the market might be wrong
There is **no clear dated mispricing signal** (Horizon Kinetics predictive attribute: **none**). **[Inference]** At today's price, the stock prices Cloud AI optionality and Search resilience together; it is not a holdco-discount or dormant-asset story. **[Inference]** A single consolidated free cash flow growth rate hides **divergent segment economics**: Services throws off most cash today while Cloud and Alphabet-level spend absorb the 2026 capex guide. **[Inference]** Lawrence ten-year math on **FY2025 free cash flow per share ($5.85)** may understate medium-term earnings if capital spending normalizes after 2027; the segment build makes that tension explicit. **[Inference]** GOOG typically trades at a small discount to GOOGL (no voting rights); that spread is not a separate business thesis but a governance preference for owners who do not need votes.
