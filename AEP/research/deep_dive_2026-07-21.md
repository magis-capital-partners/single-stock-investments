## What this business is

American Electric Power is one of the largest regulated electric utilities in the United States, serving customers across **11 states** through vertically integrated generation-and-distribution companies and standalone transmission-and-distribution utilities. **[Fact]** FY2025 operating revenues were **$21.9 billion** (+15% versus prior year). **[Fact]** Operating income was **$5.3 billion** and net income available to common shareholders was **$3.6 billion**, or **$6.66** diluted earnings per share. **[Fact]** The company is executing a **$72 billion** capital plan from 2026 through 2030 focused on transmission infrastructure, distribution upgrades, and new generation to serve rising load including data centers. **[Fact]** Consolidated debt carrying amount was **$47.7 billion** against **$197 million** of cash at December 31, 2025. **[Inference]** AEP earns allowed returns set by state regulators and FERC on invested capital (rate base), not through open-market pricing.

---

## Why the market might be wrong

Utility investors often treat AEP as a bond proxy: safe dividend, modest growth, fairly priced for the sector. **[Inference]** That framing may **underweight the rate-base growth engine** in the $72 billion capital plan and large-load interconnection pipeline, which can compound earnings faster than the current yield alone implies. **[Inference]** Conversely, the market may **under-discount leverage and dilution**: roughly **$5.6 billion** of equity issuance is planned, and **$47.5 billion** of net debt (~**$88** per share) creates refinancing sensitivity as rates stay elevated. **[Fact]** FY2025 investing cash outflows were **$11.9 billion** while operating cash flow was **$6.9 billion**, so reported free cash flow before dividends was deeply negative during the growth capex cycle. **[Opinion]** There is no obvious dated mispricing edge at today's price; the stock looks fairly valued for a quality regulated compounder, which is why Marvin lands below our **~15%** return hurdle.

## Business & moat

### What (Stahl + Lawrence bucket)

| Field | Value | Evidence |

AEP operates a **regulated infrastructure toll road**: customers pay rates set by commissions; the company earns an allowed return on rate base after prudency review. Growth comes from capital spending recovered through trackers, formula rates, and base-rate cases (10-K FY2025 MD&A).

### Mental models in plain English

**Moat durability (Munger):** Does regulation protect the franchise? **Partially yes.** Geographic service territories and state franchises limit direct competition; returns are capped but durable.

**Moat trajectory (Munger):** Is the advantage widening? **Stable to narrow.** Data-center load growth supports rate-base expansion, but affordability politics can compress allowed returns.

**Deny compounding to others (Stahl):** Can peers replicate? **No in territory.** Other utilities compete for large-load customers at the margin, not inside AEP's franchise.

**Bounded bear (Pabrai):** Filing-locked regulated earnings (~**$6.40/sh** normalized) and dividend history limit downside versus speculative growth stories, but **$88/sh** net debt and equity dilution cap dhando at **partial**.

**Time arbitrage (Horizon Kinetics):** No dated predictive attribute; regulated utilities re-rate with interest rates. Patience matters only if rate-base growth compounds through the capex cycle.

### Thesis pillars

1. **Rate-base growth:** **$72 billion** capital plan 2026-2030 (transmission, distribution, generation) recovered through state trackers and FERC formula rates.
2. **Large-load pipeline:** 10-K large-load category includes **data centers** and crypto operations; incremental interconnections add rate-base growth beyond base plan.
3. **Dividend discipline:** **$3.74** annual dividend; payout ~56% on FY2025 **$6.66** EPS supports income investor base.
4. **Scale advantage:** One of the largest US transmission owners; **$17.1 billion** of transmission assets in service with **~$11 billion** planned additions (10-K).

## Risks & inversion

**Primary risk:** Customer bill affordability triggers regulatory pushback, delaying rate recovery on the $72 billion capital plan and compressing allowed ROE.

**Secondary risks:**
- **Leverage:** **$47.7B** total debt against **$197M** cash; refinancing risk if credit spreads widen.
- **Dilution:** **~$5.6B** planned equity issuance dilutes per-share earnings growth.
- **Load risk:** Data-center pipeline may slip if interconnection timelines or power costs disappoint hyperscalers.

**Inversion:** If we are wrong, AEP is a **mispriced bond proxy** while actually compounding rate-base faster than peers: you earn high-single-digit annual returns as earnings grow and the market maintains utility premium.
