## What this business is

Gyrodyne, LLC owns two real estate properties left over from a much larger portfolio it has been selling off for two decades. **[Fact]** One is Flowerfield, an industrial park and vacant land in St. James, New York (Suffolk County, Long Island). **[Fact]** The other is Cortlandt Manor, a set of medical office buildings and vacant land in Westchester County. **[Fact]** The company is not trying to grow. It is trying to get government approval to subdivide and improve these properties, sell them for the highest price it can, pay off its debts, and then dissolve and send shareholders the cash. **[Fact]** It reports its books on a "liquidation basis" of accounting, which means the balance sheet already shows management's best estimate of what everything is worth in cash, not the price originally paid for it decades ago. **[Fact]**

---

## Why the market might be wrong

The company itself discloses, every quarter, its own best estimate of the total cash left for shareholders once the properties are sold and debts are paid: about $11.79 per share as of March 31, 2026. **[Fact]** The stock trades around $5.80, roughly half that estimate. **[Inference]** This is a small, thinly traded stock (about 2.2 million shares total, often only a few thousand shares change hands a day) with an unresolved lawsuit over one of its two properties, so index funds, brokers, and most individual investors cannot or will not hold it, even though the company tells shareholders almost exactly what it expects to distribute and roughly when.

---

## Executive summary

Gyrodyne is winding itself down. It owns two properties on Long Island and in Westchester County, is pursuing zoning approvals to raise their sale value, and intends to dissolve once everything is sold, likely by the end of 2028. The company discloses its own estimate of what shareholders should receive: about $11.79 per share as of March 31, 2026, versus a market price near $5.80. If that estimate holds and the timeline holds, buying today and collecting that amount works out to about **33.4%** per year. We treat this as an asset story, not an earnings story: the return depends on approvals, a pending land sale, and a court appeal, not on growing a business. Even in a slower, costlier case the annualized return still looks positive, so the downside looks bounded but not risk-free given thin trading and litigation. We recommend hold pending human review of the pending appeal and site-plan status, rather than moving to a larger position now.

### Moat (Munger)

There is no moat in the traditional sense. Gyrodyne is not trying to defend a competitive position; it is trying to finish selling two properties and close down. The closest thing to a "moat" here is legal and structural: the 2015 shareholder settlement sets a price floor at December 2014 appraised values, and the liquidation-basis accounting rules force the company to disclose, every quarter, its own updated estimate of what shareholders will get. That transparency is unusual for a company this small and this thinly traded, and it is the main reason this name is analyzable at all. **[Inference]**

## Risks & inversion

**Primary risk:** The Article 78 court appeal on the Flowerfield subdivision is not yet resolved (10-K FY2025 Item 3, Legal Proceedings), and the company itself says it does not expect to sell Flowerfield until that appeal is decided. An adverse ruling could force a redesign of the subdivision plan, delay the B2K sale beyond the 2028 target, and reduce the ultimate sale price below what is currently assumed in the disclosed NAV.

- **Financing risk:** management states in the 10-K FY2025 MD&A that it may need additional capital to fund operations through the 2028 target date; a dilutive raise would reduce per-share value even if the underlying real estate sells as planned.
- **Liquidity risk:** daily trading volume is often only a few thousand shares (Yahoo Finance trade history), so an investor may not be able to buy or sell a meaningful position near the quoted price.
- **Estimate risk:** the disclosed liquidation NAV in the 10-Q Q1 2026 and 10-K FY2025 has already been revised down once, from $13.91 per share (December 2024) to $11.76 per share (December 2025), as the timeline extended and costs were re-estimated; it could be revised down again.

**Lens failure mode (asset lens):** If the Article 78 appeal fails, the B2K deal collapses entirely, or the liquidation timeline extends materially past 2028 with rising costs, the dated NAV convergence this thesis depends on breaks down, and the stock could instead behave like a distressed, open-ended holding period with a shrinking payoff each year, similar to the bear scenario or worse.
