## What this business is
American International Group, Inc. (NYSE: **AIG**) is a global property-casualty insurer focused on General Insurance after the 2022 Corebridge Financial separation. **[Fact]** FY2025 net income attributable to AIG common shareholders was **$3.1 billion** and book value per share was **$76.44** (adjusted book value **$78.02** excluding Investments AOCI). **[Fact]** Adjusted after-tax income, the company's normalized earnings measure excluding investment marks, Fortitude Re noise, and Corebridge fair-value swings, was **$4.0 billion**, or about **$7.09 per diluted share** on **570.4 million** weighted average diluted shares. **[Fact]** General Insurance delivered **$2.3 billion** of underwriting income in FY2025 (combined ratio **90.1%**), supplemented by **$3.4 billion** of segment net investment income. **[Inference]** AIG is a post-turnaround compounder: it underwrites commercial and personal risk where multinational expertise creates pricing power, invests float at higher yields, and returns excess capital through aggressive repurchases (shares outstanding fell from **688.8M** in 2023 to **538.2M** at December 31, 2025).

---

## Why the market might be wrong
There is **no classic dated mispricing signal** (HK predictive attribute: **none**). **[Inference]** Generalists may anchor on GAAP net income volatility from realized investment gains and Corebridge mark-to-market moves, understating repeatable owner cash versus adjusted after-tax income of **$7.09 per share**. **[Fact]** The stock trades near **1.0 times** adjusted book value (**$78.02**), embedding continued combined ratio discipline and buybacks. **[Fact]** North America Commercial and International Commercial combined ratios were **86.8%** and **86.9%** in FY2025, while Global Personal was **99.0%** near breakeven. **[Inference]** The market may underweight the capital-return engine (share count down **22%** in two years) or overweight catastrophe tail risk without recognizing the **$920M** FY2025 cat charges already absorbed in results. Return here is **normalized owner cash at price**, not a simple price-to-book trade.

## Risks & inversion
**Primary risk:** Property catastrophe clustering (wildfires, windstorms) or casualty social inflation forces reserve strengthening and compresses commercial margins simultaneously.

**Inversion:** What would make this fail? Two consecutive years with General Insurance combined ratio above **95%**, adjusted book value per share declining, and management issuing dilutive equity rather than repurchasing.

**Lens failure mode:** Operating lens assumes normalized adjusted after-tax income persists; a soft commercial market plus elevated cat losses would break the owner-cash bridge faster than a generic compounder downturn.
