Brokerage firms, banks and other nominees can use their discretion to vote &#8220;uninstructed&#8221; shares with respect to matters that are considered to be &#8220;routine,&#8221; but not with respect to &#8220;non-routine&#8221; matters. Under applicable rules and interpretations, &#8220;non-routine&#8221; matters are matters that may substantially affect the rights or privileges of stockholders, such as elections of directors (even if not contested), mergers, stockholder proposals, executive compensation and certain corporate governance proposals, even if management-supported. Accordingly, your brokerage firm, bank or other nominee may vote your shares on Proposal Two, which is considered &#8220;routine,&#8221; without your instructions. Your brokerage firm, bank or other nominee may not, however, vote your shares on Proposal One without your instructions, which would result in a &#8220;broker non-vote&#8221; and your shares would not be counted as having been voted on Proposal One. Householding of Proxy Materials SEC rules permit companies and intermediaries such as brokers to satisfy delivery requirements with respect to two or more stockholders sharing the same address by delivering a single annual report and proxy statement or a single notice of internet availability of proxy materials addressed to those stockholders. This process, which is commonly referred to as &#8220;householding&#8221;, can reduce the volume of duplicate information received at households. While the Company does not household, a number of brokerage firms with account holders have instituted householding.
